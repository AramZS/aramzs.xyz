<h1>False Promises</h1>
<p>For more than 40 years, oil companies have been funding research at prestigious universities into climate change “solutions” that would not require the public to stop using oil and gas. Among their favored fixes is carbon capture and storage.</p>
<p>An investigation by ProPublica and Drilled has found that <a href="https://www.propublica.org/article/wedges-climate-research-bp-fossil-fuel-princeton">boosters of CCS have ignored evidence of the technology’s limitations</a>, or overstated its potential, and convinced the world it could be effective.</p>
<p>They’ve promoted this idea despite the fact that for CCS to work at the scale now envisioned, the world would need to devote almost unimaginable resources. Even if that were done, it might still prove impossible to trap so much carbon dioxide inside the earth.</p>
<p>Optimism has reigned, however, because small tests have worked and because slow global response to climate change has left few other options.</p>

<p>In 2008, the International Energy Agency projected that to stave off dangerous levels of warming, we would have to be burying around <b>1.6 billion tons</b>, or 1,600 megatons, of CO2 per year by 2025.</p>
<p>Since then, its optimistic projections have continued.</p>
<p>But deployment of the technology has never come close to those ambitions.</p>
<p>Right now, globally, we’re permanently burying less CO2 than a single large power plant can emit in a year.</p>
<p>Some experts point to the CO2 that gets pumped into the ground to help extract oil as proof CCS works. But that process, called enhanced oil recovery, isn’t designed to function the same way and isn’t monitored as stringently.</p>
<p>Global leaders are betting on carbon capture working now more than ever.</p>
<p>The models used in the latest United Nations assessment presume the technology succeeds.</p>
<p>IEA representatives and U.N. modelers say their projections reflect what the world has to do to achieve its goals of averting extreme warming.</p>
<p>To make CCS work, we would need to capture CO2 pollution in four ways:</p>

<p>The U.N. analysis now suggests that countries must inject 6 billion tons of CO2 underground each year by the middle of the century.</p>
<p>Getting 6 billion tons of CO2 a year out of the atmosphere, though, is a daunting task.</p>
<p>Imagine the neighborhoods and parks near oil, gas or coal-fired industrial plants.</p>
<p>We would need to add equipment to capture the CO2 from each facility, in some cases doubling its land footprint.</p>
<p>And we would need to devote about <b>768,000 square miles</b> of land worldwide to growing those carbon-absorbing plants.</p>
<p>That would cover an area roughly the size of Mexico — and compete for valuable land used to grow food or sustain forests.</p>
<p>If all of this works, and the CO2 is successfully captured, it must then be moved to a place where it can be buried.</p>
<figure><img src="https://static.propublica.org/projects/graphics/2026-ccs-explainer/pipeline/20260610-ccs-pipeline-bg.webp"/></figure>
<p>To cross the oceans, we would need at least <b>85 specially built tankers</b> to move the high-pressured gas. As of April, there were only three ships in the world equipped to do that.</p>
<p>Then, there is the challenge of finding a place to put 6 billion tons of CO2 a year.</p>
<p>Today, just 12 large-scale geologic reservoirs have attempted to permanently store CO2 pollution — but we would need more than 2,000 reservoirs of that size for CCS to work, each requiring years of study and engineering before it could be used.</p>
<figure><img src="https://static.propublica.org/projects/graphics/2026-ccs-explainer/globe-steps/20260611-globe-step-desktop-base.webp"/></figure>
<p>That means we would need to open a brand new geological waste site somewhere on the planet <b>every four days</b> for the next 25 years.</p>
<p>Every site would need constant monitoring for decades to ensure the CO2 doesn’t leak.</p>
<p>Even if this could be done, it would cost tens of trillions of dollars.</p>
<p>Right now, U.S. taxpayers are paying oil and gas companies $85 for every metric ton they put underground.</p>
<p>At that rate, by 2050, the world could be spending <b>half a trillion dollars</b> — more than China’s military budget, and 10 times more than the U.N.’s humanitarian and development aid budget — each year.</p>
<figure><img src="https://static.propublica.org/projects/graphics/2026-ccs-explainer/cost-chart/20260608-cost-chart-desktop.webp"/></figure>
<p>The few test sites that exist suggest that keeping carbon underground may not work at scale.</p>
<p>Since 1996, while the 12 large-scale geological storage projects have opened, plans for another 12 have been scrapped. Many CCS sites in operation — in Norway, Algeria, Australia and the U.S. — have been mired in problems, pointing to enormous challenges ahead.</p>
<figure><img alt="" src="https://static.propublica.org/projects/graphics/2026-ccs-explainer/crosssection-steps/20260608-problems-desktop-step-01.webp"/><figcaption>Some rock layers can hold far less CO2 than experts have estimated.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Finicky pipes and injection systems can get clogged or break down.</p>
<p>The rock that seals CO2 in place can crack, risking a leak. In one instance, injected CO2 caused the ground above it to bulge.</p>
<p>In another instance, CO2 escaped from an old oil industry well nearby.</p>
<p>Thorough, long-term monitoring can be expensive, but without it, such leaks could be missed.</p>
<p>Climate experts know about the costs, technical troubles and failures of CCS test projects.</p>
<p>Yet many of them have continued to boost the technology, even as they have downplayed solutions showing greater progress.</p>
<p>For example, the same modelers who overestimated the potential of geological carbon storage repeatedly underestimated solar power — one of the energy technologies that would allow more oil to remain in the ground.</p>

<p>In response to emailed questions, a spokesperson for the IEA said, “The IEA’s long-term modelling and scenarios are not designed to predict future deployment of technologies; the different scenarios we produce are intended to explore the potential implications and trade-offs of different policy, technology and investment choices.” The agency said that solar power has succeeded in part because of successful policy support for it, especially in China, and that CCS has lagged because of a lack of similar support. It added that CCS remains a part of the solution portfolio for industries that might otherwise be hard to decarbonize. The spokesperson noted that a record number of CCS projects are under construction.</p>
<p>Data for the actual CCS capacity derives from the IEA’s <a href="https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/data-product/ccus-projects-database">CCUS Projects Database</a>. The data comprises only projects that were completed and that permanently store CO2, rather than those that utilize CO2 for enhanced recovery of oil and gas or other uses, since those uses can create more carbon than they store or have looser requirements for monitoring.</p>
