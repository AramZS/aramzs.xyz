<p>Last year, <em>The Studio</em> dominated the comedy race at the Emmys, barnstorming every major category to the tune of a record-setting 23 nominations and 13 wins. With season two taking its time getting on the air, the field of potential Best Comedy nominees this year was looking rather stale — until <a href="https://www.vulture.com/tv/widows-bay/"><em>Widow’s Bay</em></a> premiered. The New England–set Apple TV series from creator Katie Dippold made landfall at the end of April to great reviews (97 percent fresh at Rotten Tomatoes) and the kind of word-of-mouth enthusiasm that is increasingly rare in an overstuffed TV landscape; Apple is happy enough that it just announced a <a href="https://deadline.com/2026/06/widows-bay-renewed-season-2-katie-dippold-deal-apple-tv-1236954233/">season-two pickup</a>. At a time when <em>Hacks</em>’s final season seems all but destined to receive a farewell Emmy in this category, with rivals like <em>The Bear</em>, <em>Abbott Elementary</em>, and <em>Only Murders in the Building</em> feeling too familiar to truly rock the boat, could a comedy-horror hybrid be the thing that actually jolts the race?</p>
<p><em>Widow’s Bay</em> concerns a fictional island town in New England whose mayor, Tom (Matthew Rhys), is sweating to get the same tourism dollars those fancier, think-they’re-so-great places like Martha’s Vineyard get. Only Martha’s Vineyard doesn’t have to deal with persistent rumors that it’s haunted by a centuries-old curse. A mainlander by birth, Tom is initially skeptical of the local lore until weird things start happening to him. Repeatedly. <em>Widow’s Bay</em> manages to scratch the same itch as recent idiosyncratic horror offerings like <em>Evil</em> and <em>Castle Rock</em>, municipal comedies like <em>Parks and Recreation</em>, and quaint small-town BritBox mysteries. Cross Mike Flanagan’s <em>Midnight Mass</em> with <a href="https://www.vulture.com/article/the-comeback-mrs-hatt-new-season-please.html"><em>Mrs. Hatt</em></a> and you’ve got it. A recent episode featuring Kate O’Flynn’s socially awkward mayoral aide Patricia running for her life from a Michael Myers–style killer is made no less thrilling by the fact that O’Flynn figured out the single funniest running form I have ever seen. The comedy of that isn’t undermined by the terror of Patricia’s plight; it’s enhanced by it.</p>
<p>“The genre mix is what is helping this achieve breakout status in my world,” one Emmys voter who works in production and development told me. They said they were recommended the show by a mix of friends and industry connections, plus the “insanely positive reviews.” Oscar-winning director Guillermo del Toro <a href="https://x.com/RealGDT/status/2060867858104750583">tweeted his praise</a> for <em>Widow’s Bay</em>, calling it the best streaming series he’d seen in a long time and “one of the most mesmerizing acts of narrative prestidigitation in Horror.” Polygon called the episode featuring Patricia’s aforementioned gumby-legged chase scene the <a href="https://www.polygon.com/widows-bay-episode-8-interview-patricia-boogeyman/">best slasher</a> (film or TV) in nearly a decade. Jeff Hiller, the <em>Somebody Somewhere</em> Emmy winner who plays mayoral staffer Dale, says he’s seeing the show’s popularity reflected in public, too. “I am occasionally stopped on the street for <em>Somebody Somewhere</em>, but it’s inconsistent. Not so with <em>Widow’s Bay</em>,” he told me this week. “People are stopping me constantly — occasionally even in the changing room at Nordstrom Rack.”</p>
<p>Hiller compared it to his experience when he guest-starred on <em>Pluribus</em>, another Apple TV Emmy hopeful from earlier this year. “When a show has lore like this,” he says, “people become so passionately invested. They like to ask questions and share their theories.” But while <a href="https://www.vulture.com/tv/pluribus/"><em>Pluribus</em></a> still feels like a homework show, where you have to listen to post-episode podcasts and read recaps to make sure you didn’t miss anything, <em>Widow’s Bay</em> is much easier to experience in the realm of pure pleasure. In recommending it to my in-laws or phlebotomist (the two classes of people I find myself recommending TV shows to most often), I don’t feel like I’m handing out a multilevel assignment.</p>
<p>The top-line talent on <em>Widow’s Bay</em> also offer compelling cases for Emmys attention. Dippold brings to the show a great comedic pedigree, having written the script for the Melissa McCarthy–Paul Feig collab <em>The Heat</em> and contributed uncredited punch-up work on their follow-up film, <em>Spy</em> (though you may know her best as the author and subject of the <a href="https://x.com/katiedippold/status/748582543583121408?lang=en">greatest Halloween tweet of all time</a>). And then there’s Matthew Rhys, who plays the mayor of Widow’s Bay, desperate to promote his island town’s tourist appeal while masking the rising terror that all the stories told about the island are true. The New York <em>Times</em> review declared Rhys’s performance his best since <em>The Americans</em>, and considering he won the Emmy for that series in 2018, that’s the kind of praise that will make voters sit up and listen.</p>
<p>Aside from being an Outstanding Actor in a Comedy hopeful for <em>Widow’s Bay</em>, Rhys — a five-time Emmy nominee all told — is also in contention in the Limited/Anthology category this year for playing a suspected murderer next door on Netflix’s <em>The Beast in Me</em>. The two roles underline the actor’s formidable versatility, all skittish panic in the former and arrogant sociopathy in the other. His marriage to his <em>Americans</em> co-star and likely 2026 Emmy nominee Keri Russell (<em>The Diplomat</em>) has “First Couple of American television” vibes. With last year’s Lead Actor in a Comedy winner, Seth Rogen, out of the race, Rhys’s top competition includes Jason Segel (<em>Shrinking</em>), Martin Short and Steve Martin (<em>Only Murders in the Building</em>), Jeremy Allen White (<em>The Bear</em>), Adam Brody (<em>Nobody Wants This</em>), and probably Steve Carell (<em>Rooster</em>). <em>The Bear</em> has been on a downward Emmys trajectory for two years, and both <em>Nobody Wants This</em> and <em>Only Murders</em> delivered buzzless seasons this year. There is absolutely room for Rhys to sneak in.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Hamish Linklater and three-time Emmy nominee Betty Gilpin — who are at the center of the season’s big flashback episode — could easily find themselves in the mix for Guest Actor and Guest Actress in a Comedy, respectively. But the real cause célèbre among the show’s cast is Kate O’Flynn, an English actress from the Mike Leigh company of performers who was already stealing scenes even before her character’s spotlight episode (“Beach Reads”) unlocked a level of dark pathos for Patricia. “In a community overflowing with crusty weirdos,” my colleague Nicholas Quah put it in our <a href="https://www.vulture.com/article/who-wont-but-should-be-nominated-for-an-emmy-in-2026.html">Don’t Not Consider List</a> two weeks ago, “Kate O’Flynn’s Patricia emerges as the undeniable standout.”</p>
<p>Behind the camera, <em>Widow’s Bay</em> also offers a strong case. Its horror elements provide the first season’s excellent roster of directors — including nine-time Emmy nominee Hiro Murai as well as filmmakers Ti West (<em>Pearl</em>) and Andrew DeYoung (<em>Friendship</em>) — ample opportunity to show off, while the unflagging comedic sensibility is a testament to the show’s writing. Even though 2025 writing/directing nominees <em>Hacks</em>, <em>Abbott</em>, and <em>The Bear</em> should all draw plenty of support once again, both the Writing for a Comedy and Directing for a Comedy categories have recognized at least one first-year show in 11 of the last 12 years.</p>
<p>With everything <em>Widow’s Bay</em> has going for it as an awards contender, this is still a show that wasn’t on anyone’s Emmys radar as of two months ago. Folks who follow the Oscars race have gotten used to the idea that studios save their big contenders for the end of the year, but the Emmys could never operate like that due to the strictures of the TV schedule: TV seasons mostly premiered in September, a handful of shows made mid-season debuts, and the big season-finale events all hit in May. Now that TV has become completely unmoored from the old release schedule, the release calendar is starting to look more like the Oscars, with many of the year’s prestige offerings choosing to wait until the season is nearly over to debut. The sweet spot for Emmys contenders seems to run from mid-March to mid-April; that’s the window that saw the premieres of past Comedy Series winners <em>The Studio</em>, <em>Veep</em>, and <em>The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel</em>, along with nominees <em>Barry</em>, <em>Silicon Valley</em>, <em>Jury Duty</em>, and <em>Palm Royale</em>. But an April 29 debut seems to be daring the Emmys voters to run out of time before they could fire up those <em>Widow’s Bay</em> screeners. Since 2009, when the Outstanding Comedy Series category expanded past five nominees, only one freshman series has premiered later than April 29 and scored an Outstanding Comedy Series nomination, and that was <em>Hacks</em> in 2021.</p>
<p><em>Widow’s Bay</em> is also facing internal competition from fellow Apple TV series <em>Shrinking</em> (a 2025 nominee) and <em>Margot’s Got Money Troubles</em>. The latter show features bona fide movie stars Michelle Pfeiffer and Elle Fanning, plus a plot — single mom starts an OnlyFans to make ends meet — that fits right in with the 21st-century trend of comedies that play like dramas. Vulture’s <a href="https://www.vulture.com/article/elle-fannings-onlyfans-comedy-is-coming-for-the-emmys.html">review</a> of the show’s South by Southwest premiere basically called <em>Margot</em>’s Emmy nominations locked. And while everyone I’ve talked to seems to think <em>Widow’s Bay</em>’s horror-comedy mix helps it stand out from the crowd, the reality is that horror-comedy has historically been a tough sell for the Emmys. Recent successes by <em>Wednesday</em> and especially <em>What We Do in the Shadows</em> gives some hope, but they’re outbalanced on the ledger by snubbed shows like <em>Los Espookys</em>, <em>Santa Clarita Diet</em>, <em>Swarm</em>, and even the comparatively un-scary <em>Ghosts</em>.</p>
<p>But as skittish as Emmys voters can be about horror, they also respond to popularity, and from what I’m hearing, <em>Widow’s Bay</em>’s popularity is real. It’s currently the No. 1 show on Apple TV’s “Most Popular Now” page, ahead of the star-studded new <em>Cape Fear</em> series and the aforementioned <em>Shrinking</em> and <em>Margot’s Got Money Troubles</em>, among others. That makes it easier to believe when, for example, <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/tv-and-radio/2026/jun/08/widows-bay-apple-tv-horror-comedy"><em>The Guardian</em></a> calls the show “the biggest word of mouth hit that television has had in years.” Meanwhile, the all-important industry buzz seems to be coalescing around <em>Widow’s Bay</em> too: One awards insider in L.A. told me they spoke to two showrunners on rival shows who both loved it.</p>
<p>“The word of mouth is mouthing,” said the Emmys voter I spoke to earlier. Now it’s a matter of whether the Emmys voters will be voting.</p>
