<p>A quarter-century ago, the United States eliminated measles. Today, it’s effectively lost that status.</p>
<p>So say an increasingly large number of scientists, including a public health expert who this week <a data-link="native" href="https://www.cidrap.umn.edu/measles/cidrap-op-ed-call-it-what-it-us-has-lost-its-hold-measles-elimination">published an essay</a> for one of the best known infectious disease agencies in the country.</p>
<p>“Call it what it is,” Jess Steier stated in the headline of her piece for CIDRAP, the Center for Infectious Disease Research and Policy. “The U.S. has lost its hold on measles elimination.”</p>
<p>Elimination is a formal infectious disease designation, and it’s awarded to a country when it has so thoroughly stopped spread of an illness like measles that it no longer is endemic — it doesn’t live in that country, and cases can only be imported. The United States eliminated measles in 2000, after decades of public health efforts that relied heavily on immunization.</p>
<p>But heated U.S. politics have seeped into public health over the past several years, in particular around vaccines, and immunization rates have nose-dived across the country. That’s led — as expected, infectious disease experts say — to large and repeated outbreaks of measles.</p>
<p>The measles that is still spreading now began as an outbreak in West Texas in January 2025 before jumping state lines. Last year, the United States reported 2,288 cases, the most since at least the 1990s; it’s already reported 2,104 cases in just the first half of this year.</p>
<p>Public health experts have warned since late last year that the country is at risk of losing its elimination status. That decision will be made by the Pan American Health Organization, an arm of the World Health Organization; it was supposed to make a call on the U.S. measles situation in April, but American officials asked for more time to collect data, and the decision will now be made at a meeting in November.</p>
<p>“Elimination is a specific defined thing. It’s not just a number of cases or a vibe,” said Dr. Jake Scott, an infectious disease expert at Stanford. “The reason a lot of us are saying that we’ve effectively lost elimination status is that the virus has been spreading person-to-person here, across multiple states, for more than a year without a clean break since Texas. We’re past the line (of elimination). But the official call doesn’t come until November.”</p>

<p>There are three clues that scientists study to determine if a disease has been eliminated. First is the size and continuity of the outbreaks — at the moment, the United States is looking at what appears to be a massive, unbroken chain of spread across the country, many scientists say.</p>
<p>Second is where the cases are coming from. Typically, about 40% of measles cases in the United States are imported. That means the person became infected in another country, then came to the U.S., where they may have seeded a small outbreak. Those numbers have changed dramatically: Only about 6% of measles cases are imported now, meaning the vast majority are homegrown.</p>
<p>And finally, scientists point to the virus’ genetic makeup as evidence that measles is spreading freely. The strain in that first Texas outbreak is almost identical to the strain circulating in an outbreak in Utah last month, “far too close to write off as a coincidence,” Steier said in her essay.</p>
<p>“What this pattern describes is a single family tree rather than a string of unrelated introductions from international travel,” she said.</p>
<h2>Consequences for the U.S.</h2>
<p>If the United States loses its elimination status in November, it doesn’t trigger any immediate fallout. “There’s no switch that flips, nothing bad happens to me or you,” Scott said. “There’s no sanctions and no funding cut, no travel ban. Where it matters is what it costs us going forward.</p>
<p>“This was one of the crown jewels of American public health,” he said. “A system we spent 40 years building has broken down, and it’s cracked and that’s not nothing. And it’s expensive to undo.”</p>
<p>Infectious disease experts say falling vaccination rates are largely to blame for the return of measles. When the disease is eliminated, imported cases that land in the U.S. hit a wall of immunity. Now there are wide gaps in that wall.</p>
<p>“Public health is fragile. If we stop doing things like vaccination, diseases come back,” said Dr. George Rutherford, a UCSF epidemiologist and infectious disease expert. “These organisms are still around, and the best way to prevent them is through immunization. And measles is one of the best vaccines we have.”</p>
<p>Immunization rates are tracked through school vaccinations. In California, 96% of kindergartners are immunized against measles, just above the 95% threshold to prevent spread of disease. Nationally, that number has hovered around 92% in recent years, but in many pockets around the country it’s now well below 80%, which leaves communities open to large outbreaks.</p>
<p>That means in California, there’s not much kindling for measles — a spark will land here and quickly be snuffed out. But in other parts of the country, one spark can set off a conflagration.</p>
<p>Cuts to public health have further complicated measles control, Scott said. “Our ability to see (measles) and stop it depends on surveillance, contact tracing, lab work — and a lot of that capacity has been cut,” he said. “You’ve got more fuel and fewer firefighters.”</p>
<p>Public health experts, though, say they don’t want to communicate that measles control is a lost cause. In California, for example, “we’ve earned a high level of protection, which is being tested and is holding pretty well,” said Dr. Matt Willis, the former Marin County public health officer who now writes a Substack on public health statewide called <a data-link="native" href="https://yourlocalepidemiologistca.substack.com/">Your Local Epidemiologist</a>.</p>
<p>“It’s super positive that we’re there, but it’s fragile,” Willis said.</p>
<p>Similarly, Scott believes that people can be convinced again to vaccinate their children. “Most parents who are wobbling (on vaccines) aren’t anti-vaccine ideologues,” he said, “they’re anxious and they’ve been handed a fire hose of misinformation. You have to meet vaccine hesitancy as a real question, and not as stupidity or with contempt.</p>
<p>“The honest message is a hopeful one: We’ve done this before, it’s absolutely winnable,” he said. “And every single vaccination is a brick in that wall.”</p>
