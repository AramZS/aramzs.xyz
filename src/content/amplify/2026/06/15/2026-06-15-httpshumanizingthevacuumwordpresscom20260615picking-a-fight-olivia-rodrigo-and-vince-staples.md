<p>Although I’m less enthusiastic about the hemmed-in Cry Baby than about <em>you seem pretty sad for a girl so in love </em>, it’s a smart, concise album from a rapper who should make a ridiculous sprawl now and again.</p>

<p>Vince Staples uses guitars like Tricky did on late ’90s touchstones like <em>Maxinquaye</em> and <em>Juxtapose</em>: sound effect as new direction, the way 2018’s <em>Big Fish Theory</em> used house beats. No mistaking his commitment: he hired a real band with whom he collaborated in the studio. The riffs rarely turn into solos, but the clang has propulsion: “The Running Man” might even show the influence of <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3TJC48BRBn8">Tones on Tail</a>. “The Big Bad Wolf” proves he can record whatever remains of trad hip-hop; Slick Rick offers grim counterpoint with his patented priss-voiced casualness. Alternating between not wanting fight systemic racism no more (“White Flag”) to calling out soldier zombies fighting in Gaza (“7 in the Morning”), he’s as confused as the rest of us. Fight or flee? “I’ve seen it all before, not far from par the course,” he reminds listeners. He can record intermittently powerful statements like <em>Cry Baby</em> while the rest of us piss around on liberal-left comment sections. Yet why is there a sense of complacency? It’s not that Staples shouldn’t rock out, it’s how clenched the rocking out sounds. As dourly accurate as <em>Cry Baby</em> is, it sounds as <a href="https://humanizingthevacuum.wordpress.com/2024/06/07/lifers-vince-staples-and-sonic-youth/">pat</a> as <em>Dark Times</em> and its predecessors. <em>Cry Baby</em> works for the converts, not swing voters.</p>
<p>Olivia Rodrigo — <em>you seem pretty sad for a girl so in love </em></p>

<p>Bearing no animus against balladry, I’ll acknowledge that she and Daniel Nigro have this time written better ones (“Cigarette Smoke,” “The Drop”), though when she scratches the ceiling of her range I bite my lip. Nevertheless, when I want to hear Olivia Rodrigo at her most potent I play “Expectations,” “U + Me = &lt;3,” “My Way,” and <em>especially</em> “Maggots for Brains,” the latter a burbling, belligerent delight. As fond as critics are of patting acts on their heads for “maturity” I’m happy to say that Rodrigo hasn’t changed much since 2021, which might mean that she was her own woman during the pandemic years and we’re only realizing it. Like Vince Staples she employs a terrific backing band that nods towards <em>One Beat</em>-era Sleater-Kinney and Greg Hawkes’ more baroque two-finger exercises, maybe a little No Doubt. Rodrigo, a master of modulated petulance, understands how good lyric writing requires an attention to how people talk. The right-onness of “It’s a little hard to stomach all your amateur moves/You know he’s with me, like obviously” isn’t the “insight,” it’s the “like” she slips before the adverb. It’s why <em>Fearless</em> and <em>Speak Now</em>-era Taylor Swift captured the imaginations of millions of young people. Bouquets go to “What’s Wrong With me,” sniffly self-loathing on which fairy godfather Robert Smith offers sympathetic sobs and an guitar solo. I’m a member of a Facebook group that dislikes this song and Smith. If ethnofascist quislings didn’t run social media, I’d report them. Meanwhile the message board I’m on isolated “carve our names into the car seat leather” as the best line she’ll ever write. That’s a tonic.</p>
