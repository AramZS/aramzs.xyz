(Jaime Alvarez/© David Maisel)</figcaption></figure><p>The National Gallery of Art’s latest exhibition, “Beneath the Surface: Mining and American Photography,” lands at a particularly apt moment, its compelling images reminding us how dependent the country is on natural resources — and the high cost of extracting them. </p><p>The show, billed as the first to chronicle the history of mining and extraction in the United States through the lens of photography, features roughly 150 works by more than 100 artists spanning 185 years. (After the National Gallery of Art, it will travel to the Milwaukee Art Museum and the Amon Carter Museum of American Art in Fort Worth.)</p><p>“Extraction is so deeply intertwined with the history of this country, in terms of the way it’s affected the environment, the landscape, communities, the demographics of the country — the movement of people,” says co-curator Diane Waggoner. “And photographers have been there from the very beginning picturing these activities.”</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cloudfront-us-east-1.images.arcpublishing.com/wapo/4NGK6AMJUNFHJFJCZZ6Q57NDBY.jpg"/><figcaption>Carleton E. Watkins's "Malakoff Diggins, North Bloomfield, Nevada County 1871." (Amon Carter Museum of American Art, Fort Worth, Texas)</figcaption></figure><p>“Beneath the Surface” brings together a range of historical photos and recent work by contemporary photographers. The forms of extraction depicted are varied, whether underground coal mining, panning for gold, open-pit copper and iron mines, or oil drilling.</p><p>Many of the older images are documentary in nature, including those commissioned by mining and transport companies to tout their infrastructure and those taken for government geological surveys. Among these are some of the earliest known underground photographs made in the United States, taken by <a href="https://americanart.si.edu/exhibitions/osullivan" rel="" target="_self" title="https://americanart.si.edu/exhibitions/osullivan">Timothy H. O’Sullivan</a> at the <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/opinions/2009/07/05/the-men-who-mined-silverplated-legends/05f56ea0-c54a-4152-80bb-ee110edfda70/" rel="" target="_self" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/opinions/2009/07/05/the-men-who-mined-silverplated-legends/05f56ea0-c54a-4152-80bb-ee110edfda70/">Comstock Lode silver mine</a> in Nevada in 1868.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cloudfront-us-east-1.images.arcpublishing.com/wapo/7IH5KS7O2ZD7RMP73XT3XHEQVE.jpg"/><figcaption>Portrait of a California gold miner with pick and shovel, c. 1855. (©2018 The Nelson Gallery Foundation.)</figcaption></figure><p>A few of the show’s early photographs were produced for individuals, such as the 1850s daguerreotype portraits of miners displayed in velvet-lined cases, while others were intended for mass consumption, including stereographs — pairs of photos taken from slightly different angles that appeared three-dimensional when viewed through a <a href="https://daily.jstor.org/in-the-stereoscope-another-world/" rel="" target="_self" title="https://daily.jstor.org/in-the-stereoscope-another-world/">stereoscope</a>, a popular 19th-century diversion. (Visitors to the exhibition can look through stereoscopes to experience the 3D effect.)</p><p>The exhibition also highlights how the field of photography itself has long relied on resource extraction, both in the early days of silver-coated copper plates used in daguerreotypes and more recently in digital technologies.</p><p>“You couldn’t have produced even that many photographs in the 19th century without the mass extraction of silver that was taking place in the American West,” Waggoner says. “Then even in the more contemporary moment where most photographers are … using digital cameras, they have copper, they have lithium. You need petroleum by-products for ink-jet prints. It’s very intertwined.”</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cloudfront-us-east-1.images.arcpublishing.com/wapo/PSTGWS5PLFD2DNN5AZOMJBU4EY.jpg"/><figcaption>Richard Avedon took this photo of Tom Stroud, an oil field worker in Oklahoma, in 1980. (© The Richard Avedon Foundation)</figcaption></figure><figure><img alt="" src="https://cloudfront-us-east-1.images.arcpublishing.com/wapo/Q4ZM3UGN5NBT5GBCNRYNCCV6YQ.jpg"/><figcaption>"Jim Faye Returning From the Mines, Harlan County, Appalachia," a Fons Ianelli photo from 1946. (Courtesy Steven Kasher Galler)</figcaption></figure><p>In the 20th century, many photographers expanded their focus to highlight social and labor issues — and the people and communities involved in mining, as seen in<a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/retropolis/wp/2018/09/02/the-incredible-photos-that-inspired-the-end-of-child-labor-in-america/" rel=""> Lewis Hine’s photos of child workers</a> and Depression-era scenes captured by New Deal photojournalists. Three poignant images in the exhibit portray miners’ daughters: one taken by Dorothea Lange in California in 1936, a 1938 photo by Marion Post Wolcott titled “Coal miner’s daughter carrying kerosene, company housing, Scotts Run, West Virginia,” and one by Paul Schutzer shot in Pennsylvania in 1961.</p><p>An even later image, a black-and-white portrait by Richard Avedon of an oil field worker in Oklahoma in 1980, is particularly arresting: The man’s face and bare arms are so covered with splatters and spills in all shades of black, gray and white that it’s hard to see his actual skin, yet he stands assertively, his eyes seeming to meet the viewer’s gaze.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cloudfront-us-east-1.images.arcpublishing.com/wapo/SGGRZD4E3ZHU5BDMZ2D2G2EY4E.jpg"/><figcaption>Will Wilson's "Shiprock Disposal Site, Shiprock, Navajo Nation" from 2020. (Courtesy of the artist and www.dineconnect.org)</figcaption></figure><p>Many works from recent decades examine the effects of resource extraction on the landscape and the environment. <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/arts-entertainment/2022/06/27/robert-adams-national-gallery/" rel="" target="_self" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/arts-entertainment/2022/06/27/robert-adams-national-gallery/">Robert Adams</a>’s stark black-and-white photo of burning oil sludge near Denver in 1974 depicts a billowing plume of thick, black smoke against a bleached-out desert landscape. Two photos by Diné (Navajo) artist Will Wilson are of a radioactive disposal site and remediation ponds in the Navajo Nation. The legacy of decades of uranium ore mining there included the <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/a-radioactive-legacy-haunts-this-navajo-village-which-fears-a-fractured-future/2020/01/18/84c6066e-37e0-11ea-9541-9107303481a4_story.html" rel="" target="_self" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/a-radioactive-legacy-haunts-this-navajo-village-which-fears-a-fractured-future/2020/01/18/84c6066e-37e0-11ea-9541-9107303481a4_story.html">1979 Church Rock uranium breach</a>, the largest nuclear waste spill in U.S. history.</p><p>Several images of environmental degradation are disturbingly beautiful, such as <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/goingoutguide/museums/national-museum-natural-history-unsettled-nature-art-review/2021/07/03/2e557b02-d505-11eb-ae54-515e2f63d37d_story.html" rel="" target="_self" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/goingoutguide/museums/national-museum-natural-history-unsettled-nature-art-review/2021/07/03/2e557b02-d505-11eb-ae54-515e2f63d37d_story.html">David Maisel</a>’s aerial shot of an emerald-green toxic waste pond associated with gold mining on the Carlin Trend in Nevada and Edward Burtynsky’s photo of the mesmerizing swirls of the 2010 Deepwater Horizon oil spill in the Gulf of Mexico. In their rich colors and textures, Belgian artist Mishka Henner’s set of 12 magnified satellite pictures of pumpjacks in California, Texas and Kansas are similarly eye-catching.</p><p>Two photos taken more than 125 years apart offer eerie parallels regarding the ecological costs of extraction. Carleton Watkins is best known for his majestic views of Yosemite, but his sepia-toned 1871 albumen print of a hydraulic gold-mining operation in Nevada County, California, shows a water pipeline crossing an eroded landscape stripped of trees. Richard Misrach’s 1998 “Swamp and Pipeline, Geismar, Louisiana” hauntingly captures a stretch of pipeline running through a wasteland of barren trees amid a pea-green swamp in what is known as “<a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/climate-environment/2021/11/28/epa-regan-environmental-justice-tour/" rel="" target="_self" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/climate-environment/2021/11/28/epa-regan-environmental-justice-tour/">Cancer Alley</a>.”</p><p>Such images cut two ways, powerfully evoking the incredible promise and the incredible peril of resource extraction in America the beautiful.</p><p><i><b>Beneath the Surface: Mining and American Photography</b></i><i> Through Aug. 23, National Gallery of Art. </i><a href="https://www.nga.gov/" rel="" target="_self" title="https://www.nga.gov/"><i>nga.gov</i></a><i>.</i></p><h4></h4></body></html>
