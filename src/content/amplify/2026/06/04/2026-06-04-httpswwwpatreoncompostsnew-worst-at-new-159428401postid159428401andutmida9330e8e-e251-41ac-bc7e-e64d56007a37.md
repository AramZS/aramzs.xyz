<h1>The "New Worst Columnist at <em>The New York Times</em>" is now running <em>60 Minutes</em></h1>
<p><img alt="Her?" src="https://c10.patreonusercontent.com/4/patreon-media/p/post/159428401/1afce01ea0784ed8b23b81176b47d938/eyJhIjoxLCJ3Ijo4MjB9/1.png?token-hash=hcy0WKaJucwEPVyDjf7ILIzWKE0mKe-yI08iPGIVcDg%3D&amp;token-time=1781395200"/></p>
<p>In general I try not to use this newsletter to revisit ancient grudges or to "dunk" on people about whom the discursive economy is already facing a supply glut of dunks. But I would feel remiss not to note <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share">the hiring of former</a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share"><em>New York Times</em></a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share">tech columnist Nick Bilton as the new executive producer of the venerable CBS newsmagazine</a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share"><em>60 Minutes</em></a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[CBS News editor-in-chief Bari] Weiss named Nick Bilton, a former New York Times technology columnist and a filmmaker who has directed and produced documentaries for HBO and Netflix, as her pick to lead the 58-year-old Sunday show. Mr. Bilton, who has never worked in traditional broadcast news, will replace Tanya Simon, who had been at the show for more than three decades.</p>
<p>CBS News also fired Cecilia Vega, the program’s first Latina correspondent, and Sharyn Alfonsi, whose segment on torture in Salvadoran prisons was <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/21/business/60-minutes-trump-bari-weiss.html">pulled off the air</a> abruptly last year by Ms. Weiss, who requested more reporting. It aired in full at a later date. Draggan Mihailovich, the executive editor of “60 Minutes” was also fired, as was Matthew Polevoy, a senior producer. [...]</p>
<p>Ms. Weiss said that she was drawn to Mr. Bilton because of his career telling stories on multiple platforms, including in print, behind the camera and in nonfiction books. She said that his coverage of technology had given him experience on how to navigate industries like broadcast news that are undergoing profound disruption.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>It is, I think, understating it to write, as the <em>Times</em> does, that Bilton "is an unconventional choice" to lead CBS News' flagship program, even given <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/media/2026/apr/30/60-minutes-sharyn-alfonsi-cbs-news">Weiss' brief to transform CBS News</a>. It's true that he is by trade an inclination a tech columnist (rather than a broadcast journalist or newsroom manager), but attentive consumers of tech media will remember him not just as any tech columnist but as, notoriously, and impressively given the competition, the dullest tech columnist of the century. (I say this, by the way, as <a href="http://patreon.com/c/maxread">someone with long experience in vapid tech columnry</a>.) <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/nick-bilton-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-1617069889">In 2014 my Gawker colleague Leah Finnegan declared him "the new worst columnist at</a> <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/nick-bilton-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-1617069889"><em>The New York Times,</em></a> <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/nick-bilton-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-1617069889">"</a> replacing Frank Bruni:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>His <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/07/10/fashion/on-social-media-memories-linger-after-a-breakup.html">first column</a> was about breakups in the digital age. Should he delete his Facebook profile after a divorce, or no? I know, I know, you are nervous about what might happen to his Facebook profile. So I will not tell you explicitly what he does, though I may subtly reveal it in a later example. But he did offer this keen observation: "Breaking up in the age of social media sometimes proves too much for people, and for that and other reasons, they delete their accounts altogether."</p>
<p>In his <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/07/10/fashion/on-social-media-memories-linger-after-a-breakup.html">second column</a>, he wonders if Hemingway would have written <em>Moveable Feast</em> if he had Twitter (probably? who cares?). Then he makes a resolution to try out some old technology: "For me, I'm making a change — albeit a small one. Last month I decided to try my own Facebook experiment. Rather than wake up in the morning and get lost on social media for an hour or more, I've started spending the early hours of my mornings reading a book."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Leah's post ruffled feathers when it was published because it's considered taboo in some journalistic subcultures to point out too clearly that prominent clubbable writers are insipid. And you might argue that it's unfair to judge Bilton entirely on his oeuvre as a <em>Times</em> columnist, a position designed to give its occupants a specific and subtle form of CTE. <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/media/pamela-paul-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-times">(He would later be eclipsed as "worst" by Pamela Paul</a>.) But nothing Bilton has done since the <em>Times</em> has suggested that Leah's verdict was unfair: His work as a <em>Vanity Fair</em> special correspondent, at least, was an improvement over his <em>Times</em> product, but that's about the only curve on which pieces like " <a href="https://www.vanityfair.com/news/2021/10/the-metaverse-is-about-to-change-everything">The Metaverse Is About to Change Everything</a>" (it didn't) or " <a href="https://www.vanityfair.com/news/2016/09/nick-bilton-on-theranos">How I Got to the Bottom of the Theranos Mess"</a> (he didn't) might receive a passing grade. <a href="https://www.breakermedia.com/p/60-minutes-execution-tbpn-s-sale-backstory-breaker-pod-finale-with-matt-friend">As Lachlan Cartwright writes today in Breaker</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Among people who previously worked with Bilton at Vanity Fair, the news was met with laughter followed by a flurry of texts between former colleagues, many of them punctuated with exclamation points and question mark emojis.</p>
<p>The tech writer was known for being MIA when big tech stories broke, generally lazy, and filing infrequently. Within the broader tech journalism world, Bilton is reviled for trying to claim credit for breaking open Theranos and <strong>Elizabeth Holmes</strong>' fraud (that title goes to former Wall Street Journal reporter <strong>John Carreyrou</strong>).</p>
<p>In a 2015 New York Times article ' <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2015/03/19/style/could-wearable-computers-be-as-harmful-as-cigarettes.html">The Health Concerns in Wearable Tech</a>', authored by Bilton, the Times added two lengthy editor's notes.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>So it's hard to look at his entire body of work and feel particularly inspired about what he might offer to <em>60 Minutes</em> as a reporter, leader, administrator, or journalistic mind.</p>
<p>But it's hard to tell what he offers to <em>60 Minutes</em> in any capacity, really. He may not be an experienced news producer, but he's also not a veteran propagandist or movement ghoul who might wholly refashion the show into the kind of evil ratings juggernaut that would be politically useful to Paramount CEO David Ellison. He's not well-respected enough as a working journalist to command the loyalty of his new staff, but he's also not a big-enough name as a brand to seem impressive externally. Did Weiss and Ellison simply want a <a href="https://www.hamiltonnolan.com/p/if-you-take-the-weasel-job-then-you">relatively pliable outsider nonentity to smooth their planned transformation</a>? (Surely if that were the case they could have hired a more experienced hatchet man?) Has the <em>60 Minutes</em> well has been so poisoned that Bilton was the biggest name they could convince take the job on the terms offered? Or do they actually think he will do a good job on the merits, such as they are? No answer is flattering to any of the people involved.</p>
<p><a href="https://nymag.com/intelligencer/article/how-nick-bilton-took-the-helm-at-60-minutes.html?utm_source=twitter&amp;utm_medium=s1&amp;utm_campaign=nym">Bilton, for his part, is unfazed, and characteristically thoughtful</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When I asked about his lack of TV experience, Bilton mentioned how he came to <em>Vanity Fair</em> as a reporter but also started hosting a podcast: “I’d never done that before,” he said. “I went off to Hollywood; I did documentaries. I’d never done that before. I went off to write screenplays and did them for Scorsese with the Rock and so on, and had never done that before. And I think it’s all storytelling at the end of the day.”</p>
</blockquote>

<h1>My reading habits, and: The return of the mailbag!</h1>
<p>Some housekeeping: I'm thinking about bringing back the once semi-regular mailbag column in which I answer questions and prompts from subscribers. (Or possibly just weaving more reader questions into the regular flow of the newsletter.) Have any queries for Read Max? Idle thoughts or burning dilemmas? Email me at <a href="mailto:maxread@gmail.com">maxread@gmail.com</a>, or drop them in the comments below. An interesting recent question from reader Evan:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em>Hi Max! Would love to know a bit more about your reading habits. Are you an eBook, eAudiobook, or print book reader? Are you a purchaser or library renter? Where do you get your recommendations? Apologies if you've answered these questions elsewhere but I am new to the Patreon and wanted to ask. Thanks!</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>I have a strong and durable preference for used and dog-eared mass market paperbacks, which I think are preferable aesthetically (they have the best covers), practically (they fit in your pocket), and spiritually (used books have a soul; new books don't, yet). I try to pick them up from local independents (Unnameable, The Strand, Better Read Than Dead) or book sales (I got a copy of the Daw <em>Nifft the Lean</em> for $3 at the Park Slope UMC sale this spring!) or popping into used bookstores on vacation. But if I really need something and it's not turning up I will order it from Biblio or Abebooks.</p>
<p>But I still read lots of new books in print, obtained from my other local indies (McNally, Center for Fiction, Greenlight) or from obliging publicists and authors, or from friends and neighbors. And in reality—as a function of convenience and necessity—I probably spend as much actual reading time reading ebooks on my phone, either through the library's Libby app or as EPUB files from NetGalley or direct from publishers. (I have also been known to track down ebook files for out-of-print books from LibGen or Anna's Archive.)</p>
<p>I listen to some audiobooks via Libby—thrillers and nonfiction—but usually only when I have a long drive or annoying task to complete. I don't personally check out many hard copy books out of the library these days, partly because I'm impatient and partly because I like having a big library at home, but we take a lot of books out for our five-year-old.</p>
<p>My absolute least favorite way to read anything is "on a PDF." Reading a PDF is as spiritually draining as reading a mass-market paperback is spiritually nourishing.</p>
<p>As for where I get my recommendations: Everywhere! Many of the best recs come from Read Max readers who vibe at the same frequency, as well as friends with whom I share taste, but I'm constantly adding to my "to read" list just based on suggestions I come across on X.com, Bluesky, Substack, etc. If you have a book (or a movie, for that matter) that you think I'd like, please drop me a line at <a href="mailto:maxread@gmail.com">maxread@gmail.com</a>.</p>

<h1>Where is the AI Homestar Runner?</h1>
<p>The comics writer Kieron Gillen posed an interesting question about AI and creativity on Bluesky yesterday:</p>
<p><a href="https://bsky.app/profile/kierongillen.bsky.social/post/3mmvgs45w5k2l"><img alt="" src="https://c10.patreonusercontent.com/4/patreon-media/p/post/159428401/899ae96539d74384965c768bc407ac9e/eyJhIjoxLCJ3Ijo4MjB9/1.png?token-hash=aIeto0Va8Y4aVyJ_bK5bx8Lm-I6h--gxMoyJWVMhgeY%3D&amp;token-time=1781395200"/></a></p>
<p>bsky.app</p>
<p>I've heard a lot of versions of this general sentiment—that any and all AI use, especially in creative domains, is being imposed from the top down, rather than embraced from the bottom up. But while it's certainly true that there's a terrifying amount of management-enforced AI in many fields, I just don't think it's true that there's no "ground-up indie" creativity being unlocked. There's an enormous amount of fascinating and creative indie/outsider AI art (if that's the right word) out there online already—you just have to check your TikTok and Instagram FYPs.</p>
<p>I'm thinking of, e.g., <a href="https://www.tiktok.com/@jonmud/video/7599897188797402398?lang=en">jonmud</a>, <a href="https://www.tiktok.com/@gangstasportivik/video/7634569653959888141">gangstasportivik</a>, and other weird and unforthcoming experiments in AI-generated video, many of them viewable alongside <a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/aivideo/top/?screen_view_count=1&amp;t=all">sloppier stuff at the r/aivideo subreddit</a>, as well as slightly more approachable and popular projects like <a href="https://www.youtube.com/c/thereiruinedit">thereiruinedit</a> and the "Balenciaga" videos that went viral three years (!!) ago. I don't want to wholly endorse any of this stuff as "great art," and the politics of its content, even setting aside the politics of the AI-generated form, are ... slippery, at best. But Gillen is here invoking Dinosaur Comics and XKCD, not Matthew Barney or Ryan Trecartin whatever, so I feel comfortable holding up something like <a href="https://www.tiktok.com/@a.i.solation/video/7612337372667071774">a.i.solation's "Shape Store" videos</a> in response.</p>
<p>Obviously, it's hard to say whether or not this stuff will "hold up" in the way that, say, Homestar Runner has, either as a going concern or as a cultural object worth revisiting. But it's hard, in considering that question, to disentangle the effects of the tool or medium—in this case, AI—from the distribution mechanism through which we encounter it—in this case, the FYP-oriented short-form video feed. Not only is it harder to build a sustainable serialized "practice" as an artist (or content creator) from a megaplatform app than it is from a regular homepage, the substance of what you're making—the creativity you're unlocking—is going to be shaped to the particular strictures of that feed. Just as some important portion of the loathing we feel for slop can be chalked up to the trough in which it appears, many of the common stylistic features of AI-generated content at this point in time are as much functions of the platforms for which that content is created, as they are manifestations of or inherent to the AI apps themselves.</p>
<p>Readers will remember that <a href="https://www.patreon.com/posts/shitposting-zone-155014300">I've been trying to make the case for AI as an incredible creative tool in a shitposting context for a long time now</a>, and I think it's good to recognize that, while the inexpensive consumer-facing tools we have right now are not going to generate video that rivals Hollywood features, they're already being used to realize strange ideas from outsiders and nonprofessionals—oftentimes specifically embracing the quirks and limitations of the tool and media. I leave to everyone else the question of whether or not this phenomenon counts as anti-gatekeeping democratization, anti-worker deskilling, or both.</p>

<h1>The "New Worst Columnist at <em>The New York Times</em>" is now running <em>60 Minutes</em></h1>
<p><img alt="Her?" src="https://c10.patreonusercontent.com/4/patreon-media/p/post/159428401/1afce01ea0784ed8b23b81176b47d938/eyJhIjoxLCJ3Ijo4MjB9/1.png?token-hash=hcy0WKaJucwEPVyDjf7ILIzWKE0mKe-yI08iPGIVcDg%3D&amp;token-time=1781395200"/></p>
<p>In general I try not to use this newsletter to revisit ancient grudges or to "dunk" on people about whom the discursive economy is already facing a supply glut of dunks. But I would feel remiss not to note <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share">the hiring of former</a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share"><em>New York Times</em></a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share">tech columnist Nick Bilton as the new executive producer of the venerable CBS newsmagazine</a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/28/business/media/nick-bilton-60-minutes-bari-weiss.html?smid=tw-share"><em>60 Minutes</em></a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[CBS News editor-in-chief Bari] Weiss named Nick Bilton, a former New York Times technology columnist and a filmmaker who has directed and produced documentaries for HBO and Netflix, as her pick to lead the 58-year-old Sunday show. Mr. Bilton, who has never worked in traditional broadcast news, will replace Tanya Simon, who had been at the show for more than three decades.</p>
<p>CBS News also fired Cecilia Vega, the program’s first Latina correspondent, and Sharyn Alfonsi, whose segment on torture in Salvadoran prisons was <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/21/business/60-minutes-trump-bari-weiss.html">pulled off the air</a> abruptly last year by Ms. Weiss, who requested more reporting. It aired in full at a later date. Draggan Mihailovich, the executive editor of “60 Minutes” was also fired, as was Matthew Polevoy, a senior producer. [...]</p>
<p>Ms. Weiss said that she was drawn to Mr. Bilton because of his career telling stories on multiple platforms, including in print, behind the camera and in nonfiction books. She said that his coverage of technology had given him experience on how to navigate industries like broadcast news that are undergoing profound disruption.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>It is, I think, understating it to write, as the <em>Times</em> does, that Bilton "is an unconventional choice" to lead CBS News' flagship program, even given <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/media/2026/apr/30/60-minutes-sharyn-alfonsi-cbs-news">Weiss' brief to transform CBS News</a>. It's true that he is by trade an inclination a tech columnist (rather than a broadcast journalist or newsroom manager), but attentive consumers of tech media will remember him not just as any tech columnist but as, notoriously, and impressively given the competition, the dullest tech columnist of the century. (I say this, by the way, as <a href="http://patreon.com/c/maxread">someone with long experience in vapid tech columnry</a>.) <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/nick-bilton-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-1617069889">In 2014 my Gawker colleague Leah Finnegan declared him "the new worst columnist at</a> <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/nick-bilton-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-1617069889"><em>The New York Times,</em></a> <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/nick-bilton-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-1617069889">"</a> replacing Frank Bruni:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>His <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/07/10/fashion/on-social-media-memories-linger-after-a-breakup.html">first column</a> was about breakups in the digital age. Should he delete his Facebook profile after a divorce, or no? I know, I know, you are nervous about what might happen to his Facebook profile. So I will not tell you explicitly what he does, though I may subtly reveal it in a later example. But he did offer this keen observation: "Breaking up in the age of social media sometimes proves too much for people, and for that and other reasons, they delete their accounts altogether."</p>
<p>In his <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/07/10/fashion/on-social-media-memories-linger-after-a-breakup.html">second column</a>, he wonders if Hemingway would have written <em>Moveable Feast</em> if he had Twitter (probably? who cares?). Then he makes a resolution to try out some old technology: "For me, I'm making a change — albeit a small one. Last month I decided to try my own Facebook experiment. Rather than wake up in the morning and get lost on social media for an hour or more, I've started spending the early hours of my mornings reading a book."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Leah's post ruffled feathers when it was published because it's considered taboo in some journalistic subcultures to point out too clearly that prominent clubbable writers are insipid. And you might argue that it's unfair to judge Bilton entirely on his oeuvre as a <em>Times</em> columnist, a position designed to give its occupants a specific and subtle form of CTE. <a href="https://www.gawkerarchives.com/media/pamela-paul-is-the-new-worst-columnist-at-the-new-york-times">(He would later be eclipsed as "worst" by Pamela Paul</a>.) But nothing Bilton has done since the <em>Times</em> has suggested that Leah's verdict was unfair: His work as a <em>Vanity Fair</em> special correspondent, at least, was an improvement over his <em>Times</em> product, but that's about the only curve on which pieces like " <a href="https://www.vanityfair.com/news/2021/10/the-metaverse-is-about-to-change-everything">The Metaverse Is About to Change Everything</a>" (it didn't) or " <a href="https://www.vanityfair.com/news/2016/09/nick-bilton-on-theranos">How I Got to the Bottom of the Theranos Mess"</a> (he didn't) might receive a passing grade. <a href="https://www.breakermedia.com/p/60-minutes-execution-tbpn-s-sale-backstory-breaker-pod-finale-with-matt-friend">As Lachlan Cartwright writes today in Breaker</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Among people who previously worked with Bilton at Vanity Fair, the news was met with laughter followed by a flurry of texts between former colleagues, many of them punctuated with exclamation points and question mark emojis.</p>
<p>The tech writer was known for being MIA when big tech stories broke, generally lazy, and filing infrequently. Within the broader tech journalism world, Bilton is reviled for trying to claim credit for breaking open Theranos and <strong>Elizabeth Holmes</strong>' fraud (that title goes to former Wall Street Journal reporter <strong>John Carreyrou</strong>).</p>
<p>In a 2015 New York Times article ' <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2015/03/19/style/could-wearable-computers-be-as-harmful-as-cigarettes.html">The Health Concerns in Wearable Tech</a>', authored by Bilton, the Times added two lengthy editor's notes.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>So it's hard to look at his entire body of work and feel particularly inspired about what he might offer to <em>60 Minutes</em> as a reporter, leader, administrator, or journalistic mind.</p>
<p>But it's hard to tell what he offers to <em>60 Minutes</em> in any capacity, really. He may not be an experienced news producer, but he's also not a veteran propagandist or movement ghoul who might wholly refashion the show into the kind of evil ratings juggernaut that would be politically useful to Paramount CEO David Ellison. He's not well-respected enough as a working journalist to command the loyalty of his new staff, but he's also not a big-enough name as a brand to seem impressive externally. Did Weiss and Ellison simply want a <a href="https://www.hamiltonnolan.com/p/if-you-take-the-weasel-job-then-you">relatively pliable outsider nonentity to smooth their planned transformation</a>? (Surely if that were the case they could have hired a more experienced hatchet man?) Has the <em>60 Minutes</em> well has been so poisoned that Bilton was the biggest name they could convince take the job on the terms offered? Or do they actually think he will do a good job on the merits, such as they are? No answer is flattering to any of the people involved.</p>
<p><a href="https://nymag.com/intelligencer/article/how-nick-bilton-took-the-helm-at-60-minutes.html?utm_source=twitter&amp;utm_medium=s1&amp;utm_campaign=nym">Bilton, for his part, is unfazed, and characteristically thoughtful</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>When I asked about his lack of TV experience, Bilton mentioned how he came to <em>Vanity Fair</em> as a reporter but also started hosting a podcast: “I’d never done that before,” he said. “I went off to Hollywood; I did documentaries. I’d never done that before. I went off to write screenplays and did them for Scorsese with the Rock and so on, and had never done that before. And I think it’s all storytelling at the end of the day.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p><img alt="" src="https://c10.patreonusercontent.com/4/patreon-media/p/post/159428401/654520535e1841f8a943938e15581e63/eyJhIjoxLCJ3Ijo4MjB9/1.png?token-hash=tFIAVjbsbNQwh9Hu10leggoMxSa2Z7otDHP6EYDSrfE%3D&amp;token-time=1781395200"/></p>
<h1>My reading habits, and: The return of the mailbag!</h1>
<p>Some housekeeping: I'm thinking about bringing back the once semi-regular mailbag column in which I answer questions and prompts from subscribers. (Or possibly just weaving more reader questions into the regular flow of the newsletter.) Have any queries for Read Max? Idle thoughts or burning dilemmas? Email me at <a href="mailto:maxread@gmail.com">maxread@gmail.com</a>, or drop them in the comments below. An interesting recent question from reader Evan:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em>Hi Max! Would love to know a bit more about your reading habits. Are you an eBook, eAudiobook, or print book reader? Are you a purchaser or library renter? Where do you get your recommendations? Apologies if you've answered these questions elsewhere but I am new to the Patreon and wanted to ask. Thanks!</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>I have a strong and durable preference for used and dog-eared mass market paperbacks, which I think are preferable aesthetically (they have the best covers), practically (they fit in your pocket), and spiritually (used books have a soul; new books don't, yet). I try to pick them up from local independents (Unnameable, The Strand, Better Read Than Dead) or book sales (I got a copy of the Daw <em>Nifft the Lean</em> for $3 at the Park Slope UMC sale this spring!) or popping into used bookstores on vacation. But if I really need something and it's not turning up I will order it from Biblio or Abebooks.</p>
<p>But I still read lots of new books in print, obtained from my other local indies (McNally, Center for Fiction, Greenlight) or from obliging publicists and authors, or from friends and neighbors. And in reality—as a function of convenience and necessity—I probably spend as much actual reading time reading ebooks on my phone, either through the library's Libby app or as EPUB files from NetGalley or direct from publishers. (I have also been known to track down ebook files for out-of-print books from LibGen or Anna's Archive.)</p>
<p>I listen to some audiobooks via Libby—thrillers and nonfiction—but usually only when I have a long drive or annoying task to complete. I don't personally check out many hard copy books out of the library these days, partly because I'm impatient and partly because I like having a big library at home, but we take a lot of books out for our five-year-old.</p>
<p>My absolute least favorite way to read anything is "on a PDF." Reading a PDF is as spiritually draining as reading a mass-market paperback is spiritually nourishing.</p>
<p>As for where I get my recommendations: Everywhere! Many of the best recs come from Read Max readers who vibe at the same frequency, as well as friends with whom I share taste, but I'm constantly adding to my "to read" list just based on suggestions I come across on X.com, Bluesky, Substack, etc. If you have a book (or a movie, for that matter) that you think I'd like, please drop me a line at <a href="mailto:maxread@gmail.com">maxread@gmail.com</a>.</p>

<h1>Where is the AI Homestar Runner?</h1>
<p>The comics writer Kieron Gillen posed an interesting question about AI and creativity on Bluesky yesterday:</p>
<p><a href="https://bsky.app/profile/kierongillen.bsky.social/post/3mmvgs45w5k2l"><img alt="" src="https://c10.patreonusercontent.com/4/patreon-media/p/post/159428401/899ae96539d74384965c768bc407ac9e/eyJhIjoxLCJ3Ijo4MjB9/1.png?token-hash=aIeto0Va8Y4aVyJ_bK5bx8Lm-I6h--gxMoyJWVMhgeY%3D&amp;token-time=1781395200"/></a></p>
<p>bsky.app</p>
<p>I've heard a lot of versions of this general sentiment—that any and all AI use, especially in creative domains, is being imposed from the top down, rather than embraced from the bottom up. But while it's certainly true that there's a terrifying amount of management-enforced AI in many fields, I just don't think it's true that there's no "ground-up indie" creativity being unlocked. There's an enormous amount of fascinating and creative indie/outsider AI art (if that's the right word) out there online already—you just have to check your TikTok and Instagram FYPs.</p>
<p>I'm thinking of, e.g., <a href="https://www.tiktok.com/@jonmud/video/7599897188797402398?lang=en">jonmud</a>, <a href="https://www.tiktok.com/@gangstasportivik/video/7634569653959888141">gangstasportivik</a>, and other weird and unforthcoming experiments in AI-generated video, many of them viewable alongside <a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/aivideo/top/?screen_view_count=1&amp;t=all">sloppier stuff at the r/aivideo subreddit</a>, as well as slightly more approachable and popular projects like <a href="https://www.youtube.com/c/thereiruinedit">thereiruinedit</a> and the "Balenciaga" videos that went viral three years (!!) ago. I don't want to wholly endorse any of this stuff as "great art," and the politics of its content, even setting aside the politics of the AI-generated form, are ... slippery, at best. But Gillen is here invoking Dinosaur Comics and XKCD, not Matthew Barney or Ryan Trecartin whatever, so I feel comfortable holding up something like <a href="https://www.tiktok.com/@a.i.solation/video/7612337372667071774">a.i.solation's "Shape Store" videos</a> in response.</p>
<p>Obviously, it's hard to say whether or not this stuff will "hold up" in the way that, say, Homestar Runner has, either as a going concern or as a cultural object worth revisiting. But it's hard, in considering that question, to disentangle the effects of the tool or medium—in this case, AI—from the distribution mechanism through which we encounter it—in this case, the FYP-oriented short-form video feed. Not only is it harder to build a sustainable serialized "practice" as an artist (or content creator) from a megaplatform app than it is from a regular homepage, the substance of what you're making—the creativity you're unlocking—is going to be shaped to the particular strictures of that feed. Just as some important portion of the loathing we feel for slop can be chalked up to the trough in which it appears, many of the common stylistic features of AI-generated content at this point in time are as much functions of the platforms for which that content is created, as they are manifestations of or inherent to the AI apps themselves.</p>
<p>Readers will remember that <a href="https://www.patreon.com/posts/shitposting-zone-155014300">I've been trying to make the case for AI as an incredible creative tool in a shitposting context for a long time now</a>, and I think it's good to recognize that, while the inexpensive consumer-facing tools we have right now are not going to generate video that rivals Hollywood features, they're already being used to realize strange ideas from outsiders and nonprofessionals—oftentimes specifically embracing the quirks and limitations of the tool and media. I leave to everyone else the question of whether or not this phenomenon counts as anti-gatekeeping democratization, anti-worker deskilling, or both.</p>
