<div>SULLIVAN,   <a data-type="place" href="https://www.wsj.com/topics/place/maine">Maine</a>—One of this year’s biggest political gambles began at 5:30 a.m. one day last July, when liberal activists Daniel Moraff and Leanne Fan showed up at the home of Graham Platner, a combat veteran and oyster farmer in this forested town. </div><p></p>
<p>Moraff and Fan had no ties to Maine or to the Democratic Party’s election machinery, which made their mission all the more audacious: to recruit a working-class candidate to run for the U.S. Senate on a populist platform. The idea, Platner recalled telling his visitors, was “quite literally the weirdest thing I’ve ever heard.”</p>
<p>Nearly a year later, it has become clear just how big a gamble it was. Platner, 41 years old, has improbably become the Democratic Party’s presumed nominee in one of its most important Senate elections. In doing so, he has saddled the party with the risks of an untested candidate who has a messy life story.</p>

<div>Platner’s campaign recently acknowledged that his wife discovered   <a data-type="link" href="https://www.wsj.com/politics/elections/graham-platners-wife-flagged-sexually-explicit-texts-to-his-senate-campaign-628ec832?mod=article_inline">sexually explicit texts with several women</a> on his phone. A woman who dated him just over a decade ago, Lyndsey Fifield, accused him of abuse in an online post Friday. </div><p></p>
<p>Platner has been forced to explain remarks about women’s role in sexual assault that he made online when he was younger, and he covered up a tattoo he wore on his chest for 18 years, saying he learned only last year that it was a symbol adopted by the Nazi’s SS paramilitary.</p>
<div>A bearded former Marine machine-gunner, Platner has drawn large crowds with a promise to take on a political system he says exists to help the wealthy while rural hospitals close and his neighbors take extra shifts at   <a data-type="company" href="https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/WMT">Walmart</a>. He has acknowledged being a frequent trash-talker online and a poor romantic partner when he suffered from post-traumatic stress disorder after returning from combat tours, a time that coincided with when he dated Fifield. </div><p></p>
<p>In an interview shortly before her accusations became public, Platner said he never harmed anyone during that time. “No, no, no. Good lord, no,” he said. He has since denied Fifield’s claims that he acted abusively.</p>

<p>The revelations have left Moraff and his partners, relative newcomers who have run a handful of campaigns, facing questions about whether they vetted Platner closely enough. Moraff said the campaign hired a firm to check his candidate’s background, which found some of the social-media posts but not all of them. Nor did it flag the tattoo, or the sexually explicit text messages.</p>
<p>“We paid a nice firm a whole chunk of money and got some stuff back. Some of what you’ve seen on the news we got back. Other stuff we didn’t,” Moraff said in an interview in late May. Based on what it revealed, “I said none of this will or should stop him from becoming a U.S. senator.”</p>
<p>In an interview after Fifield’s allegations surfaced, Moraff said it was up to the people of Maine to decide whether he had sufficiently investigated Platner.</p>

<h3 data-type="hed">A financial cushion</h3>
<p>In rising from political obscurity to challenge longtime Republican Sen. Susan Collins, Platner has left Maine voters with two disparate impressions.</p>
<p>To many, he is an authentic working-class voice, someone who traded the classroom for combat, then went through a dark and admittedly alcohol-heavy re-entry into civilian life before reconnecting with his Maine roots. Others see a dilettante who benefited from family money as he moved through his 20s and 30s, all while creating a cache of disquieting social-media posts that have become fodder for his opponents. They point out that Platner’s father financed his mortgage.</p>
<p>“His parents had enough money to help him, and not everybody has that,” said Peggy Schaffer, a former vice chairwoman of the Maine Democratic Party. “It doesn’t make him a working-class hero.”</p>

<div>Platner never imagined he would find himself   <a data-type="link" href="https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/senators-privately-ask-platner-whether-new-allegations-will-emerge-412891de?mod=article_inline">facing such scrutiny</a> on the July morning when Moraff and Fan showed up to recruit him. They found him dressed in shorts and a tank top for his workday on the water, a man who had never run for office. His only official titles were harbor master and chairman of the planning board for his town of 1,200 residents across the bay from Acadia National Park. </div><p></p>
<p>Platner sat for a series of interviews with The Wall Street Journal, which took place in February, March and late May, days before the text messages with women emerged. He described how he thought his lot in life was always to carry a gun for his country before he learned to earn a living pulling oysters from the productive waters of Frenchman Bay, near his childhood home.</p>
<p>“Of course, I have a cushion,” he said in the May interview when asked about his father providing the mortgage. “I don’t think that means I don’t work for a living. I don’t think it somehow offsets the fact that I did four tours in the infantry and served my country in intense combat.”</p>
<p>Jobs that “put calluses on your hands—those are the only jobs I’ve ever had.”</p>
<p>Along the way, Platner developed a sharp critique of a political system in which he says both parties have betrayed the working class. He wants to boost labor unions, break up alleged corporate monopolies and raise taxes on the wealthy to fund more services, an agenda in the mold of independent Sen. Bernie Sanders of Vermont, an early supporter. At the top of that list is providing government-funded healthcare to everyone.</p>

<p>“I am lucky enough that in my four combat deployments, I was blown up enough times that the VA thinks I deserve healthcare. And that is a ridiculous statement to make,” he told a weeknight crowd of about 300 earlier this year in the small town of Monmouth, Maine.</p>
<h3 data-type="hed">Warfare and web posts</h3>
<p>Platner always wanted to be a soldier, an ambition he says he can’t fully explain. He was raised in Sullivan, the child of divorced parents who were active in Democratic Party politics. His father was a lawyer, and his mother worked for food companies.</p>
<p>Platner’s grandfather, Warren, was a noted architect who helped design the interior of Windows on the World, the restaurant at the top of the World Trade Center that was destroyed in the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001. A chair he designed is known as the Platner chair and still sells, with its ottoman, for as much as $18,000.</p>
<p>At four years old, Platner said, he knew all the words of the Marine Corps hymn. He joined battle re-enactments at Gettysburg and Antietam. He was a wrestler and a theater kid, playing the leading man in a high-school production of “My Fair Lady.”</p>

<p>Platner for a short time attended The Hotchkiss School, a boarding school in Connecticut, but says he was soon kicked out for failing to attend class. He then went to another private school, an hour’s ride from home. His parents paid for the local private school, his campaign says; at the pricier Hotchkiss, he had financial aid.</p>
<p>When graduation came, Platner joined the Marines rather than go to college despite having earlier protested the Iraq war. After three tours of duty in Iraq and one in Afghanistan, he went back to Afghanistan as a contractor on the security detail for a U.S. ambassador.</p>
<p>Today, he strains to explain his decision to enlist. “You can think the war is dumb, and also like, kind of not want to miss it, you know what I mean?” he said in February.</p>

<p>Re-entering civilian life, Platner became deeply disillusioned with war. He was tending bar and taking college classes in Washington, D.C., thinking he would earn the credentials to work in an intelligence service or federal law enforcement. He was also suffering, he said, from herniated discs and torn rotator cuffs.</p>
<p>“I was coming to the realization that I had spent all of my 20s essentially fighting in wars that I thought were a waste of time, and that’s a hard thing to come to grips with,” he said in February.</p>
<p>He dropped out of college, then returned, then left again, he said, ending up about 29 credits short of a bachelor’s degree. In 2011, he was arrested for driving while intoxicated.</p>
<p>In 2017, he wrote on Reddit about masturbating in public toilets. In 2020, he called police “bastards.” Writing about sexual assault in 2013, he wrote: “How about people just take some responsibility for themselves and not get so f——d up they wind up having sex with someone they don’t mean to?”</p>
<p>Platner said his disaffection left him disconnected from others. “I was not the best romantic partner, in the sense that I was emotionally unavailable,” he said in May.</p>
<p>It was around that period that he dated Fifield, a Republican former campaign aide whose accusation was published by the New York Times in an article that included claims of bad behavior from other women. It quoted Fifield as saying Platner had grabbed her in ways that hurt but didn’t cause injury. She didn’t comment for this article beyond her social-media post.</p>
<p>About nine years ago, an offer came to return to Maine and take over a friend’s oyster farm. His father gave him a $200,000 mortgage, covering the $205,000 cost of a home just off the bay. He met his wife, Amy Gertner, took a role in town government and joined community groups supporting food banks and other causes.</p>

<p>Platner’s main income is a veterans disability pension of about $4,800 a month. He says he repays his father $954.83 a month for the mortgage. A financial disclosure form connected to his Senate campaign lists his mother’s restaurant as the only major customer paying his oyster business. Platner said he also sells his oysters elsewhere.</p>
<h3 data-type="hed">Trouble hits</h3>
<p>In August, Platner introduced himself to Maine in a campaign video that put him on the political map. “We have watched this state become essentially unlivable for working-class people. And it makes me deeply angry,” he says while piloting his oyster boat. The video drew 2.5 million viewers within 24 hours, the campaign says.</p>
<p>Then trouble landed. Platner had removed many of his social-media posts from Reddit, but some of them found their way into the media. In October, Platner says, word of his chest tattoo was spreading. He went on the liberal podcast Pod Save America to say he hadn’t understood its history when he got the tattoo with fellow Marine machine-gunners to commemorate that they had survived combat in Iraq.</p>
<p>Voters meanwhile flocked to his events, and polls and fundraising reflected strong support. Eventually, his main rival in the Democratic primary, Gov. Janet Mills, suspended her campaign. Both candidates are still on the June 9 primary ballot.</p>

<p>As the campaign was starting, Platner’s wife told aides that she had discovered sexually explicit texts with several women on his phone after they had been married. The aides decided the texts were a private matter and continued with their plans. Platner has criticized the media for its reporting, saying they “are going to run gossip instead of wanting to talk about the things that actually matter.”</p>
<p>Morris Katz, a New York-based ad maker who helped recruit Platner, said the Democratic Party needed candidates who come from outside traditional politics. Some will have complicated backgrounds.</p>
<p>“If you believe that we should have people who never before thought they’d run for office,” he said, “they will have said things that they will have regretted, especially as a new generation that’s entire history of every thought they’ve had is recorded on social media.”</p>
<p>Genevieve McDonald, a former political director for Platner’s campaign who resigned after his Reddit posts became public, said the campaign failed to conduct an adequate vetting operation. “This was a large part of why I quit,” she said. “I trusted Graham and his campaign to have done oppo research and cleared him.”</p>
<p>Platner’s social-media posts have come up on the campaign trail, with some voters pressing him about his comments suggesting that women “take responsibility” for avoiding sexual assault by not getting drunk.</p>
<p>“Everything you say, all the things I know about you, is who I would vote for,” Robert McLaughlin, 86, told Platner at a campaign event in Skowhegan in March. “But I am troubled. I am undecided, because of what you have on record on Reddit.” McLaughlin said that he had counseled people in abusive relationships and that he wasn’t sure that Platner had truly come to a different view of women.</p>
<p>“I have not always been the man I am today,” Platner responded.</p>
<p>McLaughlin now says that the recent revelations about women tell him that “the character traits of Graham Platner are unacceptable.” He is weighing whether to skip the November election. His wife, Cynthia Robertson, had campaigned door-to-door for Platner but now says she will vote for him only to defeat Collins, hoping the party eventually replaces him.</p>
