<p><em>At the heart of AI applications to scholarly communication are processes of summarisation and the ranking of “too much” information. Considering how unequal dynamics of attention, such as the Matthew effect, have shaped the existing scholarly literature, <strong>Jefferson Pooley</strong></em><em>argues academic AI tools will smuggle these biases back into new academic hierarchies in ways that are increasingly difficult to audit.</em></p>
<p>In the last year and a half, the giant U.S. AI companies have released tools with indistinguishable names: <a href="https://gemini.google/overview/deep-research/">Deep Research</a> (Google), <a href="https://support.claude.com/en/articles/11088861-using-research-on-claude">Research</a> (Anthropic), <a href="https://openai.com/index/introducing-deep-research/">deep research</a> (OpenAI), <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-365/blog/2025/03/25/introducing-researcher-and-analyst-in-microsoft-365-copilot/">Researcher</a> (Microsoft), and yes… <a href="https://www.perplexity.ai/hub/blog/introducing-perplexity-deep-research">Deep Research</a> (Perplexity). In the highly profitable world of scholarly publishing, meanwhile, some of the biggest players have launched their own products, based on their proprietary databases of the academic literature: <a href="https://www.elsevier.com/products/scopus/scopus-ai">AI Discovery</a> (Elsevier), <a href="https://clarivate.com/academia-government/scientific-and-academic-research/research-discovery-and-referencing/web-of-science/web-of-science-research-assistant/">Web of Science Research Assistant</a> (Clarivate), and <a href="https://www.dimensions.ai/discover-dimensions-ai-assistant/">Dimensions AI Assistant</a> (Digital Science). What these two bundles of tools share is the promise to summarise and cite. They each return paragraphs of distillations, complete with links to apparent sources.</p>
<p>The AI companies and publishers draw on overlapping bodies of knowledge. The products from Elsevier and company leverage the peer-reviewed literature, while the various Deep Researches cite not just academic work but reports, Wikipedia, and posts on the open web. But the tools arrive at a similar place: summary and citations.</p>
<blockquote> <p>At the core of all these tools is a series of verbs: to surface, to rank, to summarise, and to recommend</p> </blockquote>
<p>Even the marketing is hard to distinguish. Google <a href="https://gemini.google/overview/deep-research">pitches</a> its Gemini-based tool as “your personal research assistant.” OpenAI <a href="https://openai.com/index/introducing-deep-research/">touts</a> its model’s ability to produce reports at the “level of a research analyst,” with “clear citations”. Anthropic’s Research <a href="https://www.claude.com/blog/research">promises</a> “high-quality, comprehensive answers in minutes” and “easy-to-check citations”. All of that is echoed in the scholarly publishers’ marketing boasts. Elsevier’s <a href="https://www.elsevier.com/products/scopus/scopus-ai">AI Discovery</a> generates “reliable and digestible research summarization.” The offering from Clarivate, the <a href="https://clarivate.com/academia-government/scientific-and-academic-research/research-discovery-and-referencing/web-of-science/web-of-science-research-assistant/">Web of Science Research Assistant</a>, lets you “synthesize the literature more efficiently.” The <a href="https://www.dimensions.ai/discover-dimensions-ai-assistant/">Dimensions AI Assistant</a>, from Springer Nature’s corporate sibling Digital Science, will return “immediate and more relevant summary results.” Silicon Valley and big publishing are even converging on product language: Elsevier’s Scopus AI recently released its own <a href="https://www.elsevier.com/products/scopus/scopus-ai/product-release-notes">“Deep Research”-branded feature</a>.</p>
<p>At the core of all these tools is a series of verbs: To surface, to rank, to summarise, and to recommend. In this sense the AI products are already acting as knowledge arbitrators, by picking winners and losers according to what they make visible. There are two big and interlocking problems with this role: The models are trained on the scholarly past, and their filtering logic is inscrutable. As a result, they may smuggle in the many biases that mark the history of scholarship, around gender, geography, and other lines of difference.</p>
<p>In this context it is useful to revive an old concept in the sociology of science. According to the Matthew effect, named by Robert Merton <a href="https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.159.3810.56">decades ago</a>, prominent and well-cited scholars tend to receive still more prominence and citations. The flip side is that less-cited scholars tend to slip into further obscurity over time. These dynamics of cumulative advantage have, in practice, served to amplify the knowledge system’s patterned inequalities, with, for example, diminished citations for female scientists, a development aptly labeled the <a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/030631293023002004">Matilda effect</a> by Margaret Rossiter in 1993.</p>
<p>The deployment of generative AI tools in scholarship, as their use picks up, may produce an even worse Matthew effect, on the scale of Elsevier and with no paper trail. The problem is analogous to the well-documented bias-smuggling with existing generative models; image tools trained on, for example, mostly white and male photos reproduce the skew in their prompt-generated outputs. With our bias-laden scholarship as training data (“120 years of research”, in Web of Science’s <a href="https://clarivate.com/academia-government/scientific-and-academic-research/research-discovery-and-referencing/web-of-science/web-of-science-research-assistant/">boast</a>), the research models may spit out results that, in effect, double-down on inequality.</p>
<p>What is worse is that we won’t really know, due to the models’ black-boxed character. Thus, the tools may act as laundering machines—context-erasing abstractions that disguise their probabilistic “reasoning”. Existing biases, like male academics’ <a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2378023117738903">propensity for self-citation</a>, may win a fresh coat of algorithmic legitimacy. Or consider centre-periphery dynamics along North-South and native-English-speaking lines: Gaps once traceable to geopolitical history, including the legacy of European colonialism, may be buried still deeper. The models, in short, could serve as <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-02810-5">privilege multipliers</a>. <a href="https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2506.00074">Early</a> evidence <a href="https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2508.02740">suggests</a> that’s <a href="https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2509.15122">exactly</a> what’s <a href="https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2501.00367">happening</a>.</p>
<p>The Columbia sociologist Robert Merton <a href="https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.159.3810.56">named</a> the Matthew effect in 1968. Merton sourced the idea, that the already well-known accrue still-more reputational lucre, to the New Testament: “For to every one who has will more be given, and he will have abundance; but from him who has not, even what he has will be taken away” (Matthew 25:29). The reason that the scholarly rich get richer, according to Merton, is familiarity. The eminent scientist is already lodged in other scholars’ memories; when it comes time to cite or reference or read, these are the names that are ready at hand. The subsequent spread of the famous name leads to still-stickier impressions, seeding even more mentions. The result is a visibility cascade.</p>
<p>Merton framed the dynamic as a “misallocation” of recognition, an injustice to those (often junior) scientists whose credit is diverted to the undeserving few. He invoked what he called the “class structure” of science, which the Matthew effect helps underwrite. Akin to the broader society’s stratification system, the academic class structure distributes life-chances unevenly. The already famous do not just win honor and esteem; they also secure “access to the means of scientific production”, and thus the chance for still more prestige and more resources.</p>
<p>Merton heavily qualified his critique, however. Yes, the Matthew effect may be unfair to individual scientists, but it serves a healthy, system-wide purpose. Given the crushing volume of research (even in 1968), the rich-get-richer dynamic acts to tame the literature, by “focalizing” attention on important findings. This “communication function” helps to clear the field, to concentrate attention and thereby “accelerate” the march of science. He also downplayed his core point, cumulative advantage, by singling out the unique “character structure” of renowned scientists. These luminaries really are different from their peers: They are more confident and “self-directed,” and they’re better at recognising, and then framing, key findings. So perhaps it’s a good thing, after all, that they’ve won the spotlight.</p>
<blockquote> <p>Merton framed the dynamic as a “misallocation” of recognition, an injustice to those (often junior) scientists whose credit is diverted to the undeserving few</p> </blockquote>
<p>The takeaway from Merton’s famous paper is that whatever the injustice to individuals the overall <em>system</em> of science benefits. Merton’s upbeat functionalism was soon challenged, within a broader 1970s critique of his sociology-of-science program. The problem was not with the Matthew effect thesis itself; indeed, most subsequent studies furnished empirical support for the claim. The issue was with Merton’s view that the system benefits, even if individuals suffer.</p>
<p>It is not just isolated individuals who suffer, after all. The Matthew effect helps mete out the rewards of recognition in <em>patterned</em> ways, along lines of difference already inscribed in the US postwar (and world) academic system. Consider gender: The Matthew effect helped to sideline already marginal female scientists. Dynamics of cumulative <em>dis</em>-advantage worsened, and thereby perpetuated, the patterned exclusion of academic women. This was Margaret Rossiter’s charge in her <a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/030631293023002004">“The Matilda Effect”</a>, named for Matilda Gage, a late nineteenth-century US suffragist who both described and experienced the spiraling of silence. Rossiter, the late historian of science, cited the history of male-colleague credit accrual. The Matilda effect names a process of erasure that, in traceable ways, adds insult to reputational injury.</p>
<p>One of these Matildas, arguably, was Merton’s graduate student and future wife, Harriet Zuckerman. Merton’s 1968 paper (cited <a href="https://scholar.google.com/scholar?cites=15416961120655469275&amp;as_sdt=5,39&amp;sciodt=0,39&amp;hl=en">over 12,000 times</a>) was largely based on Zuckerman’s <a href="https://www.proquest.com/openview/5b46320ef55ddd51047eeea6958b6cda/1?pq-origsite=gscholar&amp;cbl=18750&amp;diss=y&amp;casa_token=7TD-_JDVJ7gAAAAA:P8BgxUBL65k3vTThFrAtpAjHdZT2Z4hhZzkYYh-us0LJrhEh9Z_dZD6fna-ypAAUBYN6vFvLEHU">1965 dissertation</a> on Nobel laureates. In a self-exemplifying irony, Zuckerman’s 1967 <a href="https://www.jstor.org/stable/2091086">journal-article writeup</a>, like her <a href="https://archive.org/details/scientificeliten0000zuck">subsequent book</a>, have attracted far less attention—and she is almost never credited as the concept’s co-author. Hence Rossiter’s half-serious suggestion to call it the “Harriet Effect” in honour of Merton’s “invisible collaborator”.</p>
<p>Rossiter’s point about doubling-down on patterned exclusion has been extended to other “have-nots”, who, like women, have been “ignored, denied credit or otherwise dropped from sight.”</p>
<p>One of Merton’s claims for the upsides of the Matthew effect was that it helped the scientific community cope with the publication onrush. In conditions of published abundance and attention scarcity, we cannot help but sift and summarize. As Ann Blair has <a href="https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300165395/too-much-to-know/">brilliantly shown</a>, there was always too much to know. A situation leading, in the early modern European case she studies, to various filtering and synopsis devices like the index and the reference work. The more recent emergence of the <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s42254-024-00741-0">journal-article abstract</a> is another concession to information overload.</p>
<p>The scholarly citation is also a summary device. On the textbook construal, references are the linchpin of the collective enterprise of science; when we cite, we stand on the shoulders of select and worthy predecessors. But the humble citation is much more than this; in-text and footnoted references to the “literature” are flexible, indeed baggy, symbols, doing all kinds of diverse work for citing authors. Citations can signal allegiance, call out a rival, establish precedence, and spread the peacock feathers. They are legitimacy set in type. And they serve to summarize and direct, to offload the burdens of evidence and support.</p>
<blockquote> <p>The bibliographic labour of scholars is, of course, the raw material from which tallies and indexes are made.</p> </blockquote>
<p>In the last half-century, we have come to collate and count citations, to distill them into lean and precise-seeming metrics. The bibliographic labour of scholars is, of course, the raw material from which tallies and indexes are made. In this second layer of abstraction, by which symbols are fashioned from symbols, a single integer stands in for each of us. A journal’s citations are counted too, and rendered with ordinal clarity to encourage citation-seeking submissions that (in an autopoetic coupling) run up both scores in addition to publisher margins. All to answer the legibility demands of rushed administrators.</p>
<p>The metric tide has, among many other things, added another recursive layer to the Matthew effect. Circa 1968, the effect hinged on scholars’ memories, on what amounted to an availability heuristic repeated across the academic system. The quantified proxies of the h-index and Journal Impact Factor focalize, to borrow Merton’s verb, visibility and attention. The scores themselves act as direct signals to fellow scholars. And they’re also fed into ranking algorithms that power, for example, first-page Google Scholar results. Thus to every one who has will more be given.</p>
<p>The example of Google Scholar is half the way to Deep Research–style AI summary. The algorithmic logic that generates the ten blue citations is a closely guarded secret. Even the Google engineers who manage the complex recommendation engine are unlikely, as individuals, to have a full grasp on how it works. Old-school Matthew effects, by contrast, could be traced and measured; the task was to follow the bibliographic paper trail left by humans. All that cumulative advantage, including its patterned character, was legible and therefore subject to scrutiny.</p>
<p>The AI-driven summary-and-cite products are flat-out inscrutable. They’re all using foundation models to power their reference-studded summaries, even the scholarly publishers (who reportedly use OpenAI’s models) to mine their proprietary databases. Anthropic and Google and OpenAI aren’t inviting outsiders to audit their LLMs, of course, but it wouldn’t much matter if they did. At the level of any particular output, it is near-impossible to look under the hood—to scrutinize the matrix of probabilistic calculations that result in well-ordered text. Nor is it possible to glean a model’s actual “sources”, a word that, within the vast oceans of training data and retrieved information, has no human-legible analogue.</p>
<p>All generative AI models, one way or another, use the past to make probabilistic predictions about the future. In the deep research case, the tools, when they turn to the academic literature, inherit the whole sordid legacy of patterned exclusion. With each probabilistic bet on <em>which</em> literature to surface, they are likely to re-inscribe those legacies. Yet we will not have any easy way to know</p>
<p>The pre-digital Matthew effect helped to stratify science and scholarship, to reproduce an academic “class structure” through visibility and all its attendant rewards. The system’s structural inequalities (around race and gender, the English language, and global center-periphery dynamics) have their own roots and causes. But they’re all propped up by dynamics of cumulative advantage.</p>
<blockquote> <p>Since visibility begets visibility, they help enact the world they purport to merely describe. </p> </blockquote>
<p>In the coming years, Silicon Valley and the big commercial publishers are likely to fight over who has rights to mine the scholarly record. The publishers are re-fashioning themselves as data-analytics companies, and they think they are sitting on a copyrighted gold mine. There will be drama, including lawsuits, licensing deals, and (perhaps) acquisitions.</p>
<p>In some ways all the deck-shuffling to come is a side show. The bigger story is the embrace of foundation models as knowledge distillers by <em>both</em> industries, the one chasing profit and the other appallingly lucrative. Of all the hyped promises of AI, LLMs’ knack for summary is disarmingly impressive. The summaries are good, they are easy to generate, and they come with citations. Yes, the models <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00969-z?utm_source=substack&amp;utm_medium=email">hallucinate</a>, and that is a problem in itself. But the Matthew effect dynamics traced here depend, ironically, on <em>real</em> references and there are lots of them.</p>
<p>The key point is that summary-and-cite AI tools are performative. Since visibility begets visibility, they help enact the world they purport to merely describe. It’s true, as OpenAI <a href="https://openai.com/index/introducing-deep-research/">puts it</a>, that the “ability to synthesise knowledge is a prerequisite for creating new knowledge”. This task was already a vexed one when humans were doing the summarising. If we hand off the work to computational probability and the profit motive, we may reproduce, or perhaps worsen, an academic “class structure” with all of its accumulated (and now hidden) injustices.</p>
