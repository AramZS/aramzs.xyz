<p>The transition of Greg Stafford’s storied Pendragon into its (presumably) final and most perfect form continues apace with <em>Gamemaster</em><em>’s Handbook</em> (2024). Can Pendragon be played without this? Probably, but I sure wasn’t going to risk it (and, in fairness, I am going to continue to impatiently wait until the revised <a href="https://www.vintagerpg.com/2017/09/the-great-pendragon-campaign-2006/"><em>Great Pendragon Campaign</em></a> emerges).</p>
<p><a href="https://www.vintagerpg.com/2025/11/pendragon-core-rulebook-2024/"><em>The Core Rulebook</em></a> contains the mechanical meat of the system, but this book lays out much of the Why. This is where the cultural context comes to life, where the march of time and its effects are laid out (the magic of Arthur spurs rapid technological and societal evolutions), an explanation of the importance of religion and how the various faiths interact, a quick gloss on magic (just make it up!) and many of the mundane activities that fill an Knight’s daily life (feasts, drinking, tournaments, hunting and so on).</p>
<p>The main bit of mechanics presented here is the battle system, for large scale combat that is fairly common during the reign of Arthur. It takes place along two parallel levels, the abstraction of the entire battle (think a helicopter view) and the zoomed in fighting of the player knights. The tides of both can affect the other, and the players can win their personal combat (which often has important narrative beats) while the larger force is defeated (and vice versa). It’s way better than that Battlesystem dreck. There’s also a wide range of NPCs templates, a bestiary and two battle-focused scenarios.</p>
<p>I think it is unlikely that the <em>Gamemaster</em><em>’s Handbook</em> is going to convert anyone. Rather, I think it enhances the experience for the true believer. This is in no small part thanks to the layout and art direction, which I mentioned last year, but bears repeating. The layout is roomy and features many marginal illustrations in the style of medieval manuscripts. I can’t recall their tone in the previous books but there are definitely some bawdy ones here (the nun picking penises from the penis tree is maybe the funniest). There are plenty of more traditional illustrations as well, but the marginalia both ground and lighten the proceedings in a way I appreciate.</p>
