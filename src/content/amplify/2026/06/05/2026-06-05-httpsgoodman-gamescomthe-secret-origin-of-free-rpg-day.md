<p>Ever wonder how Free RPG Day started? Learn about how two friends put their brains together to help bring gamers to their favorite game stores for a special event annually, and start planning to visit your local game shop for this year’s event.</p>
<p><strong>The Secret Origin of Free RPG Day</strong><br/>
<em>By Stu Horvath</em></p>
<p>It was born over curry in 2006. Joe Goodman was out to lunch at an Indian restaurant in Berkeley with Aldo Ghiozzi, owner of Impressions, the company that distributed Goodman Games products to game stores across the United States. Goodman had three ideas for growing his company scrawled in his reporter’s notebook. The first two are lost to time, but the third was a keeper: Free Adventure Module Day. Well, the <em>idea</em> was great, the name needed a little work.</p>
<p>Ghiozzi’s reaction? “That is the worst name in the <em>world</em>. You know what? We’re just gonna call it Free RPG Day.”</p>

<p>Goodman and Ghiozzi met at a Game Manufacturers Association (GAMA) trade show in 2002, a consequence of Goodman Games and Ghiozzi’s Wingnut Games having adjacent tables. A few years earlier, though, ships had passed in the night when Goodman bought a copy of <em>Battle Cattle</em>, published by Wingnut, as a birthday gift for his brother. An online purchase, Ghiozzi’s memorable name stuck in Goodman’s head, “And it was like a year or two later I realized, ‘Oh my god, you made that cow game!’” Whichever date they start counting from, it marked the beginning of decades of collaboration. Ghiozzi even appears as a type of troll lord, the Ghiozzar, in the monster section of the <em>Dungeon Crawl Classics</em> rulebook, complete with a caricature sporting horns, fangs and pointy ears!</p>
<p>Free RPG Day (FRPGD) is Goodman and Ghiozzi’s most notable collaboration, at least as far as the larger RPG hobby is concerned. Goodman was inspired by Free Comic Book Day, which began in 2002 (and featured a D&amp;D comic book called <em>Crisis in Raimiton </em>in 2004 that perhaps anticipated its tabletop cousin in some ways). His basic idea was to collect a selection of new products and sell them to retailers at a reduced rate. Retailers would then offer those products for free on a set event day. In theory, everyone wins: customers got free stuff, retailers got increased foot traffic in their stores and publishers got fresh eyes on their books.</p>
<p>Originally, Ghiozzi wanted to use Free RPG Day as a way to exclusively promote Impressions clients, which at the time included Goodman Games and Troll Lord Games (<em>Castles &amp; Crusades</em>). It quickly became apparent, though, that Impression’s pool of smaller publishers wasn’t going to be enough to make promotion enticing, nor would all of Ghiozzi’s smaller clients be able to foot the bill to participate. “No retailer was going to care,” says Ghiozzi. He needed to cast a bigger net.</p>

<p>The first big fish Ghiozzi landed was Lisa Stevens, founder and then CEO of Paizo Publishing, who loved the idea. At the time, Paizo was producing material for 3.5E Dungeons &amp; Dragons (some products, like Dragon and Dungeon Magazine, were even officially licensed by Wizards of the Coast) and operated at a much larger scale. That prominence in the hobby allowed them to contribute print quantities of their free offering that essentially floated the whole promotion. Paizo’s participation also encouraged other publishers to get in on the action. All told, the initial Free RPG Day consisted of 17 products, including <em>D0: Hollow’s Last Hope</em> from Paizo, an introductory scenario for Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay by Games Workshop, and quick start rule books for <em>Changeling: The Lost</em> from White Wolf Publishing and Chaosium’s <em>Call of Cthulhu</em>. Even as a small publisher, though, Goodman Games contributed three products in the inaugural run: <em>The Sinister Secret of Whiterock</em> for the Dungeon Crawl Classics line, <em>Temple of Blood</em> for the over-the-top violent Wicked Fantasy Factory line, and <em>Dungeonbattle Brooklyn</em> for the Xcrawl setting, where gonzo athletes complete in deadly dungeon crawls, a bit like <em>The Running Man</em>. The initial Free RPG Day experiment was capped at enough kits for 100 participating US stores. Products were stuffed into flat rate USPS boxes, “Cheap as cheap could be,” recalls Ghiozzi, “Obviously, we learned that the mail could lose things; there was tracking in the future.”</p>
<p>The real surprise was that so many stores cared about RPG material beyond the stuff from the big publishers. The second Free RPG tripled the number of participating stores. “I’m super proud of what Joe and I talked about and spun it into,” says Ghiozzi, “We got so much feedback from stores, other publishers, eventually Wizards of the Coast supported us for several years. We got some pretty neat things out of it!”</p>

<p>For Goodman, the goal was always to get products on store shelves. He had seen comic publishers effectively use Free Comic Book Day to not only get shelf space, but then capitalize on that space. “Once a retailer makes that decision to put your book on the shelf, when it sells, they tend to restock it,” he explains, “Once the habit forms, you tend to stay in that store forever. Unless you go out of business or you totally screw something up.”</p>
<p>Building on the success of Free RPG Day’s inaugural year, Ghiozzi returned to his original idea of helping Impressions clients. The result was what they called the Uber Kit, which consisted of a basic offering of free products plus the option to buy the associated core rulebooks for Impressions clients at the deeper-than-usual discount of 60% off. At its height, Free RPG Day would be selling around 400 Uber Kits a year, a sizable sales windfall for publishers like Goodman Games, especially during a time when mid- and small-tier publishers struggled to get into game shops. “I felt that it was really effective,” says Goodman, “I was guaranteed 400 sales a year of one or two hardbacks, which was enough typically to pay for my Free RPG Day release. It paid for itself, I got whatever the number was of books into people’s hands and then they’d restock.”</p>

<p>It wasn’t always sunshine and flowers of course. There was nothing really stopping store employees from cherry picking the best products for themselves, or selling products on eBay. Customers, too, could find ways to scam multiple copies, leading retailers to only make the FRPGD products free with a purchase. It also seemed to never occur to many publishers to support their local retailers on the event day. But when the system worked, it <em>really</em> worked. Goodman recalls going to Chicago area shops for the first few years of the event and while some stores would just pile stuff on the tables, others would have a line of customers out the door all day. “We are giving you a tool to make something of this,” says Ghiozzi, “Game stores and consumers participate in Free RPG Day due to the passion that they have for roleplaying games. <em>Gamers</em> own game stores!”</p>

<p>By 2012, Free RPG Day was central to Goodman’s publishing calendar. That year, the <em>Dungeon Crawl Classics</em> rulebook hit shelves a few weeks before the event, in order to take advantage of the event’s additional foot traffic. Many stores have reported to Goodman that Free RPG Day is the number one sale day for RPGs for the entire year!</p>
<p>For Ghiozzi, the real win with Free RPG Day is getting someone who used to play RPGs back into a game store. And hopefully they bring their kids. “We need new blood in this industry, you know? We’re all getting a little too gray,” he says, “We need these kids to come out and be a part of it.” Ghiozzi sold Free RPG Day to Gaming Days in 2019, who have organized the event since. And it still works.</p>
