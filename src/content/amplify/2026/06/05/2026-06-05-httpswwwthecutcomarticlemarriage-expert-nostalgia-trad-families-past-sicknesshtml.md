<h2 data-editable="displayTeaser">At home, Stephanie Coontz is fighting old gender scripts just like everyone else.</h2>
<figure><picture><figure><img data-content-img="" src="https://pyxis.nymag.com/v1/imgs/a11/448/2bddd12afaaa50c64910a23f1e78eed145-stephanie-lede.rsquare.w700.png"/></figure></picture></figure>
<p>In early May, Stephanie Coontz, America’s leading historian of the family, scurried around the kitchen of her farmhouse in Olympia, Washington. Eighty-one years old, hardly five feet tall, and in jeans and sneakers, she wore her hair in the same voluminous silver crop she’s had for decades. She was fixing me a cup of tea as her husband of 30 years, seated in a brown recliner, cheerfully explained the concept of “chorenography.” Through the windows were towering evergreens and a dock stretching out into a deep-blue lake. Coontz’s grandfather had purchased the 13 acres during the Great Depression. Before inheriting the property, Coontz, who grew up in nearby Tumwater, spent summers here as a child.</p>
<p>Chorenography is chore pornography, or, if you’ve seen it discussed on TikTok lately — Coontz’s husband, Will Reissner, 82, had not — you may know it as #choreplay. It’s the eroticization of domestic partners, most often men, taking on household labor: laundry, the dishes, any of the visible and invisible sludge of home life. Who isn’t more attracted to a man pulling his weight?</p>
<p>But Coontz, who has spent five decades studying marriage and family life and just released her seventh book on the subject, has been tracking the demographic data long enough to know that the opposite was once true. Research from the 1970s and ’80s, she told me, showed that women married to men were in fact less sexually attracted to husbands who did a more-than-average amount of housework (female-coded work that is — think making sack lunches, not cleaning the gutters).</p>
