(Igor Dudchak/Getty Images)</figcaption></figure><p>A federally commissioned study that researchers say was sidelined as the Trump administration revised U.S. dietary guidelines published Tuesday, recommending Americans limit themselves to no more than one drink a day.</p><p>Moderate drinking increases the risk of early death and diseases such as cancer, heart disease and liver disease, and provides no net positive health benefit, according to the study published in the Journal of Studies on Alcohol and Drugs. Over a lifetime, having around seven drinks a week is linked to one alcohol-attributable death per 1,000 people, the Alcohol Intake and Health study said.</p><p>“After one drink or more a day it increases substantially,” said Priscilla Martinez-Matyszczyk, a researcher at the Public Health Institute and co-author of the study.</p><p>A draft of the study, which was commissioned during the Biden administration<b> </b>to inform an update to the Agriculture Department’s dietary guidelines, was released last year but was sidelined amid criticism from the alcohol industry, a former federal health official wrote in an editorial published Tuesday alongside the study.</p><p>The study recommends the USDA update its guidance to reflect a one-drink-a-day limit. The current dietary guidelines, which were updated this year, <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/health/2026/01/08/us-alcohol-limits-debate-health-risks/">loosened</a> guidance on alcohol consumption.</p><p>“The evidence is really pretty straightforward,” said Robert Vincent, a former associate administrator for alcohol prevention and treatment policy at the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration, who wrote the editorial. “There is no safe level of alcohol.”</p><p>Several studies in recent years have <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/health-science/safest-level-of-alcohol-consumption-is-none-worldwide-study-shows/2018/08/23/823a6bec-a62d-11e8-8fac-12e98c13528d_story.html" <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/wellness/2023/03/31/moderate-drinking-alcohol-wine-risks/" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/wellness/2025/10/15/alcohol-dementia-risk/">conclusions</a> on the dangers of moderate alcohol consumption, and <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2025/08/14/alcohol-drinking-poll-beer-gallup/" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/nation/2025/08/14/alcohol-drinking-poll-beer-gallup/">polling</a> has indicated that fewer Americans are drinking, and more now consider drinking in moderation to be unhealthy. </p><p>Despite this, the federal government pulled back on its alcohol messaging in January when it released its new dietary guidelines, cautioning people to “consume less alcohol for better overall health” while omitting definitions of moderate drinking. The USDA previously defined moderate drinking as two drinks a day for men and one drink for women.</p><p>“Any characterization that the study was ‘shelved’ is inaccurate,” Emily Hilliard, a spokeswoman for the Health and Human Services Department, said in a statement. “HHS and USDA reviewed the study alongside the broader body of available scientific evidence and followed the established process for developing the 2025–2030 Dietary Guidelines for Americans. The Guidelines are informed by the totality of the scientific record, not any single report or analysis.”</p><p>Even a limit of two drinks a day is associated with greatly elevated health risks, according to the study. Having 14 drinks a week was associated with a 1 in 25 chance of an alcohol-related death.</p><p>“What many of us might have considered ‘moderate’ consumption is pretty risky,” said Tim Naimi, another co-author of the report.</p><p>Alcohol industry groups have emphasized that guidelines and the industry have long stressed moderation. </p><p>Researchers conducted the study by reviewing past studies on alcohol-related illnesses to identify what fractions of death and disease are attributable to alcohol. They then applied this to U.S. data on alcohol use and mortality and morbidity to estimate the risks of death and illnesses at different levels of alcohol use. </p><p>The study also considered alcohol-related injuries such as car crashes.</p><p>Altogether, the study found that alcohol consumption raises the risk of more than 200 diseases. Previous studies have drawn links between alcohol consumption and <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/wellness/2025/10/15/alcohol-dementia-risk/" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/wellness/2024/03/28/alcohol-heart-disease-women/">heart disease</a>.</p><p>Vincent said the research was commissioned to produce the study to inform the updated dietary guidelines, but alleged “commercial interests” interfered.</p><p>Vox <a href="https://www.vox.com/health/460086/rfk-jr-trump-maha-cancer-alcohol-study-health" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank" title="https://www.vox.com/health/460086/rfk-jr-trump-maha-cancer-alcohol-study-health">reported</a> in September that the Trump administration did not publish a final version of the Alcohol Intake and Health study after the alcohol industry lobbied against it. </p><p>A coalition of alcohol trade associations said in a January <a href="https://www.scienceoverbias.com/" rel="noreferrer" stricter drinking limits were coming from “anti-alcohol activist researchers.” </p><p>Amanda Berger, senior vice president of science and research for the Distilled Spirits Council of the United States, said in a statement that the study was “the product of a flawed, opaque and biased process.” </p><p>A January <a href="https://oversight.house.gov/release/comer-releases-new-report-on-biden-administrations-biased-alcohol-intake-study-that-undermined-american-dietary-guidelines/" rel="">report</a> by the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform criticized the study, saying that its authors were biased against alcohol consumption and that a separate study had been commissioned by Congress to inform the USDA’s dietary guidelines.</p><p>Vincent also alleged that as debate over the study and the dietary guidelines continued, he was laid off last April as part of a reduction in force that targeted service," he wrote.</p><p>Warren Thompson, a retired physician and associate professor emeritus of medicine at the Mayo Clinic College of Medicine who was not involved in the study, said that some medical questions on alcohol remain unsettled, and that some studies continue to investigate the potential health benefits of small amounts of drinking.</p><p>But Thompson said he agreed with the study’s calls to change the government’s dietary guidelines.</p><p>“I think one [drink a day] is a good limit,” he said.</p><p>Martinez-Matyszczyk, the co-author of the study, said it was important for drinkers to know how much — or how little — alcohol can cause harm.</p><p>“When you tell people, ‘Well, alcohol is not good for you, they’re going to say, ‘Well, how much?’ and that’s very reasonable,” Martinez-Matyszczyk said. “And it’s important that we’re able to give them an answer.”</p></body></html>
