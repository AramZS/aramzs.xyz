<h2>The Power of Exercise as Medicine</h2>
<p>Exercise is literally medicine. This isn’t motivational poster nonsense. It’s what every major medical organization now acknowledges. The <a href="https://www.exerciseismedicine.org/">American College of Sports Medicine</a> launched “Exercise is Medicine” as a global health initiative because the evidence is overwhelming: regular exercise can be as effective as medication for treating dozens of chronic conditions.</p>
<p>The numbers are staggering. A <a href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2091689">landmark study in JAMA</a> found that regular exercise reduces the risk of chronic disease by up to 40%. The <a href="https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/fitness/fitness-basics/aha-recs-for-physical-activity-in-adults">American Heart Association</a> reports that 150 minutes of weekly exercise delivers remarkable health benefits:</p>
<ul><li>31% lower heart disease risk</li><li>58% lower diabetes risk</li><li>30% reduction in depression</li><li>27% lower stroke risk</li><li>33% lower overall mortality</li></ul>
<p>Exercise isn’t just medicine. It’s often better than medicine. A <a href="https://www.bmj.com/content/347/bmj.f5577">BMJ meta-analysis</a> of 305 randomized controlled trials found exercise interventions performed equally to drug interventions for coronary heart disease and diabetes prevention. A <a href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38355154/">2024 BMJ systematic review</a> of 218 randomized controlled trials found that exercise is an effective treatment for depression, with effects comparable to psychotherapy and medication.</p>
<p>Here’s the part that’s criminally underutilized: your gym membership can be a tax-free medical expense. If you have any of the increasingly common health conditions that benefit from exercise (spoiler: that’s basically everyone), your CrossFit membership, Peloton subscription, or boutique fitness addiction could be completely HSA/FSA eligible.</p>
<h2>Why Gym Memberships Are HSA/FSA Eligible</h2>
<p>Your great-grandfather didn’t need a gym membership. He got 20,000 steps a day just living his life. <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/nature23018">Research from Stanford</a> shows that Americans now average 4,774 steps per day, compared to estimates of 18,000+ for pre-industrial humans. We’ve engineered movement out of our lives so successfully that we now have to schedule and pay for it.</p>
<p>The sitting crisis is real. <a href="https://www.acpjournals.org/doi/10.7326/M15-1150">A study of over 1 million people</a> found that sitting for 8+ hours a day increases mortality risk by 59%. That’s worse than smoking for many people. This is why gym memberships have become medical necessities, not lifestyle luxuries.</p>
<h3>What the IRS Says</h3>
<p>According to <a href="https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p502.pdf">IRS Publication 502</a>, gym memberships qualify as eligible medical expenses when used for “diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease” and when they “affect any part or function of the body.”</p>
<p>The critical requirement: The membership must be <strong>primarily to alleviate or prevent a physical or mental condition</strong>.</p>
<p>The <a href="https://fsafeds.gov/">FSAFEDS website</a> is explicit: health club dues can be eligible when a doctor or other licensed practitioner certifies medical necessity and documents condition, treatment description, and length of treatment.</p>
<h3>Medical Conditions That Qualify</h3>
<p>Gym memberships can qualify for HSA/FSA reimbursement when used to prevent or treat:</p>
<p><strong>Metabolic Conditions</strong></p>
<ul><li>Obesity (BMI over 30) and overweight (BMI 25-30) with risk factors</li><li>Type 2 diabetes and pre-diabetes</li><li>Metabolic syndrome and PCOS</li><li>High cholesterol</li></ul>
<p><strong>Cardiovascular Conditions</strong></p>
<ul><li>Hypertension and high blood pressure</li><li>Heart disease and post-cardiac rehabilitation</li><li>Family history of cardiovascular disease</li><li>Stroke recovery</li></ul>
<p><strong>Mental Health Conditions</strong></p>
<ul><li>Depression and anxiety disorders</li><li>ADHD and PTSD</li><li>Chronic stress and stress-related disorders</li></ul>
<p><strong>Musculoskeletal Conditions</strong></p>
<ul><li>Chronic back pain and arthritis</li><li>Osteoporosis and joint problems</li><li>Post-surgical rehabilitation</li></ul>
<p><strong>Other Qualifying Conditions</strong></p>
<ul><li>Sleep disorders and chronic fatigue syndrome</li><li>Fibromyalgia and autoimmune conditions</li><li>Cancer recovery</li></ul>
<p>The key requirement is that your healthcare provider must document the gym membership as medically necessary for managing your specific health condition. This documentation comes in the form of a Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN).</p>

<h3>Step 1: Identify Your Qualifying Condition</h3>
<p>Many people are already actively managing or reversing a health condition, like obesity, diabetes, hypertension, depression, chronic pain. If that’s you, your gym membership likely qualifies.</p>
<p>But here’s what most people overlook: the IRS also allows eligibility when you’re trying to <strong>prevent</strong> a health condition in the first place. According to <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p502">IRS Publication 502</a>, you can include medical expenses paid for the “diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or <strong>prevention</strong> of disease.” That “prevention” piece is huge. You don’t need to wait until you’re sick to qualify.</p>
<p>Common qualifying conditions include:</p>
<ul><li>BMI over 25 (overweight) or over 30 (obese)</li><li>High blood pressure or hypertension</li><li>Pre-diabetes or Type 2 diabetes</li><li>Depression, anxiety, or chronic stress</li><li>Chronic back pain or joint problems</li><li>Family history of heart disease</li><li>High cholesterol</li><li>Sleep disorders</li></ul>

<h2>What Makes a Valid Letter of Medical Necessity</h2>
<p>A Letter of Medical Necessity isn’t a doctor’s note saying “exercise is good for you.” That won’t cut it. To satisfy IRS requirements and get your HSA administrator to approve reimbursement, your LMN needs specific elements.</p>
<p><strong>Required Components:</strong></p>
<p>Your LMN must include:</p>
<ol><li><strong>Patient Information</strong> — Full name, date of birth, and contact information</li><li><strong>Provider Credentials</strong> — Licensed healthcare provider’s name, credentials (MD, DO, NP, PA), license number, and contact information</li><li><strong>Diagnosis</strong> — Specific ICD-10 diagnosis code(s) for your qualifying condition(s)</li><li><strong>Medical Necessity Statement</strong> — Clear explanation of why a gym membership is medically necessary for treating or preventing your health condition</li><li><strong>Treatment Recommendation</strong> — Specific recommendation for regular physical activity as part of your treatment plan</li><li><strong>Duration</strong> — How long the recommendation is valid (typically 12 months)</li><li><strong>Date and Signature</strong> — Provider’s signature and date of issuance</li></ol>
<p><strong>What Disqualifies an LMN:</strong></p>
<p>HSA administrators reject letters that are vague or incomplete. Common problems include:</p>
<ul><li>Generic statements like “patient should exercise more”</li><li>Missing diagnosis or ICD-10 codes</li><li>Unsigned or undated letters</li><li>Letters from non-licensed providers (personal trainers, health coaches)</li><li>Backdated letters — your LMN must be dated before or on the date of your first eligible expense</li></ul>
<p><strong>Why This Matters:</strong></p>
<p>The difference between a compliant LMN and a rejected one is the difference between saving 30-40% on your gym membership and paying full price with after-tax dollars.</p>
