<html><body><figure><img alt="" src="https://cloudfront-us-east-1.images.arcpublishing.com/wapo/KNUUMSB32Q6YNKPYJJQ7BQ7DOM.jpg"/><figcaption>Acting attorney general Todd Blanche appears before a House panel Tuesday. (Andrew Harnik/Getty Images)</figcaption></figure><p>Acting attorney general Todd Blanche said Tuesday<b> </b>that the Justice Department would abandon plans for a $1.8 billion fund to compensate people who claim they were unfairly investigated. </p><p>His declaration was an attempt to end an unusual standoff between the Trump administration and Republican lawmakers who have refused to fund immigration enforcement agencies over concerns about the payout fund.</p><p>Dissolving the fund — before it could begin disbursing payments — allows the Trump administration to assuage lawmakers’ concerns that taxpayer money might be used to compensate people who attacked the U.S. Capitol on Jan. 6, 2021.</p><p>“We are not moving forward with the fund, period,” Blanche said.</p><p>“Not moving forward with the fund ever?” Rep. Grace Meng (D-New York) asked him during a hearing of the House Appropriations Committee panel overseeing the Justice Department.</p><p>“Correct,” Blanche said.</p><p>The Trump administration seemed to have little choice but to declare the fund dead to salvage a key part of its aggressive and costly immigration agenda. Some Republican lawmakers said that, if the administration wanted money for immigration enforcement agencies, they would accept nothing less than an explicit guarantee from Blanche that there would be no payout fund.</p><p>Blanche gave that guarantee during oral testimony but suggested that the Justice Department wouldn’t issue a formal statement declaring the fund’s demise beyond the hearing.</p><p>“I’m not committing to putting anything in writing,” Blanche said after Meng argued that such a statement would help instill trust in the Justice Department’s plan. “I don’t know what the purpose is of putting something in writing. I’m telling you what we are doing.”</p><p>The Trump administration’s retreat on the fund was a rare instance of congressional Republicans defying President Donald Trump and prevailing, and it comes roughly five months before midterm elections that will decide control of Congress. The payout fund laid bare strains between the president and a Republican Party that has — more often than not — bent to his will.</p><p>The now-scrapped fund was created last month as part of an expansive settlement in exchange for Trump dropping a $10 billion lawsuit he filed against the IRS over the <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/dc-md-va/2024/01/29/irs-contractor-leak-trump-taxes-sentence/" rel="">2019 leak of his confidential tax records</a>.</p><p>Blanche made clear that one of the deal’s most valuable provisions to Trump would survive without the fund. Under the settlement, the Justice Department agreed it was “forever barred” from prosecuting potential crimes over tax filings by Trump, his family or his companies from before the agreement. </p><p>Democratic lawmakers criticized Blanche for keeping that part of the settlement intact. They also said it was a conflict for Blanche — who was Trump’s personal defense attorney in multiple cases before he returned to the White House in 2025 — to sign off on a settlement for the president. </p><p>“What are you saying is a conflict of interest?” Blanche asked. “The fact that I used to have a job, and I now have a current job?”</p><p>At least one Republican said the testimony settled the matter in his mind. “The testimony I heard from the attorney general was categorical, it was unequivocal, it was unconditional,” Sen. John Neely Kennedy (Louisiana) told reporters. “I take him at his word. He said not now, not ever. And so for me, that’s enough. I’m ready to move forward.”</p><p>For days, Senate Republicans expressed alarm at Trump’s decision to set up the fund. Republicans defied Trump by leaving Washington last month without passing legislation to fund immigration enforcement agencies because of their concerns about the payout fund.</p><p>Then on Monday, the Justice Department said it would comply with a court order temporarily blocking the fund, a striking reversal from days earlier, when it vowed not to let judges’ “policy preferences” stand in its way.</p><p>But some Senate Republicans thought that move was not enough — and wanted to hear from the president himself. The court order blocked the administration from making payouts from the fund only for two weeks and did not weigh in on its legal merits. </p><p>In the hours leading up to Blanche’s testimony Tuesday afternoon, Senate Majority Leader John Thune (R-South Dakota) said he believed after speaking with the acting attorney general Tuesday that Blanche’s testimony would make clear that the fund was no more.</p><p>“I think his statements are going to be very definitive, very clear, and create the certainty that I hope all of our members and House members need as well in order for us to proceed on the reconciliation bill,” Thune told reporters, referring to the stalled immigration enforcement legislation. “But I’m not guaranteeing that happens yet.”</p><p>Even if the administration had not abandoned the fund, it was likely to face serious legal hurdles. Two courts have taken steps to scrutinize aspects of the fund, and other challenges were pending.</p><p>In one decision in the Eastern District of Virginia, U.S. District Judge Leonie M. Brinkema scheduled a hearing for next week to determine whether the fund should remain blocked while the court considers the merits of a lawsuit that argues the fund is illegal. </p><p>Republicans are working to pass legislation important to Trump that would fund two agencies — Immigration and Customs Enforcement, and Customs and Border Protection — for the next three years. Trump had asked Congress to pass the legislation by Monday, but Senate Republicans refused until the administration addressed the controversy over the payout fund.</p><p>Senate Republicans plan to use a special process known as reconciliation to pass the government funding bill without Democratic votes, but the party’s 53-47 majority in the chamber means they need the support of all but three Republican senators if every senator is voting.</p><p>Democrats said Blanche’s verbal guarantee was worth little without a binding commitment. “Blanche and Trump’s words are worthless,” Senate Minority Leader Charles E. Schumer (D-New York) <a href="https://x.com/SenSchumer/status/2061923099830861984" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank" title="https://x.com/SenSchumer/status/2061923099830861984">wrote on X</a> during the testimony. “The only way to stop Trump’s nearly $2 billion MAGA slush fund and his blank check to commit tax fraud is to abolish it by law — permanently.” </p><p>Rep. Jamie Raskin (Maryland), the top Democrat on the House judiciary panel, echoed that, saying he wants legislation to permanently bar any such fund in the future, regardless of Blanche’s assurances.</p><p>Democrats had signaled that they would try to force Republicans into politically uncomfortable votes on amendments related to the fund if the GOP moved to jump-start Trump’s immigration agenda.</p><p>Shortly before Blanche’s testimony, at least one Republican, Sen. Thom Tillis (North Carolina), who has criticized attempts to downplay the Jan. 6 attack, said he also planned to introduce an amendment to bar the administration from resurrecting the fund in the future.</p><p>“I just feel like we just need to do a Wayback Machine and just pretend like this never existed, and take whatever steps are necessary to make sure it can never exist or disburse,” Tillis told reporters. “Not in the current environment. There are other mechanisms for people who were over-prosecuted.”</p><p>On Tuesday morning, a spokesman for Trump’s personal legal team defended the underlying case, saying the IRS had “wrongly allowed a rogue, politically motivated employee” to leak confidential information about Trump, his family and his company to news organizations. “President Trump continues to hold those who wrong America and Americans accountable,” the spokesman said.</p><p>In addition to the Brinkema ruling, another federal judge in Florida last week ordered Trump and his family to respond to “grievous allegations” that the fund <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/05/29/judge-probes-whether-deal-creating-trumps-18-billion-fund-constitutes-fraud/" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/05/29/judge-probes-whether-deal-creating-trumps-18-billion-fund-constitutes-fraud/">constituted an act of fraud</a>, using a rare judicial authority to investigate misconduct against the court. That judicial investigation is in its nascent stages; the judge asked Trump and his family to respond to the allegations of misconduct by June 12.</p><p>Even if the administration follows through with abandoning the fund, Trump could find other ways to reward allies who claim they were victims of a weaponized Justice Department. </p><p>For example, under the Federal Tort Claims Act, people are entitled to file claims against the federal government if they think they were wrongly hurt by government actions. Thousands of such claims are filed each year, including many that have come from Jan. 6 defendants.</p><p>Those claims have kept coming amid the uncertainty on the payout fund. Peter Ticktin, an attorney who has filed for compensation on behalf of Jan. 6 defendants, said he sought payments Friday for nine more clients, arguing in a court filing that each was owed more than $1 million for medical bills, lost income, pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of liberty, and damage to their reputations. </p><p>“If there’s a fund, that makes it all a little bit easier,” Ticktin said. “But if there’s no such fund at all, the government needs to settle these lawsuits.”</p><p>The Justice Department could decide to settle such claims before they are brought to court, effectively deciding who deserves to be paid for damages by the federal government. That claims process is typically not made public.</p><p><i>Meryl Kornfield, Mariana Alfaro, Anna Liss-Roy and Jeremy Roebuck contributed to this report.</i></p></body></html>
