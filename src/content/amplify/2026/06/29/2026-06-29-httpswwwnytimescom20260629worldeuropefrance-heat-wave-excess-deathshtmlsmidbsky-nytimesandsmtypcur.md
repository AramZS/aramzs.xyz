<p><i><p><i>By </i><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/by/segolene-le-stradic">Ségolène Le Stradic</a></p><blockquote><p>Reporting from Paris</p></blockquote></i></p>
<p>Roughly 1,000 excess deaths were reported in France between Wednesday and Saturday as temperatures in the country rose to their highest recorded levels, according to Santé Publique France, the national public health agency.</p>
<p>Excess deaths were particularly high in the west and center of the country, according to the agency, which compared the average daily death toll with that of the previous two months. Eighty-five percent of the dead were aged 65 or older, though increases were seen across all age groups, the agency said in <a href="https://www.santepubliquefrance.fr/sites/default/files/2026-06/cp_premiers_chiffres_mortalite_canicule_20260627.pdf">a statement</a> on Sunday.</p>
<p>The full toll may take months to calculate, but for now it is still far lower than the toll during a heat wave in 2003, when 15,000 people were estimated to have died.</p>
<p>Deaths during last week’s heat wave increased in hospitals and nursing homes, the statement added, and “a particularly sharp increase in deaths at home has been observed,” especially in the Paris region.</p>
<p>“This observation serves as a reminder of the need for measures to support isolated individuals and those experiencing profound loneliness, including in highly urbanized areas,” the agency said.</p>
<p>Élisabeth Charrier, the head of the national funeral federation, <a href="https://www.lefigaro.fr/actualite-france/mortalite-en-periode-de-canicule-les-tensions-se-concentrent-sur-paris-ou-les-deux-funerariums-sont-complets-20260628">told the French daily newspaper Le Figaro</a> that “high activity” had been reported in the country’s mortuaries. “The pressure is concentrated in Paris,” Ms. Charrier said. “For the past two days, the only two facilities located within the city limits have been at full capacity.”</p>
