<h1>They Looked Like They Were Getting Rich on Polymarket—but None of It Was Real</h1>
<h2>The prediction market has flooded social media with deceptive videos by paid creators, according to a Wall Street Journal investigation</h2>
<p>In his videos, George Makihara appears to have a lucrative side hustle making bets on Polymarket.</p>
<p>In January, the college student posted a video that showed him winning $100,000 on a wager that President Trump would publicly say the word “McDonald’s” that month.</p>
<p>The bet was one of 145 that Makihara appeared to place on Polymarket’s website between January and mid-May, based on his videos—bets adding up to almost $410,000.</p>
<p>But none of those bets were real, according to a Wall Street Journal investigation.</p>
<p>Makihara jumps up as Trump says “McDonald’s”—which he never said publicly that month. The clip is from two months earlier.</p>
<p>Makihara, who declined to comment, is one of dozens of mostly college-age creators Polymarket paid to film themselves making fake trades and sometimes scoring fake wins, according to an analysis of more than 1,100 videos by the Journal, along with instructional materials and interviews with creators who have worked with the company.</p>
<p>On Polymarket’s actual site, more than 50 accounts made the McDonald’s bet in January, public data shows. All of them lost.</p>
<p>In its push to draw users to its unregulated platform, Polymarket has flooded social media with videos like Makihara’s, which appear genuine at first glance. In reality, Polymarket built near-perfect copies of its website, then instructed creators to make simulated trades on those dummy sites and hide that they were being paid by Polymarket.</p>
<p>Polymarket said in a statement that it was “committed to maintaining accurate, fair, and transparent markets. We are part of a rapidly growing industry and are constantly evaluating ways to improve how we’re engaging and earning the trust of our audience.”</p>
<p>The company said it plans to conduct a comprehensive audit of active promotional content.</p>
<img src="https://asset.wsj.net/wsjnewsgraphics/uploader/casinomarketing-tell-annotation-1.png"/>
<p>Federal advertising laws require brands to be truthful about what they are promoting and require people who are paid to endorse a product to disclose their ties, although there is some gray area about what’s permitted. Commodities law, which governs prediction markets, also bars deceptive and misleading practices.</p>
<p>The Commodity Futures Trading Commission, which oversees prediction markets, has previously brought enforcement actions against companies that use simulated trades to market their products and make unrealistic claims about profitability.</p>
<p>In 2022, Polymarket settled allegations it was operating an unregistered options exchange by agreeing to stop offering its crypto-based exchange to U.S. customers and formally reincorporating in Panama. Late last year, it launched a regulated U.S. version of its platform, available only as a mobile app. The app has a fraction of the trading volume of the offshore crypto exchange.</p>
<p>Now, Polymarket is seeking to reverse the outcome of the 2022 settlement and bring the crypto platform back into the U.S., according to a person familiar with the matter.</p>
