title: Army general abruptly steps down as Hegseth’s Pentagon purge intensifies
---
<figure><img alt="" src="https://www.ms.now/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/steve-benen-msnbc_1.png?w=120"/></figure>
<p>Most Americans probably don’t immediately recognize Army general Chris “C.D.” Donahue’s name, but they’ve probably seen a memorable picture of him: When U.S. forces withdrew from Afghanistan in 2021, Donahue was <a href="https://www.usarcent.army.mil/News/Features/Article/2759739/last-american-soldier-leaves-afghanistan/">the last American service member to exit</a> the country.</p>
<p>In the years that followed, the general took on other high-profile duties, becoming the head of Army forces in Europe and Africa. He was also widely seen as the next chief of staff of the Army. This week, however, Donahue’s career became notable for a very different reason. <a href="https://thehill.com/homenews/administration/5937459-donahue-exit-army-europe/">The Hill reported</a>:</p>
<blockquote> <p>Gen. Chris Donahue, commander of U.S. Army Europe and Africa, submitted his paperwork to retire after a little over a year in his position, a Pentagon official told The Hill. </p> <p>The Pentagon official spoke on condition of anonymity to discuss internal military deliberations.</p> </blockquote>
<p>An Army spokesperson soon after confirmed Donahue’s departure in an official statement, thanking the general “for his leadership of U.S. Army Europe and Africa.”</p>
<p>While military leaders retire with some regularity, there’s reason to believe that Donahue’s decision — announced after just 18 months in his position — was not altogether voluntary. CBS News, citing multiple sources, <a href="https://www.cbsnews.com/news/gen-chris-donahue-to-retire-army-commander-latest-departure-military-trump-hegseth/">reported</a> that the general exited the military after a lengthy and decorated career because he had “earned the ire of Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth.”</p>
<p><a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/national-security/2026/06/army-general-pentagon-hegseth/687675/">The Atlantic published</a> a related report, describing Donahue as “the latest casualty” in Hegseth’s “purge of the military’s senior ranks.” (The reporting has not been independently verified by MS NOW, and the secretary and the Pentagon declined to comment.)</p>
<p>Indeed, Hegseth has been awfully busy throughout his tenure, not just <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/pete-hegseth-scouting-america-demands">fighting assorted “culture war” battles</a> but also ousting key military leaders who failed to meet his vision to one degree or another. Just two months before Donahue’s exit, for example, the defense secretary <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/hegseth-navy-secretary-john-phelan">also forced out</a> Secretary of the Navy John Phelan.</p>
<p>Just three weeks before Phelan’s ouster, Hegseth <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/hegseth-ousts-the-armys-top-general-further-destabilizing-the-military-in-middle-of-a-war">also fired</a> his Army chief of staff, Gen. Randy George, the Army’s top officer; Gen. David Hodne, the head of Army Transformation and Training Command; and Maj. Gen. William Green Jr., the chief of chaplains.</p>
<p>Those developments came on the heels of Hegseth <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/hegseths-ongoing-pentagon-purge-continues-to-destabilize-the-military">forcing out</a> Col. Dave Butler, who worked closely with George, which came after the defense secretary <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/three-star-general-latest-part-ways-pete-hegseths-pentagon-rcna240843">parted ways</a> with three-star Lt. Gen. Joe McGee, which came just two weeks after the public learned about <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/key-us-admiral-parts-ways-hegseths-pentagon-controversial-boat-strikes-rcna238150">Adm. Alvin Holsey resigning as head of the U.S. Southern Command</a>, reportedly <a href="https://www.wsj.com/politics/national-security/hegseth-asked-top-admiral-to-resign-after-months-of-discord-9e7b357f?mod=hp_lead_pos1">at Hegseth’s request</a>.</p>
<p>Unfortunately, that’s just the start. Just days before Holsey stepped down at Southern Command, the Pentagon chief fired Navy chief of staff Jon Harrison. (His ouster roughly coincided with two high-profile military retirements — Gen. <a href="https://thehill.com/policy/defense/5537202-general-fenton-retires-ussocom/?tbref=hp">Bryan Fenton</a>, the head of U.S. Special Operations Command, and Gen. <a href="https://thehill.com/policy/defense/5535490-top-air-force-general-retirement/">Thomas Bussiere</a>, a top Air Force commander — though it’s unclear whether their departures had anything to do with Hegseth.)</p>
<p>There was no ambiguity, however, when in late August the defense secretary fired Lt. Gen. <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/hegseth-reportedly-fires-dia-chief-pushing-pentagon-purge-ridiculous-n-rcna226659">Jeffrey Kruse</a>, who served as director of the Defense Intelligence Agency, and Rear Adm. <a href="https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/national-security/hegseth-fires-head-defense-intelligence-agency-rcna226677">Milton Sands</a>, a Navy SEAL officer who oversaw the Naval Special Warfare Command.</p>
<p>Four days earlier, Gen. David Allvin, the chief of staff of the Air Force, <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/pete-hegseth-pentagon-purge-air-force-david-allvin-rcna225831">was also shown the door</a>.</p>
<p>The broader purge also includes Air Force general Timothy Haugh, who was both the head of U.S. Cyber Command and the director of the National Security Agency; Gen. Charles Q. Brown Jr., who was chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff; Gen. James Slife, former vice chief of staff of the Air Force; Adm. Linda Fagan, the commandant of the U.S. Coast Guard; Adm. Lisa Franchetti; Lt. Gen. Jennifer Short; Lt. Gen. Joseph B. Berger III, the Army’s top military lawyer; Lt. Gen. Charles Plummer, the Air Force’s top military lawyer; and Navy Vice Adm. Shoshana Chatfield, the only woman on NATO’s military committee.</p>
<p>Political scientist Caitlin Talmadge, a Massachusetts Institute of Technology professor who specializes in foreign policy and military operations, recently <a href="https://x.com/ProfTalmadge/status/2039874879751586083">noted</a> via social media, “Firing senior officers for cause is one thing. Firing them repeatedly on this scale and with no explanation is unprecedented in our nation’s history.”</p>
<p>The consequences matter: There are growing concerns that a scandal-plagued former Fox News host is destabilizing the U.S. military.</p>
<p>In fact, the New York Times <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/07/us/politics/hegseth-firing-military-leaders.html">reported in November</a> that Hegseth had fired or sidelined dozens of officials “with little explanation,” creating “an atmosphere of anxiety and mistrust” within the department. Politico published <a href="https://www.politico.com/news/2025/10/09/hegseth-culture-of-fear-navy-firing-00598159">a similar report</a> the month before, noting that the secretary’s firings have “injected a fresh wave of fear into the Pentagon over the cost of speaking up and who might be next.”</p>
<p>Early last year, five former defense secretaries, including retired Gen. Jim Mattis, Donald Trump’s first defense secretary, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/02/28/us/politics/trump-military-firings-defense-secretaries-letter.html">condemned the pattern of firings</a> as “reckless.” In a joint letter, addressed to Congress, they asked the House and Senate to hold “immediate hearings to assess the national security implications” of the dismissals.</p>
<p>Hegseth and the administration appear to have ignored those concerns; the purge is <em>still</em> going on; and GOP leaders on Capitol Hill have scheduled no such hearings.</p>
<p>Democratic Rep. Seth Moulton of Massachusetts, who served as a Marine officer in Iraq and now serves on the House Armed Services Committee, <a href="https://www.politico.com/news/magazine/2025/05/25/pete-hegseth-generals-purge-seth-moulton-00360166">spoke to Politico</a> about Hegseth’s purges, which the congressman described as politically motivated.</p>
<p>“That’s a recipe not just for a politicized military, but an authoritarian military,” Moulton said. “That’s the way militaries work in Russia and China and North Korea.”</p>
<p>The Massachusetts Democrat made those comments a year ago. The problem is far worse now.</p>
<p>For his part, Hegseth recently defended the pattern during congressional testimony, telling lawmakers who asked about his personnel purge, “Under Barack Obama, 197 general officers were removed. So this is not something specific to this administration.”</p>
<p>We learned soon after that the statistic the secretary cited was <a href="https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/pressed-on-pentagon-purge-hegseth-pushes-false-claim-about-obama-era-firings">entirely made up</a> and had no basis in fact.</p>
