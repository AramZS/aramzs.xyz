<p>Investing in interplanetary defense isn’t expensive, and it could save the planet.</p>
<p>Earth’s orbit</p>
<p><strong>Sun</strong></p>
<p><strong>Earth</strong></p>
<p>NASA <a href="https://www.technologyreview.com/2023/05/15/1073084/earth-safe-killer-asteroid/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">has catalogued</a> more than <b>40,000 asteroids</b> whose paths approach Earth’s orbit. Here is where they are now.</p>
<p>Earthlings have been <b>tracking asteroids</b> since the 1800s, but most were discovered in recent years.</p>
<p>There are <a href="https://news.mit.edu/2003/asteroid-0910?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template"><b>approximately 1,000</b></a><b> civilization-ending asteroids</b> near Earth with diameters larger than two-thirds of a mile.</p>
<p>NASA <a href="https://www.technologyreview.com/2023/05/15/1073084/earth-safe-killer-asteroid/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">has catalogued</a> more than 95 percent of them. Here is where they are in June.</p>
<p>But a more likely threat to the planet is the <b>25,000 smaller “city killers.”</b> These asteroids are still big: roughly seven times the size of the Chelyabinsk meteor. Yet NASA <a href="https://science.nasa.gov/science-research/planetary-science/planetary-defense/near-earth-asteroids/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">has found only</a> 45 percent of them.</p>
<p>That means more than 14,000 near-Earth asteroids — those closer than 30 million miles from Earth’s orbit — big enough to destroy a city aren’t accounted for.</p>

<p>What happens when a massive asteroid meets an unprepared planet?</p>
<p>You don’t have to look back to the dinosaurs for an answer: On Feb. 15, 2013, a <a href="https://www.planetary.org/articles/what-was-the-chelyabinsk-meteor-event?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">66-foot rock</a> streaked over Chelyabinsk, Russia, releasing about 30 times more energy than the Hiroshima atomic bomb and injuring roughly <a href="https://www.nasa.gov/solar-system/five-years-after-the-chelyabinsk-meteor-nasa-leads-efforts-in-planetary-defense/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">1,600 people</a>.</p>
<p>That meteor broke up in the atmosphere, sparing the city from worse. But Earth might not be so lucky next time.</p>

<p>What would one of these city killers do to a population center? According to a <a href="https://cneos.jpl.nasa.gov/pd/cs/pdc25/PDC25-IAWN_Impact_Notification2.pdf?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">2025 NASA-led simulation</a>, a 460-to-520-foot asteroid would explode in a high-energy, low-altitude airburst. The resulting energy release, estimated between 60 and 105 megatons (the bomb dropped on <a href="https://www.armscontrol.org/pressroom/2020-07/reality-check-atomic-bombings-hiroshima-nagasaki?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">Hiroshima was 0.015 megatons</a>), would generate a destructive blast incinerating everything within a nine-mile radius. Serious damage would extend 60 to 75 miles from ground zero.</p>
<p>The likelihood of an impact isn’t low. A <a href="https://arxiv.org/html/2508.02418v1?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">2025 analysis</a> led by the planetary scientist Carrie Nugent estimates asteroids 460 feet or larger strike Earth roughly once every 11,000 years — a 0.009 percent chance annually. But those odds are 1,000 times greater than your <a href="https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/engineering/plane-crash-survival?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">annual risk</a> of dying in an airplane crash.</p>
<p>The planet needs a risk-management strategy. Billions are spent to <a href="https://www.worldbank.org/ext/en/topic/resilience-and-disaster-management?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template#:~:text=In%20FY25%2C%20the%20World%20Bank,earthquakes%2C%20floods%2C%20and%20cyclones.">prepare for natural disasters</a> worldwide. The world can’t prevent earthquakes or tsunamis. But it can stop an asteroid impact if it takes the threat more seriously. Earth needs a rapid planetary defense program based on two pillars: detection and deflection.</p>
<p>Detection means finding these asteroids before they find Earth. The technology to do this is rapidly improving. The Vera C. Rubin Observatory’s <a href="https://rubinobservatory.org/explore/how-rubin-works/technology/camera?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">3,200-megapixel camera</a> scans the sky from a mountaintop in Chile. The observatory released its first images last year, and it <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/jamiecartereurope/2026/04/04/11000-asteroids-have-been-found---and-33-are-near-earth-objects/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">should triple</a> the current known asteroid count over the next decade.</p>
<p>But ground-based telescopes have a blind spot: They can’t see objects blocked by the sun’s glare. To compensate, scientists need space-based telescopes such as NASA’s Near-Earth Object Surveyor. An <a href="https://science.nasa.gov/mission/neo-surveyor/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">infrared space telescope</a> scheduled to launch in late 2027, Surveyor will spot the dark, heat-absorbing asteroids that ground observers can’t see. Researchers aim to <a href="https://www.jpl.nasa.gov/missions/near-earth-object-surveyor/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">identify two-thirds</a> of the asteroids over 460 feet within five years of the telescope’s launch.</p>

<p>Deflection is more complicated. The only proven deflection technology is NASA’s Double Asteroid Redirection Test, which fires a spacecraft into asteroids at high speeds. DART <a href="https://www.nasa.gov/missions/dart/nasas-dart-mission-changed-orbit-of-asteroid-didymos-around-sun/?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">successfully nudged</a> an asteroid’s orbit in 2022, but building and planning the <a href="https://www.planetary.org/space-policy/cost-of-dart?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">$325 million</a> mission took years.</p>
<p>If a dangerous asteroid were discovered tomorrow, NASA would need to plan, build and launch a deflector from scratch, since NASA doesn’t keep spacecraft similar to DART in storage. That would be like facing a charging gorilla and needing to build a rifle. NASA should have a shelf-ready fleet of impactors modeled after DART that it can quickly deploy if it detects a threat.</p>
<p>However, a stronger deflector exists. Nuclear bombs could destroy an asteroid, but the <a href="https://history.state.gov/milestones/1961-1968/limited-ban?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">1963 Limited Test Ban Treaty</a> bans detonating these weapons in space. Could the treaty’s signatories establish an exception for planetary defense? A city-killer asteroid, when it appears, could hit New York, Moscow or Tokyo. That’s a call for good-faith cooperation between countries if there ever was one.</p>
<p>But would the defense be worth the cost? NASA already pays for detection technology, such as the NEO Surveyor. For deflection, a reserve of 10 impactors modeled after the DART spacecraft — built from interchangeable parts that can target different kinds of asteroids — would cost roughly $3.25 billion, based on the original mission’s price tag. As a comparison, the new Washington Commanders stadium <a href="https://www.cnbc.com/2025/04/28/nfl-washington-commanders-return-to-dc-in-3point7-billion-deal.html?itid=lk_inline_enhanced-template">will cost nearly</a> $4 billion.</p>
