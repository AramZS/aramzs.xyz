<p><i><p><i>By </i><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/by/alex-vadukul">Alex Vadukul</a></p><p><i>Visuals by </i>Ye Fan</p></i></p>
<p>On Sunday, crowds of thousands rode ferries to Governors Island to attend a kind of New York-centric Coachella for Jewish food and culture.</p>
<p>With glistening waters and the Statue of Liberty in sight, the droves then descended upon a lush lawn for the festival, the <a href="https://thegreatnosh.com/">Great Nosh</a>. Revelers in flip-flops held inflatable green pickles while they danced on the grass. People wearing Hebrew-lettered jewelry and Star of David necklaces waited in line for portable toilets. Couples strolled together with cups of Van Leeuwen ice cream paired with black-and-white cookies from Zabar’s.</p>
<p>The festival’s stalls were filled with Jewish institutions that had buddied up with buzzy establishments to serve New Yawky gastronomic fusions, a concept that is the Great Nosh’s guiding idea. Katz’s Deli paired with Santo Taco for a pastrami taco slathered with habanero and avocado salsas. Russ &amp; Daughters paired with the Williamsburg-based Chinese restaurant Bonnie’s for daikon radish cakes dolloped with crème fraîche and smoked salmon.</p>
<p>At the Katz’s and Santo Taco stand, where men carved slices of the deli’s fabled pastrami, <a href="https://ny.eater.com/2015/1/28/7919135/meet-jake-dell-the-27-year-old-keeping-katzs-delicatessen-alive-and">Jake Dell</a>, the establishment’s fifth-generation owner, kept an eye on things.</p>
<p>“The beauty of this festival is its blending of food traditions,” Dell said. “Jewish food, in and of itself, is such a diaspora. Katz’s is the oldest institution here today, and Santo Taco is among the youngest, and there’s something beautiful about that, mixing it all together. Pickles and corn tortillas go hand in hand.”</p>

<p>Samuel Halpern, a senior at the Bronx High School of Science, braved a long line for a chicken tikka-stuffed bagel from Moonrise Bagels and the clouty downtown Indian restaurant <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2021/05/10/dining/dhamaka-review.html">Dhamaka</a>.</p>
<p>“The Lower East Side used to be Jewish; that’s why we have Katz’s Deli and Russ &amp; Daughters there,” Halpern said. “Now, everybody is in the Lower East Side, and Dhamaka is just blocks away from Russ &amp; Daughters. New York City is a melting pot, so why wouldn’t our food continue to reflect that?”</p>
<p>Now in its second year, the Great Nosh is staged by the <a href="https://www.jewishfoodsociety.org/">Jewish Food Society</a>, a nonprofit organization that seeks to preserve and celebrate Jewish culinary heritage. “Jewish food is a story — it’s more than a flavor,” said Naama Shefi, the founder and director of the nonprofit, as she savored some shade at a picnic bench. “Russ &amp; Daughters is like my synagogue, because I can feel the layers of flavor and time there. But these places that are the keepers of our tradition are also finding ways to keep it relevant.”</p>

<p>Audiences gathered inside the “Grandmas Tent” for a cooking presentation, staged in a studio kitchen with a checkered floor hosted by the food writers Gail Simmons and Nilou Motamed. Together, they presented cooks who taught family recipes for cabbage piroshki and Hungarian palacsinta. And spinning hits from a D.J. stage was Max Kulchinsky, who performed as part of a Jewish arts and music collective called Havurah.</p>
<p>“All the music you’re about to hear is 100 percent kosher,” Kulchinsky yelled, playing remixes of songs by artists like Amy Winehouse and the Beastie Boys.</p>
<p>As the afternoon waned and bellies filled with challah and wine, a graphic designer named Dalit Shalom considered the festival’s fusion concept.</p>
<p>“Jewish food in New York is a blueprint of Jewish history and immigration to New York,” said Shalom, wearing a Zabar’s cap adorned with daisies. “Jewishness is a culture of evolution, and the food here today is picking up traces of where it’s come from and where it’s going.”</p>

<p>One of the most popular side tent attractions was a beading workshop offered by the designer Susan Alexandra. And sitting in the grass beading bracelets of her own was <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2023/04/01/style/the-last-days-of-becketts-a-smoky-new-york-literary-salon.html">Meg Spectre</a>, an actress and performance artist. She’d spent the day tasting confections like the tikka masala-stuffed bagel and the black-and-white cookie sundae.</p>
<p>When asked what her favorite Jewish staple was, however, she grew pensive.</p>
<p>“My mother’s latkes,” Spectre said. “My mother learned it from her mother. And someday, I will know how to make them, too.”</p>
