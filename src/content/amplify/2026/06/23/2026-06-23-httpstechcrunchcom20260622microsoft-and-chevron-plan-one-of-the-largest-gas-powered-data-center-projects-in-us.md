<p>Microsoft and Chevron announced plans on Monday to develop a 2.67-gigawatt natural gas power plant in West Texas to serve the tech company’s AI and cloud data centers.</p>
<p>Under the 20-year power purchase agreement, the plant will provide dedicated electricity to a Microsoft-operated data center. Two large GE Vernova turbines will generate most of the power, with a Caterpillar subsidiary Solar Turbines providing the rest. (The Solar Turbines name might sound familiar — xAI uses them in its <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/18/xai-is-facing-a-lawsuit-for-operating-over-400-mw-of-gas-turbines-without-permits/">unpermitted power plant</a> near Memphis.) Microsoft will buy power from the power plant for 20 years.</p>
<p>The project will be “among the largest co-located natural gas power and data center developments in the U.S.,” Chevron said in a <a href="https://www.chevron.com/newsroom/2026/q2/chevron-signs-20-year-power-agreement-with-microsoft-for-west-texas-data-center">press release</a>.</p>
<p>Though Microsoft has been <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/03/ai-companies-are-building-huge-natural-gas-plants-to-power-data-centers-what-could-go-wrong/">telegraphing the move</a> for months now, it’s still a significant shift for a company that has been vocal about its sustainability efforts. Microsoft has pledged to eliminate its carbon emissions by 2030, a goal that will be harder to reach with this new power plant.</p>
<p>Project Kilby, as the power plant is known, will potentially release more than 13 million tons of carbon dioxide, 3,200 tons of <a href="https://www.epa.gov/criteria-air-pollutants">criteria air pollutants</a>, and 278,000 pounds of <a href="https://www.epa.gov/haps">hazardous air pollutants</a>, <a href="https://oilandgaswatch.org/facility/rec_d41om0ckv0k9e3gugc80">according</a> to the Environmental Integrity Project.</p>
