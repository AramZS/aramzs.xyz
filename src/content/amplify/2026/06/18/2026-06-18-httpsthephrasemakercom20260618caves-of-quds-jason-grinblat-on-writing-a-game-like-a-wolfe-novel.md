title: Caves of Qud’s Jason Grinblat on Writing a Game Like a Wolfe Novel
---
<p><em>Caves of Qud is not your usual RPG. Last year it won the Hugo Award for Best Game or Interactive Work and the Independent Games Festival’s award for Excellence in Narrative. That’s an unusual amount of literary credibility to rack up for a video game, let alone a roguelike.</em></p>
<p><em>Jason Grinblat, who co-created Qud with Brian Bucklew starting in 2007, has a theory for why. It’s no accident that Grinblat names Gene Wolfe’s The Book of the New Sun as a touchstone. Both master the art of giving out just the right amount of information for the reader. In this conversation, Jason and I discussed Qud as a literary object, and the genres it belongs to.</em></p>

<p><strong>You pitched Qud as “language-driven science fiction” and described it as a game obsessed with language. When you set out in 2007, did you think of yourselves as making a book that you play?</strong></p>
<p>Not in those terms, no. But we played a lot of text-based games – mostly MUDs and roguelikes, but also interactive fiction. We were drawn to these games in part because of the imagination it takes to “complete” them, much like what happens when you read traditional fiction. And neither of us is a visual artist, so as for what we could actually make, a text-based game felt miles more reachable than the alternatives.</p>
<p>As a writer, the quickness and accessibility of words appealed to me as a way to give dimension to abstract representations interacting on screen. The full extent to which language would catalyze the play systems in Qud wasn’t obvious to me in the beginning.</p>
<p><strong>Qud is one of the few games where players are encouraged to read a lot. What do you think games as a medium can do with prose that the novel can’t, and where does the novel still beat you?</strong></p>
<p>I think about this a lot. Something I notice in our work is how powerful a pairing the expressive, flowery prose is with the ontologically “harder” cogs of the game’s mechanics. We do a lot of grognardy simulation: water can be frozen, every object has a melting point, every wall can be dug out into a tunnel, etc. There’s something about the posture this kind of fine-grained simulation puts you into as a player that makes a baroquely poetic passage hit harder.</p>
<p>Those weighty physics lend a kind of grainy authenticity to the worldbuilding; they are effective at convincing you this isn’t merely a story at an author’s whim, but an actual (read: aesthetically nonfictional) place governed by a lattice of rules and subject to the complex dynamics that make real-world stories compelling. And so the prose has a different texture than if I’d just handed it to you as a collection of short fiction.</p>
<p>The flip side is that players can get overly attentive to those hard cogs, and given the freedom inherent to games as an interactive medium, narrative pacing can be very difficult. The novel has us beat there. To make up for this I try to rely on sharp, storyful moments that can be assembled out of order rather than smooth arcs that really depend on precise emotional pacing.</p>

<p><strong>What does the actual writing process look like for a game like Qud? When you’re world-building, are there linear texts written as preparatory scaffolding that the player never sees directly? Or is it composed fragmentary from the start?</strong></p>
<p>There was a broad linear outline for the main plot I made in about 2008 that I revisited recently and was surprised to note how closely we followed it. This is how it tends to go for the big narrative beats. We definitely had a set of secrets about the world that we routed out for you to uncover through the course of your playthrough. That said, a lot of the worldbuilding is fragmentary and incomplete. My favorite settings are like wet sponges that always give more on another squeeze, and that kind of richness harmonizes with the ritual of roguelike gameplay, where you are ever coming back for another run.</p>
<p>On a more local level, like when I’m writing the description for an object, I have high in mind that text is very much the rendering engine in a game like Caves of Qud. I start with imagining the player character in the presence of the object. What are they seeing, what are they feeling (if they’re touching it), and then I deviate from there. I try to find an angle of detail on the object that’s unexpected. Qud has over 2,000 of these, but I didn’t want to phone any of them in. Each is a little poem, a chance to arrange the world pebble by pebble. The object descriptions are where a lot of the voice of the game lives.</p>
<p>Because we developed Qud over so many years, a lot of this stuff began as intuition that we only unpacked later. When we brought on our second writer in 2018 (Caelyn Sandel), I was forced to distill and linearize the voice and tone into a style guide, something I hadn’t consciously done before. So the version of events you’re getting here is from the perspective of someone who’s done this reflection and can find congruities he wasn’t aware of while doing them. Unreliable narrator, etc.</p>
<p><strong>You’ve called The Book of the New Sun one of your great influences and drawn the specific parallel that Qud, like Wolfe, makes the player puzzle out the basic facts of a refracted world. Wolfe does this through Severian, a narrator with perfect memory who is nonetheless unreliable. Qud has no narrator at all. How do you reproduce that Wolfean feel without a narrating “I”?</strong></p>
<p>The game’s interface is an implicit narrator. We do something fun where technological relics initially appear to you as strange objects, literally called in the interface things like “weird artifact”, “odd trinket”, “strange tubes”. Only if you’re able to examine and correctly identify them do we give you more information.</p>
<p>There is a continuous stream of ancient-world and fantasy-coded encounters that you have more and more reason to question as you get familiar with the science fictional nature of the world of Qud. I think a fun aspect of science fantasy and Dying Earth as genres is the affordances they give you to decode the fictional present and link it up to some fictional past that is recognizable to us in the contemporary real world. Games give us a lot of tools to do this (and also subvert player attempts to do this).</p>
<p><strong>Wolfe’s archaic diction works because every strange word is a real word doing precise work. Qud has its own semi-invented register. Where’s the line for you between a word that estranges productively and one that just obscures?</strong></p>
<p>Good question. It often comes down to what feels right to me. I’m not doing as much with reclaimed words as Wolfe is, but I am using a lot of neologism or reconfiguration from anglicized Hebrew and Arabic. And at the sentence level, I want to defamiliarize the world. Not to trick anyone, just to knock you off your footing and get you to see something from a new angle (which is so much the social function of sci-fi/fantasy). I guess that’s the line for me: am I merely confusing you, or am I prompting you to see something familiar in a different light?</p>

<p><strong>The “dying earth” tradition has a specific texture that I immediately recognized and loved with Qud. What is the single most important decision you’ve taken about the game to convey this feeling of an ancient place?</strong></p>
<p>Oof, that’s hard to say, since the game is really such a gestalt of its parts. The “weird artifact” mechanic I mentioned above is a good candidate. Even more than that, though, are the little text snippets I’m able to attach to objects, parts of the interface, etc that – because of how naturalized the expectations are for there to be some kind of interface in a video game – let me inject a lot of voice in small doses.</p>
<p>So, for example, I can name a faction that shows up in your Reputation menu “newly sentient beings”, appearing right alongside more prosaic entries like “dogs” and “the Merchants’ guild”. Or I can give you a sweeping disembodied perspective on time and space via a world map description, how these vine-swathed ruins were “spun on the cyclopean lathe in an ageless past” or how the world’s rivers “[once] were ravenous, feasting on shale and limestone before taking bloated respite in the Great Salt Sea.” Games have a lot of little nooks for text to fit in, and I try to make use of them all.</p>
<p><strong>Qud strikes me as belonging less to classic dying-earth SF than to the New Weird. Do you see Qud in that lineage?</strong></p>
<p>There are a few ways we think of Qud as deviating from the center mass of Dying Earth as a genre. A key one is: the earth isn’t dying. It’s actually on the verge of a great thawing and stirring. My co-creator Brian and I have tossed out the term Burgeoning Earth to capture this tone. Yes, unthinkable spans of deep time have passed; what was once a constituent-world of a grand, galaxy-spanning collective is now fallen, age-weary, provincial. But there is still so much (unthinkably much) life and culture in the present, a million species of fungus growing on the bones of arcological ruin.</p>
<p>As much as Qud wants to reflect on the past it also wants to soak in the present, think through the myriad ways life expresses itself under such a long shadow of crumbled civilization, and remain open to the potential of its future(s).</p>
<p><strong>Miéville built New Crobuzon as a city of grotesque and politically legible bodies, which is his vehicle for social criticism. Qud’s mutations and chimerism are player freedom. Do you think Qud’s bodily plasticity carries political weight?</strong></p>
<p>Absolutely. This is where we benefited from making a game over two decades. A lot of that political weight was deeply latent in the beginning, as we drew from our inspirations (like the early TSR tabletop roleplaying game Gamma World) without really unpacking them. Much later, while in early access, Qud attracted a following of trans players, several of whom told us they were drawn to the game for its diverse body morphologies and the radical autonomy you had as a player-character in shaping your own body. This coincided with and contributed to our growth as writers/designers/people, where we began to develop a richer and more explicit understanding of Qud’s (and the wider genre’s) thematic resonances. Then we were able to lean in.</p>
<p>I think the game has a lot to say about the social construction of all types of categories, bodies and communities as examples. There is a trait of diversity in form and instability in type that is reflected in the underlying simulation driving the roleplaying game– a simulation that is big and messy and sometimes breaks down, affording bugs and exploits that let you rocket-jump through the boundaries of body, class, species, space, time, etc. And we know what we’ve done by making a maximalist, intractable mess of a game, so unless an exploit is truly game-destroying, we tend to wink and look the other way.</p>

<p><strong>A defining New Weird move is hybridity as a worldbuilding principle. Qud’s procedural generation automates that hybridity. Is procedural generation a natural engine for the Weird?</strong></p>
<p>It’s certainly a natural engine for hybridity. Umberto Eco anticipated the joy of emergence-based systems-driven gameplay in his concept of the “open work”, which he describes as an artpiece that arrives unfinished and must be completed (via performance, in his examples) in ways that the creator couldn’t anticipate. Some procgen falls on the other side of that definition, where a few authored components are being combined in limited ways to produce author-anticipated compounds. But the procgen space we like to play in is very much of the open variety, where the possibility space is too large to prefigure, and so, although we authored the components and shaped the space, we’re still constantly surprised/delighted by the results. (We like to say we let our generators run wild.)</p>
<p>This is a real commitment to hybridity. We allow hybrids to flower in the dynamics of our runtime with little ability to reign them in. In this way, I often think of our procedural systems as posing questions rather than giving answers. The system produces a juxtaposition, the rest of the authored game components provide an interpretative lens to read that juxtaposition through, sure, but the player is the one to ultimately resolve it into something meaningful. And in concert with the game systems we’ve developed a far-future, Weird-adjacent setting that acts as a soft landing bed for whatever chimeras the system coughs up.</p>
<p><strong>Both Bas-Lag and Urth keep the reader unsure whether they’re in the far future of our Earth. Qud sustains the same ambiguity. Is that uncertainty something you protect?</strong></p>
<p>It is. We offer some strong opinions at key moments in the game, but it’s always kept subtextual, importantly. The ambiguity feels fundamental, for our take on the genre especially. Dovetailing with how hybridity is open and underspecified by the procedural systems, unknowability is a big theme of the game. The past is in some ways as unknowable as the future, and there are no sufficient answers that will place us rightly in the universe. The Earth-ambiguity rhymes well with this.</p>
<p><strong>If a literature professor put Qud on a syllabus next to The Shadow of the Torturer, what would you want students to notice?</strong></p>
<p>One thing I love across all art mediums is how medium-specific lacunae allow signature kinds of expressivity. A film shot might linger on someone’s ambiguously emoting face without accompanying dialogue to resolve the ambiguity. Wolfe might describe something young Severian is doing through the voice of old Severian the narrator and omit a detail that, to the readers, keys us in on what Severian isn’t seeing.</p>
<p>In games, I’m obsessed with how narrative emerges through play and how such a narrative, extruded through the combined sieve of the game’s systems and the storyworld they are ever failing to approximate, has a signature texture. I’d want students to pay attention to this, and how systems themselves are abstractions activated by the multidisciplinary mix of a game’s visuals, music, sound effects, and – particularly in Qud – its words.</p>
<p><strong>You’re sentenced to live the rest of your life in Qud. What’s your build and what’s the first thing you do? </strong></p>
<p>I think I’d have to take Temporal Fugue because I’ve long been curious how poorly I’d get along with myself (something that’s very much reflected in Qud when fights break out between your nominally-allied fugue clones). After that, I’d go take a long nap. Some of my most baroque descriptions are dedicated to imagining a world where unprecedented levels of rest are possible. A hammock strung between musa trees in the shadow of a gigantic tomb, the hum of a hedron engine on a long-haul starfreighter… this is the stuff dreams are made of.</p>
