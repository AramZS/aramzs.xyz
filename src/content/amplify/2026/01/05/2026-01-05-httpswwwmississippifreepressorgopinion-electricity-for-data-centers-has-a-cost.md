<p>The amount of electricity data centers use in the U.S. in the coming years is <a href="https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-new-report-evaluating-increase-electricity-demand-data-centers">expected to be significant</a>. But regular reports of <a href="https://news.constructconnect.com/michigan-chosen-to-build-multi-billion-dollar-openai-stargate-data-center-facility">proposals for new ones</a> and <a href="https://www.utilitydive.com/news/georgia-power-large-load-data-centers/806300/">cancellations of planned ones</a> mean that it‚Äôs difficult to know exactly how many data centers will actually be built and how much electricity might be required to run them.</p>
<p>As a researcher of energy policy who has <a href="https://theconversation.com/why-doesnt-the-u-s-bury-its-power-lines-104829">studied the cost challenges</a> associated with new utility infrastructure, <a href="https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=yxN_35oAAAAJ&amp;hl=en&amp;oi=ao">I</a> know that uncertainty comes with a cost. In the electricity sector, it is the challenge of <a href="https://www.naruc.org/">state utility regulators</a> to decide who pays what shares of the costs associated with generating and serving these types of operations, sometimes broadly called ‚Äú<a href="https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/us-grid-investments-take-off-power-demand-hikes-2025-01-28/">large load centers</a>.‚Äù</p>
<p>States are exploring different approaches, each with strengths, weaknesses and potential drawbacks.</p>
<h3><strong>A New Type of Customer?</strong></h3>
<p>For years, large electricity customers such as <a href="https://www.etextilecommunications.com/news/textile-industry-energy-requirements-for-today-and-the-future/article_5954bfbe-d277-11ec-b16d-f3a35e584597.html">textile mills</a> and <a href="https://blog.se.com/energy-management-energy-efficiency/2024/10/01/process-electrification-helps-cut-co2-in-refining/">refineries</a> have used enough electricity to power a small city.</p>
<p>Moreover, their construction timelines were more aligned with the development time of new electricity infrastructure. If a company wanted to build a new <a href="https://shawinc.com/Newsroom/Press-Releases/Shaw-Invests-$250-Million-in-Andalusia,-Ala-Fiber">textile mill</a> and the utility needed to build a new <a href="https://www.eia.gov/analysis/studies/powerplants/capitalcost/pdf/capital_cost_AEO2025.pdf">gas-fired power plant</a> to serve it, the construction on both could start around the same time. Both could be ready in <a href="https://bush.tamu.edu/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/V5-3-AI-Data-Centers_Final.pdf">two and a half to three years</a>, and the textile mill could start paying for the costs necessary to serve it.</p>

<p>Modern data centers use a similar amount of electricity but <a href="https://dgtlinfra.com/building-data-center-construction/">can be built</a> in nine to 12 months. To meet that projected demand, construction of a new gas-fired power plant, or a solar farm with battery storage, must begin a year‚Äîmaybe two‚Äîbefore the data center breaks ground.</p>
<p>During the time spent building the electrical supply, computing technology advances, including both the capabilities and the efficiency of the kinds of calculations artificial intelligence systems require. Both factors affect how much <a href="https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/10/24/what-we-know-about-energy-use-at-us-data-centers-amid-the-ai-boom/">electricity a data center will use</a> once it is built.</p>
<p>Technological, logistical and planning changes mean there is a lot of uncertainty about how much electricity a data center will ultimately use. So it‚Äôs very hard for a utility company to know how much generating capacity to start building.</p>
<p><strong>Handling the Risks of Development</strong></p>
<aside><h3><figure><img alt="üì¨" data-recalc-dims="1" src="https://i0.wp.com/fonts.gstatic.com/s/e/notoemoji/16.0/1f4ec/32.png?w=780&amp;quality=80&amp;ssl=1"/></figure></h3></aside>
<p>This uncertainty costs money: A power plant could be built in advance, only to find out that <a href="https://www.utilitydive.com/news/georgia-power-large-load-data-centers/806300/">some or all of its capacity isn‚Äôt needed</a>. Or no power plant is built, and a data center pops up,<a href="https://ceea.us/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Virginias-Energy-Crossroads-CEEA-Oct-2025-Final.pdf">competing for a limited supply of electricity</a>.</p>
<p>Either way, someone needs to pay‚Äîfor the excess capacity or for the increased price of what power is available. There are three possible groups that might pay: the utilities that provide electricity, the data center customers, and the rest of the customers on the system.</p>
<p>However, utility companies have largely ensured their risk is minimal. Under most <a href="https://www.ourenergypolicy.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/COSR_history_final.pdf">state utility-regulation processes</a>, state officials review spending proposals from utility companies to determine what expenses can be passed on to customers. That includes operating expenses such as salaries and fuel costs, as well as capital investments, such as new power plants and other equipment.</p>
<p>Regulators typically examine whether proposed expenses are useful for providing service to customers and reasonable for the utility to expect to incur. Utilities have been very careful to provide their regulators with evidence about the costs and effects of proposed data centers to justify passing the costs of proposed investments in new power plants along to whomever the customers happen to be.</p>
<p>Regulators, then, are left to equitably allocate the costs to the prospective data center customers and the rest of the ratepayers, including homes and businesses. In different states, this is playing out differently.</p>
<h3><strong>Kentucky‚Äôs Approach to Usefulness</strong></h3>
<p>Kentucky is attempting to address the demand uncertainty by conditionally <a href="https://www.lpm.org/news/2025-10-28/kentucky-regulators-grant-lg-e-ku-permission-to-build-power-plants-for-future-data-centers">approving two new natural gas-fired generators</a> in the state. However, the utility companies‚ÄîLouisville Gas &amp; Electric and Kentucky Utilities‚Äîmust demonstrate that those plants will actually be needed and used. But it‚Äôs not clear how they could do that, especially considering the time frames involved.</p>
<p>For instance, suppose the utility has a letter of agreement or even a contract with a new data center or other large customer. That might be sufficient proof for the regulator to approve charging customers for the costs of building a new power plant.</p>
<p>But it‚Äôs not clear what would happen if the data center ends up not being built, or needing much less power than expected. If the utility can‚Äôt get the money from the data center company‚Äîbecause they bill customers based on actual usage‚Äîthat leaves regular consumers on the hook.</p>
<h3><strong>Ohio‚Äôs ‚ÄòDemand Ratchet‚Äô and Credit Guarantee</strong></h3>
<p>In Ohio, the major power company AEP has a <a href="https://www.aepohio.com/company/about/rates/data-center-tariff/">specific rate plan for data centers</a> and other large electricity customers. One element, called a ‚Äúdemand ratchet,‚Äù is designed to mitigate month-to-month uncertainty in electricity consumption by data centers. The data center‚Äôs monthly bill is based on the current month‚Äôs demand or 85% of the highest monthly demand from the previous 11 months‚Äîwhichever is higher.</p>

<p>The benefit is that it protects against a data center using huge amounts of electricity one month and very little the next, which would otherwise yield a much lower bill. The ratchet helps ensure that the data center is paying a significant share of the cost of providing enough electricity, even if it doesn‚Äôt use as much as was expected.</p>
<p>This ratchet effectively locks in the data center‚Äôs payments for 12 months, but regulators might expect a longer commitment from the center. For instance, Florida‚Äôs utilities regulator has <a href="https://www.floridapsc.com/pscfiles/library/filings/2025/15178-2025/15178-2025.pdf">approved</a> an <a href="https://www.floridapsc.com/pscfiles/library/filings/2025/08075-2025/08075-2025.pdf">agreement</a> that would require a data center company to pay for 70% of the agreed-upon demand in their entire electricity contract, even if the company didn‚Äôt use the power.</p>
<p>Another aspect of Ohio‚Äôs approach addresses the risk of changing business plans or technology. AEP requires a credit guarantee, like a deposit, letter of credit or parent company guarantee of payment, equal to 50% of the customer‚Äôs expected minimum bill under the contract. While this theoretically reduces the risk borne by other customers, it also raises concerns.</p>
<p>For example, a utility may not end up signing contracts directly with a large, well-known, wealthy technology company but with a subsidiary corporation with a more generic name‚Äîimagine something like ‚ÄúWestside Data Center LLC‚Äù‚Äîcreated solely to build and operate one data center. If the data center‚Äôs plans or technology changes, that subsidiary could declare bankruptcy, leaving the other customers with the remaining costs.</p>
<h3><strong>Harnessing Strength in Flexibility</strong></h3>
<p>A key advantage to these new types of customers is that they are extremely nimble in the <a href="https://www.texastribune.org/2024/01/03/texas-bitcoin-profit-electricity/">way they use</a> electricity.</p>
<p>If data centers can <a href="https://www.texastribune.org/2024/01/03/texas-bitcoin-profit-electricity/">make money based on their flexibility</a>, as they have in Texas, then a portion of those profits can be returned to the other customers that shared the investment risk. A similar mechanism is <a href="https://www.ksdk.com/article/news/local/missouri-new-data-center-rules-shielding-residents-from-infrastructure-and-power-costs/63-fae42629-d6c5-449d-a5c1-30bd77474039">being implemented in Missouri</a>: If the utility makes extra money from large customers, then 65% of that revenue increase is returned to the other customers.</p>
<p>Change is coming to the U.S. electricity system, but nobody is sure how much. The methods by which states are trying to allocate the cost of that uncertainty vary, but the critical element is understanding their respective strengths and weaknesses to craft a system that is fair for everyone.</p>
