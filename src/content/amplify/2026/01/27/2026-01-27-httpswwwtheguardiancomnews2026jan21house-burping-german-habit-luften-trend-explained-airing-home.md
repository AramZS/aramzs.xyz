<h2 data-gu-name="standfirst">It’s a simple way to keep homes fresh and mould-free, and has caught on in the US. But there are a few potential downsides ...</h2>
<p><strong>Name:</strong> House burping.</p>
<p><strong>Age:</strong> New to America, ancient in <a data-component="auto-linked-tag" data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/germany">Germany</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Appearance:</strong> Breezy.</p>
<p><strong>House burping? Obviously, I try to burp at home and not in our open-plan office, but it depends on what I’ve had for lunch. Sometimes it just has to come out, you know?</strong> That’s more information about your digestive tract than I need, given we’re talking about whether houses themselves need burping.</p>
<p><strong>A house doesn’t have a stomach. So: no</strong><strong>. </strong>“House burping” is what America’s content creators have christened the German practice of <em>Lüften</em>, or airing out their homes by opening windows, presumably because it’s a bit like burping a baby. TikTok is full of them enthusiastically describing it as a “<a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.tiktok.com/@momslovehacks/video/7563328517098704142">mom hack</a>”, or explaining it’s “<a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://vm.tiktok.com/ZNRBmSKjS/">supposed to keep sickness away</a>”.</p>
<p><strong>Does this </strong><em><strong>Lüften</strong></em><strong> keep sickness away, then? </strong>It’s supposed to shift stale air, ensure adequate ventilation and prevent mould buildup, all of which are good for people and places (<em>Lüften</em> is even mandatory in some leases in Germany). The idea that it’s a health essential got even more popular during the pandemic, when <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2020/sep/30/germans-embrace-fresh-air-to-ward-off-coronavirus">Chancellor Merkel said</a><em> Lüften</em> could be “one of the cheapest and most effective ways” to prevent the spread of Covid.</p>
<p><strong>And what do the Germans think of </strong><em><strong>Lüften</strong></em><strong> going viral Stateside? </strong>Mostly they seem to be mocking these TikToks with their own <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.tiktok.com/@niemandnurnessa/video/7591454272823266582">amused reaction videos</a> and comments like “America discovers fresh air. God help me …” and “<a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://vm.tiktok.com/ZNRBmSKjS/">Lüften is a TIKTOKTREND?!</a>”.</p>
<p><strong>But they must basically approve, right? Make America Fresh Again! </strong>Actually, <em>Lüften</em> isn’t uncontroversial, even in Germany. Paradoxically, Germans are also reputed to be extremely averse to draughts. There’s a <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2020/sep/30/germans-embrace-fresh-air-to-ward-off-coronavirus">well-known retort</a> to people who insist on airing when it’s Baltic: <em>Erfroren sind schon viele, erstunken ist noch keiner</em>.</p>
<p><strong>Meaning? </strong>Many people have frozen to death, but no one has ever died from a bad smell.</p>
<p><strong>That’s not what my colleagues say when I’m eating my sardine and egg salad al desko. Are there other objections? </strong>Yes – <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2214629620304631?via%3Dihub#!">in 2021</a>, researchers suggested <em>Lüften</em> could be undermining energy efficiency, thus contributing to greenhouse emissions. As researcher Amelie Bauer <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.politico.eu/article/germanys-energy-efficiency-open-windows-ventilation/">told Politico</a>, despite living in well-ventilated buildings that didn’t need airing, residents “stuck to their habits and kept opening windows”. Plus <em>Lüften</em> can lead to relationship breakdown.</p>
<p><strong>What?! </strong>In a report on the house-burping trend, <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2026/01/20/luften-open-windows-house-burping/">the Washington Post</a> spoke to a woman who said her half-German boyfriend’s insistence on airing their Berlin flat overnight, however cold it was, “actually became one of the bigger friction points in our relationship” (apparently three bats flew in one night). They split up.</p>
<p><strong>Yikes. Are you supposed to leave windows open all night? </strong>No. According to a <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.instagram.com/reel/DSw_WxRD4jT/?igsh=azk2eTA0bWVtOGY%3D">German foreign ministry</a> Instagram account that explains German culture to the uninitiated, <em>Stoßlüften</em> (that’s shock ventilation – opening a few windows wide) only takes a few minutes. Freezing to death and bat visitors are definitely optional.</p>
<p><strong>Do say:</strong> “Open the <em>Drehkippfenster</em> (tilting window), <em>bitte</em>, it’s time to <em>querlüften</em> (cross-ventilate).”</p>
<p><strong>Don’t say:</strong> “Someone crack a window, I think this flat has farted.”</p>
