<p>The EU and India signed a landmark trade agreement as both look to bolster commercial alliances amid threats from Washington.</p>
<p>The EU expects to <a href="https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/da/ip_26_184">double exports to India by 2032</a> as part of the deal, which was negotiated for two decades, while Delhi, subject of 50% tariffs from Washington, anticipates EU-bound exports to soar. “<a href="https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/crrnee01r9jo">We have concluded the mother of all deals,</a>” the European Commission president said.</p>
<p>US President Donald Trump’s threats have instilled a sense of urgency in the EU, which has accelerated talks with several trading partners.</p>
<p>Washington, meanwhile, remains steadfast in using its economic weight to extract concessions from allies: On Monday Trump announced 25% duties on South Korea for “<a href="https://edition.cnn.com/2026/01/26/economy/tariffs-trump-south-korea">not living up</a>” to an agreement.</p>

<p>A growing number of experts warned of the risks of inflation accelerating in the US, even as markets bet that policymakers will lower borrowing costs.</p>
<p>Traders are pricing in <a href="https://www.cmegroup.com/markets/interest-rates/cme-fedwatch-tool.html">at least two cuts this year</a>, believing the Federal Reserve will prioritize a soft labor market. But inflation could yet worsen, two leading economists warned in a LinkedIn post, projecting it <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/risk-higher-us-inflation-2026-peter-orszag-c6z8e">could top 4% in 2026</a>, while Germany’s Kiel Institute projected that the Trump administration’s tariffs meant “US companies will be confronted with shrinking margins and consumers with <a href="https://www.kielinstitut.de/publications/news/americas-own-goal-americans-pay-almost-entirely-for-trumps-tariffs/">higher prices in the long run</a>.”</p>
<p>In a note to clients, the investment firm Bridgewater warned that rising protectionism and the race to build out AI infrastructure were both likely to drive inflation.</p>

<p>Brazilian leader Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva agreed to visit Washington following a call with US President Donald Trump, a sign that bilateral tensions are easing.</p>
<p>Trump imposed 50% tariffs on Latin America’s biggest economy last year over what he said was the unfair treatment of former Brazilian president and ideological ally Jair Bolsonaro.</p>
<p>Though the row has calmed in recent months, Lula has criticized Washington’s capture of Venezuela’s leader and called on Trump to <a href="https://www.reuters.com/world/americas/brazils-lula-holds-call-with-trump-agrees-visit-washington-2026-01-26/">limit the focus of his Board of Peace</a> to Gaza, citing concern it could rival the United Nations.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, in a call with China’s leader last week, <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-23/china-s-xi-speaks-with-brazil-s-lula-vows-to-deepen-ties">Lula vowed to work with Beijing</a> as Trump vies to reshape the global geopolitical order.</p>

<p>UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer headed to China for a visit that will see him walking a tricky tightrope.</p>
<p>Starmer wants to rekindle Britain’s flagging economy, especially as Washington becomes more unpredictable. But he is <a href="https://www.politico.eu/article/keir-starmer-china-donald-trump-emmanuel-macron-europe/">loath to antagonize the White House</a>, Politico reported. A further complication is that his government plans to <a href="https://www.ft.com/content/9bfe5659-751b-43b2-b69d-93d49c845e99">heighten scrutiny of Chinese espionage</a>, the Financial Times noted.</p>
<p>The West is courting China — the leaders of Canada, Finland, France, and Germany have also either visited recently or will soon — but a former Canadian diplomat jailed by China warned that drawing close to an authoritarian, expansionist Beijing also has risks. “[If] your long-term partner [becomes] abusive,” he told Semafor, you shouldn’t “<a href="https://www.semafor.com/article/01/26/2026/michael-kovrig-on-the-risks-of-middle-powers-turning-to-beijing">hop into bed with another serial abuser</a>.”</p>

<p>William Foege, the mastermind behind the world’s eradication of smallpox, died aged 89. The disease was among the world’s deadliest: It killed around a third of those infected, and as recently as 1967, there were an estimated 15 million cases worldwide. But Foege’s strategy of “ring vaccination” — inoculating close contacts of a case — <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/01/24/us/william-h-foege-dead.html">helped control outbreaks efficiently</a>. The last recorded case was in 1977.</p>
<p>Foege also led efforts to end polio, and said in 2013 that the project was close to success. That victory, though, may be further off: A senior US health official recently argued that the polio vaccine <a href="https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/01/do-we-really-need-polio-shots-deep-thoughts-by-rfk-jr-advisor-get-dragged/">does not reduce the disease’s spread</a>, part of a wider vaccine-skeptical shift in the Trump administration.</p>

<p>Guinea said exports of bauxite jumped by 25% last year to meet soaring demand from China, which has recently tightened its grip on the African nation’s resources.</p>
<p>Government data showed that 74% of Guinea’s bauxite — used to produce aluminum, key to the global energy transition — went to China; <a href="https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/guineas-bauxite-exports-jump-25-183-million-tons-2025-chinese-demand-2026-01-26/">Guinea’s biggest bauxite producer</a> is a Chinese-owned firm.</p>
<p>Beijing has intensified its push to control African resources in the face of competition from the US. On Monday Chinese mining giant Zijin Gold agreed to buy a Canadian firm <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-27/china-s-zijin-gold-jumps-19-after-4-billion-africa-mine-deal">with vast operations on the continent</a> for $4 billion. Washington has made moves of its own: Envoys of some of Africa’s biggest mining nations are due to visit the US capital next month <a href="https://africa.businessinsider.com/local/markets/uschina-mineral-race-heats-up-as-africas-top-mining-nations-head-to-washington/dchw1h2">for a high-level summit</a>.</p>

<p>A brutal cold snap in the US drove natural gas prices higher globally, highlighting the world’s growing dependence on American fossil fuels.</p>
<p>The Trump administration has, largely successfully, prioritized the export of liquefied natural gas, with Europe particularly dependent on US supplies after slashing its reliance on Russia.</p>
<p>But the storm — which has left 10 people dead and a million without power — has led to surging gas prices in the US, up around 120% in the past week, as suppliers have had to shut drilling sites because of the cold and prioritize domestic demand. “<a href="https://think.ing.com/articles/the-commodities-feed-have-us-natural-gas-prices-peaked270126a/">Prices have likely peaked</a>,” ING commodities analysts forecast. As one Reuters columnist put it: “When the US freezes, <a href="https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/when-us-freezes-global-lng-market-catches-cold-2026-01-26/">the global LNG market catches a cold</a>.”</p>

<p>Venezuela’s interim President Delcy Rodríguez moved to strengthen her grip on power as she faces domestic and foreign threats.</p>
<p>She has shaken up the country’s military, long the basis of power for the ruling Socialist party, replacing <a href="https://www.elperiodico.com/es/internacional/20260122/delcy-rodriguez-remueve-parte-cupula-125982787">at least half a dozen top officials</a>.</p>
<p>Rodríguez has also responded to US pressure by fast-tracking a law to open the state-controlled oil sector to private and foreign investment. The move appears to have worked on at least one person: US President Donald Trump, who has pressed global oil companies to invest tens of billions of dollars in Venezuela in order to tap the nation’s huge crude reserves, said Rodríguez was showing “<a href="https://x.com/AristeguiOnline/status/2014560002049020150">very strong leadership.</a>”</p>

<p>Russia, Ukraine, and the US will hold talks in Abu Dhabi today.</p>
<p>The meeting, which officials said would be the first trilateral talks since Moscow’s 2022 invasion, comes after a US envoy met Russian President Vladimir Putin in Moscow, and Washington and Kyiv agreed to postwar security guarantees. All sides will send senior negotiators, in what the BBC called “<a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cx20011w4zzo">a measure of the seriousness of the talks</a>,” but there are sticking points, notably the Kremlin’s demand that Ukraine give up 25% of the Donetsk region, still partly controlled by Kyiv.</p>
<p>The EU is also looking to the postwar period: The European Commission said a multi-billion-dollar plan to rebuild Ukraine and integrate it into the EU single market was “<a href="https://p.dw.com/p/57H90">close to an agreement</a>.”</p>

<p>Analysts and economists downplayed the possibility of foreign investors exiting US assets en masse, in large part because they have few other options.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.cbsnews.com/news/danish-pension-fund-treasuries-government-finances-trump-greenland/">A Danish pension fund</a> and <a href="https://www.ft.com/content/9b2f8903-4350-45a5-a915-a58b6f9b35fb">a major global bond investor</a> said this month they were diversifying away from US bonds, spurring President Donald Trump to threaten “<a href="https://www.barrons.com/articles/trump-retaliation-sell-america-1dde1429?gaa_at=eafs&amp;gaa_n=AWEtsqecuHEv5E4MzMPLXK6n9ttCS2M7bvybZI7imGDG8tK3Ya0Wpl08rZ2WZAVWsSc%3D&amp;gaa_ts=697341bb&amp;gaa_sig=KUqd6U13RS_j7T_j7Hcc-4SSxiA8tbW11YsC98wjZ9h9uJytWpONLkzJNDOHKszRQtv35kRcwR9THRVJOqizxg%3D%3D">big retaliation</a>” were Europeans — cumulatively the largest holders of US Treasurys — to dump them.</p>
<p>But if European nations did try, the sheer size of the American market means they would <a href="https://www.foreignaffairs.com/podcasts/erosion-sources-american-economic-power">struggle to reallocate those resources elsewhere</a>, a former Federal Reserve vice-chair noted on the Foreign Affairs podcast. And a fire sale could end up <a href="https://www.ft.com/content/beeaf869-ca12-4178-95a1-bfb69ee27ae4">harming European investors even more than Washington itself</a>, the Financial Times warned.</p>

<p>Latin American exports boomed last year despite US tariffs, with many nations increasing their reliance on China as their biggest goods market.</p>
<p>Since the start of his second term, US President Donald Trump has vowed to outmuscle China in the Western Hemisphere, part of <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/17/world/americas/trump-latin-america-monroe-doctrine.html">his “Donroe Doctrine” of regional dominance</a>. But Washington’s levies, paired with Beijing’s insatiable appetite for commodities such as metals, beef, and soy, have only strengthened Latin America’s ties with the Chinese economy while helping reduce its exposure to US policy changes.</p>
<p>The shift is <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-01-22/latin-american-exports-and-trump-s-tariffs">reshaping geopolitics</a>, Bloomberg reported: Trump’s moves have deepened China’s footprint, and even some of the White House’s closest regional allies recently <a href="https://www.independentespanol.com/noticias/mundo/europa/argentina-recibe-el-primer-envio-de-autos-electricos-chinos-en-medio-de-la-crisis-economica-b2905285.html">boosted trade ties to Beijing</a>.</p>
