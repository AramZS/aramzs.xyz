<h2>Independent research identifies few learning gains</h2>
<p>Share</p>
<figure><img alt="4th graders work on a laptop at school." data-nimg="1" sizes="(min-width: 960px) 700px, 95vw" src="https://marber-cdn.economist.com/foundations/latest/images/image/image-placeholder.svg"/><figcaption>Out with the chalkboardPhotograph: Getty Images</figcaption></figure>
<p>Jan 22nd 2026|New York|4 min read</p>
<p>McPherson Middle School in Kansas had been burned before by education technology, but in 2022 school leaders were ready to try again. They selected a digital programme called IXL from a statewide recommendation list. It promised instruction tailored to each student’s level, igniting quick gains. The school used it to assign most in-class independent maths work. “We thought it was going to be really magical,” says Inge Esping, the principal.</p>
