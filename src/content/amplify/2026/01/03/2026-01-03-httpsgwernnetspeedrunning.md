<p>On statistical and psychological grounds, we probably are not losing many future Einsteins to speedrunning or streaming or e-sports etc.</p>
<blockquote> <p>the main issue with video games is that a guy who, if he lived in 1820s germany, would have done something like document every type of beetle in his local province instead ends up making a 26 part youtube series about how to get all the rings in every sonic game</p> <p><a data-href-mobile="https://nitter.net/owenbroadcast/status/1288932593199656960" data-link-icon="twitter" data-link-icon-color="#1da1f2" data-link-icon-type="svg" href="https://x.com/owenbroadcast/status/1288932593199656960">owen cyclops‚Å†</a>, 2020-07-30</p> </blockquote>
<p><a data-dropcap-type="kanzlei" data-letter="W" href="https://gwern.net/dropcap#kanzlei">W</a>hile funny, on purely statistical grounds, we can be highly confident that the YouTuber in question would <em>not</em> have done anything particularly remarkable; so the series did not come at the expense of our next Linnaeus or Einstein.</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#base-rates">Base Rates</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>From a statistical perspective, we can safely say that the probability that our hypothetical <em>Sonic</em> streamer would have been a noted entomologist is near zero, because the probability for any given person of becoming a noted <em>anything</em> is near zero.</p>
<p>Few people are remembered centuries later. Thousands of German men passed through academic life in that province of 1820s Germany without leaving any mark more notable than their payslips in the ledger-books. A major, successful video maker is a person who is one in millions; let‚Äôs say that our entomologist is merely one in tens of thousands, and there is in fact a strong correlation between ‚Äògood at making <em>Sonic</em> videos‚Äô and ‚Äògood at cataloguing beetles‚Äô. Then the probability that our video maker would also have been the top entomologist would be‚Ä¶ <a data-attribute-title="‚ÄòCalculating The Gaussian Expected Maximum ¬ß Probability of Bivariate Maximum‚Äô, Gwern 2016" data-filesize-bytes="74535" data-filesize-percentage="48" data-link-icon="gwern" data-link-icon-type="svg" href="https://gwern.net/order-statistic#probability-of-bivariate-maximum">surprisingly close‚Å†</a> to one in tens of thousands. (Note how few Nobel Prize winners, out of the <a data-link-icon="üèÖ" data-link-icon-color="#cc9b40" data-link-icon-type="text" href="https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/lists/all-nobel-prizes/">~1,000 to date‚Å†</a>, manage to win <em>two</em>.)</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#randomness-in-success">Randomness in Success</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>Indeed, our video maker would not be so successful at <em>videos</em> either in another history of the world; the Salganik/Watts <a data-filesize-bytes="128926" data-filesize-percentage="9" data-link-icon="LW" data-link-icon-color="#7faf83" data-link-icon-type="text" data-url-archive="https://gwern.net/doc/www/www.greaterwrong.com/9bf41079ea14071c469b830a74f3258742bc0587.html" data-url-iframe="https://www.greaterwrong.com/posts/Sx6SBmyut73u6atgB/the-best-frequently-don-t-rise-to-the-top/comment/zDownzL7eqSshFtRX?format=preview&amp;theme=classic" href="https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Sx6SBmyut73u6atgB/the-best-frequently-don-t-rise-to-the-top?commentId=zDownzL7eqSshFtRX">‚Å†experimental results on randomizing media markets‚Å†</a> suggest that when an author like <a data-attribute-title="J. K. Rowling" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._K._Rowling#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._K._Rowling">J. K. Rowling‚Å†</a> is #1 in our world, they might be thousands of rungs down in another world‚Äîor worse!‚Å†<a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#fn1"><sup>‚Å†1‚Å†</sup></a> Random chance and <a data-attribute-title="Matthew effect" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_effect#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_effect">network effects‚Å†</a> may elevate an ordinary, mediocre rival of little talent, and force him out of the video game, and the more randomness, the more likely the winners were ordinary people who are thus not ‚Äòwasted‚Äô.</p>
<p>We can predict success for groups, but not individuals; forests, not trees. For individuals, ‚Äútime and chance happen to them all‚Äù.</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#regression-to-the-mean">Regression to the Mean</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>Given that random chance often elevates individuals whose specific talents align perfectly (and perhaps luckily) with one narrow niche, <a data-attribute-title="Regression toward the mean" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regression_toward_the_mean#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regression_toward_the_mean">it follows that‚Å†</a> these same individuals are unlikely to be exceptional across the board. This is why elite performers in one area so often disappoint in another.</p>
<p>Anyone who is world-class is closer to an <a data-attribute-title="Idiot savant" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiot_savant#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiot_savant">idiot savant‚Å†</a> of their specialty than a Renaissance man. The skillsets, while they may seem similar from a distance, up close in detail, look different: ‚Äòcollecting beetles and rings‚Äô may be written with the same verb, but finding a hidden beetle is nothing like having the lightning reflexes to execute memorized <em>Sonic</em> timings, and someone who is world-class at the former may be rubbish at the latter.</p>
<p>It can be shocking how narrowly specialized some expertise can be: no chess grandmaster has ever become an equally adept Go player, for example. When such people retire or turn over a new leaf, they are rarely heard from again.‚Å†<a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#fn2"><sup>‚Å†2‚Å†</sup></a></p>
<p>This is why highly successful people often turn out to have been good at a hobby like chess, but not <em>great</em>; and those who are great, aren‚Äôt great at anything else‚Äîthe lightning did not strike twice, as they simply regress to their mean. So we might find that a notable scientist spent time as a teen trying to collect that last darn ring, or perhaps has a hobby after a long day of work of trying to perfect his <em>Sonic</em> speedrun‚Ä¶ but there‚Äôs no problem with any of that. (And we can note that there are no cases of great researchers announcing that they are quitting their tenured positions at the peak of their careers to stream instead‚Äîto go into industry and especially Wall Street, or politics, or to become science popularizers, perhaps, but not into <em>Sonic</em> videos.)</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#obsession-as-key-input">Obsession As Key Input</a><figure></figure></h1>
<figure><img alt="A crude crayon drawing by a kindergartener of the ‚ÄòSanic‚Äô meme character saying ‚ÄòGotta go fast‚Äô. Created by Gwern Branwen 2025-05-01 using DALL¬∑E 3 to humorously illustrate a page on softwre optimization." data-aspect-ratio="1 / 1" src="https://gwern.net/doc/ai/nn/transformer/gpt/dall-e/3/2024-05-01-gwern-dalle3-sanic-gottagofast.jpg"/><figcaption>You gotta go faster. No, faster! Faster! (Don‚Äôt ask why.)</figcaption></figure>
<p>From a closer view, we can see why. Great achievement is the final product of multiple factors: idiosyncratic talent, preferences, health, good luck‚Äîand particularly key is ‚Äòmotivation‚Äô, or more pointedly, <em>obsession</em>. Generally, someone who documents every kind of beetle, does not feel neutrally about them, and is not simply clocking in 9‚Äì5PM for the paycheck: he is obsessed. When we look at streamers or figures like MrBeast, they do not seem exceptionally smart or talented in general.‚Å†<a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#fn3"><sup>‚Å†3‚Å†</sup></a></p>
<p>Obsession cannot be predicted or learned, and you cannot just want what you want; it strikes out of nowhere (much like sexual fetishes, also often acquired in childhood for mysterious exogenous reasons). One child plays <em>Sonic the Hedgehog</em> for a few months and enjoys it and moves on with their life with some memories and a lingering nostalgia for the <a data-attribute-title="Green Hill Zone [&lt;em&gt;Sonic The Hedgehog&lt;/em&gt; OST]" data-link-icon="youtube" data-link-icon-color="#ff0033" data-link-icon-type="svg" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=G-i8HYi1QH0">‚Å†classic theme‚Å†</a> of <a data-attribute-title="Green Hill Zone" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Hill_Zone#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Hill_Zone">‚ÄúGreen Hill Zone‚Äù‚Å†</a>; but another is transfixed by it, so 20 years later they are making videos on it‚Ä¶ And no one can say why.‚Å†<a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#fn4"><sup>‚Å†4‚Å†</sup></a> If one becomes obsessed with something, one must hope that one <a data-attribute-title="The Lottery of Fascinations" data-link-icon="SSC" data-link-icon-color="#5175c2" data-link-icon-type="text,tri" href="https://slatestarcodex.com/2013/06/30/the-lottery-of-fascinations/">‚Å†wins the lottery of fascinations‚Å†</a> for a prize that the world will reward.</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#reward-structure-fit">Reward-Structure Fit</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>Indeed, we might wonder if, rather than risking cannibalizing greatness, such people are <em>less</em> likely to achieve greatness. Does a willingness to become obsessed with a small, static, fixed problem in exchange for rapid social media-style gamified rewards bode well for their counterfactual career? (What should we think of someone who is both willing and able to spend all that time on ring collecting, but who never grows beyond that comfort zone of <a data-attribute-title="Flow (psychology)" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(psychology)#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(psychology)">flow‚Å†</a> and eventually yearn to, eg. make their <em>own</em> ring collecting games, or at least level mods?)</p>
<p>Greatness often requires the ability to endure years or decades of anxiety and uncertainty with no sign of progress, flexibility and openness, an ability to redefine or exploit serendipity; the obsession should be with a broad topic or goal, rather than fixated. (And would such a person be able to collaborate on research, or be a good principal investigator managing a lab?)</p>
<p>Perhaps we should be glad that such individuals can find satisfying careers elsewhere‚Äîrather than wasting their time (and our resources) in STEM‚Ä¶?</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#stem-supply-versus-demand">STEM Supply versus Demand</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>Once we leave the individual anecdote, it is unclear if there is any <em>group</em> loss either. After all, we produce a vast amount of research these days, vastly more than 1820s Germany ever did (even allowing for the extreme plunge in per capita productivity), and produce far more researchers than we can gainfully employ; so there is no <em>prima facie</em> case that we are ‚Äòwasting‚Äô our scholarly potential.</p>
<p>Nor are there that many video makers: how many people in the USA could reasonably be said to make such things fulltime as a life-long career rather than as a hobby, or an experiment before they switch gears and get ‚Äòa real job‚Äô? Sure, many people may dabble or aspire, but there just aren‚Äôt that many in total (who will watch the videos, or pay their bills?).</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#video-value">Video Value</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>Given the oversupply of R&amp;D and <a data-attribute-title="Diminishing returns" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diminishing_returns#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diminishing_returns">diminishing returns‚Å†</a> (as seen in things like <a data-attribute-title="Multiple discovery" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_discovery#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_discovery">multiple discovery‚Å†</a> and a deluge of worthless papers being published), with <a data-attribute-title="Pareto distribution" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pareto_distribution#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pareto_distribution">Pareto distributions‚Å†</a> everywhere, perhaps we should not scoff too quickly at our video makers.</p>
<p>Such documentation is itself valuable. How historians would love to have similar ephemera from centuries or millennia ago! I daresay that a couple of such pop culture books would be of more value now that a catalogue of German beetles‚Ä¶</p>
<p>Nor can we just assume future historians will take care of this, when we live in a <a data-attribute-title="Digital dark age" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_dark_age#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_dark_age">‚Äúdigital dark age‚Äù‚Å†</a>. Knowledge is lost online all too quickly, and while perhaps for a classic <em>Sonic</em> console game we can assume that there will always be a working emulator &amp; ROM <em>somewhere</em>, for many aspects of pop culture, this is not true: if it is not documented <em>now</em>, it never will be, because the websites won‚Äôt be archived (eg. Discord, TikTok) or the video game may be unavailable in any standalone form, or DRM blocks archiving. Such video-makers are our era‚Äôs folklorists &amp; ethnographers &amp; anthropologists.</p>
<p>(We might also hope that videos might be good demonstrations of how to do research, present information etc, in a more entertaining form than school, and that ‚Äòhoney helps make the medicine go down‚Äô. The alternatives may be worse.)</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#perfect-conditions-never-come">Perfect Conditions Never Come</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>Nor is it if the 1820s lacked for ways for people to waste their lives. Perhaps our entomologist could have instead been nerd-sniped by Christian theology (which was a large part of the reason 1820s Germany <em>had</em> a higher education system at all) and spent the rest of his life writing turgidly Teutonic tomes explaining how Calvinism was correct about everything and Catholicism a vulgarly corrupt transmontane heresy. We would have been happier now had he become obsessed by something that people then considered trivial, such as a meticulously researched history of how citizens of 17<sup>th</sup> century Madagascar made an omelette. And2020s America has little to be ashamed of compared to, say, the 1820s (<a data-attribute-title="Pedestrianism" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedestrianism#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedestrianism">pedestrianism‚Å†</a>, <a href="https://burialsandbeyond.com/2023/11/05/the-weird-world-of-mummy-parties/">mummy-unrolling</a> <a data-link-icon="atlas-obscura" data-link-icon-color="#ad8f68" data-link-icon-type="svg" href="https://www.atlasobscura.com/articles/victorian-party-people-unrolled-mummies-for-fun">parties‚Å†</a>) or 1920s (literal <a data-attribute-title="Philipp von Ferrary" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philipp_von_Ferrary#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philipp_von_Ferrary">stamp collecting‚Å†</a>, <a data-attribute-title="Dance marathon" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dance_marathon#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dance_marathon">dance‚Å†</a> <a href="https://www.historylink.org/file/5534">marathons</a>, <a data-attribute-title="Pole sitting" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole_sitting#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole_sitting">flag pole sitting‚Å†</a>, or <a data-attribute-title="Goldfish swallowing" data-link-icon="wikipedia" data-link-icon-type="svg" data-url-iframe="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldfish_swallowing#bodyContent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldfish_swallowing">goldfish swallowing‚Å†</a>); if we lived in any of those eras, we would find <em>plenty</em> to roll our eyes at. (At least no goldfish suffered in the making of a <em>Sonic</em> video‚Ä¶)</p>
<h1><a href="https://gwern.net/speedrunning#net-impact-nil">Net Impact: Nil</a><figure></figure></h1>
<p>So, as cute as it is, I think Mr Cyclops is ultimately incorrect in his assertion: our would-be beetle-collector wasn‚Äôt lost‚Äîhe‚Äôs just browsing a different section of the infinite library.</p>
<p>And if all those streamers and videos disappeared overnight and were replaced by other forms of recreation or entertainment, we would not observe any particular renaissance of science from all our beetle bros. We would simply observe an entire different batch of (relatively) ordinary people raised to prominence for their inherent idiosyncrasies happening to match some other peculiar niches; and those niches would, in the grand scheme of things, seem equally absurd.</p>
