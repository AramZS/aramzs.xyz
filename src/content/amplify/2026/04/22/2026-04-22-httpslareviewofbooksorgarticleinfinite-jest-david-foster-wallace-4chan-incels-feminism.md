<h2>On the internet reception and collective reading of David Foster Wallace’s magnum opus, on the occasion of its 30th anniversary reissue. </h2>

<p>DAVID FOSTER WALLACE’S novel <em>Infinite Jest</em>, which celebrates its 30th anniversary this year, is the perfect parable for reading in the internet age. This is true of the novel itself, but it is even truer of its extremely online reception: it’s hard to think of a work of literary fiction that belongs to the internet more than <em>Infinite Jest</em>. The novel’s digital reception has been big, weird, and political, ranging from sincere blog posts to absurd Twitter/X <a href="https://xcancel.com/kylerseibel/status/1795186706615791955">memes</a>; from feminist <em>Reductress</em> <a href="https://reductress.com/post/why-im-waiting-for-the-right-man-to-tell-me-to-read-infinite-jest/">parodies,</a> YouTube stunts, and BookTok <a href="https://www.tiktok.com/@interviewmag/video/7388176724388777259">videos</a> to misogynistic threads on 4chan, a message board known as a breeding ground for far-right extremism, and copypastas on Reddit; and even from Aaron Swartz’s <a href="http://www.aaronsw.com/weblog/finij">blog</a> to Luigi Mangione’s Goodreads <a href="https://lithub.com/luigi-mangione-is-currently-reading-what-can-we-really-learn-about-the-uhc-ceos-killer-based-on-the-books-hes-read/">catalog.</a></p>
<p><em>Infinite Jest </em>was published in the earliest days of the web, and even by 1998, Wallace still claimed to have never used it. Yet somehow, over the last three decades, this bandana-clad author and his magnum opus have risen to the upper echelons of online adoration and ire. Wallace is a key figure in the internet canon, especially the literary one—that special contingent of books and authors that are endlessly quoted, shared, memed, flamed, and just plain talked about online. He thus offers a way to understand the internet, and the internet, in turn, a way to understand Wallace, though admittedly in a fun house mirror kind of way, where both his searing insights and his flaws are magnified and distorted.</p>
<p>Recently, an X user <a href="https://x.com/StolenDans/status/2020708805429154163">remixed a clip</a> of Kid Rock performing at the Turning Point USA “All-American Halftime Show” by superimposing images of <em>Infinite Jest</em> and Wallace over Kid Rock’s references to the Bible and Jesus Christ. “There’s a book that’s sitting in your house somewhere that could use some dusting off,” Kid Rock preaches. “There’s a man who died for all our sins.” The post received more than 600 retweets and 4,000 likes, but it’s near impossible to explain to anyone who’s not chronically online. The joke hinges on Wallace’s ironized messianic status in internet spaces and his understood role as an almost fully decontextualized punch line (along with a communal desire to roast Kid Rock).</p>

<p>Wallace, often referred to by just his initials, has been the subject of perpetual online discourse since at least the 2010s, amplified by the 2015 film <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9Jl6qBNfQC4"><em>The End of The Tour</em>,</a> starring Jason Segel as Wallace. During this period, Wallace’s public reception split into two competing caricatures: a douchey, self-serious “litbro” who loves DFW, and his mythologized counterpart, a feminist hipster woman who hates DFW and his bros. Online and in person (though mostly online), <em>Infinite Jest</em> became a symbolic lightning rod for gendered debates about reading. Should we read the works of problematic men, the works enjoyed by problematic men?</p>
<p>In the most recent round of discourse, revitalized by the novel’s anniversary, the pendulum has swung toward “yes.” Several writers have advanced compelling defenses of the novel, and <a href="https://www.the-independent.com/arts-entertainment/books/features/infinite-jest-david-foster-wallace-men-b2912363.html">passionate</a> defenses of—or <a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/culture/2026/02/bro-meme-lit-bro-tech-bro/686143">demands</a> for further clarification about—these so-called “litbros.” For what it’s worth, I like the novel too, but these rebuttals only glancingly acknowledge the novel’s uptake in some of the darker corners of the web by misogynistic trolls and the Far Right. After the Me Too movement exploded in 2017, and Wallace’s ex-partner, the poet and memoirist Mary Karr, revealed more <a href="https://x.com/marykarrlit/status/992545700004139008">serious allegations</a> of the novelist’s abusive behavior, the dueling <em>Infinite Jest </em>fan and anti-fan narratives progressively hardened into a culture war script that traveled to some heinous places. In some corners of the internet, Wallace’s association with abuse and toxic masculinity is not a deterrent but an attraction.</p>
<p>This adoption is hinted at by Michelle Zauner, the front woman of Japanese Breakfast and author of <em>Crying in H Mart </em>(2021), who wrote the foreword to Little, Brown and Company’s new anniversary edition. While in the official foreword Zauner describes <em>Infinite Jest </em>as part of the “litbro” tradition, she describes it <a href="https://www.them.us/story/japanese-breakfast-michelle-zauner-androgyny-melancholy-brunettes-sad-women-infinite-jest">elsewhere</a> as part of the “incel canon,” suggesting that it is a favorite among communities of “involuntarily celibate” (and very online) men whose interest in, yet discomfort with, women bleeds into sexism and even violence. In the last several years, a number of <a href="https://pureportal.strath.ac.uk/en/studentTheses/the-incel-bookclub-inceldom-toxic-masculinity-and-the-literary-ca/">scholars</a> and writers such as <a href="https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674241411">Donna Zuckerberg,</a> <a href="https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517917081/speculative-whiteness/">Jordan S. Carroll,</a> and Erin Spampinato have examined connections between literature and incel types or the “alt-right,” with Spampinato <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/books/2018/jun/04/incel-movement-literary-classics-behind-misogyny">arguing</a> that the incel may even be “a product of Anglo-American literary culture,” and that Wallace may be its “patron saint.”</p>
<p>How far “down the rabbit hole” do Wallace bros really go? To follow the radicalization of the DFW bro, I started collecting data from the far-right-leaning and often extremely misogynistic message board 4chan. Tracing how<em> Infinite Jest</em> has traveled online over the last 30 years, from blogs to Twitter to the bowels of 4chan, reveals something important not just about the novel but also about literature in contemporary American culture, and how the internet is transforming our relationship to literature.</p>
<p>One thing is clear: we have, as Wallace might put it, major-league levels of anxiety around reading.</p>
<p>Reading requires deep attention, and managing one’s attention in a media-saturated, ultra-entertaining historical moment is one of the primary fixations of <em>Infinite Jest</em>. This idea is staged through the sheer size of the book itself. Can the reader sustain their interest across a thousand difficult pages, especially as media like TV or TikTok promise to deliver a quicker dopamine hit?</p>
<p>The novel takes place in a near-future, dystopian version of the United States, led by an overly tan celebrity-turned-politician who wears a toupee (prescient, indeed). This USA has aggressively absorbed Canada and Mexico, and the high-tech entertainment media of the day is designed to be addictive and make people feel bad about themselves. There is also a mysterious circulating film so beguiling that it either kills its viewers outright or leaves them permanently catatonic. And, yes, the film is titled <em>Infinite Jest</em>.</p>
<p>The idea that entertainment media might destroy us, literally and metaphorically, is one that Wallace consistently circled around, not just in <em>Infinite Jest</em> but in his <a href="https://jsomers.net/DFW_TV.pdf">essays</a> and prominent <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yiwHm6sbwR0">interviews</a> as well. In the 1990s, what Wallace worried about most was television. Today, this philosophical agenda has become popular online, where it circulates among those who (ironically) fret about what the internet and other digital technologies are doing to us. The internet has become our favorite worry machine, and we do our best worrying about it on the internet itself.</p>
<p>Last October, Eugenia Kuyda, the founder of <a href="https://replika.com/">Replika</a>—a company that makes AI “companions”—used Wallace to ruminate on whether modern technologies, and the companies behind those technologies, are spiritually (and financially) sucking us dry. “I’ve been thinking about this David Foster Wallace quote more and more recently,” Kuyda <a href="https://xcancel.com/ekuyda/status/1980515271002714316">posted,</a> sharing a section of Wallace’s conversation with journalist David Lipsky—from Lipsky’s <a href="https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/201491/although-of-course-you-end-up-becoming-yourself-by-david-lipsky/">memoir,</a> later dramatized in <em>The End of the Tour</em>—in which Wallace revisits a theme from <em>Infinite Jest</em>:</p>
<blockquote>The technology is just gonna get better and better and better and better. And it’s gonna get easier and easier, and more and more convenient, and more and more pleasurable, to be alone with images on a screen, given to us by people who do not love us but want our money. Which is all right. In low doses, right? But if that’s the basic main staple of your diet, you’re gonna die. In a meaningful way, you’re going to die.</blockquote>
<p>This Wallace quotation was reposted more than 2,000 times on X, and received more than 15,000 likes. “[H]oly shit, you founded Replika and you’re saying this??” one X user asked. “What kind of cognitive dissonance level are you on??” “Very high,” Kuyda replied.</p>
<p><em>Infinite Jest</em> explores the value of reading fiction in a hypermediated, hamster-wheel age through its own formal and stylistic choices. The novel subjects the reader to the experience of information deluge, presenting a riotous torrent of characters, nonlinear storylines, encyclopedia dumps, tangents, and endnotes in the hundreds. This ricocheting narrative ride is, like any good antidote, seemingly both of the internet and against it, subject to its own attention deficit while demanding readers’ rigorous, patient concentration. The novel hovers closest to the perspectives of Hal, a high school student at a competitive tennis academy who struggles with a weed addiction, and Don Gately, a man in recovery and staff member at a halfway house.</p>
<p>But the frenzied narrative is ultimately united by Wallace’s singular authorial voice—a pervasive, roaming narrative consciousness that dips into the minds and voices of characters but always remains distinct, as if it emanates from a single person’s mind and maybe even soul. This voice is so pervasive, in fact, that it’s almost hard to pinpoint specific examples, although there are several metafictional intrusions where the narrator makes clear that <em>he</em> is telling the story: “She didn’t literally say shitstorm,” the narrator clarifies, in an endnote, of a character’s dialogue. Wallace’s quintessential, quippy style suffuses everything; it ferries the reader along while also letting them into the narrator’s way of seeing things.</p>
<p>Describing just how digitally connected people are in this near-future world, the narrator explains:</p>
<blockquote>Half of all metro Bostonians now work at home via some digital link. […]</blockquote>
<blockquote>Saying this is bad is like saying traffic is bad, or health-care surtaxes, or the hazards of annular fusion: nobody but Ludditic granola-crunching freaks would call bad what no one can imagine being without.</blockquote>
<p>This cohesive, <a href="https://www.theparisreview.org/blog/2026/02/23/whats-so-funny-about-infinite-jest/">funny</a> voice testifies to the power that prose fiction holds in an attention-sapped world: through fiction, human minds can connect across time, space, and noise. This connection, Wallace believed, was urgently important and unique to prose fiction. Fiction can be an “exchange between consciousnesses, a way for human beings to talk to each other about stuff that we normally can’t talk about,” Wallace said in an <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cj0JgqOnK2M">interview</a> with Charlie Rose. “We’re all terribly, terribly lonely,” he said in <a href="https://www.thehowlingfantods.com/dfw/upcoming-publications/news-galore-and-a-newold-dfw-interview.html">another interview.</a> “And there’s a way, at least in prose fiction, that can allow you to be intimate with the world and with a mind and with characters that you just can’t be in the real world.”</p>
<p>Is this, finally, a cure to the loneliness epidemic we’ve heard so much about? For many readers, including, honestly, me, <em>Infinite Jest</em> achieves a meaningful intimacy and social nourishment that staves off at least some desolation and dread. But the promise of a thousand-page sadness tonic, mostly marketed to boys, makes at least half of the internet roll their eyes.</p>
<p>Despite the fact that Wallace had (supposedly) never logged on to the internet before writing <em>Infinite Jest</em>, some early readers felt like it spoke to the fragmented, overwhelming experience of “cruising” the mid-1990s web. When <a href="https://www.chicagotribune.com/1996/02/23/the-next-big-thing-10/">an interviewer</a> raised this potential connection to Wallace in 1996, he demurred. “This is sort of what it’s like to be alive,” Wallace explained. “You don’t have to be on the Internet for life to feel this way.” In 1998, Wallace told <a href="https://slate.com/news-and-politics/2010/11/it-s-not-very-good-for-me-when-people-treat-me-like-a-big-shot-david-foster-wallace-on-nonfiction-1998-part-5.html">another interviewer</a>: “I sometimes use a computer to type when I’ve got a lot of corrections to do, but I don’t have a modem, I’ve never been on the Internet.”</p>
<p>So, was Wallace really as offline as he claimed? In his 1996 conversations with David Lipsky, Wallace spoke about the web with more authority and seemingly more personal experience. “Because this idea that the Internet’s gonna become incredibly democratic?” Wallace pontificated. “I mean, if you’ve spent any time on the Web, you know that it’s not gonna be, because that’s completely overwhelming. There are four trillion bits coming at you, 99 percent of them are shit, and it’s too much work to do triage to decide.” This perceptive account of the internet—and Wallace’s rhetorical appeal to personal time spent on it—suggests that he had at least <em>a little</em> internet experience. And if he did, it’s curious that he denied it. Maybe he wanted to preserve the mystique that his work was apart from that world and yet somehow so prophetic about it.</p>
<p>While early readers may have detected a connection between <em>Infinite Jest</em> and the web, the novel didn’t really become an internet phenomenon until after Wallace’s death. This provided the first of many tests: how would this dense, difficult novel fare online? After Wallace took his life in September 2008, people across the internet—even those who had never read him or hadn’t liked him—began to mourn him and dig into his works. The most notable example was the <a href="https://infinitesummer.org/index">Infinite Summer project,</a> a virtual book club launched in 2009 by Seattle writer Matthew Baldwin. Readers around the world joined in, including a 25-year-old <a href="https://archive.ph/20120715170113/http:/voices.washingtonpost.com/ezra-klein/2009/06/a_supposedly_fun_thing_i_plan.html">Ezra Klein,</a> actor John Krasinski, novelist John Green (his Infinite Summer <a href="https://infinitesummer.org/archives/1486">blog post</a> is worth reading), and the Decemberists front man <a href="https://infinitesummer.org/archives/1615">Colin Meloy.</a></p>
<p>Across the project’s blog, Facebook group, LiveJournal, Shelfari, and Twitter hashtag (#infsum), readers engaged in strikingly genuine discussion and emotional conversation. To some, Infinite Summer represented the platonic ideal of an online literary community—proof that the web could produce real connection and deep engagement with literature. “[T]he Internet is frequently imagined to be one of the causes of reading’s decline, a key factor in so diminishing our attention spans that we can no longer sustain ourselves through the long-form narrative,” literary critic Kathleen Fitzpatrick <a href="https://works.hcommons.org/records/5pvbv-svz47">wrote</a> in 2012. “[A]nd yet here is a reading group inspired and sustained by its Internet context.” Fitzpatrick admitted that she might be overidealizing.</p>
<p>She was right to hedge.</p>
<p>Throughout the 2010s, the challenge of reading <em>Infinite Jest</em> became increasingly attractive to a certain segment of male readers—on the subway, at parties, and, of course, on the internet. In 2014, the 4chan literature community (/lit/) held its first annual vote for the 100 best books of all time. The winner <a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/books/comments/3rdjck/4chan_2015_book_poll_results/">that year?</a> <em>Infinite Jest</em>. The following year, it won again.</p>
<p>In January 2015, <em>Newsweek</em> ran a digital cover with an illustrated portrait of Wallace and the headline “The Grating American Novel.” Its <a href="https://www.newsweek.com/2015/01/16/turbulent-genius-david-foster-wallace-297688.html">essay</a> opened with this stunning fanboy self-declaration:</p>
<blockquote>Read what follows with a stern <em>caveat emptor</em> in mind, for it has been written by an unabashed David Foster Wallace fanboy, one of those forlorn, bespectacled young men covertly handed a copy of <em>Infinite Jest</em> in his formative years, and who subsequently recited passages from the novel the way early Christians, hiding in dim catacombs, must have read with secret, feverish ecstasy from the epistles of Paul.</blockquote>
<p>Increasingly, the Wallace fanboy was not self-described by male readers but imagined <em>by</em> women and defined in visible, fraught relation <em>to</em> women. In 2014, the satirical Twitter account @GuyInYourMFA—run by author Dana Schwartz, channeling the most insufferable white male writer in any MFA program—<a href="https://xcancel.com/GuyInYourMFA/status/533315241132974080">tweeted</a>: “I’ll be right back. Going to whisper ‘David Foster Wallace’ three times into my bathroom mirror.”</p>
<p>Things only ramped up from there. “I once dated a David Foster Wallace fanboy,” Emily Perper <a href="https://longreads.com/2015/07/01/the-cult-could-become-a-church-on-david-foster-wallace/">wrote</a> in 2015. “You know who I mean: He’s white. He’s straight. He went to a small liberal arts college. He interrogates you on which DFW books you’ve read—the novels, or just the essays?” Molly Fischer <a href="https://www.thecut.com/2015/08/david-foster-wallace-beloved-author-of-bros.html">doubled down</a> on the same idea, arguing that the fanboy is “the type who’s pestered at least one woman to the point that she quit reading <em>Infinite Jest</em> in public.” In 2017, Deirdre Coyle <a href="https://electricliterature.com/men-recommend-david-foster-wallace-to-me/">claimed</a> that “it’s hard to distinguish [her] reaction to Wallace from [her] reaction to patriarchy.”</p>
<p>My favorite: in 2016, writer and comedian Jamie Loftus began conspicuously <em>not</em> reading <em>Infinite Jest </em>and instead <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3eO2QfEGK6U">eating pages</a> of it. She ate folded-up pieces from a Lay’s barbecue chip bag, she dipped pages in a milkshake, she ate strips of it with spaghetti. Later, she <a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/we-talked-to-the-woman-who-is-butt-chugging-infinite-jest/">“butt chugged”</a> it with a turkey baster. This performative refusal to read the novel was the viral stunt version of literary critic Amy Hungerford’s more academically rooted <a href="https://www.chronicle.com/article/on-not-reading/">refusal,</a> published the same year.</p>
<p>These feminist critiques had real grounds. In his works, Wallace complicatedly represents—and sometimes reifies—misogyny, racism, and sexual violence. In <em>Infinite Jest</em>, characters hurl racial, ethnic, and ableist slurs. There are disturbing depictions of rape, incest, and pedophilia. In real life, Wallace could also be a threat. He threw a coffee table at his ex-partner, Mary Karr; he climbed onto her balcony unannounced after a breakup; and he once tried to push her from a moving car. He also slept with students and behaved badly in other ways.</p>
<p>All this may have attracted, even encouraged, Wallace’s sexist fans. At the very least, it didn’t do much to deter them.</p>
<p>The feminist backlash against <em>Infinite Jest</em> emboldened some fans and online trolls, inspiring them to embrace the novel even more. What becomes canonical on the internet is not always what is most admired, but sometimes what is most argued over, and nowhere is this clearer than on 4chan.</p>
<p>One piece of evidence that points to Wallace’s place in the forum’s canon is a widely circulated image of books “trending on 4chan,” which supposedly represents a 4chan-specific bookstore display. You can see the blue-and-yellow cover of <em>Infinite Jest</em> prominently in the center. (Many 4chan and Reddit users point out that this image is obviously doctored because <em>Infinite Jest </em>is not thick enough.)</p>

<p>Stacked around <em>Infinite Jest</em> are books that represent the worldview espoused by many 4chan users, both ironically and seriously: Adolf Hitler’s <em>Mein Kampf </em>(1925), Jordan B. Peterson’s <em>12 Rules for Life:</em> <em>An Antidote to Chaos </em>(2018), Oswald Spengler’s <em>The</em> <em>Decline of the West </em>(1918), and Agatha Christie’s novel <em>And Then There Were None </em>(1939) under its original title (which includes a racial slur). There are also more traditionally canonical literary works—like J. D. Salinger’s <em>The Catcher in the Rye </em>(1951), James Joyce’s <em>Ulysses</em> (1922) and <em>Finnegans Wake </em>(1939), Thomas Pynchon’s <em>Gravity’s Rainbow </em>(1973), and Vladimir Nabokov’s <em>Lolita </em>(1955)—many of which are associated with men behaving badly or worse.</p>
<p>The 4chan conversation about Wallace seems to have reached its peak (in terms of absolute volume) in 2015, but the discussion clearly entered a new, more heated phase post–Me Too. Often when Wallace and <em>Infinite Jest</em> were invoked in this period, 4chan users were debating, or fretting about, literature, arguing its merits and relevance with a white supremacist, chauvinistic, homophobic spin. Their anxieties could be distilled as follows: Are straight white men being <a href="https://archive.4plebs.org/pol/thread/490922378/#q490923722">forced out</a> of the publishing industry—usurped by women, queer people, and people of color? Will there ever be another David Foster Wallace? Is reading fiction <a href="https://archive.4plebs.org/pol/thread/490722406/#q490729267">“gay”</a>?</p>
<p>Many 4chan users, in other words, treat the rejection of <em>Infinite Jest</em> and DFW bros as emblematic of the (perceived) rejection of straight white men from literary life. “Reminder that the exodus of white men from literature began when women vilified men on a massive level for liking DFW… so much so, that admitting to liking Infinite Jest was seen as a massive social blunder,” one 4chan user <a href="https://warosu.org/lit/thread/24967267">announced</a> just a few months ago.</p>
<p>The apprehension about whether men—especially straight white men—are not reading, not being read, or not being published extends beyond 4chan. Over the last two years, in <em>The</em> <em>New York Times </em>alone, writers argued that <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2024/12/07/opinion/men-fiction-novels.html">“the disappearance of literary men should worry everyone,”</a> asked <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/06/25/style/fiction-books-men-reading.html">“why did the novel-reading man disappear?,”</a> and evaluated <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/07/03/arts/straight-white-male-novelists.html">“the death and life of the straight white man’s novel.”</a> (Unsurprisingly, these articles get a lot of traction on 4chan.)</p>
<p>The <a href="https://lareviewofbooks.org/av/where-have-all-the-cowboys-gone-are-literary-men-in-crisis/"><em>LARB Radio Hour</em></a> dedicated a whole episode to the subject, where the hosts tellingly invoke Wallace as one of the most recent, memorable examples of a culturally relevant, straight white male author. They conclude that the panic is largely overblown, dogged by “zombie statistics”—“The truth is that most American adults, regardless of their gender, simply do not read very many books at all,” as Constance Grady <a href="https://www.vox.com/culture/392971/men-reading-fiction-statistics-fact-checked">put it.</a> Real gender disparities may be a product of literature’s declining economic value, as Sarah Brouillette <a href="https://defector.com/the-plight-of-the-white-male-novelist">argues,</a> potentially leading men to higher-value pastures like film, TV, and podcasting.</p>
<p>Seeing this mainstream conversation unfold in a completely unfiltered way on 4chan reveals the nastiness behind it and shows how much this white male anxiety and resentment is a <a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/books/archive/2024/06/diversity-publishing-backlash-study/678734/">backlash</a> to the successes of the Me Too and Black Lives Matter movements, and a resistance to progressive change in the publishing industry. “The rise of Donald Trump or Andrew Tate is not because of hordes of male novelists who didn’t get published,” novelist Sam Kahn told <em>The New York Times </em>for their postmortem on “the straight white man’s novel.” “But the two aren’t totally unrelated.”</p>
<p>It’s scary to see this resentment running rampant in the 4chan echo chamber, especially because this niche internet community has proved its ability, time and again, to spill over into legitimate positions of cultural and political power, from QAnon to Gamergate to the <a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2025/03/gleeful-cruelty-white-house-x-account/682234/">current presidential administration.</a></p>
<p>Paradoxically, <em>Infinite Jest</em>’s association with bad white men has drawn in readers who are not that at all. “I wanted to see for myself what exactly this community of young men was attracted to,” <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/books/2026/feb/07/never-mind-the-lit-bros-infinite-jest-is-a-true-classic-at-30">writes</a> Michelle Zauner, a bisexual Korean American musician and writer, in her new foreword for Wallace’s book. Reading <em>Infinite Jest</em> potentially offers not only a window into that culture but also a charge of pleasurable transgression.</p>
<p>This role reversal sits at the center of what Hermione Hoby recently described in <a href="https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/02/02/infinite-jest-david-foster-wallace-anniversary-book-review?utm_campaign=cm&amp;utm_source=crm&amp;utm_brand=tny&amp;utm_mailing=TNY_SubPersRec_Cygnus_092025&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_term=NYR_CYGNUS_PERSRECS"><em>The New Yorker</em></a> as the novel’s “feminization,” a new phase in its reception: an embrace by women commentators, readers, and critics. While seemingly its opposite, this phase was only made possible by all the women who parodied, critiqued, and flamed the novel and its fans, especially in public, on the internet. The backlash against Wallace—the insistence on naming the toxic masculinity bound up with his persona, work, and fandom—opened space for more women to engage with the novel critically, on their own terms, free from the male-cult framing, even as the book was more closely embraced by misogynistic channer types.</p>
<p>Expanding <em>Infinite Jest</em>’s imagined audience beyond litbros and online trolls is an exciting prospect. When friends ask if they should read the novel, I usually say that Wallace’s writing doesn’t require your attention, but if you choose to give it, your attention will likely be rewarded (a case made convincingly by Hoby as well as by Hannah Smart, who <a href="https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/nothing-ever-happens-mister-squishy-and-the-year-of-the-sentence-diagram/">diagrammed a 900-word sentence</a> from another of his stories). This is both despite and because of the fact that the novel veers into 4chan thread territory. (Wallace’s most incel work remains the 1999 collection <em>Brief Interviews with Hideous Men</em>, which Erin Spampinato <a href="https://www.taylorfrancis.com/chapters/edit/10.4324/9780367520090-9/hideous-men-david-foster-wallace-brief-interviews-hindsight-erin-spampinato">details at length.</a> It’s uncanny.)</p>
<p>For me, the bigger question is how the internet—and the “internet canon,” with its bookish memes, viral quotations, writerly parodies, and more—will continue changing our relationship not just to <em>Infinite Jest</em> but also to literature more broadly. When it comes to reading, we are clearly not okay. Everyone from AI founders to educators is expressing fear that Americans aren’t <a href="https://www.nationsreportcard.gov/highlights/ltt/2023/">capable of reading</a> anymore, or that <a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/2026/01/reading-crisis-solution-literature-personal-passion/685461/">we don’t want to read,</a> given all the other tempting options. And whether it’s literary critics like Amy Hungerford pushing back against the problematic male canon or 4chan bros complaining about the overproduction of romance fiction, there’s a shared anger over what we’re being told to read, or what’s being published and made available to read, given our increasingly limited time and attention spans.</p>
<p>The way we’re still arguing about <em>Infinite Jest</em> is probably a bad sign for the internet and literature, a sign that, in this new media environment, complex works are largely reduced to symbols, memes, and endlessly generative discourse machines, devoid of real meaning or depth. But maybe there’s a glimmer of hope there somewhere: people on the internet are still talking about literature—a lot. And the idea of reading is obviously still important to many of us, even if the very capacity to do so is slipping from our grasp, even if the people and institutions best equipped to preserve that capacity are being <a href="https://pen.org/book-bans/">targeted</a> and <a href="https://www.publicbooks.org/the-misuses-of-the-university/">hollowed</a> <a href="https://www.chronicle.com/article/ai-may-ruin-the-university-as-we-know-it">out.</a> At least we’re not going down without a fight.</p>
<p>In this way, astonishingly, the internet has returned us to some of the novel’s, and Wallace’s, biggest questions. Will addictive media destroy our souls? What happens if we lose the ability to read, to connect with other human consciousnesses? Who, and what, deserves our precious attention?</p>
