<p>Sometimes, when I find myself with nothing pressing to do, I treat myself to a visit to a Wikipedia page called <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Unusual_articles">“Unusual articles.”</a> I’ve been working my way through the entries that catch my eye in this mammoth listing for about a year now. I’m currently up to the Swedish explorer S.A. Andrée’s <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9e%27s_Arctic_balloon_expedition">ill-fated 1897 attempt</a> to reach the North Pole by hot air balloon. I have read about a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Om_Banna">shrine to a motorbike</a> in India believed to be sentient, Cologne’s <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cologne_sewerage_system">sewer system</a>, a security guard in a park in Cuba whose job is to supply the John Lennon statue there <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Lennon_Park">with a pair of glasses upon request</a>, a psychiatric disorder that caused European nobles in the Middle Ages to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_delusion">believe they were made of glass</a>, how to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog_battery">make a battery out of a frog</a>.</p>
<p>I do not know who wrote these articles. They were drafted, cited, refined, rewritten and tweaked by thousands of people all over the world, for no remuneration, simply for love of the game. Because Wikipedia is such a commonplace part of the internet, it’s easy to forget how extraordinary it is. There are more than 7 million articles in the English-language Wikipedia alone, which have attracted billions of visitors in its lifetime. Wikipedia also exists in more than 340 other languages, including Cheyenne, Samoan and Aramaic, comprising almost 10 times the number of articles in English. Three hundred thousand people across the globe contribute to the work of editing Wikipedia every month. It is a staggering feat of human endeavor. The key word, for our purposes, being “human.”</p>
<h2>Will AI kill off Wikipedia? And if it won’t, who is the volunteer army defending it against the rise of the machines, and against the loud voices and big wallets of people who want the machines to win?</h2>
<p>Last month marked 25 years since Wikipedia officially launched, and its survival has never been under greater threat. First, there is the growing volume of anti-Wikipedia sentiment from right-wing agitators. Wikipedia’s fundamental premise, that consensus can be reached on what is true and what is not true through the process of courteous debate and reference to expert sources, feels depressingly antithetical to the way that society seems to be going. Voices on the right, including that of Larry Sanger, Wikipedia’s co-founder who has long been estranged from the site, would have us believe that Wikipedia is in dire need of reform to stamp out its supposed anti-conservative slant. This past summer, two Republican representatives from the U.S. House Oversight Committee wrote a letter to Wikimedia, the nonprofit that runs Wikipedia, requesting an inquest be made into “the efforts of foreign operations and individuals at academic institutions subsidized by U.S. taxpayer dollars to influence U.S. public opinion” through their editing of Wikipedia, with particular focus on the site’s supposed anti-Israel bias. Elon Musk is another vocal hater of Wikipedia. He has offered the site 1 billion dollars to change its name to “Dickipedia,” and in October launched his own “version” of Wikipedia, Grokipedia, which is written by Grok, X’s chatbot. This has made Wikipedia a hot topic recently, giving rise to a <a href="https://www.theverge.com/cs/features/717322/wikipedia-attacks-neutrality-history-jimmy-wales">slew</a> <a href="https://www.wsj.com/tech/wikipedia-conservative-complaints-ee904b0b?gaa_at=eafs&amp;gaa_n=AWEtsqekPo1DlhYDqNon_kPqN9iTvjm_MDj70dXn7N6hP2uZfPWDbpStnuk7qq5S93o%3D&amp;gaa_ts=698b1d85&amp;gaa_sig=qDLEcUTPKacyf1B2YXObRSOXeyfmEe2etpqaZfMA2DRz_eC2sqt6Sp0kOlNBy1irvZl7dQEuicWLd_4kP4sJUw%3D%3D">of</a> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/video/podcasts/100000010468816/the-culture-wars-have-come-for-wikipedia.html">articles</a> discussing its politics or lack thereof. Jimmy Wales, the site’s other co-founder, has also been in the news, promoting his new book, <a href="https://bookshop.org/a/90971/9780593727461"><em>The Seven Rules of Trust</em></a>, which argues that society would be greatly improved if more people and organizations adopted the Wikipedia ethos. Reliable information, and where we want to be getting it from, is much on people’s minds.</p>
<h2>There were no signs anywhere, and the librarian behind the desk didn’t know what I was talking about. Eventually I found the Wikipedians, a group of six people milling about some computers in a back corner. A more low-key event would be difficult to conceive.</h2>
<p>And here is where the threat from the right dovetails with the other, perhaps greater threat that Wikipedia now faces: AI. If people can ask ChatGPT for information, or get fed an AI summary in a Google search, what need will they have of Wikipedia pages? As someone with a personal, sentimental fondness for Wikipedia, I’ve been thinking a lot about, and fearing for, its future. I wanted to know whether it was doomed. Will AI kill off Wikipedia? And if it won’t, who are the editors now, the volunteer army defending it against the rise of the machines, and against the loud voices and big wallets of people who want the machines to win?</p>
<p>When I arrived at Brixton Library in South London one evening in late October, I wondered if I had got the date wrong. There were no signs anywhere indicating that a Wikipedia event was taking place, and the librarian behind the desk didn’t know what I was talking about. Eventually I found the Wikipedians, a group of six people milling about some computers in a back corner. A more low-key event would be difficult to conceive, but apparently this monthly meetup, put on by a group of libraries in the area, is more of a social thing than anything else, an opportunity for old hands and newbies to Wikipedia to hang out, ask questions and do a little editing on the library computers if they feel like it.</p>
<p>Some events like these are run by the nonprofit Wikimedia itself. Others are less official, organized the world over by institutions or Wikipedia enthusiasts unaffiliated with the site in any formal way, as this one was. One purpose of such events is to teach people what Wikipedia actually is, and how it works. Wikipedia’s goal is to be a “sum of all human knowledge,” as Jimmy Wales puts it, but there are highly specific and clearly delineated ways in which it seeks to achieve that. At the library in Brixton I met Tom, a long-time Wikipedia administrator with a special interest in English and Welsh Law (administrators are editors who have volunteered to take on responsibilities like blocking vandals and deleting defunct pages). Tom was here in a social rather than an official capacity, but he was giving an overview of Wikipedia’s rules to Petra, a woman in her 50s who had come along today out of curiosity. “I just like to learn about new things,” she told me.</p>
<p>Wikipedia articles, according to policies it <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Five_pillars">publishes</a> on the site itself, must have a neutral point of view, be verifiable with reliable sources and give detailed, encyclopedic, but not necessarily exhaustive, coverage of the topic. A subject must be “notable” to be included on Wikipedia, and popularity does not automatically confer notability. (One often cited example is the YouTube web series “Battle for Dream Island,” which is extremely popular but, until recently, was not deemed sufficiently notable because it hadn’t received enough coverage in independent media.) In order for information to appear on Wikipedia, it has to have been published <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources">somewhere reputable</a> first: a book or a newspaper, say, or a trustworthy website.</p>
<p>Some Wikipedia editors are experts in a particular field or, through the process of editing Wikipedia, have become experts in the work of compiling an encyclopedia, while others are just well-meaning enthusiasts who strive to maintain the site’s standards. But because anybody can edit any page at any time, some editors are chancers or people with an ax to grind who will try to input wrong information. There have also been <a href="https://www.thebureauinvestigates.com/stories/2026-01-14/london-pr-firm-rewrites-wikipedia-for-governments-and-billionaires">investigations</a> into business tycoons, companies and even national governments paying PR firms to edit their Wikipedia pages — not by introducing falsehoods, but through tactics such as deleting or burying less favorable information, and swapping out critical media sources for more positive ones. If inaccurate or misleading information is introduced to a Wikipedia article, however, it can easily be challenged in the article’s associated discussion page, where edit histories are logged. These talk pages are now, <a href="https://www.theverge.com/cs/features/717322/wikipedia-attacks-neutrality-history-jimmy-wales">according to the Verge</a>, at least five times as large as the encyclopedia itself.</p>
<h2>Wikipedia is among the best of an imperfect set of tools we have for gathering what might be thought of as the truth about how things are and have been. </h2>
<p>Tom and Petra’s conversation circled around to AI. The subject is on the mind of everyone interested in Wikipedia. Tom told us that AI has been in use in some form on Wikipedia for the last decade. ORES (Objective Revision Evaluation Service) is an AI tool built by Wikimedia engineers and first deployed in 2015. It grades the quality of Wikipedia edits and flags potentially problematic edits for human editors to review; editors can choose whether or not to use it. But since the explosion of services like ChatGPT, some Wikipedians are spending a lot of time combatting AI slop introduced to the site by human editors who are using ChatGPT and other chatbots to write their entries. Tom pulled up a Wikipedia page on his laptop called WikiProject AI Cleanup, and another on how to spot signs of AI writing.</p>
<p>I do my rabbit-holing of unusual articles on Wikipedia because someone — or rather, a large and disparate group of someones — has thought to aggregate these weird and wonderful pieces of knowledge in one place. (We might call the subjects of these articles notable but not popular.) I also do it because I know that, by virtue of it being on Wikipedia, the information will be more or less correct. I say more or less, because definite knowledge is a slippery thing. Mistakes happen. Information is lost or misinterpreted. A published book, with its reassuring hardback and the name of a venerable author on the front, would count as a valid source for citation on Wikipedia. But can you be sure that it was fact-checked rigorously? Wikipedia is among the best of an imperfect set of tools we have for gathering what might be thought of as the truth about how things are and have been. AI now threatens the site’s accuracy further upstream, too. As digital news media — a trusted source for Wikipedia — relies increasingly on AI to write its articles, it becomes vulnerable to AI-generated inaccuracies that can be passed down to Wikipedia entries.</p>
<p>Tom is worried about AI, but more worried about a general decline in the number of Wikipedia editors. (The main reasons for this decline are unclear but may include editors leaving after clashing with other volunteers or finding the site’s policies too confusing.) Tom admits, too, that editors are still overwhelmingly white and male. But they’re good people, in Tom’s experience. As Wales put it in a recent <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/18/magazine/jimmy-wales-interview.html">interview</a> with the <em>New York Times</em>, “they’re a bunch of nice geeks.”</p>
<p>This is part of what maddens the right about Wikipedia: It cannot be bought, it cannot be investigated, it cannot be influenced because “it” does not exist in the way that they perceive it to exist. There is no cabal of pinkos sitting in a secret lair deciding to make Wikipedia a left-leaning entity. It’s just people, in rooms like this one.</p>
<p>On a desktop computer, Betty and Patricia, both in their 60s, were updating Wikipedia pages on Black Londoners. They told me they were editing the page on Frank Bruno, and I asked who that was. “You don’t know Frank Bruno? The boxer? Too young, too young,” Betty tutted Patricia, newer to Wikipedia than Betty, asked her how we could be sure its pages were trustworthy. “Because lots of people are checking it,” Betty said. Then they hit publish on Patricia’s very first edit to Bruno’s page, one more tiny morsel of knowledge added to the encyclopedia.</p>
<p>A few weeks later, I attended a second Wikipedia event, at Hackney Library in East London. And it was this event that gave me confidence that Wikipedia might survive despite AI, and even thrive as a reaction against it.</p>
<p>The event was one of a series of monthly sessions that have been run by the local history group Hackney Society since last May. It was better attended than the one in Brixton, with 16 participants, mostly women, aged from their early 20s to their 70s. The primary purpose of the event, led by Janet Chapman, chair of the Hackney Society, and Sumit Surai from Wikimedia, was for attendees to add information from a <a href="https://www.hackneysociety.org/publications/women-from-hackneys-history-vol-ll/">new book</a> published by the Hackney Society, on notable Hackney women from history. But people had a variety of reasons for being there: Some wanted to learn about adding uncopyrighted images to Wikimedia Commons, the photo library that provides pictures to Wikipedia pages, or had just been looking for something to do on a rainy Saturday afternoon.</p>

There is no cabal of pinkos sitting in a secret lair deciding to make Wikipedia a left-leaning entity. It’s just people, in rooms like this one.</h2>
<p>I found myself drawn to a woman in her early 20s with lime green eyeshadow and bleached blond eyebrows, who stood out in the otherwise drab room. Isidora grew up in Hackney and has been volunteering with Hackney Society in their archives since graduating university a year ago. She attended her first Wikipedia event earlier in the year and has been coming ever since. “I pictured Wikipedia as these very intelligent, niche groups of people who spend all day editing. But what I learned from doing that session was that it’s volunteer based. It’s just something that people enjoy doing.”</p>
<p>She enjoys it, too. She showed me the first edit she ever did, to the page for Edith Thompson, a Hackney resident who was executed for the murder of her husband in 1923. Isidora’s contribution was to note that the road on which Thompson was born, Norfolk Road, is now Cecilia Road. Then she helped me make my own first edit. I flicked through the new book on Hackney women and found someone called Theodora Bonwick, an educator and suffragette who lived in Hackney but trained as a teacher around the corner from my current flat in south London. She had a Wikipedia page already, but it was sparse. I clicked edit, added the information that Bonwick attended school in London’s Maida Vale, and cited the Hackney Society book. And there it was.</p>
<p>It was thrilling, more thrilling than I had expected it to be. “It’s really satisfying,” said Isidora as we beamed at my laptop screen. To her, the value of Wikipedia is its accessibility. “Anyone who has internet access can use Wikipedia, anyone can make an account, can edit and join this community,” she said. It’s human, to put it simply. “Interacting with the behind-the-scenes elements, there’s so much human connection and community in spreading knowledge, which we need in this age of AI. It’s kind of hopeful, I guess.” Sumit, who has been volunteering with Wikimedia and running training events for them for the past decade, said much the same. Wikipedia is open to anyone, and, unlike AI, won’t try to come up with an answer, true or untrue, simply because you’ve asked it a question. “It only says what the source says,” he said.</p>
<p>These municipal rooms, full of people swapping stories about their history with Wikipedia over take-out cups of coffee, are places where you can feel the optimism that underlies the site. Such gatherings are totally counter to AI’s onslaught of decontextualized content. I overheard one young woman telling another that her dad had looked up his old school in the U.K. and found that some trickster had listed Robert Mugabe as an alumnus. He went in and fixed it. I listened to a group of women from three generations discussing how nice it is to feel they can give historical women a voice in this way. The older women commiserated with their younger counterparts about how expensive Hackney has become.</p>

<p>A few weeks after the Hackney event, Wikipedia announced a slate of new AI deals with companies including Meta and Amazon. Large Language Models (LLMs) are trained on Wikipedia content already, but under these new deals, Wikipedia will be paid for it. “I’m very happy personally that AI models are training on Wikipedia data because it’s human curated,” Wales told <a href="https://apnews.com/article/wikipedia-internet-jimmy-wales-50e796d70152d79a2e0708846f84f6d7">the Associated Press</a>. There are those who argue that Wikimedia should give no quarter to the tech giants, but the deal makes sense: The more money the organization has, the more it can afford to host, maintain and increase the global network of humans who contribute to the knowledge it holds.</p>
<p>Sustaining this network is essential for Wikipedia’s survival. The site has one major similarity with LLMs: Neither can generate new knowledge. For information to appear on Wikipedia, it must already have appeared elsewhere. Even if LLMs become the primary search tools that people use to find knowledge, they rely on humans generating that knowledge in the first place. If today’s AI introduces inaccuracy-riddled slop into Wikipedia, tomorrow’s AI, trained on said slop, will be less accurate still. Humans can produce and access physical archives, libraries full of copyrighted books that are not yet available to AI scraping. LLMs cannot. Crowdsourcing only works if you retain the crowd.</p>
<p>A 2011 survey of more than 5,000 Wikipedia contributors asked why they edited the site. One of the main reasons given was simple and obvious, really: that it’s fun. I went home from Hackney and dove back into my unusual articles list. The tone of Wikipedia articles themselves is decidedly and intentionally bland. But in this list, the humanity of the editors is legible, as is their enjoyment in doing what they do. Each entry on the list has a little summary next to it. A man named Horace Burgess built a treehouse church in Tennessee. The summary reads: “A tree house built by a minister who claimed to have received a vision from God. God apparently didn’t like it, since it burnt down.” And my hope, naive as it may prove to be, is that Wikipedia will live on, so long as people want it to. So long as people — human beings — are having fun with it.</p>
