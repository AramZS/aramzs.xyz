<h1>There’s More to Worry About for Tweens Than the Screen</h1>
<h2>Every generation panics about girlhood. The latest, over the negative consequences of social media for tweens, hides a bigger problem in girl world: They’ve got nothing else.</h2>
<p>By Katya Ungerman | Illustrations by <a href="https://www.wsj.com/news/author/alexandra-citrin-safadi">Alexandra Citrin-Safadi</a></p>
<p>April 20, 2026 12:02 pm ET</p>
<img src="https://images.wsj.net/im-48527750?width=1080&amp;height=1920"/>
<p>In every decade, America rediscovers that girlhood is in crisis.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, it was the heroin chic and waifish models who graced not only the pages of Vogue, but YM and Seventeen, setting a dangerous beauty standard for an entire generation of young women.</p>
<p>By the <a href="https://link.springer.com/rwe/10.1007/978-3-031-56681-3_6">raunchy</a> early 2000s, the worry had shifted squarely to hypersexualization. Were young women being sold a version of confidence that looked like objectification? <a href="https://www.apa.org/monitor/sep06/dolls">Bratz dolls</a>, all lip gloss and heavy lids, were suddenly cooler than Barbie, who looked conservative by comparison. Abercrombie Kids was selling <a href="https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-2002-jun-27-fi-tweens27-story.html">thongs</a> and, later, <a href="https://www.cnn.com/2011/BUSINESS/03/26/abercrombie.bikini.controversy/index.html">push-up</a> bikini tops to elementary- and middle-school-age girls.</p>
<p>A decade later, we are deep in the latest girlhood panic cycle, and this time the antagonist is the phone.</p>
<p>Congress has moved toward age-verification measures to keep kids under 13 off social media. Recently, juries in <a href="https://www.wsj.com/us-news/law/landmark-trial-tests-claims-that-social-media-harms-teens-74409b2f?mod=article_inline">California</a> and <a href="https://www.wsj.com/tech/landmark-verdict-says-meta-harmed-children-allowing-adults-to-prey-on-them-cb3ad674?mod=article_inline">New Mexico</a> found social-media company Meta liable for harming the mental health and safety of children and teens on its platforms. Jonathan Haidt’s “The Anxious Generation,” with its warnings about the vulnerability of teen girls, gave voice to the panic and pushed an accompanying policy agenda into mainstream politics.</p>
<p>It’s true that smartphones pose some perils to young people—though the science is mixed, there’s some evidence that they disrupt sleep quality and might exacerbate depression and anxiety.</p>
<img src="https://images.wsj.net/im-83413849?width=700&amp;height=1049"/>
<p>But amid all the fear is a reality: Today phones aren’t just a way to call your friends. They’re where teen culture is made.</p>
<p>Comedy, confessionals, clothing trends, music, celebrity gossip—girls used to get this stuff from magazines and catalogs, TV shows that went on for years and books. That media ecosystem and brick-and-mortar world doesn’t exist in the same way anymore. There is nowhere else to go. Take away the feed and the group chat—both of which parents can and should impose limits on—and you’ve taken away their world.</p>
<p>**AROUND A DECADE AGO**, there was a fully fledged commercial universe scaled to a body and a sensibility that was no longer a child’s and was not yet a woman’s.</p>
<p>Juniors and tween retailers, aimed at girls 8 to 12, filled malls across the country. By 1998, Delia’s mailed 55 million catalogs a year. By the mid-2000s, Limited Too had more than 600 stores.</p>
<p>From the Disney Channel and Nickelodeon to the WB and later the CW, television shows geared toward tweens and teens dominated screens. Millions watched these shows—“Buffy the Vampire Slayer,” “Dawson’s Creek,” “Gilmore Girls.” They may have starred actors who were in their 20s, but the stories were centered on girls who were supposed to be like them.</p>
<p>In parallel retail spaces, the old media disappeared as tweens migrated to YouTube, Instagram and TikTok, where the biggest creators aren’t kids and aren’t quite adults either.</p>
<p>**SOCIAL MEDIA** is a land mine—that much is obviously true. But you can’t fight the culture (says every parent from every older generation that has tried). More important, it doesn’t have to be all or nothing.</p>
<p>Plenty of what circulates on TikTok, Instagram and YouTube is funny and inventive, made by girls for other girls, surviving precisely because it doesn’t require a corporation’s approval.</p>
<p>The algorithm doesn’t care which age group is watching, reading or buying. <a href="https://www.vulture.com/article/why-the-baby-sitters-club-was-canceled-at-netflix.html">Rachel Shukert</a>, the showrunner of Netflix’s “Baby-Sitters Club” reboot, described the problem: “Girls are expected to go straight from Doc McStuffins to ‘Euphoria.’”</p>
<p>Do I wish that there were more mechanisms for digital control? Yes. Do I wish we had more that was for kids only? As a parent of two daughters, of course, yes. But the answer isn’t confiscating the screen that replaced the world I grew up in. It’s about rebuilding a real world that can go alongside the virtual one.</p>
