<h1>The majority of the Columbia local’s members previously voted in favor of setting April 23 as its deadline to strike.</h1>
<figure><img src="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/resizer/v2/HDFJTIWSORHUHD2ZVR4DPKN5LE.jpg?auth=256dd0f34deff1721952cba7748e5bb12995e03a402d82e51a4a8417c92b987c"/></figure>
<p>Columbia’s student workers’ union no longer plans to go on strike during the spring semester following its parent union’s decision to not approve the strike earlier this month. As of April 14, members had voted in favor of setting April 23 as their deadline to strike.</p>
<p>The Student Workers of Columbia-United Auto Workers, a labor union representing over 3,000 graduate and undergraduate student workers, began bargaining for a successor contract with the University in March 2025, three months before its first contract expired in June 2025. Since then, bargaining sessions have yet to yield a successful contract, with the University writing in its most recent bargaining <a href="https://studentbenefits.columbia.edu/news/bargaining-session-11">update</a> that the union and the University remain “far apart” on most issues.</p>

<p>While 82.2 percent of SWC-UAW’s members voted in favor of setting a strike deadline of April 23, its parent union, United Auto Workers, rejected the union’s request to strike, according to an April 14 email from SWC-UAW to its members obtained by Spectator. UAW recommended that SWC-UAW delay its strike until it appears that the University will not concede further without a strike, according to the email.</p>
<p>Despite the majority of voting SWC-UAW members supporting a strike, without UAW approval, the union’s members would not get paid during the strike. The rejection is the latest instance of scrutiny from the parent union after it <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2026/04/03/swc-uaw-narrows-demands-ahead-of-strike-following-consultation-with-parent-union/">asked</a> the local to narrow its contract demands earlier this year.</p>
<p>SWC-UAW is in communication with UAW and is committed to bargaining over the summer, Hagen Feeney, CC ’26, an SWC-UAW spokesperson, wrote in a statement to Spectator.</p>
<p>“We hope we can avert a fall strike, but if Columbia doesn’t give us a reasonable counter offer on our priority demands, we will have no choice but to withhold labor in the fall,” Feeney wrote.</p>
<p>The union threatened to go on strike on April 23 if the University did not reach an agreement on at least one of three of its 26 proposed articles—compensation, noncitizen worker protections, and teaching and research appointments.</p>
<p>Those three articles require that the University provide protections for student workers in teaching assistant roles, grant opportunities for graduate student workers to teach classes, increase student workers’ yearly salaries to over $76,000, and provide protection for international student workers.</p>
<p>The union has sought to make Columbia a sanctuary campus—a campus that <a href="https://immigrantsrising.org/wp-content/uploads/Immigrants-Rising_Understanding-the-Sanctuary-School-and-Safe-Zone-Movement.pdf">creates</a> policies to protect undocumented students and employees—after one of its members, Ranjani Srinivasan, a doctoral candidate at the Graduate School of Architecture, Planning and Preservation, was <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2025/03/25/columbia-doctoral-candidate-ranjani-srinivasan-forced-to-flee-united-states-following-threats-from-ice-she-writes-in-statement/">forced</a> to flee the country after facing threats from Immigration and Customs Enforcement.</p>

<p>A University official recognized the significance of SWC-UAW’s concerns over international students in a statement to Spectator but wrote that many of the requests made by the union are not subject to collective bargaining and would require the University to violate the law.</p>
<p>The University has filed three unfair labor practice charges against the union since August 2025, alleging that the union conditioned negotiations on subjects unrelated to employment.</p>
<p>After nearly a year of stalled contract negotiations, 91.5 percent of union members <a href="https://www.instagram.com/sw_columbia/p/DVuAfG6EYuk/">voted</a> in March to <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2026/02/25/swc-uaw-opens-strike-authorization-vote-to-ramp-up-pressure-during-bargaining-over-a-new-contract-with-columbia/">authorize</a> union leadership to hold a later strike vote to <a href="https://x.com/SW_Columbia/status/2024915048330568178">“ramp up the pressure”</a> for the University to meet the union’s demands.</p>
<p>The strike would have been the union’s first since the 2021-22 academic year, when it went on strike for 10 weeks and <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2022/01/28/after-a-10-week-strike-and-years-of-organizing-swc-uaw-members-vote-to-ratify-historic-contract/">secured</a> a contract guaranteeing student worker pay raises, robust health benefits, and a provision guaranteeing the right to arbitration for Title IX cases.</p>
<p>Feeney said in an interview with Spectator that the University has seemed more willing to negotiate since the strike authorization vote.</p>
<p>“Pressure against the University through withholding our labor is the only way that we’ve actually been able to achieve those concessions,” Feeney said.</p>
<p>A University spokesperson pushed back on that characterization in a statement to Spectator.</p>
<p>“In every bargaining session, the University is focused on making meaningful progress on employment-related issues, with a goal of achieving a strong successor contract that would materially benefit every student employee at Columbia,” the spokesperson wrote. “The University looks forward to the scheduled upcoming bargaining sessions and hopes SWC will move with us toward that result.”</p>
<p>The union has also faced internal pressure to change its bargaining strategy as it enters its 13th month of negotiations with the University for a successor contract.</p>
<p>Nearly 150 union members, as of April 26, signed a petition calling on SWC-UAW to adopt a new bargaining strategy because of obstacles that factions within the union have been slow to address, noting the parent union’s lack of support for a strike. The petition called for the union to bring its bylaws into compliance with the UAW constitution, hold elections for bargaining committee seats, and prioritize six of the union’s 26 platform demands: wages, noncitizen worker protections, healthcare, protection from discrimination and harassment, protection against discipline, and prioritization of job security and protection of union rights.</p>
<p>The petition was created by the Coalition to Renew Our Workplace Democracy, a group of union members that write the “Workbench Bulletin,” a series of reflections regarding the union’s bargaining strategy.</p>
<p>The union has addressed these concerns before and after the petition by prioritizing three of its contract articles and holding an upcoming election for its bargaining committee.</p>
<p>The union has also faced external pressure from Graduate Researchers Against Discrimination and Suppression, an anonymous group of graduate student workers backed by the National Right to Work Legal Defense Foundation. The foundation is a legal aid organization that supports right to work laws—regulations that prohibit employers from requiring workers to join a union as a condition of employment.</p>
<p>GRADS filed its second unfair labor practice charge against the union March 30, after its initial September 2025 <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2026/02/26/columbia-students-criticize-universitys-handling-of-antisemitism-at-federal-civil-rights-listening-session/">charge</a>. The group <a href="https://www.columbiaspectator.com/news/2025/09/28/anonymous-graduate-student-worker-group-files-unfair-labor-practice-charge-against-swc-uaw/">claims</a> that SWC-UAW has withheld negotiations over nonmandatory subjects of bargaining, such as its “Cops Off Campus” article, which would restrict the New York Police Department’s access to campus.</p>
<p>The second charge added that the demand to designate Columbia’s campus as a “sanctuary space” also falls under the scope of nonmandatory subjects of bargaining under the National Labor Relations Act, since it is unrelated to wages and working conditions.</p>
<p>“This union seems to be focused on bargaining over things that are not mandatory subjects of bargaining,” Glenn Taubman, a staff attorney at the National Right to Work Legal Defense Foundation, told Spectator.</p>
<p>The University and the union are set to meet at the bargaining table April 28, May 4, and May 12.</p>
