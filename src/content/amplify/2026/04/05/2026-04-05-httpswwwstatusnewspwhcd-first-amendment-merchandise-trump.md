<p>On a Saturday evening in late April, the ballroom of the <strong>Washington Hilton</strong> will feature the annual tradition of reporters dressing up in tuxedos and gowns to mingle, network, and celebrate their profession. But this year, when <strong>Donald Trump</strong> looks out into the crowd, he might spot some of those attending the <strong>White House Correspondents’ Association’s</strong> gala sporting unusual accessories: pocket squares and pins with the words of the <strong>First Amendment</strong>, in a subversive gesture supporting press freedom, reflecting the news media’s consciousness of the perilous moment it’s in, and why.</p>
<p>The First Amendment <a href="https://donate.rcfp.org/first-amendment">merchandise</a> is part of a campaign, first reported here by <em>Status</em>, from the <strong>Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press</strong>, designed to provide the press corps with a visible way of signaling support for press freedom on the one night of the year when the industry gathers in full force. Like members of <strong>Congress</strong> coordinating colors to make a political statement during the State of the Union Address, the display will be subtle, not to take away from the purpose of the event, but it serves as a symbolic demonstration that Trump won’t be able to miss from his seat on stage.</p>
<p>The idea for the limited-run line, organizers told <em>Status</em>, grew out of a recurring question: how can people show up for the First Amendment in a tangible way? The question carries added weight this year withTrump <a href="https://www.status.news/p/donald-trump-whca-dinner-press-correspondents-reaction">set to attend</a> the WHCA dinner after skipping the event throughout his first term—an absence that underscored his hostility toward the press. His scheduled appearance at the dinner stands in stark contrast to his ongoing assault on the press in his second term.</p>
<p>Whether items like pocket squares, tote bags, and phone wallets bearing the text “Congress shall make no law abridging the Freedom of Speech or of the Press”—a hand-drawn design by <strong>CNN’s Jake Tapper</strong>—rise to the occasion while rubbing elbows with a president who regularly labels journalists “enemies of the people,” will surely be debated. But the effort speaks to the elephant in the room: this is no ordinary WHCD, given the Trump administration’s overt attempts to discredit and undermine the press.</p>
<p>It’s worth retracing the degree to which Trump has exerted pressure on the media since his return to the presidency. His administration has barred outlets from <strong>White House</strong> events, <a href="https://www.status.news/p/whca-white-house-press-trump">stripped</a> the White House Correspondents’ Association of its traditional control over the press pool, <a href="https://www.status.news/p/associated-press-donald-trump-white-house">booted</a> the <strong>Associated Press</strong> over its refusal to use “Gulf of America,” defunded <strong>NPR</strong> and <strong>PBS</strong>, dismantled <strong>Voice of America</strong>, <a href="https://www.status.news/p/pete-hegseth-pentagon-reporters-press-coverage">barred</a> photojournalists from <strong>Defense Department</strong> briefings, and killed access for all reporters at the <strong>Pentagon</strong> during the U.S. military action in <strong>Iran</strong>. Trump has also waged legal battles against major news organizations, including <strong>ABC News,</strong> the <strong>BBC</strong>, <strong>CBS News</strong>, <strong>The</strong> <strong>Wall Street Journal</strong>, and <strong>The New York Times</strong>, clearly as a means of seeking more favorable coverage. Meanwhile, he has encouraged his attack dog at the <strong>Federal Communications Commission</strong>, chairman <strong>Brendan Carr</strong>, to <a href="https://www.status.news/p/brendan-carr-media-cpac-trump-fcc">openly threaten</a> regulatory action against broadcasters and even l<a href="https://www.status.news/p/jimmy-kimmel-pulled-fcc-decision">ate-night hosts</a> for what they say. The result is a coordinated campaign that has tested the boundaries of the First Amendment.</p>
<p>In the background of this unprecedented press assault, the Reporters Committee has ramped up its response to the increasingly hostile environment. Communications director <strong>Jenn Topper</strong> told <em>Status</em> that the organization “is expanding our legal work pretty significantly,” and saw the WHCD as an opportunity “to tap into people who are really engaged on the issue, and raise awareness around the legal needs and the services that we offer.”</p>
<p>Organizers stopped short of explicitly tying the campaign to Trump’s attendance. But with the president set to return to the dinner after years of open hostility toward the press, it’s hard not to read the effort as something more than decorative. The merch serves as a way for journalists to publicly express their solidarity to Trump’s face, in a room where his strained relationship with the media will be on full display.</p>
<p>Top journalists have also embraced the effort. “I am so excited to wear this pocket square—especially at events in D.C. where there are many people who know how vital the First Amendment is, and also quite a few who might need to be reminded of its importance,” Tapper said.</p>
<p>Proceeds from the merchandise go to the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press, a pro bono legal group that helps journalists nationwide navigate everything from urgent legal threats to full-scale courtroom battles.</p>
<p>In that sense, while the pocket squares and pins may offer a visible display of solidarity, they represent a far more consequential fight playing out in courtrooms, regulatory agencies, and in the broader reshaping of the media business. For the Reporters Committee, the goal is not just to make a statement, but to channel that into resources to sustain and expand its legal work at a moment of heightened need.</p>
<p>The WHCD has traditionally been a night to put down the gloves, a brief détente between reporters and those they cover before lacing them up again and getting back to work. That reporters now feel compelled to make such a statement, even silently, offers a sober reminder of how far that relationship has deteriorated during the Trump era. A small gesture, perhaps, but one that speaks to the moment.</p>
