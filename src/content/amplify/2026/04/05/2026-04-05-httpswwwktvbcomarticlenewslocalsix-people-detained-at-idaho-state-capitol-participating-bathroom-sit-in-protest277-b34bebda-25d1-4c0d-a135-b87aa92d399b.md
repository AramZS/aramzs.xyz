<p>Protesters were arrested on a trespassing charge at Idaho's Capitol after a sit-in against new bathroom laws that critics argue target the transgender community.</p>
<p>BOISE, Idaho — Six protesters were arrested on Friday after participating in a sit-in gathering at a State Capitol bathroom to speak up about transgender rights following the passage of multiple bills, including the recent broad bathroom ban law.</p>
<p>The protest began at noon, and a flyer advertising the action says, "people will purposely go into bathrooms that do not align with their gender."</p>
<p>Idaho State Police (ISP) said its troopers responded to a report of a disturbance at the Capitol involving 12 protesters.</p>
<p>"The group used whistles and bullhorns inside the building, then went into a restroom and blocked the door with their bodies to keep others out," ISP said in a news release. "Troopers gave numerous warnings about the disturbance, and the group was trespassed from the building. The group refused to leave when instructed and remained in the restroom, further disrupting the situation."</p>
<p>KTVB's reporter Kristian Lopez saw protesters gathering in the bathrooms and using whistles and chanting, "trans rights are human rights." Lopez saw Idaho State Police officers, accompanied by Boise Police, interacting with the protesters.</p>
<p>Lopez said around 3 p.m., Idaho State Police (ISP) troopers then went into the restroom and pulled 6 people out, and handcuffed them. ISP said those six protestors were charged with misdemeanor trespass, and one was resisting arrest.</p>
<p>Before the detentions, protester Kali Sherman said the demonstration was intended to show support for transgender individuals.</p>
<p>"We see you just like every other human here. I’m glad you’re here and alive," Sherman said. "Trans people, we are here and we see you."</p>
<p>When asked whether recent detentions of protesters affected participation, Sherman said it did not change her perspective.</p>
<p>"Trans people have to go through so much worse," she said. "We’re here for them."</p>
<p>Following the incident, Sherman was seen with a large bump on her head and said she needed medical attention.</p>
<p>"During the arrests, one person sustained a minor injury while leaving a bathroom stall," ISP said. "They were taken to a local hospital for a precautionary evaluation before being booked."</p>
<p>ISP said the Boise Police Department assisted and transported the protesters to the Ada County Jail.</p>
<p>Friday's protest follows Wednesday's protest when over 30 people gathered inside the Idaho State Capitol, in front of the governor's office, for a sit-in. Nine people were arrested on Wednesday who have since been released.</p>

<p><a href="https://legislature.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/H0752.pdf">House Bill 752 </a>criminalizes any person who "knowingly and willfully" enters a bathroom, locker room or changing room that does not align with their sex assigned at birth, creating the strictest guidelines for bathroom use in the nation. On Tuesday, which is the international Transgender Day of Visibility, Gov. Brad Little signed the bill into law.</p>
<p>The legislation is stricter than Idaho's penalty for indecent exposure, which includes acts like public urination, under the <a href="https://legislature.idaho.gov/statutesrules/idstat/title18/t18ch41/sect18-4116/">state code.</a> It is also stricter than the state's punishment for <a href="https://larsencriminaldefense.com/idaho-dui-laws-statutes/">driving under the influence.</a></p>
<p>A first-offense misdemeanor means up to a year in jail. Upon a second offense, that person could spend five years behind bars.</p>
<p>Another bill of contention is<a href="https://legislature.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/H0822.pdf"> House Bill 822</a>, which would require school or medical staff, and childcare providers to notify the child's parent within 72 hours, if the child is a minor and asks to "socially transition." As of Friday afternoon, the bill remains on Gov. Little's desk.</p>

<p>The bills have faced stark criticism from the transgender community. Some transgender Idahoans have said they could leave the state following the enforcement of the "bathroom" law, which takes effect on July 1, 2026.</p>
<p>But supporters of the law have said the purpose of the bill is to protect women and children and to assist in privacy rights.</p>
