<h1>Scammed into scamming</h1>
<p>Across Southeast Asia, a multibillion-dollar fraud industry has emerged, staffed in part by victims of trafficking.</p>
<p>In lawless regions of the Myanmar-Thai border, compounds run by Chinese criminal gangs contain thousands of people forced to scam strangers online or face brutal punishment. Thailand has become a key transit hub for trafficking victims, Reuters found.</p>
<aside>By <a href="https://www.reuters.com/authors/adolfo-arranz/">Adolfo Arranz</a>, <a href="https://www.reuters.com/authors/poppy-mcpherson/">Poppy McPherson</a>, <a href="https://www.reuters.com/authors/devjyot-ghoshal/">Devjyot Ghoshal</a> and <a href="https://www.reuters.com/authors/han-huang/">Han Huang</a> <br/> Published Sept. 18, 2025 Last updated Sept. 20, 2025 03:00 AM EDT</aside>
<p>The sequence of events and dialogues that follow are based on several months of interviews with nine people from Africa, South Asia and Southeast Asia trafficked to scam centers in Myanmar between 2022 and 2025. Although Reuters couldn’t confirm every aspect of their stories, their narratives were broadly consistent.</p>
<p>While other media reports have documented the existence of the scam centers, Reuters’ reporting exposes the pivotal role in the criminal gangs’ trafficking operations played by purported immigration officials at Thai airports. It also details the harrowing routine of life in the compounds, which still thrive in Myanmar’s borderlands despite a multinational crackdown.</p>

<p>He didn’t know it yet, but he had arrived in Myanmar’s KK Park, a notorious enclave known to international law enforcement and diplomats for one thing: scams.</p>
<p>KK Park’s sprawling compound and others nearby are run primarily by Chinese criminal gangs and guarded by local militia groups aligned to Myanmar’s military.</p>
<p>Myanmar’s military government said in a statement to Reuters it had not approved any of the illegal activities carried out in remote border areas controlled by ethnic armies, where its authority was limited.</p>
<p>Naing Maung Zaw, a spokesperson for the Karen National Army, one of two militias operating in the area, which has been sanctioned by the U.S., acknowledged it had leased land and facilities to businesses for the purpose of “regional development”. But he said the call centers grew “so much without our knowledge”.</p>
<p>The second militia, the Democratic Karen Buddhist Army (DKBA), did not respond to Reuters’ questions. The DKBA has previously said it is collaborating with efforts to dismantle the scam centres and to free trafficked workers.</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration shows many airplanes parked at an airport." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/19.jpg"/></figure>
<p>Hundreds of thousands of people from dozens of countries have made similar journeys to scam centres across Southeast Asia since the start of the decade, according to the United Nations.</p>
<p>Of the nine people Reuters spoke to, all but Oly were lured by promises of highly paid jobs. Six said they were escorted through Bangkok's two international airports by presumed immigration officials before being spirited away to Myanmar by car.</p>
<p>Reuters was unable to establish independently the identity of the purported Thai officials involved in the trafficking of the interviewees or on what basis they participated in the scheme.</p>
<p>Lindsay Kiptiness, Kenya’s ambassador to Thailand, who has helped rescue hundreds of his citizens from scam centers in Myanmar, told Reuters they consistently described being escorted through Thai airports by officials.</p>
<p>Kiptiness said he had provided Thai authorities with detailed information about the abductions but had received no follow-up.</p>
<p>A spokesperson for Thailand’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs said they had not received any reports about the allegations but were ready to investigate any “credible information or evidence”.</p>

<p>Part II</p>
<p>Life in the</p>
<p>camps</p>
<p>Since emerging in 2020 as a small settlement on the banks of the Moei river that forms the border between Myanmar and Thailand, KK Park and nearby compounds have mushroomed into one of the largest and most active criminal enclaves in Southeast Asia, according to the U.N.</p>
<figure><img src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/24.jpg"/></figure>
<figure><img alt="An illustration depicts armed police officers guarding high walls and barbed wire." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/25.jpg"/></figure>
<p>Behind high walls and barbed wire, shady companies run a range of criminal operations targeting people across the world. They are guarded by armed soldiers from the Karen National Army and Democratic Karen Buddhist Army.</p>
<p>The KNA spokesman, Naing Maung Zaw, said the militia did not profit from the centres and that it was now working to eliminate remaining centres and “repatriate those who are suffering”. Reuters couldn’t independently confirm the militia's financial arrangements with the Chinese gangs controlling KK Park nor the extent to which it had enforced a crackdown.</p>
<p>Some militia fighters were previously part of a border guard force operating under Myanmar’s military and have continued cooperating with the junta, which long turned a blind eye as scam centres such as KK Park emerged. In the statement to Reuters, the junta denied profiting from the camps and said it was working with Thailand and China to shut down the scam centers.</p>
<p>China’s foreign ministry told Reuters it was “actively cooperating with Myanmar and other countries in…jointly eradicating the cancer of online gambling and wire fraud”. It said “a number of wire fraud dens have been eradicated and a large number of suspects have been arrested” but it referred questions about the nature of the gangs to the local investigating authorities.</p>
<p>Inside KK Park, Oly said the hours were long and conditions grim.</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration depicts the buildings inside a scam centre." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/26.jpg"/></figure>
<p>He slept with nine other people in a small room with a toilet. They started work at 3pm and ended at 6am, mimicking daylight hours in Western countries where targets were located.</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration shows Oly in a crowded room with bunk beds." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/27.jpg"/></figure>
<p>He was ordered to contact men online and pretend to be "Alicia" - a clothing designer in Singapore. Then he would flirt with them and dupe them into handing over their money by luring them into a fake investment scheme.</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration depicts Oly sitting in front of a computer." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/28.jpg"/></figure>
<p>The gangs called them “clients”. At first, the clients would appear to make a profit, inducing them to make a substantial investment in the scheme – then the gangs would disappear with their money.</p>
<p>The men lost tens of thousands of dollars at a time, Oly said. The Chinese bosses celebrated each victory.</p>
<p>“They forced you to stand up and clap.”</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration depicts Oly and others in a room communicating with their clients, while being overseen by another person." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/29.jpg"/></figure>

<p>Part IV</p>
<p>Rescue Camps</p>
<p>In January, the abduction of a Chinese actor by the scam syndicates prompted a crackdown by Chinese, Thai and Myanmar authorities.</p>
<p>Under pressure, gangs operating the centres and the Myanmar armed groups freed thousands of victims, including Oly. But some of the liberated people found themselves in rescue camps run by the militias.</p>
<p>Conditions were poor, detainees said at the time. The camps were watched by armed militia guards and food was scarce, they said.</p>
<p>Oly messaged Reuters from the camp.</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration showing Oly crouching on the ground, eating from his own boxed meal beside another person." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/50.jpg"/></figure>
<p><em>"</em><em>We are getting two meals per day... The food is not enough</em><em>" </em></p>
<p>The camps were unsanitary, with a handful of toilets for hundreds of people and no sanitary pads for women.</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration showing a long queue of people waiting to use the limited toilets, with trash scattered across the ground." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/51.jpg"/></figure>
<p>"We live like animals"</p>
<p>After spending two months in the rescue camp, Oly was taken across the border to Thailand as part of a rescue effort led by Thai authorities and eventually flew home.</p>
<p>He had been gone five months.</p>
<figure><img alt="An illustration depicting an airplane flying in the sky." src="https://www.reuters.com/graphics/SOUTHEASTASIA-SCAMS/mypmxwdwwvr/cdn/images/part/52.jpg"/></figure>
<p>The KNA, which runs some of the rescue camps, told Reuters around 2,000 people were waiting to be repatriated due to “incomplete information”; some of them were receiving “treatment due to poor health”.</p>
<p>Thousands remain in Myanmar where the fraud centers continue to operate, according to Thai officials.</p>
<p>The Thai foreign ministry spokesperson said the country was committed to bringing people home but counted on the assistance of victims’ embassies that faced “staffing constraints or budgetary challenges” which delayed the process.</p>
<p>Oly said he has lost his job and is struggling to pay rent and medical bills for his pregnant wife.</p>
<p>He is relieved to have escaped the scam centres and no longer be trapped at his desk.</p>
