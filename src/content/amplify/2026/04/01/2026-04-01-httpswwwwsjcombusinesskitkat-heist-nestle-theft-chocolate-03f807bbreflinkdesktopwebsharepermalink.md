<h1>How a Massive KitKat Heist Turned Into Crisis PR Gold</h1>
<p>The Nestlé response to the stolen chocolate shows how there’s no bad news if you can turn it into a meme</p>
<p>By</p>
<p><a href="https://www.wsj.com/news/author/natasha-khan">Natasha Khan</a> and <a href="https://www.wsj.com/news/author/kelly-cloonan">Kelly Cloonan</a></p>
<p>March 31, 2026 5:30 am ET</p>
<img alt="The KitKats were taken somewhere on the way from a factory in central Italy to Poland." src="https://images.wsj.net/im-27855933?width=700&amp;height=466"/>
<p>Just how much are 12 metric tons of stolen KitKat bars worth? A lot of promotional gold, it turns out.</p>
<p>It was the brazen chocolate heist heard around the social-media world: Over the weekend, Nestlé confirmed that thieves had swiped 413,793 units of KitKats somewhere along their way from a factory in central Italy to Poland. Both the chocolate bars and the truck carrying them remain missing, though no one was hurt in the theft, it said.</p>
<p>What the Swiss company lost in chocolate, though, it gained back in a public-relations coup—as did multiple other companies quick to hop on the meme bandwagon.</p>
<p>“We’ve always encouraged people to have a break with KitKat—but it seems thieves have taken the message too literally and made a break with more than 12 metric tons of our chocolate,” the company said in a statement. A spokesman confirmed the heist wasn’t an early April 1 joke.</p>
