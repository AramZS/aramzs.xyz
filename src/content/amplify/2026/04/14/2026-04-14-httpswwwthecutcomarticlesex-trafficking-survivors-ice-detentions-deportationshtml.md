title: They Survived Sex Trafficking — Then ICE Detained Them

<figure><img data-content-img="" src="https://pyxis.nymag.com/v1/imgs/e92/8f5/5087ff76051226e05b4792acac68569ba4-ice-2.rsquare.w700.jpg"/></figure>
<p>Flora immigrated to the United States in 2023 because of a promise. The man with whom she shared an infant son swore they’d be able to start a new life as a family in the Midwest, where he was living. But the second she arrived, the young Venezuelan mother says, she was trapped in a nightmare. The man immediately demanded that Flora surrender every cent she had, she says, telling her she owed him money for the journey and that she’d have to work to pay off her debt. For the next year and a half, the man held Flora hostage in his home — she alleges he repeatedly raped her, forced her to perform domestic labor and work for his small business without pay, physically abused her and denied her food, and threatened to harm her and her child. Flora had become one of the estimated <a href="https://www.state.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/TIP-Report-2024_Introduction_V10_508-accessible_2.13.2025.pdf">27 million people worldwide</a> who are victims of human trafficking.</p>
<p>Within months, Flora became pregnant again as a result of the repeated sexual violence. (Flora is not her real name — we’re protecting her identity owing to safety concerns. Our account of her experience is based on legal documents The Cut reviewed and interviews with her legal team; <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/mar/25/mother-trafficking-ice-detained"><em>The Guardian</em></a> was the first to report her story.) She gave birth to her second child and, in early 2025, was able to escape her alleged trafficker with the help of a local nonprofit organization. Flora fled to the DMV area and took refuge in a church. The man then allegedly stalked her, threatening to report her to immigration authorities and take her children away; Flora obtained a restraining order against him and was able to connect with the nonprofit <a href="https://htlegalcenter.org/">Human Trafficking Legal Center</a> for assistance. On January 30, the Center helped her report her alleged trafficker to federal authorities. On the same day, the unthinkable happened: Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents detained Flora at a routine check-in appointment and shipped her 1,200 miles away to a detention center in Louisiana, separating her from her 4-year-old son and 18-month-old daughter.</p>
<p>It was the first time in the Center’s 13-year history that an immigrant survivor had been detained, so staff sprang into action, relentlessly contacting federal agencies about Flora’s case. She had an active asylum claim — Flora’s alleged abuser had filed one on her behalf, she says; once she obtained a work permit as part of her application, he demanded she work even more — and the Center was in the middle of preparing her application for a <a href="https://www.uscis.gov/humanitarian/victims-of-human-trafficking-t-nonimmigrant-status">T visa</a>, which provides immigration relief for survivors of sex and labor trafficking. “We just wanted to make sure that everybody in the government knew they had a trafficking survivor in detention, that this was contrary to the law, that they shouldn’t have detained her and she needed to be released,” says Caroline Pizano, a senior staff attorney at the Human Trafficking Legal Center who is part of Flora’s legal team. “We honestly didn’t know why she’d been detained. It is inexplicable because she was going to an appointment.”</p>
<p>Flora spent two months <a href="https://www.thecut.com/article/immigration-ice-detention-does-child-body.html">in detention</a>, reliving her time in captivity and struggling due to being separated from her children, including the baby she was still breastfeeding at the time of her arrest. Her mental health deteriorated.</p>
<p>“What message is that sending?” says Casey Swegman, the director of public policy at the Tahirih Justice Center, which offers free legal and social services to immigrant survivors of gender-based violence. “Well, even if you do all the right things — even if U.S. Immigration reviews your case and grants you legal status that they have deemed you are entitled to under the law — you could still be a victim of this overly aggressive immigration-enforcement environment.”</p>
<p>President Donald Trump has long cast himself as a crusader against human trafficking. He has <a href="https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/factcheck/2020/10/17/fact-check-trump-signed-2-executive-orders-against-human-trafficking/3642001001/">promised</a> to make this issue a top policy priority and has <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/video/6343135274112">gleefully embraced</a> those supporters who see him as a <a href="https://www.politico.com/news/magazine/2022/09/23/trump-is-a-messianic-figure-in-the-qanon-calls-00058671">messianic figure</a> willing to take down a purported cabal of elites and Democrats who they baselessly believe are sex traffickers, particularly of children. And yet not only has Trump <a href="https://www.thecut.com/article/how-trump-is-decimating-anti-trafficking-services.html">decimated anti-trafficking services</a> during his second term, but as his administration <a href="https://www.thecut.com/article/maria-de-jesus-estrada-juarez-daca-ice-deportation.html">indiscriminately detains immigrants</a> to comply with his mass-deportations agenda, trafficking survivors have been caught in the dragnet — even when they have legal status. The situation has become so dire that several groups <a href="https://www.centerforhumanrights.org/icwc-v-noem">filed a class-action lawsuit</a> against the Trump administration last fall, accusing ICE of detaining and deporting survivors of gender-based abuse, trafficking, and other serious crimes even though, by law, they are meant to be shielded from enforcement.</p>
<p>On April 2, ICE voluntarily let Flora go just hours before a court hearing on the habeas corpus petition seeking her release. (In the immigration context, habeas challenges argue “this person is detained and they shouldn’t be” and request that detainees are either released or are allowed to have a bond hearing.) But the damage was done: Flora and her children were retraumatized.</p>
<p>It wasn’t meant to be this way — the U.S. government was supposed to protect immigrant survivors. In 2000, the <a href="https://www.congress.gov/106/plaws/publ386/PLAW-106publ386.pdf">Victims of Trafficking and Violence Protection Act</a> criminalized human trafficking in the United States. The legislation created victim-services programs at federal agencies and earmarked spending for anti-trafficking programs domestically and abroad; it also established the T visa as a form of immigration relief for survivors. Congress recognized, on paper, that they are especially vulnerable: Many immigrant survivors do not have a social safety net, may not be fluent in English, and are dealing with the long-lasting effects of physical and emotional trauma. “This visa was created to help eliminate one of the major fears that they have, which is deportation,” Pizano says. “It created a pathway for noncitizen survivors to obtain legal status in this country, and it was also to enable them to assist in criminal investigations.”</p>
<p>The visa program has been an important tool for trafficking survivors, though it has come with limitations. A large backlog means long wait times for their cases to be processed, says Pizano. And while the U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services caps T visas at 5,000 per year, the agency has never granted that many in a single year, she adds. The most recent data from the Department of Homeland Security shows that the most T visas the federal government has approved were fewer than 3,800 in the 2024 fiscal year. The number of T visas approved in the 2025 fiscal year decreased by more than half, to around just 1,400.</p>
<p>It seems that Trump’s return — and the ways his administration has diverted resources away from certain federal agencies to fund the widening scope of immigration enforcement — has made the already long wait even worse. “Survivors who do come forward and file for immigration relief are facing processing backlogs like we’ve never seen. We’re seeing all of these resources committed to enforcement and deportation, even within USCIS — which has historically been a benefits-processing agency, not an enforcement agency,” says Swegman. “It’s really retraumatizing for a trafficking survivor to sit and wait and feel that they really have no control over their future. And on top of all of that, trafficking survivors with legal status are now facing arrest and deportation in violation of their rights and due process.”</p>
<p>These victims are often being apprehended in what has also become <a href="https://apnews.com/article/immigration-fears-appointments-detained-deported-a6d465c177eb4880699e9c40210508fb">a common trend</a> for other immigrants under Trump 2.0: at <a href="https://missionlocal.org/2026/03/sf-ice-data-arrests-check-ins-asylum-immigration-deportation/">mandatory check-ins</a> with immigration authorities, which can range from biometrics appointments or credible-fear interviews for asylum cases to more routine check-ins during which someone offers updates such as a change of address or is provided with a new date for their court hearing.</p>
<p>Shortly after taking office, Trump also rescinded a long-standing ICE <a href="https://www.ice.gov/doclib/foia/policy/11005.4.pdf">policy</a> protecting victims of trafficking and other crimes from detention and deportation. To make matters worse, the administration also <a href="https://www.dhs.gov/news/2025/01/21/statement-dhs-spokesperson-directives-expanding-law-enforcement-and-ending-abuse">ended a Biden-era policy</a> that banned immigration-enforcement actions in sensitive locations, such as churches, schools, and hospitals. These changes “really created a heightened-risk environment for the clients,” says Swegman. As a result, survivors now have to worry they’ll encounter ICE in places they previously felt safe, including domestic-violence shelters, rape crisis centers, courthouses, and health-care centers.</p>
<p>“One of the more heartbreaking patterns that we’ve seen is former clients — whose cases were long ago approved and won — getting back in touch with us, possibly needing legal services again,” she says. Even though these clients have active visas, they are seeking legal guidance in case they are detained. “We’re doing safety planning with them, and if they are facing enforcement challenges, we are trying to find resources for them. That’s just something we never thought we would have to do.”</p>
<p>Available data shows there’s cause for concern, says Ashley Gravlin, a third-year law student at Georgia State University College of Law in Atlanta who has been tracking habeas corpus petitions. In her research, Gravlin found that since January 2025, there have been at least 68 habeas petitions that involve trafficking survivors in ICE detention.</p>
<p>“You may look at this number and think,<em> That’s not that bad</em>. But when you consider how small the population of T-visa beneficiaries already is, that’s alarming. You just don’t see this level of volume of trafficking-survivors habeas cases before January 20, 2025,” she says. “That figure also does not include people who are trafficking survivors but they are too scared to file or who are survivors but they don’t have counsel filing a habeas petition on their behalf. It’s a very sobering realization that this is a small piece of the puzzle.”</p>
<p>Court documents Gravlin shared with me outlined allegations that have perturbed her. At least two habeas petitions involve the detention of trafficking survivors who were minors when they arrived in the U.S. Another petitioner who experienced sex trafficking alleges that ICE has refused to provide adequate medical care for their seizure condition. Two other cases involve immigrant survivors who are parents of U.S.-citizen children, and a third involves a detainee who gave birth while in custody, was released on an order of supervision, then was detained once again following a check-in with ICE. (The Cut is withholding more details about these cases to protect survivors’ privacy.)</p>
<p>“These cases are happening across the country. A lot of these individuals are getting taken into custody for trying to comply with what they were supposed to do — such as going to a biometrics appointment — which is ridiculous,” she says. “It’s really quite concerning, because while the habeas cases do tend to be successful, the time to that success varies. And success can be immediate release, which we are seeing in some cases, or getting a bond hearing, which is a little harder to get numbers on. And even then, it’s like, ‘Okay, you got a bond hearing, but the immigration judge said you’re a flight risk.’”</p>
<p>Another issue worrying Gravlin is that, owing to the many legal challenges against the Trump administration’s immigration policies, there’s currently a patchwork of court decisions that can impact a survivor’s ability to be freed. “The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit had a <a href="https://www.cbsnews.com/news/appeals-court-trump-administration-immigration-detainees-without-bond/">decision last month</a> in the case <a href="https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca8/25-3248/25-3248-2026-03-25.html"><em>Avila </em>v.<em> Bondi</em></a> which basically said, ‘If you entered the country without inspection, you can’t get a bond hearing anymore,’” she says. “I don’t think I need to fill in how horrifying it is for someone to potentially be in an Eighth Circuit court as a result of trafficking, and if they didn’t enter the country with inspection, they’re now being penalized for being the victim of a crime.”</p>
<p>Most of the anti-trafficking organizations I contacted for this story declined to share specific anecdotes about survivors’ experiences with ICE because of safety concerns, but all of them said they have had clients who have been impacted. In the case of the Tahirih Justice Center, the organization is having to think about planning around detentions and deportations for the first time in its three-decade history.</p>
<p>“That has never been the reality of our clients’ experiences or the work that we do. We’re trying to scale up defending the legal and human rights of our clients in the multiple jurisdictions where we are operating and working to build more robust pro bono partnerships in that regard,” says Swegman. “All agencies that have historically not worked in removal defense are having to ask those questions and make plans. There is a fear that this will continue and potentially become a greater demand of us.”</p>
<p>The panic survivors are facing is hard to overstate. A <a href="https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5b9f1d48da02bc44473c36f1/t/6939b75619bb9e221999a83a/1765390166359/AIS-Report_Fear-and-Silence_Dec2025.pdf">December 2025 report</a> by the Alliance for Immigrant Survivors, a national network of advocates, found that about 50 percent of the organizations surveyed reported that victims who were concerned about contacting law enforcement or going to court ultimately chose not to do so out of fear. About one-third of advocates reported they worked with victims who dropped their civil or criminal cases against their abusers because they were scared of ICE. For survivors, it feels as if they are stuck between two horrific scenarios: No matter whether they speak up or stay silent, they could face their abusers’ wrath or be trapped in the Trump administration’s relentless targeting of immigrants today. They are terrified that either way they’ll lose.</p>
<p>Pizano is seeing a similar trend among the survivors the Human Trafficking Legal Center is currently assisting. “Traffickers use the threat of deportation a lot of times with survivors,” she says. The fear of being removed from the country — and, in some cases, being separated from their families — already makes it extremely challenging for survivors to speak up. She adds, “So when you see this type of aggressive immigration enforcement without regard to somebody’s status as a victim of trafficking, it actually reinforces the message that folks receive from traffickers themselves: <em>They don’t care about you</em>.<em> You shouldn’t come forward, you shouldn’t seek help, because no one’s going to help. They’ll just deport you</em>.”</p>
<p>She says clients are choosing to skip in-person meetings with the Center because they are afraid to leave their homes. A lot of their communications have moved online as a result, which has made the administrative process slower. The Center is working with other advocacy organizations to help immigrant survivors create emergency plans in the instance they are detained. Her client Flora had one of such plans, which meant someone was able to care for her two children over the two months she spent in immigration detention. Otherwise, her preschooler and toddler would have likely ended up in foster care.</p>
<p>The result of this climate of fear is that both trafficking victims and their communities are less safe. “When you detain a trafficking survivor, it’s discouraging other survivors from coming forward. It’s undermining trust in law enforcement,” Pizano says. “It’s weakening our trafficking prosecutions. And, most importantly, it’s going against the clear purpose of the federal law that was enacted to protect folks.”</p>
<p>In Flora’s case, the Center filed her T-visa application in early February while she was still detained in Louisiana. After her release, she was able to reunite with her children and go back to live in a safe location. She’s now in a holding pattern, waiting for a resolution on her asylum claim and the visa. Meanwhile, her alleged trafficker remains free.</p>
