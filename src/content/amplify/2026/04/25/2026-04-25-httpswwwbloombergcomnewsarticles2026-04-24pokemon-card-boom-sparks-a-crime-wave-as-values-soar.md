<body><div><i>As a childhood hobby becomes an asset class, theft and fraud are spreading across the fast-growing market.</i></div><figure><img alt="" src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iu0Jajg6E8pk/v0/-1x-1.webp"/><figcaption><p><em>Illustration: Daniel Guerrero Fernández for Bloomberg</em></p></figcaption></figure><div><p>Over the Lunar New Year holidays in February, Jeffrey Law’s phone lit up with security alerts in the early hours of the morning. The 25-year-old rushed to his store in Hong Kong’s Chung Mei Center within minutes, to find a smashed window and boxes strewn across the floor.</p><p>The loot wasn’t cash but sealed packs of Pokémon cards.</p><p>Losses at the specialist trading card store totaled about HK$15,000 ($1,915), and 10 security cameras were sprayed with red paint. Still, Law was relieved that a prized Pokémon card worth about HK$100,000 — the most valuable of his stock of collectibles — remained locked away.</p><p>“You don’t expect someone to break into what is essentially a toy shop,” he says.</p><p>The incident reflects a broader rise in theft and scams in Asia, the US and Europe as the emergence of trading cards as an alternative asset class fuels price increases. In February, YouTuber Logan Paul’s ultrarare Pikachu Illustrator card <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cp32jgkqx0lo">sold</a> for over $16 million at auction, more than triple what he paid in 2021 and a public sale record for the franchise.</p><p><a href="https://www.strategicmarketresearch.com/market-report/trading-cards-market">By one estimate</a>, the global trading cards market, valued at $15.8 billion in 2024, is projected to reach $23.5 billion by 2030. But the boom has also brought risks.</p><p> <p class="rw-outer-content"><span>Some content could not be imported from the original document.</span> <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-24/pokemon-card-boom-sparks-a-crime-wave-as-values-soar">View content ↗ </a></p></p><p><a href="https://www.police.gov.sg/Media-Hub/News/2026/02/20260206_man_arrested_for_series_of_e_commerce_scams_involving_pokemon_trading_cards">Singapore alone</a> has logged more than 600 e-commerce scams involving Pokémon cards since October 2025, with losses of at least S$1.1 million ($864,000). In a single case in the US, scammers attempted to cheat trading card collectors out of more than <a href="https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdny/pr/perpetrator-nationwide-sports-and-pokemon-trading-card-fraud-convicted">$2 million</a>, while a string of <a href="https://edition.cnn.com/2026/04/04/business/pokemon-cards-crime-spree#:~:text=Wallis%20would%20put%20trading%20cards,also%20affected%20their%20insurance%20coverage." target="_blank">card shops</a> across the US and Europe have been <a href="https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c4gvvnpl5kno">broken into</a> in recent months.</p><p>Three decades after the franchise made its debut, Pokémon cards have evolved from a playground pastime into a genuine market, with values driven higher by millennials and Gen Z collectors returning to their childhood passion with newly disposable income.</p><p>Among them is Adhithyan B.M., who recalls saving his S$2 daily allowance as a child for Pokémon card packs. Now 30 and working in wealth management, he buys selectively — focusing on sets featuring his favorite characters and purchasing multiple S$100 boxes to open in a single sitting when the right release comes along.</p><p>“You got garbage most of the time, but just opening the packs, that was fun,” he says, reminiscing about his childhood.<span><strong> </strong></span>“Now if I wanted to, I can sell the card and take the money out.”</p><p>For some, the hobby has brought enormous returns. Chua Ming Guang, 20, has built a collection worth more than S$118,000 since he started collecting at age 9. His crown jewel, a Charizard X Poncho Pikachu card bought for S$14,000 last June, now draws offers of about S$30,000. In 2025 he expanded beyond trading online by setting up a physical collection point inside a flower shop, where a shelf displays selected cards for collectors to browse and scan a QR code to pay.</p><figure><img alt="Chua Ming Guang showcases some of his Pokémon card collection, worth more than S$118,000." src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/i2PTQj3rEB3k/v1/-1x-1.webp"/><figcaption>Chua Ming Guang showcases some of his Pokémon card collection, worth more than S$118,000. / <em>Photographer: Srinidhi Ragavendran/Bloomberg</em></figcaption></figure><p>The surge in value isn’t lost on those behind the production of cards. Japanese illustrator Kouki Saitou, who <a href="https://www.artofpkm.com/illustrators/14/products">designed</a> more than 700 Pokémon cards over nearly three decades, says the franchise now feels “completely different.”</p><p>At a trading event in Singapore in February, fans queued for autographs on cards he’d illustrated years ago that are now worth thousands of dollars. His Poncho Pikachu cards — highly collectible items featuring the character Pikachu wearing a hood — are among the most coveted, fetching up to $100,000.</p><p>Watching collectors treat his work as prized assets “feels bizarre,” he says.</p><p>As valuations climb and participation widens, governments are taking notice. Singapore is looking to regulate the sale of so-called blind boxes to discourage gambling like behavior. The concern centers on a pack-opening culture where buyers purchase sealed packs without knowing what’s inside, chasing the thrill of pulling a rare card.</p><p>Singapore’s Ministry of Home Affairs said it was considering introducing some regulations by midyear and will subsequently also consider age restrictions and a requirement to disclose the odds of obtaining items in the box. Singapore’s efforts echo regulations in <a href="https://www.gov.cn/lianbo/bumen/202306/content_6886698.htm">China</a>, which bans the sale of blind boxes to children under 8. Operators there are also required to prominently disclose key details such as the probability of an item being in the box and its value.</p><p>New services are being introduced<span><strong> </strong></span>to support trading and reduce risk. MemeStrategy, a Hong Kong-listed digital asset company, is partnering with fine art storage provider Crown Fine Arts to provide a dedicated <a href="https://www.crownfineart.com/articles/crown-fine-art-partners-with-memestrategy-to-set-a-new-benchmark-for-trading-card-preservation" target="_blank">museum-grade vault</a> for trading cards in the city. While similar services exist in more mature markets such as the US, the company said this will be Asia’s first professional-grade trading card vault. The service will operate as a secure facility where collectors can deposit high-value cards, which are then authenticated, cataloged and <a href="https://grade10.com/vault" target="_blank">digitally recorded</a>. Each item is assigned a blockchain-based “digital twin,” allowing ownership to be transferred and managed remotely.</p><p>The facility, which is currently being developed with trading card businesses in China and Hong Kong, isn’t yet open to individual collectors. The model mirrors a broader shift in asset storage, with some consumers opting to keep even small amounts of physical gold in secure vaults rather than at home, prioritizing security and ease of transfer.</p><p>“When you trade high-value products, there should be better facilities to do that,” says MemeStrategy Chief Executive Officer Ray Chan, likening the model to stock markets where ownership is transferred digitally while assets remain in central custody.</p><p>New platforms like Singapore-based Kyo Cards are trying to bring more structure to a fragmented market — consolidating listings across social media, online forums and private chats into a single searchable hub. It’s similar to TCGplayer in the US and Cardmarket in Europe, which centralize listings and provide price transparency for buyers and sellers.</p><p>Such platforms help prevent collectors from being burned by online scams — like Singapore fitness trainer Chin Nian Kang who lost S$850 after cards he preordered on Telegram never arrived. Asked if he had learned any lessons, he shrugged. “Not really. It’s a matter of risk and return.”</p><p>“People who talk down on the hobby and call it a bubble don’t realize how much real money is moving,” he adds, undeterred. “If you buy 10 stocks and one of them burns you, you don’t stop investing forever.”</p><p>As more money flows into the hobby-turned-asset-class, the systems supporting it — and the risks surrounding it — are growing in tandem. For those on the ground, like Law and his specialist trading card store, the stakes are already high. “We have decided to hire a security guard to keep watch,” he says.</p></div><div><ol></ol></div></body>
