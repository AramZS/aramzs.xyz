<p>I don’t want to freak anyone out, but I’m getting a little worried about this whole war with Iran situation.</p>
<p>I’m not (just) talking about Trump threatening to destroy their civilization, or the price of gas going up. I’m worried about food, and how the closing of the Strait of Hormuz is going to affect global food production.</p>
<p>Right now, it’s planting season. You only have a brief window to start getting food in the ground. Planting food on an industrial, global scale requires nitrogen and fertilizer on an equivalent industrial scale. <a href="https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/the-planting-season-is-now-but-war-in-iran-has-sparked-a-global-fertilizer-shortage">About one third of fertilizer</a> used the world over travels through the Strait. None of that is moving now.<a href="https://hedge.hrdcopy.com/"> Europe’s pre-war buffer of oil has run out,</a> and the United States’ is right behind. Places like India and Vietnam are already feeling the shocks of this, which will likely get worse. As always, the global north will likely fair better off than the global south, but this is the beginning of a global crisis, and even if the war ends tomorrow, these ripple effects will be felt for a while.</p>

<p>I’ve been what I call a low-key prepper for a while now. What I mean by that is I’m not trying to build a bunker or survive a nuclear war. I’m not buying land or homesteading, and in general, I don’t spend a lot of time thinking about <em>preparing</em>. I simply just don’t think normal, or what we have as normal now, is going to last that long, and I want to be prepared for what’s coming. There will be ruptures and disruptions. We have already seen this with Covid, ICE raids, and the war in Ukraine. Things are falling apart, and what comes after might be better in the long run, but there are going to be increasing bumps on the way.</p>
<p>My general vibe is this: I have enough food, water, and general supplies for a few weeks if I can’t leave my apartment for whatever reason. If food supply chains break down, I have backups. If things get really scary where I live, I have contingency plans mapped out with friends who live in my area, and elsewhere.</p>
<p>I think these are smart things to have in place, but when I tell people they should prep, they always kind of freak out. They imagine zombie movies and, quite frankly, the kind of imperial violence America inflicts on people abroad, and Black and brown communities at home, that allows for their relative normalcy. When (white) people imagine preparing for disaster, it’s often very lonely and scary. You have to buy a bunch of stuff, get a gun, haul up in a bunker, and pray that doom arrives so you don’t look and feel very stupid.</p>
<p>If you are like me, and are mildly worried about the continuing fall out from the war with Iran, as well as other building crises, allow me to introduce you to the concept of Chill Prepping. When you prep in a chill way, you know you can’t predict every bad scenario, but you want to have some stuff in place just in case. In chill prepping, we understand that people and community are more important than bunkers and compounds.</p>

<p>The following is advice I give to people who ask me what they should do when they want to prep in a chill way. It is not comprehensive, does not account for all people, scenarios, circumstances and so on. It’s a jumping off point, not an end point. I’m speaking from personal experience, and as a result am mostly addressing My Fellow Americans here, although many people may find this advice applicable. Ok, now that I’ve made all those caveats and said all that no one can get mad at me ok :)</p>
<p>I’m going to lay out the Do’s and Don’ts of chill prepping, then end with some resources that might be helpful. By the end, you’ll have a starting off point to become a chill prepper like me.</p>
<h2>Don’t:</h2>
<h3>-Don’t Freak out</h3>
<p>I know this might feel counter intuitive, but it’s kind of the most important point on this list. Prepping can become addictive because, conceivably, there is always some disaster you <em>could</em> prep for. It gives you the illusion of control, when what we are trying to do is mitigate total disaster.</p>
<p>It is literally impossible to imagine or prepare for every possible disaster. Following the news too closely will make you see patterns that aren’t there and foresee conclusions that aren’t certain. Try not to imagine only the worst case scenario and spend all your money on stupid shit from grifters that you will never use. You are preparing now and getting a plan in place so you don’t have to freak out later.</p>
<h3>-Don’t buy a gun*</h3>
<p>This is complicated so let me explain. If you already have a gun, that’s cool. If you have your heart set on getting one, that’s cool. Hunting might be a thing you want to do to help cover food costs, and depending on your circumstances having a gun might be a smart investment for self defense. I’m not going to get into politics around gun ownership here. Follow the laws in your state/region.</p>
<p>I’ve noticed over the last few years that the topic of gun ownership has changed a bit with liberal friends of mine. There is less of a stigma, and more interest in owning one. However, many people, newly radicalized liberals and anxious conservatives alike, follow what I call the “zombie apocalypse” theory of gun ownership. That is, they think having more is better, and that in a scary scenario, they will magically learn how to shoot a gun really well, without ever training at a range. This is essentially like watching a car chase and going “I could do that…in fact I will need to do that someday.” and buying several muscle cars, all while never having driven before.</p>
<p>If you do not know how to operate and care for a gun, you are more likely to hurt yourself and your loved ones than any perceived target. If you are shooting in self defense, several things have gone horribly wrong already, and it is smarter to game out and try to prevent those things before jumping straight to getting a firearm. If you are a liberal shaking your head at this while imagining Handmaid’s Tale type scenarios, or a conservative freaking out about losing your rights to a government you voted for, let me just tell you that ship has fucking sailed. I need you to be for real about the world we live in. You are not going to fight off the US military or a bunch of cops all by yourself. Buying a gun when you have never even fired one before is a very anxious and potentially dangerous move. It might be the right move for you, but if it’s the first and only thing you jump to, you aren’t being logical.</p>
<h3>-Don’t hoard</h3>
<p>I’m going to keep hitting this point over and over because it bears repeating, but whether it is guns, food, water, or land, most Americans can only conceive of protection and freedom through a lens of consumption. The logic is: the more stuff I have, the safer I am, but also the more people will want to take my stuff, so I have to buy more stuff to protect the stuff I have.</p>
<p>Remember that the reason for food shortages is not a lack of food, but an intentional lack of distribution. Don’t mirror the mindset that got us into this. When you buy supplies or gather resources, think about what people in your community might need. In a true disaster, you will have to borrow and share. That’s a good thing. It builds networks.</p>
<h2>Do:</h2>
<h3>-Game things out logically</h3>
<p>It is impossible to predict every possible scenario for how things can or will go wrong. Doing so <a href="https://substack.com/@citymysticc/p-184046133">will drive you crazy</a>. Instead, sit down and think through what disasters, disruptions, or changes are most likely to impact you, and decide how you would like to respond now, while things are relatively calm. Don’t go 0 to 60 with this and panic, or act jaded and get complacent. If you are reading this you have lived through a pandemic. You have lived through crisis. Use what you learned then.</p>
<p>What are 2-3 likely scenarios you could see happening to you personally, your community more broadly, and the place you live on a wide scale? If that happens, what do you think you will wish you had done now to prepare? Break that down into small, workable pieces, and go from there.</p>
<h3>-Assess your own personal risk</h3>
<p>When we talk about preparedness, we are talking about privilege. I am using that word neutrally. If you can read this, you have privilege. Part of what you need to prepare will come down to how personally affected you will be, and how likely the worst case scenario actually is for you. Immigrants will have different risks than citizens, men will have different risks than women, trans people will have different risks than cis people, disabled people and people with health issues will have different risks all together, and so on. Be honest about your personal risks and privileges. Take your ego out of it. What privileges do you have that can support those who are at risk? What extra help might you need and do you have people who can support that? Start building community resilience now. To that point…</p>
<h3>-Get to know your neighbors</h3>
<p>When Americans think about preparing for disasters, they mostly think about buying stuff. This reveals just how deeply consumerism and toxic individualism has seeped into our psyches. Sure, it’s a good idea to buy some stuff. Extra batteries, food, water, medicine, and so forth are good things to have. However, people don’t survive disasters, communities do.</p>
<p>Talk to the people around you. Get to know them. What are their skills and needs? Who is vulnerable in case of disrupted food or medicine supplies? Who has resources like arable land, a car, a back yard, extra storage, and so on, that they are willing to share? Is that person you? Talk with friends and people you trust, about what you are afraid of in the coming months and years, and make a plan. Learn to have conversations in person, away from technology, when possible.</p>
<h3>-Get some supplies.</h3>
<p>Look around your house and think about stuff you use every day. Soap, detergent, tampons, toothpaste, and so on. It’s probably a good idea to have some extras of these things around. While you are at it, get a first aid kit. Don’t do this all at once, build up supplies gradually so you don’t overspend or make yourself panic.</p>
<h3>-Buy some shelf-stable food</h3>
<p>Finally, yes, you should probably buy some extra food. Ideally, you should have about two weeks worth of food on hand.</p>
<p>Panic buying a bunch of canned food all at once is not chill. Maybe it’s something you can afford to do and it makes sense to you, but this is more likely to trigger panic in you than anything else. I recommend buying just a couple extra things when you go grocery shopping over the next month or so, so you can build a steady supply. Buying a can of beans? Toss a couple extra in there. A bag of rice? Buy one more. You can build up a supply over time gradually. Remember, you aren’t going to live in a bunker for ten years. You are building a cushion so you and those you care for can have a cushion if/when there is disruption.</p>
