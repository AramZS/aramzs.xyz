<h3> Check My Ads Institute </h3>
<h4> We're the independent digital advertising watchdog. We are building a transparent and fair digital advertising market. </h4>
<p>At the end of last year, I <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcheckmyads%2Eorg%2Fnewsletter%2F2025-was-only-the-teaser-2026-will-be-the-reckoning-for-adtech-in-europe%2F&amp;urlhash=LH10&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">promised that 2026 would be a reckoning for the adtech industry in Europe</a>. Well, the reckoning has begun—and it has kept us so busy that only now, at the end of March, are we able to return with the first issue of Adtech Policy Watch in 2026, focusing mainly on the Digital Omnibus proposal currently debated in the EU.</p>
<h3>Next stop: the EU legislators</h3>
<p>The Digital Omnibus proposed by the European Commission in <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fdigital-strategy%2Eec%2Eeuropa%2Eeu%2Fen%2Flibrary%2Fdigital-omnibus-regulation-proposal&amp;urlhash=5vmb&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">November 2025</a> has been causing panic in online ad spaces, and rightly so: among other things, it introduces amendments to the ePrivacy Directive and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in a way that centralises the end-user consent framework for online ads within a browser (<a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Feur-lex%2Eeuropa%2Eeu%2Flegal-content%2FEN%2FTXT%2F%3Furi%3DCELEX%3A52025PC0837&amp;urlhash=ZA1j&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">notorious Article 88b</a>). The proposal is now with the two EU legislative bodies: the Parliament and the Council, where representatives of 27 EU states are debating it.</p>
<p>In 2016, the Eurobarometer survey found that <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Feuropa%2Eeu%2Feurobarometer%2Fsurveys%2Fdetail%2F2124&amp;urlhash=aJBG&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">89% of Europeans</a> wanted privacy settings enabled by default in their browsers. This survey was the basis of a similar browser-based requirement introduced in the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Feur-lex%2Eeuropa%2Eeu%2Flegal-content%2FEN%2FTXT%2F%3Furi%3Dcelex%3A52017PC0010&amp;urlhash=piZC&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">ePrivacy Regulation</a> <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Feur-lex%2Eeuropa%2Eeu%2Flegal-content%2FEN%2FTXT%2F%3Furi%3Dcelex%3A52017PC0010&amp;urlhash=piZC&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Proposal</a> in 2017. If the framework was adopted and users chose the private option in a browser, the monopolist Big Tech and some parasitic adtech companies feared they would lose technical access to the data they monetised. Which would result in a big loss for them. Eventually, <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Ftechcrunch%2Ecom%2F2021%2F11%2F01%2Fdigging-into-googles-push-to-freeze-eprivacy%2F&amp;urlhash=vs3r&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">the Google-led lobbying effort</a> resulted in the proposal being withdrawn in 2025. Womp womp. That win for Google was temporary, as the European Commission decided to reintroduce the consent framework in the Digital Omnibus.</p>
<p>As <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fprivacy%2Eca%2Egov%2F2026%2F01%2Fcalifornias-opt-me-out-act-your-privacy-just-got-easier%2F&amp;urlhash=2sVK&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">California</a> and <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fstatutes%2Ecapitol%2Etexas%2Egov%2F%3Ftab%3D1%26code%3DBC%26chapter%3DBC%2E541%26artSec%3D&amp;urlhash=ucD-&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Texas</a> have already mandated browser-based opt-out signals, the EU, which takes pride in having the strongest privacy and personal data protection framework in the world, is unlikely to back down on mandating this framework, but exactly what it will look like remains up for discussion. We have submitted <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcheckmyads%2Eorg%2Fpolicy-brief%2Fcheck-my-ads-institute-comments-on-the-digital-omnibus-regulation-proposal%2F&amp;urlhash=YOYH&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">our comments on the Digital Omnibus</a> to the EU authorities, supporting a standardised, centralised consent framework. Cheers to a right to privacy for everyone (well, one day, we are shooting for the stars)!</p>
<h3>Spin, spin, spin</h3>
<p>There are two sets of actors that do not want meaningful user choice at the browser level. Big Tech monopolists (Google, Meta) and parts of the adtech industry that depend on continuous access to end-user data for their business models. Advertisers and publishers—currently dependent on these intermediaries—are understandably concerned about potential disruption. This is precisely why opponents of browser-level consent seek to capitalise on this fear of the unknown to undermine legislative efforts that would restore user control.</p>
<p>There is nothing new in this dynamic. As early as 1997, the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) issued <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Erfc-editor%2Eorg%2Frfc%2Frfc2109%2Ehtml&amp;urlhash=kShk&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">RFC 2109</a>, which recommended blocking third-party cookies (3PCs) by default. They were really on to something. At the time, DoubleClick (now owned by Google) <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Ewired%2Ecom%2F1997%2F03%2Fdoubleclick-tries-to-force-hand-into-cookie-jar%2F&amp;urlhash=xz4d&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">led industry resistance</a>, and Netscape—searching for a viable business model—chose not to implement the recommendation.</p>
<p>The pattern repeated in 2009 with the amendment of the ePrivacy Directive, which introduced a consent requirement for accessing or storing information on user devices. Regulators did not require cookie banners: <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Feur-lex%2Eeuropa%2Eeu%2Feli%2Fdir%2F2009%2F136&amp;urlhash=qX9y&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Recital 66</a> made clear that consent could be expressed through browser settings: “Where it is technically possible and effective… the user’s consent… may be expressed by using the appropriate settings of a browser or other application.” Around the same time, <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eeff%2Eorg%2Fissues%2Fdo-not-track&amp;urlhash=9RbH&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Do Not Track (DNT)</a> emerged as an attempt to operationalise this model.</p>
<p>How did the industry respond to DNT—a technical standard designed to give users a simple, global choice to opt out of tracking? <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eiab%2Ecom%2Fnews%2Fiab-supports-consumer-control-over-internet-ad-experiences-says-machine-driven-do-not-track-systems-limit-users-freedom-of-choice%2F&amp;urlhash=42rF&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">By claiming it would limit users’ choice.</a> Classic “IAB-speak.” Because the ePrivacy Directive allowed—but did not mandate—such mechanisms, industry pushed instead for a fragmented, consent-per-site model. The result was the proliferation of cookie banners, which are now universally disliked. It was malicious compliance at its finest. As frustration grew, responsibility was shifted back to regulators, with blame placed on Article 5(3), now called a “cookie rule.”</p>
<p>Regulators recognised that a legal possibility was not enough. The 2017 proposal for an ePrivacy Regulation sought to make browser-level consent legally mandatory.</p>
<h3>Google playbook: divide, conquer, and paralyse the regulator</h3>
<p>As momentum for stronger privacy protections grew following the ePrivacy Regulation proposal, and as browsers like <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Ewired%2Ecom%2Fstory%2Fapple-macos-high-sierra-features-tracking%2F&amp;urlhash=qWK5&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Safari</a> and <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Ewired%2Ecom%2Fstory%2Ffirefox-browser-cookie-blocking-default%2F&amp;urlhash=HMBl&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Firefox</a> began implementing tracking restrictions, Google positioned itself as the defender of the adtech ecosystem—an ecosystem that, according to competition authorities in both the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eusvgoogleads%2Ecom%2F&amp;urlhash=zFNE&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">US</a> and the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/posts/aleksandre-zardiashvili-51733991_check-my-ads-submits-comments-to-the-european-activity-7419674770083913728-pBwH/?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">EU</a>, it has abused for years. It <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fblog%2Egoogle%2Fproducts-and-platforms%2Fproducts%2Fchrome%2Fbuilding-a-more-private-web%2F&amp;urlhash=lLcf&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">argued</a> that unilateral restrictions would harm independent adtech and push actors toward more opaque practices such as “fingerprinting”—the familiar “boogeyman” invoked to alarm regulators.</p>
<p>Instead, Google proposed the Privacy Sandbox: a set of browser-based APIs framed as privacy-preserving alternatives that would address both privacy and competition concerns. The practical effect was to delay the deprecation of 3PCs until Google could design a replacement. In reality, this also provided a justification for not adopting Safari- or Firefox-style privacy protections, which could have materially affected Google’s revenues at the time.</p>
<p>In the meantime, Google strengthened its own position—shifting revenue toward owned-and-operated (O&amp;O) environments and redefining “first-party” boundaries in ways that preserve large-scale data extraction. Now, as Google is once again cornered by the prospect of a browser-level consent requirement under the Digital Omnibus, its objective appears twofold: either (1) to block Article 88b entirely, or (2) to shape it in a way that advantages its O&amp;O, “first-party” advertising model, the latter increasingly becoming the bottom line.</p>
<p>We know how that played out. During this period, Google not only maintained but updated and reinforced its revenue-sharing agreements with Apple and Mozilla to secure default search status in Safari and Firefox—arrangements that <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Ejustice%2Egov%2Fopa%2Fpr%2Fdepartment-justice-wins-significant-remedies-against-google&amp;urlhash=7w6j&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">US courts later found to be anticompetitive</a> (ikyky). It mobilised industry opposition to the ePrivacy Regulation, and despite repeated <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Egov%2Euk%2Fcma-cases%2Finvestigation-into-googles-privacy-sandbox-browser-changes&amp;urlhash=nYKE&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">concerns raised by the UK Competition and Markets Authority</a>, it did not meaningfully address the competition issues inherent in the Privacy Sandbox. These unresolved issues then became the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fprivacysandbox%2Egoogle%2Ecom%2Fblog%2Fprivacy-sandbox-next-steps&amp;urlhash=rh-v&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">pretext for retaining the pre-2019 status quo in Chrome</a>—without meaningful browser-level privacy protections or user choice.</p>

<h3>What now? Should we stay with the broken system or build a new one?</h3>
<p>As negotiations remain unsettled, many across the industry—especially publishers—are concerned about the framework that will ultimately emerge. These concerns are well-founded. Without careful design, dominant players like Google will shape any carve-outs for data processing—such as “first-party” or “contextual”—in ways that they can exploit, while competitor publishers cannot. We aren't saying they've done similar before, but we aren't not saying it either.</p>
<p>But this is not an unsolvable problem. These boundaries can be drawn in the public interest. Properly designed carve-outs can create a level playing field and, where appropriate, explicitly support media organisations that underpin democratic societies. With data minimisation as a guiding principle, publishers can play a central role in standard-setting, helping ensure fair remuneration and sustainable business models. Workable solutions are possible and may include <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eiccl%2Eie%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2021%2F10%2FSustainable-without-surveillance%2Epdf&amp;urlhash=kPWx&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">contextual advertising models</a>, which, under the reformed legal framework, would not require user consent, and media ad networks (e.g., the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fozoneproject%2Ecom%2F&amp;urlhash=IXvI&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Ozone project</a>) that users could easily opt into.</p>
<p>Yet countervailing forces reinforce the narrative that meaningful reform is impossible, nudging both publishers and policymakers back toward the status quo—from which they personally benefit—despite its well-documented harms to fundamental rights. For actors that pride themselves on innovation, the preference for a broken system over the uncertainty of reform is striking. This resistance to change is understandable: it entails risk, a short-term shock for everyone, and a loss of revenue and control for current winners. But in the long term, reform can help ensure a healthy global information ecosystem that supports democracies worldwide—something all stakeholders ultimately benefit from.</p>
<p>Article 88b is not about destroying business models, but about fixing them. It would redirect advertising revenues away from monopolistic platforms and extractive intermediaries toward publishers operating with responsible data practices. This redistribution will impose costs on dominant tech firms and adtech intermediaries, but it will strengthen publishers, improve outcomes for advertisers, support legitimate adtech providers, and ultimately serve the public interest. We explained in detail how to achieve this in our <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcheckmyads%2Eorg%2Fpolicy-brief%2Fcheck-my-ads-institute-comments-on-the-digital-omnibus-regulation-proposal%2F&amp;urlhash=YOYH&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">submission</a>.</p>
<h3>Larger Adtech reform</h3>
<p>Article 88b and the Digital Omnibus are only the beginning. Momentum is building for a broader recalibration of the online advertising ecosystem. We couldn't be more hopeful for the reforms coming our way.</p>
<p>The European Consumer Organisation (BEUC), together with 87 civil society organisations, including Check My Ads, and over 100 individuals, has <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Ebeuc%2Eeu%2Fletters%2Fjoint-call-ambitious-digital-fairness-act&amp;urlhash=L5T0&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">called</a> for an ambitious Digital Fairness Act (DFA). The DFA seeks to modernise EU consumer protection by addressing harmful commercial practices, including addictive design and exploitative forms of personalisation, which are deeply embedded in today’s online advertising models.</p>
<p>BEUC recently published its <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Ebeuc%2Eeu%2Freports%2Fusers-still-stuck-mud-analysis-metas-2026-changes-its-consent-ads-mechanism-against-eu-law&amp;urlhash=GM3M&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">analysis</a> of Meta's latest update to its "consent-or-pay" model, finding that the company keeps European consumers stuck in the mud. We have reported on this case in the previous newsletter, as it directly sets the boundaries for Meta's online advertising business model in Europe. Check My Ads has co-written a <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcheckmyads%2Eorg%2Fstatement%2Fjoint-open-letter-to-enforce-the-digital-markets-act-against-meta%2F&amp;urlhash=bcGQ&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">joint open letter</a> with our friends at <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/noybeu/?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">noyb</a> and <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/irish-council-for-civil-liberties/?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Enforce/ICCL</a>, to the European Commission, urging it not to fall into complacency but to wield the DMA as intended and curb the exploitation of European consumers and businesses.</p>
<p>At the same time, a wider European movement is emerging to rethink how the web is governed and monetised. In February, the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/forbrukerradet/?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Norwegian Consumer Council</a> (Forbrukerrådet) published a <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Eforbrukerradet%2Eno%2Fbreakingfree%2F&amp;urlhash=Ih39&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">report</a>—accompanied by a pointed (and notably sharp) <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fvimeo%2Ecom%2F1168468796%3Ffl%3Dpl%26fe%3Dvl&amp;urlhash=LMaL&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">video</a>—on breaking free from the “enshittification” driven by the current ecosystem. We have also joined their open letter to European institutions calling for structural change.</p>
<p>The European Commission’s Digital Fitness Check further reflects this moment of reassessment. While framed as an effort to streamline the digital rulebook, it also creates an opportunity to address long-standing structural failures in adtech governance. We used this consultation to set out our own proposals for reform.</p>
<p>This momentum is now converging. In March, the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/forum-information-democracy/?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Forum for Information Democracy</a> (FID) published a <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Finformationdemocracy%2Eorg%2F2026%2F03%2F20%2Fthe-online-advertising-market-needs-urgent-structural-reform-to-support-democracy-and-journalism%2F&amp;urlhash=zwh-&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">policy brief</a> also calling for structural reform of the online advertising market, including supply-chain transparency, mandatory due diligence for intermediaries, and binding standards for audience measurement, which are well aligned with our policy solutions. Shout-out to Iesha White, our Director of Intelligence, and Anastasia Micich, our Research Analyst, for <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcheckmyads%2Eorg%2Freport%2Fcheck-my-ads-submits-paper-to-forum-on-information-and-democracy-outlining-solutions-for-a-fair-digital-ad-market%2F&amp;urlhash=nYyF&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">contributing</a> to FID’s work, and to Claire Atkin, our CEO, for co-authoring the report’s foreword.</p>
<p>Taken together, these initiatives signal a shift toward a more fundamental rethinking of how online advertising operates in Europe.</p>
<h3>What about the UK?</h3>
<p>UK authorities, notably the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) and the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO), have developed significant expertise in online advertising. The CMA’s 2019 <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Egov%2Euk%2Fcma-cases%2Fonline-platforms-and-digital-advertising-market-study&amp;urlhash=Y3fd&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">market study</a>—spanning over 1,000 pages—identified many of the issues now at the centre of policy debates, including privacy and competition concerns, ad fraud, and online safety. It has since informed key legislative initiatives, such as the Online Safety Act and the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act (DMCCA), and helped catalyse discussions on a broader governance framework for online advertising.</p>
<p>One such initiative is the <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Egov%2Euk%2Fgovernment%2Fgroups%2Fonline-advertising-taskforce&amp;urlhash=Ps41&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">Online Advertising Taskforce</a>, intended to bring together public authorities, industry, and civil society to develop solutions in the public interest.</p>
<p>On 17 March, Ian Murray, Minister for Creative Industries, Media and Arts, provided an <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fquestions-statements%2Eparliament%2Euk%2Fwritten-statements%2Fdetail%2F2026-03-17%2FHCWS1407&amp;urlhash=whc6&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">update</a> on the Taskforce’s work. He noted that seven industry-led working groups are now active across priority areas, including AI, age assurance, and ad fraud prevention. While the Taskforce’s progress report points to “significant progress,” one may be sceptical: IAB UK, now co-leading the ad fraud working group, has <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fuk%2Ethemedialeader%2Ecom%2Fiab-uk-reaffirms-metas-membership-status-after-tech-giant-expelled-from-iab-sweden%2F&amp;urlhash=zwpa&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">reaffirmed</a> the “gold standard” certification to Meta, despite concerns about its practices and its <a data-test-link="" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate="" href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fiabsverige%2Ese%2Fiab-sverige-nyheter%2Fnytt-beslut-fran-iab-sveriges-styrelse-med-anledning-av-uteslutningsarende-mot-meta%2F%3Fref%3Dppc%2Eland&amp;urlhash=WxB-&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block">expulsion</a> from IAB Sweden for failing to meet ad fraud standards.</p>
<p>Overall, the UK approach remains uncertain—both regarding a centralised consent framework and broader adtech reform. Much will depend on which way the political winds will flow. What is clear, however, is that momentum for regulating adtech is building on both sides of the channel. Adtech Policy Watch will keep you updated as these developments unfold</p>
