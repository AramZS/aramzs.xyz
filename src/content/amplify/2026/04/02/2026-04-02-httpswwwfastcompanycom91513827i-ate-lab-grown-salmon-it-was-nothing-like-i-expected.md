<p>What do you do if you want to eat fish, but you hate the idea of harming wild animals?</p>
<p>Or if you’d like a nice lox and bagel, but you’re concerned about mercury and microplastics—or the broader climate risks of industrial fishing. What are your options?</p>
<p>One San Francisco startup has an answer: Grab cells from a salmon, grow them in giant tanks in a lab-like setting filled with a warm bath of nutrients that mimic the inside of a real fish, and then coax them onto veggie-based scaffolds to form a piece of premium fish that’s never touched an ocean.</p>
<p>That’s the vision driving <a href="https://www.wildtypefoods.com/">Wildtype</a>, a lab-grown fish company based in San Francisco’s trendy Dogpatch neighborhood. I stopped by, met Wildtype’s cofounder Dr. Aryé Elfenbein, and tried some of the company’s lab-grown salmon firsthand.</p>
<figure><img alt="" sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, (max-width: 1023px) calc(100vw - 160px), 600px" src="https://images.fastcompany.com/image/upload/f_webp,q_auto,c_fit,w_1024/wp-cms-2/2026/03/i-1-91513827-i-ate-lab-grown-salmon-it-was-nothing-like-i-expected.jpg"/><figcaption>[Photo: Thomas Smith]</figcaption></figure>
<p>From the outside, Wildtype’s headquarters look like a nondescript industrial building. The only identifying mark is a stylized, W-shaped sign.</p>
<p>Inside, though, it more closely resembles a high-end sushi restaurant.</p>
