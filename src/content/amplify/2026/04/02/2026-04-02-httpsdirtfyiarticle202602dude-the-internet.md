<p>In the penultimate episode of<i> Halt and Catch Fire</i>, the team at a fictional web-indexing startup called Comet watches a commercial for the Internet. Or, more specifically, a commercial for what they might be able to find on the Internet. The ad, a paragon of 90s diversity culture, features different people from all walks of life describing things they’re looking for.</p>
<p>“Supplies,” someone says. “Shoes,” says another. “For the meaning of life, bruh” a hippie holding a bongo cries out. Different voices all saying “I’m looking for” blend together over stock video footage of galloping horses, the universe, and a newborn baby, before the ad cuts to a male college student who grins at the camera and says, “Dude, the Internet.”</p>
<p>The year is 1994. Netscape is about to launch the Netscape Navigator browser with Yahoo! as a built-in component of its toolbar. The Internet was a year out from the end of its last remaining anti-commercial guardrail. The possibilities were endless; the future was here.</p>
<p>This <i>Halt and Catch Fire </i>episode aired on October 14, 2017. By then, the Internet had lost its utopian sheen. The<a href="https://www.splcenter.org/resources/extremist-files/alt-right/"> “alt-right”</a> had gained mainstream awareness for its white nationalism through strategic uses of online forums and economic anxieties about the rise of platform-based gig work had reached the <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/proof-of-a-gig-economy-revolution-is-hard-to-find-1437932539">upper</a> echelons of <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2015/06/18/business/uber-contests-california-labor-ruling-that-says-drivers-should-be-employees.html">politics</a>. And yet, despite beating a retro drum of hopefulness, <i>Halt and Catch Fire </i>struck a chord. Critics hailed it as one of the <a href="https://www.thrillist.com/entertainment/nation/amc-halt-and-catch-fire-best-show-of-the-decade">best</a> <a href="https://www.vox.com/culture/2019/12/17/20992510/best-tv-2010s-game-of-thrones-fleabag-emily-todd-vanderwerff">shows</a> of <a href="https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/lists/10-best-tv-shows-decade-1258464/">the</a> <a href="https://time.com/5722419/best-tv-shows-2010s-decade/">decade</a>. The delta between reality and fiction was its value, many of them wrote. During its AMC run from 2014 to 2017, the show provided an alternate history to live in—one where a person could still believe that the whole point of tech was to connect people, not alienate them from each other.</p>
<p>At the time, critics mostly took for granted <i>Halt and Catch Fire</i>’s greater value: it’s an excellent television show that grew in sophistication over multiple seasons, isn’t based on existing IP, and isn’t a cookie-cutter genre exercise. The encroachment of the tech industry into Hollywood itself has potentially made prestige television shows like <i>Halt and Catch Fire </i>one of the television industry’s last products that can stand the test of time. Firstly, because the quality of television has cratered in the past decade. Secondly, by focusing its narrative on the tech industry itself, <i>Halt and Catch Fire’s </i>staying power has only increased. The story it tells still has something to say about our <a href="https://dirt.fyi/article/2026/02/the-feeling-of-the-old-world-fading-away">present-day reality</a>.</p>
<p>The show tells the story of the Silicon Prairie and Silicon Valley in the 80s and early 90s, focusing on the careers and ambitions of a handful of hardware and software engineers, a restless ideologue, and the unsuspecting people who got caught up in their dreams. The creators of the show made a point to avoid revolving its narrative around the Steve Jobses and Bill Gateses of the world. Instead of retreading hagiographic stories, then, <i>Halt and Catch Fire</i> wove a more relatable and interesting one, wherein the core team experienced false starts, genuine breakthroughs, and internal competition in conversation with mainstream tech success stories: i.e. the laptop, Apple’s Macintosh, online credit card payments.</p>
<blockquote><p>Being online is the narrative telos of the series, even before the characters know what the Internet is. </p></blockquote>
<p>Being online is the narrative telos of the series, even before the characters know what the Internet is. When the protagonists first read the research paper that describes the World Wide Web at the end of the third season, the plot transforms from that of a period piece to that of an origin story. This is <i>the whole point</i>, according to Joe, the aforementioned restless ideologue and the show’s oracle, the one who, at nearly every juncture, bets on the accurate future, but the wrong product. The idea is that we, as viewers, have always known that being online is the whole point.</p>
<p>It isn’t until the final episode in 2017 that being online (in the world of the show) had ceased to hold the propulsive charge it held for the whole series and had finally become commonplace. The next frontier, as hinted by the last scene of the show, would instead be a world where digital connectedness extends far beyond the desktop and modem—it would be a world where the Internet was everywhere.</p>
<p>2014 to 2017 may be considered a watershed era for <a href="https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2019/07/29/americans-have-become-much-less-positive-about-tech-companies-impact-on-the-u-s/">a shift in public opinion</a> about the wave of consumer tech companies that came to define the resurgent industry post-dot-com bubble. Gamergate exposed new campaign tactics from the darkest corners of forums and social media, the <i>Wall Street Journal </i>investigated the scam at the root of Theranos, Travis Kalanick left Uber after a string of sexual harassment claims at his company, to name a few prominent tech stories of the era. Trump entered the White House for the first time to the shock of pundits and much of the public, in no small part because of his campaign’s ability to leverage Facebook.</p>
<p>This same era also included the rise of streaming television. When <i>Halt and Catch Fire</i> aired, it rode the coattails of the contemporary golden age of television (which lasted from <a href="https://theankler.com/p/hollywood-golden-age-prestige-tv-1999-2016">1999 to 2016</a> or <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Television_(2000s%E2%80%932023)">2023</a>, depending on who you ask), which had primed viewers to seek out longform narrative complexity on their television screen. And AMC, specifically, was leveraging its status as the small network that produced the cultural juggernauts <i>Mad Men,</i> <i>Breaking Bad, </i>and <i>The Walking Dead. </i></p>
<p>But the cracks in the television industry were already starting to show when <i>Halt and Catch Fire </i>was airing. Netflix premiered <i>House of Cards</i>, its first bid at original programming, in 2013, and by 2016 Netflix and Amazon were <a href="https://variety.com/2016/digital/features/amazon-studios-movies-marketing-sxsw-1201724314/">spending billions of dollars</a> on original content. In 2019, Apple, HBO, and Disney announced their own streaming platforms. Other legacy television networks and studios would follow suit. Years of growing programming glut across networks, basic and premium cable, and streamers would turn into a deluge; by 2022, <a href="https://deadline.com/2024/02/peak-tv-over-original-series-drop-2023-1235693814/">600 scripted series</a> would premiere, over double the already-high 288 series in 2012.</p>
<blockquote><p><i>Halt and Catch Fire</i> got in under the wire. The biggest legacy of the years of free-flowing streaming money isn’t one of quality expansion; it’s one where shows began to air and disappear in the blink of an eye.</p></blockquote>
<p><i>Halt and Catch Fire</i> got in under the wire. The biggest legacy of the years of free-flowing streaming money isn’t one of quality expansion; it’s one where shows began to air and disappear in the blink of an eye. The growth-no-matter-the-cost economics of tech meant these companies would spend any amount of money to get a deeper bench of original series into their online libraries, the result of which lessened the possibility for any individual show to break through.</p>
<p>From the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_2022_American_television_debuts">2022 programming peak</a>, for example, some shows have increased viewership and cultural relevance, like <a href="https://deadline.com/2025/08/the-gilded-age-season-3-finale-ratings-series-high-record-1236487348/"><i>The Gilded Age</i></a><i>, </i><a href="https://deadline.com/2025/02/severance-ratings-season-2-apple-most-watched-series-1236294760/"><i>Severance</i></a><i>, </i>and <a href="https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/tv/tv-news/summer-i-turned-pretty-season-3-70-million-viewers-1236396809/"><i>The Summer I Turned Pretty</i></a>, while the rest of that year’s slate is riddled with big swings that either lost relevance, never obtained it to begin with, or quickly lost the opportunity to do so with an early cancellation: Amazon’s <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Rings_of_Power"><i>The Lord of the Rings: Rings of Power</i></a> or <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_League_of_Their_Own_(2022_TV_series)"><i>A League of Their Own</i></a><i>, </i>HBO’s <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winning_Time:_The_Rise_of_the_Lakers_Dynasty"><i>Winning Time: The Rise of the Lakers Dynasty</i></a>, Hulu’s <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_I_Met_Your_Father"><i>How I Met Your Father</i></a> or <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bear_(TV_series)"><i>The Bear</i></a>, Showtime’s <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Pumped_(TV_series)"><i>Super Pumped</i></a>, for example. What counts as good, and what counts as popular, is all relative.</p>
<p>The end of <i>Halt and Catch Fire</i> looked towards a future where you can be online everywhere. The outcome of that future has proven to be more aggressive; it’s one where everything becomes tech. And in a world where everything becomes tech, all television becomes content. Streaming now takes up <a href="https://www.nielsen.com/news-center/2025/streaming-reaches-historic-tv-milestone-eclipses-combined-broadcast-and-cable-viewing-for-first-time/">half of all television viewership</a>, and the biggest competitor of the bunch is not Netflix or Disney but Google’s YouTube.</p>
