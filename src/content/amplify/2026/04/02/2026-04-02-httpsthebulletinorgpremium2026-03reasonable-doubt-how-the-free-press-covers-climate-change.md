title: Reasonable doubt? How The Free Press covers climate change
---
<p>In January 2021, after firing herself from the <em>New York Times</em> opinion page, political commentator and former book review editor Bari Weiss launched a Substack newsletter called Common Sense (Weiss 2020). In her original pitch, she described a world that “has gone mad,” in which, “Science is at the mercy of politics. Identity trumps ideas. In the name of progress, art is erased and history is rewritten” (Weiss 2021). Five years later, that statement seems remarkably prescient—though probably not in the way Weiss meant it.</p>
<p>Common Sense, since rechristened <em>The Free Press</em>, grew into a full-fledged media organization that was acquired last year by Paramount Skydance for $150 million. The deal also installed Bari Weiss as the editor-in-chief of CBS News. At the time, <em>The Free Press</em> had 1.5 million subscribers, 170,000 of those paid (Paramount 2025).</p>
<p>The publication’s most recent mission statement says that it prioritizes “stories that are ignored or misconstrued in the service of an ideological narrative” and “provocations from those thinking outside the lines” (Free Press 2026). But there is an innate risk in basing a journalistic enterprise on provocative contrarianism: That you miss a significant part of the truth.</p>
<p>One place this risk can be seen clearly is in <em>The Free Press</em>’s climate coverage. Taking a contrarian stance on climate change—an issue on which there is overwhelming scientific consensus that the Earth is rapidly warming, that the warming is primarily due to greenhouse gas emissions from human activities, mostly from burning fossil fuels, and that climate change could bring about significant environmental, social, and economic upheaval—almost inevitably will lead to publication of opinion and analysis that runs contrary to scientific consensus (NASA 2026). And to the public interest.</p>
<p>Take a look at some of the climate change coverage in <em>The Free Press</em>:</p>
<p>“I Thought Climate Change Would End the World. I Was Wrong.” (Nordhaus 2025b)</p>
<p>“I Helped Make Standing Rock Go Viral. Now I Regret It.” (Biggers 2024)</p>
<p>“I Left Out the Full Truth to Get My Climate Change Paper Published.” (Brown 2023)</p>
<p>These headlines, and the stories that follow, have elements in common: First, they appeal to the authority of authenticity. The authors align themselves with a specific in-group: climate expert, scientist, activist, influencer, or environmentalist. Once the author has assumed the mantle of group representative, he or she proceeds to undermine, denigrate, or renounce the efforts of that group. The authors are positioned as valiant free-thinkers, bravely pushing back on groupthink. But for readers, these performative mea culpas may confirm their own biases against climate science or environmentalism; if someone on the “other side” of an issue says they’re wrong, it satisfies a desire for schadenfreude.</p>
<p>A detailed look at two of these articles, both authored by people connected to The Breakthrough Institute, illustrates the general thrust of climate coverage in <em>The Free Press</em>.</p>
<p><strong>“I Thought Climate Change Would End the World. I Was Wrong.”</strong></p>
<p>Ted Nordhaus is the cofounder and president of The Breakthrough Institute, a California-based think tank that promotes technological solutions to human and environmental problems; for the problem of climate change, that means vigorous support for nuclear power and carbon capture and storage. Breakthrough is explicitly ecomodernist; I would describe its philosophy as techno-optimism, or the belief that human and environmental salvation will come through innovation and economic development. It’s the kind of organization that will spend more than a decade calling for investment in new energy technologies and infrastructure and then, after the passage of the Inflation Reduction Act, lavish 4,600 words on explaining all the ways the act was flawed (Nordhaus 2023).</p>
<p>In his October 2025 essay for <em>The Free Press</em>, Nordhaus claims he once thought “that if the world kept burning fossil fuels at current rates, catastrophe was virtually assured.” To demonstrate this, he quotes the book he co-authored with Michael Shellenberger<a href="https://thebulletin.org/premium/2026-03/reasonable-doubt-how-the-free-press-covers-climate-change/#_edn1">[1]</a> in 2007, <em>Break Through</em>:</p>
<p>“[T]he heating of the earth will cause the sea levels to rise and the Amazon to collapse and, according to scenarios commissioned by the Pentagon, will trigger a series of wars over basic resources like food and water.”</p>
<p>Now, “I no longer believe this hyperbole,” Nordhaus writes. “At the time, I, like most climate experts, thought that business-as-usual emissions would lead to around five degrees of warming by the end of this century. That assumption was never plausible. It assumed high population growth, high economic growth, and slow technological change. But fertility rates have been falling, global economic growth slowing, and the global economy decarbonizing for decades.”</p>
<p>Of course, as Nordhaus acknowledges in the original version of his <em>Free Press</em> essay, global sea levels have already risen 8-9 inches since 1880, and could rise another 2-3 feet by the end of the century (Nordhaus 2025a).</p>
<p>But the Amazon rainforest is also dangerously close to collapse, violence over water resources is increasing, and studies have linked high temperatures during growing seasons to lower agricultural yields and greater conflict (Drollette 2025; Gleick 2025; Jun 2017; Sova 2023).</p>
<p>In email correspondence and a phone interview, Nordhaus dismissed this evidence, arguing that because climate change is not the sole factor behind these events, his argument still stands. “The fact that agricultural yields may not rise as fast over the next 50 years as they did over the last 50 or that conflict over water is rising for reasons mostly not having to do with climate change is a far cry from claiming that climate change will lead to resource wars over food and water,” he wrote. “Similarly, insofar as the Amazon is at risk of partial collapse, it is due to deforestation”—I would argue deforestation <em>and </em>climate change—“not rising temperatures due to increased CO2 concentrations. The desire to conflate all these things as climate change is exactly the problem I am writing about. Just ignoring that and conflating them is not a serious rebuttal.”</p>
<p>In my view, Nordhaus’s argument reflects a narrow understanding of the climate crisis, which is not now and never was happening in a vacuum. And the fact that the growth in <em>global</em> agricultural yields has slowed but continued its upward path is, I imagine, little comfort to those countries enduring regional droughts, food shortages, and increased conflict.</p>
<p>This article is not intended to be a point-by-point fact check of Nordhaus’s article (however tempting that may be). But some of the assertions in his piece are worth examination, because they illustrate a larger trend at <em>The Free Press.</em></p>
<p>When Nordhaus writes, “I, like most climate experts, thought that business-as-usual emissions would lead to around five degrees of warming by the end of this century,” he says he was referring to the 2014 Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Fifth Assessment. In its Representative Concentration Pathway 8.5, or RCP8.5, that IPCC report gave a likely global temperature increase by 2100 of 2.6 to 4.8 degrees Celsius relative to a reference period of 1986 to 2005. This would translate to an increase of 3.2 to 5.4 degrees Celsius relative to pre-industrial temperatures by the end of the 21st century.</p>

<p>But IPCC reports describe a range of possible climate outcomes, outlined in four specific scenarios, based on how countries around the world could respond to the climate crisis. RCP8.5 was always the high-end emissions scenario—a prediction of what would happen in “a dystopian future that is fossil-fuel intensive and excludes any climate mitigation policies,” as the former director of Breakthrough’s climate and energy program, Zeke Hausfather, and Norwegian climate expert Glen Peters explained in a 2020 <em>Nature</em> article (Hausfather 2020). Hausfather has explored how this unlikely scenario came to be misleadingly described by many scholars and journalists as “business as usual,” but the crucial point is that it was never the most likely trajectory (Hausfather 2019).</p>
<p>Nor was 5 degrees Celsius of warming the most likely temperature increase under that extreme scenario. The IPCC report says, “Warming above 4°C by 2081–2100 is <em>unlikely</em> in all RCPs…except for RCP8.5, where it is <em>about as likely as not</em>.” Even the most apocalyptic climate article of the period, “The Uninhabitable Earth” by David Wallace-Wells, projected only “four degrees of warming by the beginning of the next century, should we stay the present course” (Wallace-Wells 2017).</p>
<p>Saying that climate experts thought there could be 5 degrees Celsius of warming by 2100, while nominally true, is a deliberate choice by Nordhaus that reduces a complex report to a single talking point that best supports his argument. It seems designed to show climate experts in the least flattering light possible: Look how wrong they were. In doing so, Nordhaus insinuates that climate science itself is suspect, faulty.</p>
<p>Nordhaus continues: “Despite close to 1.5 degrees of warming over the last century, global mortality from climate and weather extremes has fallen by more than 96 percent on a per-capita basis.” This assertion is based on his analysis of a database of global death rates from natural disasters from 1920 to 2020, minus non-climate-related disasters.</p>
<figure><a href="https://thebulletin.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Free-Press-Graph-2.png.webp"><img alt="death rates from natural disasters" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" src="https://thebulletin.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Free-Press-Graph-2.png.webp" srcset="https://thebulletin.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Free-Press-Graph-2-1024x726.png.webp 1024w, https://thebulletin.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Free-Press-Graph-2-300x213.png.webp 300w, https://thebulletin.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Free-Press-Graph-2-768x545.png.webp 768w, https://thebulletin.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Free-Press-Graph-2-522x370.png.webp 522w, https://thebulletin.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Free-Press-Graph-2.png.webp 1430w"/></a><figcaption>Nordhaus took his statistics on climate mortality from Our World in Data, removing non-climate related disaster deaths from their data on natural disaster mortality over time. <p>However, climate-related mortality is an incredibly complicated statistic to calculate. For example, a 2024 Nature study found that the average tropical cyclone in the United States will indirectly cause 7,000 to 11,000 excess deaths in the 15 years following the storm (Young and Hsiang 2024). These excess deaths could stem from loss of housing, reduced access to essential services, increased pollution, and social and economic upheaval. Nordhaus writes that “the economic costs of climate extremes continue to rise” but ignores the fact that those economic costs can have long-lasting impacts on human health, too.</p>
<p>When I said I planned to push back on this rosy characterization, Nordhaus argued that there is better reporting on climate-related death in the modern period, and that climate-related mortality in the first half of the 20th century was probably even higher. “Ask yourself how plausible it is that excess mortality due to extreme heat (which is the primary nexus for climate change produced excess mortality) is far greater today than it was in an era before anyone had air conditioning?” he wrote.</p>
<p>Indeed, humanity has made enormous progress over the past century in preparing for and responding to natural disasters and extreme weather events. We have better weather forecasting and communication networks, so people have more time to prepare and evacuate; a better understanding of health and disease, so people are less likely to die from bacterial infections like cholera in the wake of a disaster; superior humanitarian aid networks and delivery systems. And, yes, we have air conditioning.</p>
<p>But is there not something a little absurd about making your point of comparison for climate disaster mortality an era that predates penicillin, radar, and artificial satellites?</p>
<p>Nordhaus claims to be a reformed climate doomer, but the substance of his essay encourages a conflation of “climate catastrophist” with “someone concerned about the effects of climate change” and by attempting to refute the position of the former, undermines and mocks the latter. What begins as an arguably reasonable statement—climate change won’t <em>end the world </em>by 2100—ends up being far more extreme: You don’t need to worry about climate change much at all!</p>
<p>This position is taken explicitly towards the end of the article: “[W]hat should be clear is that climate change is contributing very little to present-day disasters. This also means that the scale of anthropogenic climate change that would be necessary to very dramatically intensify those hazards is implausibly large.”</p>
<p><strong>“I Left Out the Full Truth to Get My Climate Change Paper Published”</strong></p>
<p>Although Nordhaus is not a climate scientist, he works with climate scientists like Patrick Brown, who has also contributed to <em>The Free Press </em>and is a senior fellow at The Breakthrough Institute. In this essay, Brown claims that a paper he wrote about how climate warming increases wildfire risk in California was published in a prestigious journal because he stuck to climate (Brown 2023).</p>
<p>According to Brown, researchers contort their work to fit prestigious journals’ preferred narratives about climate; journals reject articles that don’t conform to those same narratives, and media outlets only cover the most dire climate research. Together this warps the entire field and public perception of climate science.</p>
<p>As a scientist, did Brown try to test this hypothesis? Did he write and submit a more nuanced paper that factored in the ways forest management or fuel load impact wildfire behavior, before submitting his study? No. He intuited that the journal only wanted one kind of paper and delivered that document. The basis of the article is essentially a fantasy, concocted around a hypothetical paper Brown did not research, write, or submit.</p>
<p>In a Zoom interview, Brown said that since publishing his essay in 2023, he has run that experiment, submitting a paper about how wildfire adaptation can reduce wildfire intensity in California in a warming world to <em>Nature</em>, <em>Science</em>, and other “high-impact journals,” which all rejected it (Brown 2025). The article was subsequently accepted by <em>Environmental Research Letters</em>. In his <em>Free Press</em> essay, he writes that he often had to settle for publishing in less-prestigious outlets when he “deviate[d] from the formula.”</p>
<p>“I don’t think this was a one-off thing,” he told me. Rather, his opinion was formed from his personal experience over years, as a new assistant professor and researcher, and as a peer reviewer for <em>Nature</em>: “You get this feedback from doing that, and just being in the community, just kind of understanding the, you know, the themes that you’re dealing with socially. You get an understanding of the way that things work.”</p>
<p>Brown emphasizes that this article is his opinion. “I don’t think I’m making conclusions that are not justified,” he said, adding, “at the end of the day, it’s an op-ed, and I think that that’s understood from a reader’s perspective, that you’re getting someone’s opinion.”</p>
<p>As Brown points out, the most prestigious journals are also the most competitive. <em>Nature</em> accepts less than 8 percent of submitted manuscripts (Nature 2026). Most papers submitted will be rejected, and pinning a grand conspiracy about climate narratives to even a handful of rejected papers seems, to me, a stretch.</p>
<p>But let’s posit that climate-change bias at the most prestigious academic publishers is real. This still seems to me a minor quibble. Was his research into fuel reduction and wildfire intensity suppressed, censored, or blacklisted? No, it simply wasn’t published by the absolute top journals.</p>
<p>In fact, scientific journals—and media outlets—regularly publish stories about wildfire that look at risk factors beyond climate change as well as possible solutions. I know; I am a climate journalist and have used those scientific papers in my reporting. Last year, I published a feature on wildfire risk in Greece that covered the social, political, and, yes, climate factors at play (McKenzie 2025).</p>
<p>At the top of Brown’s article is a list of news articles that he asserts overplay the role of climate change in wildfires:</p>
<p>Here’s the AP: <em>Climate change keeps making wildfires and smoke worse. Scientists call it the “new abnormal.</em>”</p>
<p>And PBS NewsHour:<em> Wildfires driven by climate change are on the rise—Spain must do more to prepare, experts say</em>.</p>
<p>And <em>The New York Times</em>: <em>How Climate Change Turned Lush Hawaii Into a Tinderbox</em>.</p>
<p>And Bloomberg: <em>Maui Fires Show Climate Change’s Ugly Reach.</em></p>
<p>Two can play that game (and here, I cite the same outlets as Brown):</p>
<p>Here’s the AP: <em>Bare electrical wire and leaning poles on Maui were possible cause of deadly fires.</em></p>
<p>And PBS News Hour: <em>A look at Southern California’s wildfire response and resources.</em></p>
<p>And <em>The New York Times</em>: <em>More Americans Than Ever Are Living in Wildfire Areas. L.A. Is No Exception.</em></p>
<p>And Bloomberg: <em>The Outdoor Space Around a Home Is Key to Its Survival in Fires</em></p>
<p>Does the mainstream media cover how climate change influences wildfire risk and behavior? Yes. But as Brown acknowledges, “climate change <em>is</em> an important factor affecting wildfires over many parts of the world.” Is that the only story that the press tells about wildfire? Not at all.</p>
<p><strong>At what point does misleading become misinformation?</strong></p>
<p>These and other articles published by <em>The Free Press</em> have been seized upon by conservative and right-wing news outlets and climate deniers who decry a supposed “climate scam” (Yoder 2024). <em>Free Press</em> articles and social media posts may not constitute climate denial per se, but they provide fodder and fuel for climate denialists (or, as they are sometimes known, truthers, realists, and skeptics), buoyed by the professional credentials and subject matter expertise of their authors. This forms the basis of what some disinformation experts call “the new climate denial” (Center for Countering Digital Hate). And as a strategy in the sphere of public influence, it may be far more effective than the naïve disinformation of outright climate change denial.</p>
<p>When asked what he thinks about his article being shared by climate deniers, Nordhaus said, “I don’t actually care.” He said that too often people use the threat of climate deniers weaponizing information as a cudgel to stifle debate: “If you follow that to its logical conclusion, one can never actually challenge things that are just incorrect in the way that the environmental community and left of center political actors have described the problem.”</p>
<p>Brown expressed greater unease. “I’m not thrilled by that at all,” he said. “Anyone that wanted to use this to say that climate change is not real is wrong, and it’s not what the op-ed says.” On the other hand, he says, “climate scientists as a community are way too concerned with giving ammunition to the bad team, and it has undermined their credibility.”</p>
<p>But the reality is that these articles have been widely disseminated and used to cast doubt on climate science as a whole. They provide a veneer of respectability to and justification for the anti-science rhetoric of the Trump administration, which has singled out “the climate change religion” as a target to destroy, a “false gospel,” a “cult,” and of course, a “con job,” “scam,” and “hoax.” <em>The Free Press</em>’s climate change coverage generally reinforces that worldview.</p>
<p>For example, <em>The Free Press</em> has published two Q&amp;As with Steve Koonin, former BP scientist and a member of the Trump administration’s Climate Working Group, which produced an error-ridden climate report last summer before being disbanded (Simon 2025). In these interviews, Koonin floats the idea that climate change could be “beneficial,” says “it has become an ideology,” and asserts that “it’s simply not plausible to assert that a rise in global temperature over the next hundred years comparable to the one that happened over the past 100 years will be catastrophic” (Rowley 2025, Yoffe 2025).</p>
<p>Emily Atkin, editor-in-chief of the HEATED newsletter, has published a succinct and comprehensive overview of many well-worn fossil fuel talking points that <em>The Free Press</em> has published under the guise of brave truth-telling (Atkin 2025b, Gentile and Gupta 2025). These boil down to: “Climate impacts are not that serious”; “Fossil fuels are the only path to prosperity”; “Solutions are impossible/improbable”; and “Climate impacts are exaggerated.” The publication “proudly” claims to not be a “political monolith,” but on climate, its authors largely seem to speak as one.</p>
<p><em>The Free Press</em> may also put an editorial finger on the scale with headlines that make the articles they publish sound more inflammatory and scandalous than the actual text says. Brown said he raised concerns when his headline, “The Not-So-Secret Formula for Publishing a High-Profile Climate Change Research Paper,” was changed to “I Overhyped Climate Change to Get My Paper Published.” Brown wrote back, “I do think that ‘I overhyped climate change to get my paper published’ goes too far and is inaccurate. Ironically, using that title would be in the same vein of the issue I’m trying to shed light on in the piece. In this case, the incentives for <em>The Free Press</em> are to overhype how much climate change was overhyped in the research.” The editor responded, “your suggested headline is far too long. It’s not going to connect with our readers who don’t care about what it takes to publish a research paper in a science journal.” Brown said he did not anticipate how the final headline, specifically the phrase “full truth,” which he meant as shorthand for big picture, would be interpreted as salacious research malfeasance.</p>
<p>Now that Weiss is the head of CBS News, the question is whether a lopsided, fossil-fuel friendly perspective on climate will follow her there. Early signs are not encouraging. Shortly after Skydance Media merged with Paramount Global in August, CBS News dismantled its climate team, laying off three staffers and reassigning another, leaving just one national environmental correspondent (Atkin 2025a).</p>
<p>Recent hires may also offer a clue. In January, CBS News announced 19 new contributors, including Arthur Brooks, the former president of the American Enterprise Institute, who has said that, “Capitalism is the best thing that ever happened to the environment in the United States, because it reflects the changing set of ecological goals in the United States”; the historian Sir Niall Ferguson, who has written that he is sympathetic to the contrarian view espoused by Bjorn Lomborg and Michael Shellenberger that climate change “is a slow-moving problem that can be addressed with affordable mitigation measures, and that at least some of the drastic remedies touted by youthful activists could do much more harm than good”; and Reihan Salam, the president of the conservative think tank Manhattan Institute and a former board member of The Breakthrough Institute (CBS News 2026, Ferguson 2021, Vagel 2019). The press release also mentioned hiring Derek Thompson, the author of <em>Abundance</em> and figurehead of the Abundance Movement—which has a lot in common with Breakthrough-style ecomodernism—although Thompson later said on social media that he signed on as a CBS contributor seven years ago.</p>
<p>Weiss says it is CBS News’ job to “present people with the fullest picture—and the strongest voices on all sides of an issue—and then trust them to make up their own minds.”</p>
<p>But she also says, echoing the mission of <em>The Free Press</em>, “We need to commission and greenlight stories that will surprise and provoke,” and promised “scoops of ideas, scoops of explanation.” This suggests there will be more contrarian climate takes to come.</p>
