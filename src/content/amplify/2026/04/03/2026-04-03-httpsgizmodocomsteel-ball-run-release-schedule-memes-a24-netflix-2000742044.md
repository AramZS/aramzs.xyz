<p>Since the <a href="https://kotaku.com/jojos-bizarre-adventure-stone-ocean-netflix-binge-watch-1849846467"><em>Stone Ocean</em></a> anime quietly finished on Netflix in 2022 with little social media activity from Netflix about the release of their exclusive anime, <em>JoJo’s Bizarre Adventure</em> fans have been anxious about history repeating itself. The hope was that its seventh part, <a><em>Steel Ball Run</em></a>, would be released weekly like other seasonal anime. Instead, it seems there’s a secret third, worse outcome at play: the series releasing in “stages” through longer episodic cuts, with a <a href="https://gizmodo.com/steel-ball-run-will-return-this-year-eventually-2000739350">second episode scheduled to premiere sometime in 2026</a>, and fans are plaguing every Netflix social media post about it.</p>
<p>If you click on any <a href="https://x.com/NetflixAnime/status/2038632339954184301?s=20">Netflix Anime post on X/Twitter</a> updating fans about when new shows are releasing on the streamer each week, the comments and quote retweets of such posts are littered with a barrage of horror-core edits of<em> Steel Ball Run</em> protagonist Johnny Joestar—most notably, <a href="https://x.com/JJBAxFortnite/status/2037457521070178436?s=20">one where he’s depicted with the Mangekyo Sharingan from <em>Naruto</em></a>. The <em>SBR</em> meme image is a spoof of another meme of Chicago drill rapper <a href="https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/king-von-anti-piracy-screen-king-von-stare">King Von</a>, who is known for writing songs about killing people. You don’t need me to help you connect the dots on the relevance of <em>JoJo</em> fans spamming the meme under Netflix posts that are hyping up <em>SBR</em>‘s first episode, which cracked the <a href="https://x.com/i/status/2037454580913459357">top 10 global non-English shows on the streamer</a>, while essentially playing in fans’ faces about its lack of a release schedule. Though if we had to hand it to them, the meme is a touch clever since a whole part of <em>JJBA</em> is pop culture music references.</p>

<p>Essentially, every post sends the same message: telling Netflix they don’t care about what fans want to know and that fans are eager for updates on the next <em>SBR</em> episode. This kind of social media protest from fans makes sense (at first), as <a href="https://gizmodo.com/jojos-bizarre-adventure-steel-ball-run-release-schedule-netflix-2000736037">Netflix has been strangely vague</a> about the show’s release schedule, and even the anime’s director, Yasuhiro Kimura, told <a href="https://www.anitrendz.com/news/2026/03/20/steel-ball-run-jojos-bizarre-adventure-director-yasuhiro-kimura-talks-how-he-rode-a-horse-for-research">AniTrendz</a> he isn’t exactly sure when the next episode will air. The lack of transparency around the series has left fans especially angry, as if Netflix is intentionally killing the hype for a show that, during its weekly Crunchyroll releases, fans affectionately called “JoJo Fridays” to celebrate.</p>

<p>The whole situation has gotten so out of control that A24 has seemingly joined in on the matter, like Donald Glover walking in on a house on fire in <em>Community</em>, getting ahead of the trend by posting its own SBR meme under a Netflix post about <em>Beef</em> Season 2 of the show’s logo in the same typography as the anime’s. Naturally, the only thing the meme accomplished was fanning the flames of an already aggravated beehive, leading outraged <em>JJBA</em> fans to pestering other fandoms that have nothing to do with whatever David Production and Netflix have going on with <em>SBR</em>‘s release.</p>

<p>To be honest, even as a <em>JoJo</em> fan, the meme trend has gotten annoying really fast. Not just because it’s showing up everywhere, but particularly when its fans hijack the hype for weekly-releasing shows like <em>Dorohedoro</em>. It’s pretty much the evil eye, with <em>JJBA</em> fans upset that <a href="https://gizmodo.com/dorohedoro-brings-more-chaos-with-season-2-in-april-2000731068"><em>Dorohedoro</em></a> has a weekly release on Netflix when their show doesn’t, not taking into account that it has a lot to do with the show sharing streaming rights with Crunchyroll, in a similar fashion to <em>Dan Da Dan</em>. Regardless, it’s led to <em>Dorohedoro</em> fans feeling pretty confused being thrown into a fandom war about release schedules despite the fact that their show has had an even longer journey to get a second season on account of animation studio <a href="https://gizmodo.com/netflix-mappa-deal-ai-subtitling-2000712454">Mappa</a> being busy animating <a href="https://gizmodo.com/chainsaw-man-assassins-arc-mappa-2000702280"><em>Chainsaw Man</em></a>, <a href="https://gizmodo.com/jujutsu-kaisen-keeps-looking-so-damn-good-2000713456"><em>Jujutsu Kaisen</em></a>, and <a href="https://gizmodo.com/attack-on-titan-last-attack-compilation-film-2000488240"><em>Attack on Titan</em></a> in the six-year interim of its first season hitting Netflix.</p>
