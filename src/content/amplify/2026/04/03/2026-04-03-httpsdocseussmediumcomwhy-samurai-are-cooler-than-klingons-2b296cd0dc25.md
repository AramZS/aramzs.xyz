<p>Before this, I used to climb cliffs, drive down to Southern Kansas and fly rockets, go skiing every once in a while. I’ve been whitewater rafting, ATV driving, hiked further than most, and I <em>loved</em> it. I’ve slept in a ruined cabin at a long-abandoned Colorado silver mine, summited peaks, got a scholarship so I could get my pilot’s license, done a million things.</p>
<p>I don’t get to do that anymore, except in games.</p>
<p>The truth is that I am tired.</p>
<p>My health, at the best of times, is not great — the best way I can describe it is like experiencing the fatigue that came with the worst flu you’ve ever had, all dry heaves and shivers. Now imagine parts of your body contracting when they shouldn’t — nerves misfire, bam, everything hurts.</p>
<p>It’s a disease influenced by a specific gene, as I understand it, something called cytochrome p450. My body does not get rid of the things it should — like benzene — and does get rid of things it shouldn’t — like magnesium. I am in pain all the time. I am tired all the time, not a usual 30-something adult “I’m tired,” but a kind of bone-tiredness that I can only equate to that one time I canoed around 50 miles, including through a storm that required us to bail our canoe out while also paddling desperately looking for a campsite with some family members up in the Boundary Waters.</p>
<p>We finally found a campsite — climbed up a steep, muddy incline with half a week’s worth of supplies — and passed out from exhaustion around 1 PM. I woke up something like 9 PM. It was pitch black outside. I think my uncle was awake. I wandered off into the trees and took a piss. Went back to my tent. Passed out again, don’t think I woke until sunlight the next day.</p>
<p>I feel exhausted like that all the time now.</p>
<p>I don’t get to go out and have the adventures I want anymore. Sometimes I sit there and look at a Moza AB9 setup — that’s a force feedback joystick — and know I’d need something like a thousand dollars to pay for the whole thing, just to remember what it was really like to fly planes. I loved it. It was everything to me.</p>
<p>But I got sick and I lost it all.</p>
<p>And other people made it worse.</p>

<p>One of the oldest forms of discrimination in the world — quite likely the oldest — was against the disabled. People like me, whose bodies have betrayed them, whose lives are already worse than most because of that simple fact, get <em>punished</em> for having dysfunctional, extremely painful lives by people who wish we weren’t so inconvenient to them. Building owners added wheelchair ramps because the government had to force them to.</p>
<p>As for me, I have been screamed at, physically and verbally assaulted, forced into places with toxic chemicals, lied to, stolen from, lied about, told “God made you this way,” told “You must have done something, [person] wouldn’t just do that to someone,” in response to being assaulted for… sitting at my desk doing data entry. I have even been hospitalized, and had to beg for help.</p>
<p>I’ve worked sixteen hour days and been threatened with pay cuts for it not being enough. I’ve been hurt by so many people in so many ways. When I asked a friend why people treated me this way, the simple answer was “You’re disabled. People think you’re too weak to fight back.”</p>
<p>And it’s getting worse as I have to cook, clean, take care of myself and my living space, run a company where I can make sure no one will ever discriminate against disabled people, and direct a video game. And that’s not enough to cover my medical care, so I have to write articles like this, rattle the tip jar, and hope people can help me keep my head above water. I’d kill for a break, but I’m thousands of dollars in medical care away from getting that.</p>
<p>Games are my refuge.</p>
<p>People ask how I can finish so many games in a year, well, this is how. I’m disabled. I can’t do all the things you can. But I can sit here on my couch and play games sometimes. Other times, even that’s not enough.</p>
<p>I can’t climb mountains anymore, but I can boot up a game and go climb them. I can remember what it was like to stand there at the top of a mountain peak overlooking Baldwin Lake and just… feeling the air. It was a beautiful, cool, cloudy day, and I could <em>feel</em> it in a way games can never really match, but… at the same time, games are the only way I can even come close to approximating that feeling again, because games can provide a place to go, explore, feel, experience in ways my body will not allow me to do anymore.</p>
<p>The tips from articles like these? They help offset the process. Last year, I made some significant headway towards paying off the medical debt I’ve accrued, but I’m not there yet. It stresses me out, knowing I’m still far away from where I need to be, and knowing I’m here in part because people saw I was disabled, and decided to make it even more of a problem for me to make life easier for themselves.</p>
<p>Playing games is the <em>only way</em> other than writing fiction to make the pain go away.</p>
<p>And one of my favorite games in the entire world to play has been Ghost of Tsushima, because the world it creates — one with that rich, natural splendor that I loved exploring so dearly in my teens — is a spectacular place to be.</p>
<p>No one ever really talks about it, but I also think the game owes a great debt to the Chinese films of Zhang Yimou, particularly Hero, which looks like this:</p>

<p>The developers included a “Kurosawa mode” in the first game, a stripped down, black and white homage to the filmmaker Akira Kurosawa, and they touted their partnership <a href="https://ew.com/gaming/ghost-of-tsushima-akira-kurosawa-estate-cinematic-mode/">with the Kurosawa estate</a> as a big part of the reason why.</p>
<p>Akira Kurosawa is one of the world’s greatest filmmakers. I say ‘is,’ even though he’s dead, because, let’s be real, very few people have compared to him. When the greatest of the greats, Stalker director Andrei Tarkovsky, says he’s a massive fan, that should tell you something. When you think about how everyone from George Lucas to Martin Scorsese cites him as an influence, it gives you a sense of his importance.</p>
<p>…and then you watch his movies, and by god, you <em>get </em>it. Not just Rashomon or Seven Samurai or Hidden Fortress, the films everyone knows and understandably reveres, but the <em>really</em> good shit, like Stray Dog (which influenced Disco Elysium’s whole ‘missing gun’ plot) or Ikiru.</p>

<p>This guy saw <em>people</em> as they really were, as living, breathing, muddy, courageous, fearful, spiteful, happy, miserable, and <em>vibrant</em>. He saw it all and he depicted it all; it’s the humanity that defines his films.</p>
<p>Kurosawa mode does not look like much more than a black and white filter (though, notably, they cranked up the wind speed in the trees and ostensibly looked at the color curves from older film); it feels as respectful to Kurosawa’s legacy as Brian Herbert’s novels are to Frank Herbert’s Dune.</p>
<p>Sure, yeah, the estate owns the filmmaker’s likeness, but it doesn’t mean they <em>get</em> it any more than the team, which initially just had a black and white mode that they slapped the Kurosawa name on.</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">As we got closer and closer to making that a reality, we were like, ‘What do we call this special mode that we created, this black-and-white throwback?’ We threw out a bunch of different words and we thought, ‘What would be awesome would be if we could call it Kurosawa Mode.’</p></blockquote>
<p>Skill and perspective are not heritable traits. They’re things you have to learn, things you have to choose to embrace, things you must actively incorporate into your life every day, lest you become a billionaire who suggests introspection is an evil ‘modern’ invention because you’d rather live <a href="https://bsky.app/profile/moreperfectunion.bsky.social/post/3mh6vizvrgk2e">no thoughts, head empty</a>.</p>
<p>When I look at Kurosawa mode — or the more recent “Miike mode” and “Watanabe mode” from Ghost of Yotei — I don’t see something that looks at the creators with any intent. Watanabe mode, for instance, predominantly changes the soundtrack to be more like Nujabes’ work (if you’ve heard about lo-fi hip hop beats to study to, you’ve been listening to a <em>lot</em> of Nujabes). Supposedly, Watanabe himself oversaw what little music there is in the mode, and he did collaborate with Nujabes on Samurai Champloo before the former’s untimely passing, but… it doesn’t actually <em>feel</em> like a Watanabe story, and it barely feels like it has Nujabes’ music.</p>
<p>So I often find myself running around these stellar worlds that Sucker Punch has made, having a great time slicing dudes up (that said, people die so much slower in Ghost of Yotei that it feels way, way worse to engage in combat than in Ghost of Tsushima), exploring things, doing quests — there’s some genuinely great writing throughout both games — and having a grand old time.</p>
<p>I rarely replay games; if I do, then that is one of the highest bits of praise I can heap on it. I have replayed Ghost of Tsushima, an open world game, a major time commitment, and played every single quest I could find, clearing the entire map, because I do, truly, love the experience of playing that game.</p>
<p>But… I can’t… help but find myself <em>constantly</em> frustrated by the game’s attempts at authenticity. If there is an uncanny valley for authenticity, well, <em>Ghost of Tsushima</em> is that.</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">dishonorable discharge</h2>
<p>A warrior, who the audience connects with, perhaps because he is young or because he is an outsider introducing himself to a culture he is unfamiliar with, finds himself as a member of or participant in a traditional warrior culture. He is expected to do some violent war stuff, but he has to follow some weird, stupid rules, and if he messes any of it up, well, that’s a quick way to losing his honor, and that’s bad, because in a warrior culture like this, <em>nothing</em> matters more than honor. <em>Nothing</em>.</p>
<p>Soon, our protagonist realizes that strict adherence to his own moral code and that culture’s honor-obsession puts him at odds with its more conservative elements. Maybe he performs a guerilla attack on an enemy base, dishonorably saving thousands of lives. Never mind that he did, in fact, save thousands of lives, because he did it <em>dishonorably</em>. He did the right thing, but he <em>broke the code</em>.</p>
<p>So our fella, being a pragmatic new hero for a new age, finds himself battling against his desire to remain part of this culture that he’s connected to <em>and also, like, saving lives and shit</em>. He wants to be a good person, but these traditions! They hold him back! If only he could be something different… if only… he could be something other than a samurai…</p>
<p>If only ninjas were real.</p>
<p>As you may be aware, ninjas were in fact real. I mean, maybe they still are, I don’t know (I watched Ninja Assassin in theatres when it came out so I’m just sayin’…), but like, historically, we know ninjas existed, and if samurai were supposed to be these super honorable dudes who’d do whatever the emperor ordered and literally <em>end their own lives</em> if they somehow breached their rigid honor code, ninjas… well, I mean… dude, they’d stab people in the back. They were sneaky. They <em>definitely</em> weren’t what you would call honorable.</p>
<p>So, riddle me this one, Batman:</p>
<p>How come any time a Westerner writes a story about Japan, they always gotta bring honor into it and write that archetypal story of “a guy confronts a system that doesn’t make sense and ultimately rebels against it” when it’s got no basis in reality? Where did this perception come from? And why are Westerners determined to keep writing what’s honestly a really goddamn boring story?</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">Does art say things? (how we can learn falsehoods)</h2>
<p>I wrote an essay a while back that was broken into two parts, called “<a data-discover="true" href="https://docseuss.medium.com/should-art-say-things-5229ab9c8909">should art say things?</a>” and “<a data-discover="true" href="https://docseuss.medium.com/does-art-say-things-2b55f293cb8c">does art say things?</a>” The premise was simple: a lot of people think all art conveys something “political,” but to some people, when they hear “is art political?” they hear “does art convey a specific idea of governance?” while other people hear “does art express your worldview?”</p>
<p>My general theory was that this is a communication error that could be resolved if the people who argue “everything is political” would realize that the subject would be understood much easier if it was framed as “everything contains, to some extent, the author’s worldview.”</p>
<p>If I write a story where known piece of shit King Leopold II of Belgium, possibly the worst human being who has ever lived due to his almost singlehandedly fucking up Africa for everyone who lived there, gets shot in the face, you can <em>probably</em> assume that in my worldview, King Leopold II is not a good person.</p>
<p>A lot of dumb people think that video games make people violent because people do violent things in games. They intuit this to mean that there’s a kind of ‘training’ that happens; that a person who plays games will be deadened to violence or something. Instead, all research on the subject suggests the opposite: people who play violent games are <em>less</em> violent people. Maybe it’s catharsis, I don’t know, but the key takeaway here is that art does influence you, but it doesn’t influence you <em>in the way you might think</em>.</p>
<p>There’s primarily one dude from the University of Iowa, Craig Anderson, who is behind a lot of the studies on video games and violence. He’s claimed, repeatedly, that he’s <em>conclusively</em> proven that video games cause violence. He is, of course, <a href="https://www.techdirt.com/2010/03/02/long-time-video-game-critic-claims-conclusive-evidence-that-violent-video-games-cause-aggression-conclusive-except-that-it-isnt/">completely full of shit</a>.</p>
<p>You exist in a living, breathing world. You are a human being, which means that you learn, in part, by seeing other human beings doing things. I’m no zoostronomy expert science man, but it is my understanding this is a trait shared by all primates and most social animals; we see things, we learn from them. As humans, when we see a human do something, some part of us is going to assume it’s <em>possible</em>, unless we have information to the contrary.</p>
<p>On the tee-vee, Superman just seems capable of flight, but the only way I’ve ever managed to levitate involved a leaf blower, a 2 x 4, a round bit of plywood, a carpenter’s stapler, and a shower curtain.</p>

<p>But I know I can’t fly like Superman. There is an elasticity to what is believable and what isn’t; this is determined by our lived experiences. The more we’ve lived, the more data we have to pull from, and the easier it is for us to intuit — and it is intuition — what is and is not possible.</p>
<p>So, if I see Superman flying in a movie somewhere, I’m not going to think “maybe I can fly too,” because that’s far outside the bounds of human experience. However, if I watch a movie where someone gets hit on the head, passes out, and wakes up fine, well, I <em>might</em> get the idea in my head that getting hit on the head is fine and not something that will literally kill you or leave you with permanent brain damage.</p>
<p>All those thousands of movies where someone gets hit in the head with a rifle butt, gets knocked out, and wakes up groggy later? Yeah! That’s not a thing! If you get hit so hard you get knocked out, you could just spontaneously die of brain bleed a week later.</p>
<p>If I see that trope play out in several movies, but I myself never have the experience, I might assume it’s true, just because it doesn’t appear to be questioned. If I believe it because it <em>seemed</em> plausible, and so do a lot of other people, well… now that’s what we call a “common misconception.”</p>
<p>And that’s what common misconceptions are — it’s how they spread. Memorable anecdotes, scenes, and what have you implant themselves in our brains, and in the absence of contradictory information, we just kind of assume that’s the way things work. The more easy-to-remember the information, the more easy it is to believe it’s true.</p>
<p>That’s one of the funny things about humans, we <em>love</em> to learn some weird little factoid (here’s a fun little fact: the word “factoid” is not ‘a small fact,’ it’s a <em>false idea </em>that’s been repeated so often that it’s assumed to be a fact) and share it around. The more pleasing-to-hear the sound bite, the more play it gets. Truth becomes irrelevant.</p>

<p>“The average person swallows eight spiders in their sleep in a given year” is a factoid that Lisa Holst wrote for PC Professional in 1993 as an example of how quickly misinformation spreads around the internet. She made up some ‘fake facts’ that could be spread around to show how ridiculous chain emails had gotten and bam, before she knew it, people began to think the factoid was a fact.</p>
<p>Even when we do our absolute best, humans do not fact check <em>everything</em>. If they hear something that sounds compelling and plausible enough, they will <em>believe it</em>. It’s convenient! It’s cute! It’s memorable! It’s All Bullshit.</p>
<p>But it’s also just a side effect of the way human brains learn. It’d be really difficult to function if every time you learned something new, you automatically refused to believe it and had to research it in depth to come to a conclusion. It’s okay — as a social animal — to trust other animals in your social group when they say some shit like “hey there’s a sale going on at that store you like” so you hop in the car and drive down and the sale is there. Like… most of the time you hear something basic like that, trusting it is fine.</p>
<p>But sometimes, factoids, beliefs, whatever? They get through.</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">Is it okay to be wrong about stuff? (i don’t want you to feel bad if you, in good faith, believed something untrue)</h2>
<p>Depends.</p>
<p>Generally, I think we should do our best to be right. This does not mean that if we discover we are wrong, we go “no, I have to be right, I will make everyone say that I am right even if the facts disagree.” It means if we discover we were wrong, we change <em>ourselves.</em></p>
<p>We’re human, we’re fallible, and because we like and trust people around us, we’re <em>going</em> to believe things told to us, especially if we know they were in good faith! Heck, I might be wrong about what time the doctor’s appointment I have is because an ink smudge made it hard to tell if that was an 8 or a 9 AM on my appointment card!</p>
<p>So sometimes we’re wrong about things, which sucks! It can be embarrassing! It can be costly (especially if the doctor’s office charges for no-shows). But I mean… consider the alternative to a little ego shattering.</p>
<p>If I’m a reporter and I cover a story and I get the facts wrong… maybe a politician acts on those facts. Suddenly we’ve got the government spending millions of dollars trying to track down Havana Syndrome — which, as has been conclusively proven, <strong>does not exist</strong> — instead of giving medical care to people who <em>actually need it</em>.</p>
<p>If we believe things that aren’t true, we make decisions that can impact people based on falsehood. That usually has bad results. Resources are taken away from people who deserve it and given to people who don’t. I’ve watched monsters given money while children starve, seen rich biz types get loans forgiven and then sue to stop any student loans from being forgiven too.</p>
<p>And I’ve seen racial stereotypes shared around by well-meaning people because they engaged with fiction that uncritically shared those stereotypes.</p>
<p>As a disabled person, I am <em>acutely</em> aware of how negative stereotypes can affect people. I’ve had people go out of their way, wasting their own time, to check on me and make sure I’m not ~lying~ about being disabled, because god forbid my life be hard enough as it is without someone constantly looking over my shoulder. I’ve literally given up doing things that would make me happy because I know the cost of doing them will result in people trying, actively, to make my life worse.</p>

There are people out there, right now, with way more social media clout than me publicly saying they can abuse me as a treat, because they’ve convinced themselves that I’m an acceptable target. I’m disabled, I’m exhausted from all the extra work I have to do to stay alive, and people with comparatively easy lives got nothing better than to do but try to make my life worse than it already is. My already miserable life gets made more miserable by desperate able-bodied shitheads playing off of dated tropes to try and feel good about themselves.</p>

<p>You do not need to make people’s lives worse through your own ignorance. We need to get things right because these things affect <em>people</em>. <p>Most people who are ableist to me do so accidentally. Yes, there are people who actively try to harm me <em>because</em> I am disabled, but there are way more who do harm me because of their lack of understanding, often without realizing they’re doing it.</p>
<p>So it’s important, when we write fiction, to be thoughtful about who we’re depicting and how. Are we just repeating other stories we’ve seen before? Because a copy of a copy of a copy isn’t necessarily rooted in truth, and it definitely isn’t good. When you make fiction, particularly about <em>real people in history</em>, you don’t want to just copy the copies, y’know? You can only xerox something so many times before it becomes an illegible mess.</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">Cause And Effect (“can believing falsehoods actually be <em>that</em> bad?”)</h2>
<p>Throughout history, a lot of people have been put through some alarming bullshit because a lot of other people have believed <em>incorrect</em> things about them.</p>
<p>Now, normally, when I write these pieces, I write them to an audience that I presume to be an expert in <em>something</em>. I assume that everyone reading these pieces are pretty smart, maybe even smarter than me, but they might not know the things I know, so instead of high-tech computer jargon, I do my best to explain everything with a clarity that, hopefully, is never condescending.</p>
<p>I respect you, person who’s reading this, because I’m assuming you’re a neurosurgeon who’s saved ten thousand lives, and you’re interested in knowing about how games work now, maybe so you can do some more neurosurgeon stuff, but maybe you’re totally new to the idea of game terminology, so you can follow along if I leave out the jargon.</p>
<p>But, uh,</p>
<p>When I’m writing this section, I keep hearing, in my head, the little voice of some of the more annoying “why should I cooperate with anyone?” people I’ve known over the years, and I can’t help but feel like I need to write to them for a bit, so please, Friendly Neurosurgeon, don’t take this next bit too personally.</p>
<p>“Why would I care if someone lies about other people?”</p>
<p>Well, if we live in a society where there are no consequences for damaging the lives of other people, what is to stop anyone who doesn’t like you from trying to cause problems for you? There are all people out there more powerful than us — even Elon Musk, once the richest man in the world, lost a great deal of his power <em>because</em> there were people who could, and did, have the ability to impact his personal wealth. We all — literally all of us — gotta answer to somebody, as much as we’d love to be completely independent actors, answerable only to ourselves.</p>
<p>When it comes to racism, imagine someone lying about you in a way you can’t even refute, because the lies were made before you were even born, and a not insignificant number of people will treat you based on the little factoids (like we said above — things that may sound true, but aren’t) they heard over the years. Imagine never getting a fair chance to express yourself, never being seen for who <em>you</em> are, because a lot of people have already made up their minds about you based on information that’s been xeroxed so heavily, it can only ever be a distortion of the truth.</p>
<p>That’s completely out of your control! People are just dicks to you for <em>no reason at all</em>! That sucks!</p>
<p>Society functions because of consensus — a society where you are at risk of losing your ability to be treated fairly because other people are able to lie about you with impunity would be a terrible society! It would be impossible to live in!</p>

<p>So, when you think “how does this affect me?” consider it like this: for a society to treat you fairly, it must treat all its participants as if they are of equal worth, and things like racism or ableism are about poisoning society so some people <em>don’t</em> get that fairness. If we let it happen to one group, who knows when society might let it happen to you? Are you <em>really</em> valuable enough to the powerful in society for them to ensure your absolute safety and well-being? Are you completely without enemies or people who don’t like you? The best protection is found in your solidarity with other people.</p>
<p>I’ve seen people who did everything right — treated people kindly and fairly, bent over backwards to do the right thing — and still, the second it was possible to fuck them over, people fucked them over. Sometimes it was jealousy, other times greed, other times for fear of getting caught doing something bad on their own and needing a scapegoat. No matter how good a person you are, there are people who will try to cause you problems. The only thing that can protect you is a fair society.</p>
<p>So, even if you don’t believe in justice, you do, at least, have to consider that a society that can be bad and unjust to you will be; all you can do is fight for a more just society — helping other people helps you too.</p>
<p>Even if we toss out things like morals or ethics or being a reasonable person who cares about making sure no one is needlessly suffering, if we just look at the Polyanna-naive idealistic libertarian “everyone should be able to act how they want” worldview, we realize that a society that allows any misconceptions about any of its members is a society where misconceptions could eventually be applied to you too.</p>
<p>There are, unfortunately, bad actors who will attempt to sublimate any system to your liking. You might just want to be doing your own thing, but there are people who <em>will</em> try to fuck with you because they are selfish and want power for themselves. No amount of minding your own business will stop someone from tracking their shit all over your apartment if you let ‘em.</p>
<p>The unequal application of justice will ultimately lead to you suffering an injustice yourself. So even in a purely selfish scenario, it’s <em>still </em>better to work towards a society where we say “if I don’t want to be treated like this, then I must ensure no one else is treated like this either.” There is literally no downside.</p>
<p>Should be pretty simple, but I’ve often found that people who only think about how things affect them are short-sighted and ignorant. It’s the kind of person who says “yes, let’s let the government strip rights for people I don’t like,” and then a few years later, they’re going “wait, now the government is stripping <em>my</em> rights?” because they were too fucking stupid to see that this was coming. If you are interested in being even remotely intelligent you cannot permit racism or any other kind of bigotry to exist in any form.</p>
<p>Do <em>you</em> want to end up getting screwed? No? Then it’s in your best interest to work for a society where <em>everyone</em> is treated with fairness and honesty, and not to tolerate the bullshit that comes with things like racism.</p>
<p>Bigotry can and will cause harm. And one day, you might be the person feeling that harm. Do you really want that? Nah? Then fight against that shit.</p>

<p>You can tell yourself “I’m not a bigot ,” but if you go around thoughtlessly repeating stereotypes, you can make life worse for the targets of your stereotyping; by creating false expectations about people that are divorced from reality, you can make life tangibly worse for people without even meaning to.</p>
<p>While it’s great that you didn’t <em>mean</em> to do a bad thing, you did still actually <em>do</em> it, whether it was trying to find people ‘faking’ their disability or gender because you can’t mind your own business, or write a story that repeats stories you’ve consumed without doing the proper research, <em>you can cause harm</em>, even if you don’t realize it.</p>
<p>If you do cause that harm, you have to own it, because intentional or not, it still happened. Your thoughtlessness is not an excuse, and people who excuse the harm they caused as “well I know in my heart that I’m not a bigot” are refusing to internalize their thoughtlessness and will fuck it up again.</p>
<p>It’s best not to do that.</p>
<p>So! What have we learned so far?</p>
<ol><li data-selectable-paragraph="">Fiction can have an impact on people, but it’s not usually didactic; it’s often more surreptitious. Games won’t teach you to kill, but a lot of movies might teach you that it’s cool to get punched in the head, you’ll wake up fine later. If you then punch someone in the head, thinking it’s fine, you might end up getting charged with second degree murder. “But I didn’t know he’d die” isn’t a justification for your repeating something you saw in a movie once, not knowing the <em>actual</em> results.</li><li data-selectable-paragraph="">It is our responsibility as artists not to inadvertently slip thoughtless, incorrect things into our narratives, because people might actually take the things we express as factual information, and they may operate on that information in ways that cause harm. We cannot control what other people do, but we control ourselves. Therefore, the onus is on us to ensure that, when representing real people, places, and times, we do a good job with it, and do not promote stereotypes that are inaccurate.</li><li data-selectable-paragraph="">Our lives are <em>all</em> improved by working to remove these things, because a society that tolerates any intolerance will, ultimately, cease to tolerate <em>you</em>. There is no crab bucket here; the safest you can be is living in a safe community. A person who lives alone and surrounds their house with barbed wire and searchlights is way less safe than a person who lives in a sleepy neighborhood of a hundred other people who all like living here. The safest place to be is in a group where all members are treated well.</li></ol>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">why do execs ruin game stories?</h2>
<p>Most game stories suck, but <em>why</em>? I know a lot of amazing writers in games — better writers than their peers in other fields, in fact. So… what fucks ’em up? It’s got to be more than Sturgeon’s “90% of everything is crap” law, right?</p>
<p>A young, aspiring game writer might have great taste — they can <em>see</em> that Fellini’s masterpiece La Strada hits them much harder than most video games — so even if they haven’t matured enough to understand that acclaim and quality are separate, they can still feel it in their gut, because their taste is <em>telling them</em> “I’ve never played a game this good.”</p>
<p>We could do a really deep dive into the organizational dysfunction that hampers a story’s ability to be told, how game companies first started forming, how those cultures led to certain biases, and we’d be right to do so, but that would triple the length of this essay so we won’t be getting into it beyond what we’ve said in previous essays: game writers do not get the respect they deserve, they get forced into doing impossible tasks, and it’s a miracle anything ever gets written.</p>
<p>As an example, I once wrote the bulk of a video game — some 60 sidequests — in just two weeks, without changing anything else. Couldn’t change the mission objective, the characters speaking, or even the number of lines (beyond adding one or two more lines, as I recall). The game shipped something like five months later, I believe? This was because the team didn’t have an actual writer on board and hadn’t considered the storytelling important until they realized their story just wasn’t that good.</p>
<p>There’s a reason for the idea that everyone in Hollywood has a script — it’s cause everyone thinks they can tell a story, because we all tell stories every day. But you know when you meet that guy who <em>really </em>wants to tell you about “that time my buddy and I…” and then he just completely bores the shit out of you because he failed to make you care?</p>
<p>Ever been on a phone call with someone who wants to tell you about something, but they get caught in the weeds being like “Doc, you remember Martha? She and I used to go to church two decades before you were born. She’s Charlene’s sister. You remember Charlene, right? Kinda short, grey hair, babysat you when you were three one time?” and you’re going “what the fuck does this have to do with anything what are you trying to tell me”?</p>
<p>(fun fact: I wrote Edie in Adios to match this kind of personality)</p>
<p>There’s a story about how 50 Cent’s son thought there should be a helicopter in the video game 50 Cent: Blood on the Sand, so the team was forced to put it in. It made the game worse, because a kid’s ideas aren’t exactly going to necessarily make for a great AAA video game, and it made the team feel disrespected. They weren’t being trusted to do their <em>jobs</em>. They were being second-guessed by people who knew nothing about what it takes to make a video game, and what makes video games <em>fun</em>.</p>
<p>In games, narrative designers are asked to do that <em>every day</em>. Even people who <em>like me</em> and should know to trust me by now — literally every game I’ve made has won game of the year awards and critical praise for the narratives that I wrote by myself — will criticize my work in a way that would <em>infuriate them</em> if I did it to them. If I said “write your code like this instead, it’s easy, I could do a better job,” they’d feel insulted, but they’re okay with doing it to me, <em>because they don’t respect my craft enough to understand it</em>.</p>

<p>My point is, writers don’t get a lot of respect. I’ve lived it. A lot.</p>
<p>A lot of execs will see that a story isn’t good, and instead of going “ah, the issue is because the game director doesn’t know shit about writing,” or “ah, it’s because we brought the writer in late and gave them nothing to work with,” or “we kept trying to change the writer’s writing without bothering to learn anything about how writing works or what it <em>does,</em>” they’ll go “our writers are bad, we need to bring in a Good Writer,” and every time they do this, they bring in someone from outside the medium. Their goal is to bring in someone who they’ve <em>heard of, </em>assuming acclaim translates to skill.</p>
<p>I think this is a mistake.</p>
<p>For example, while I’m great at writing dialogue, I’m not so great at descriptive prose; you wouldn’t want me to ghostwrite a novel for you, because I wouldn’t do a good job. My being an award-winning writer in one medium doesn’t mean I’m a great writer in another.</p>
<p>So you’ve got a lot of people who run things, who will go “tell a story and make it like this,” because they’ve seen a few movies, and the writers do what they’re told.</p>
<p>Let’s be clear; sometimes, the writers are the problem — plenty of Gen X types will get handed some big IP and then change it because the sarcasm generation thinks it’s got to convince you that it’s cleverer than what it’s adapting, which is how you get things like the <em>dreadful</em> Powerpuff Girls pilot or the Cowboy Bebop show.</p>
<p>Buuuuuuuut… so often, in video games, it’s the execs who are the <em>real</em> problem. They hold all the power, and therefore, ultimately bear all the responsibility. I am going to be criticizing the storytelling of Ghost of Tsushima here, but I don’t want you to think I’m blaming the writers. At the end of the day, I do not know <em>exactly who</em> called for the core plot of Ghost of Tsushima and demanded the game be themed around the idea of honor, but I do know that Ghost of Tsushima also had some absolutely excellent, well-written stuff.</p>
<p>If you’re reading this article looking for someone to blame, sorry, I don’t know who was responsible for Ghost of Tsushima’s missteps. All I know is that it made those missteps, and we <em>do</em> need to talk about them.</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph=""><strong>a cowboy movie</strong></h2>
<p>Imagine someone saying “we’re going to make a super accurate conquistador movie, it’s really accurate, we did all this research… oh yeah and uh, all our conquistadors are dressed like cowboys, by the way.”</p>
<p>That’s… something of a three hundred year gap, my friends. How’d you get there?</p>
<p>Imagine that the director of the new movie responded to you by saying something like “honestly, the 16th century armor is pretty jarring looking, it’s not what you’d expect. It’s really boxy. It doesn’t look aspirational. And we wanna make sure that what we give you is your fantasy of what being a conquistador is.”</p>
<p>So you boot up this game, and boom: Ten gallon hat, chaps, spurs, probably some tassels, that kind of thing? Six shooter, lasso… you get the idea. Except this is a story about these guys going to war with the Aztecs in 1519, and you’ve got a guy with a tin star and a handlebar moustache who says “I’m your huckleberry…” with a cheeky grin.</p>
<p>It’d feel kinda fuckin <em>weird</em>, right?</p>
<p>Well, I didn’t write that quote. It’s not a hypothetical. I tweaked the <a href="https://comicbook.com/gaming/news/ghost-of-tsushima-historically-accurate/">original</a>, which reads like this:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">Honestly, the 13th century armor is pretty jarring looking, it’s not what you’d expect. It’s really boxy. It doesn’t look aspirational. And we wanna make sure that what we give you is your fantasy of what being a wandering samurai is.</p></blockquote>
<p>Now, don’t get me wrong; historical inaccuracy can fuckin <em>rule</em>. I’ve been playing Team Ninja’s new game Wo Long, and I definitely don’t remember <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhang_Liang_(Western_Han)">Zhang Liang</a> ever gaining tentacle arms alongside a demon form at any point in human history.</p>
<p>(protip, if you want to beat him in Wo Long, get really, really good at the counter mechanic, then do nothing <em>except </em>counter him on his second phase. Assuming you’re using an Xbox controller, use the Y and B buttons when the prompt appears about half way through his health bar on the second form)</p>
<p>(yes he’s tough)</p>
<p>But here we have one of the lead developers at a big, first party Sony studio saying basically “our fans are fucking dumb and have stereotypical views of what samurai look like, so we wanted to respect that.”</p>
<p>Now, you might be going “well, Doc, I know you said racism can cause harm, but this seems kinda harmless, right?”</p>
<p>It might be! But I am establishing a point: the people at Sucker Punch play a bit fast and loose with the whole accuracy thing.</p>
<p>Back on Kurosawa for a second: what makes Kurosawa movies work isn’t “it’s black and white.” It’s the script! The blocking of the shots! The reason the black and white doesn’t look natural in the game is because it’s still color grading Ghost of Tsushima, a colorful game that looks like a Zhang Yimou movie despite being about Japan.</p>
<p>Fun fact about black and white movies: their sets looked awful because they’d come out looking better in black and white that way.</p>
<p>Check out this color and black and white picture of The Addams Family television show set. It’s clown vomit in the color version, but in black and white? It looks great. How color — like red — becomes a greyer tone in black and white filmmaking is vital to understanding what shit has to look like. It’s also why most black and white filters on games look bad; the real black and white photographers controlled lighting and color heavily so it’d look good when filmed in black and white. Ghost of Tsushima, to my knowledge, does not do this.</p>

<p>And even then, Yimou’s image control is often much stronger because he isn’t just playing with color, he’s controlling lens lengths and light to get this shot. That’s much harder to do in a real time game, because you’re not having a bunch of crew with lights moving around to change how things look, and in a video game, changing the focal length or fucking around with zooms can cause headaches — literal headaches — in the player.</p>
<p>Now, am I saying that it’s kinda weird that Sucker Punch may have built their game’s visual identity around Zhang Yimou, who is Chinese, rather than looking at Japanese movies? Hmm. I don’t think so. I think it’s okay to, say, hear a beat from one source and want to incorporate it into a different genre of music.</p>
<p>As Eliot once said:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">One of the surest of tests is the way in which a poet borrows. Immature poets imitate; mature poets steal; bad poets deface what they take, and good poets make it into something better, or at least something different. The good poet welds his theft into a whole of feeling which is unique, utterly different from that from which it was torn; the bad poet throws it into something which has no cohesion. <strong>A good poet will usually borrow from authors remote in time, or alien in language, or diverse in interest.</strong></p></blockquote>
<p>So it’s okay to fuse ideas. That’s not really authenticity. China does not look like Zhang Yimou envisions; it is a stylistic choice, and in <em>video games</em>, it’s particularly fine to do a bunch of specific, color-driven areas.</p>
<p>I mean, ever looked at the regions in Breath of the Wild or Tears of the Kingdom? Climb a tower, look around, and really think about how a lot of the areas feel like big blobs of color. That’s the desert (sandy brown), that’s the meadows (green), that’s the snowfields (blue and white), that’s the volcano area (lots of greys, blacks, and oranges), and so forth. Simple, big color theming like that does a great job of helping you navigate through the world.</p>
<p>So if I encounter a large forest full of gingko leaves or Japanese maple, I get a sense of where I am. I think the art team really knocked it out of the park, even though I do wish Zhang Yimou got mentioned more. It feels to me, as a person who loves Ghost of Tsushima and read everything I could about it when it was first releasing, that they <em>should</em> mention it, but likely refrained from doing so because it’s not Japanese, and the executive leadership was putting a <em>lot</em> of emphasis on the game’s sense of authenticity.</p>
<p>So Ghost of Tsushima may take inspiration from a lot of <em>very cool</em> sources, and I think it’s absolutely worth checking them out. For whatever reason, the publisher messaging was largely focused on “our game’s totally legit” while talking about how the team went to Tsushima for photo references or got the Kurosawa Estate to authorize their use… they were also just going “hey, yeah, so our fashion’s three hundred years out of date.”</p>

<p>But if you were telling me how you also went to Verona to get a bunch of photo references and how you have a Shakespeare mode from his estate or some shit, I would raise an eyebrow because it would come off like you’re trying to sell a game on a bunch of bullet points about its creation rather than <em>what it actually is</em>.</p>
<p>If you’re going to break from the norm, be loud and proud about it in the work itself. In fact, Akira Kurosawa is seen as The Preeminent Japanese Filmmaker by a lot of people, but they forget that some of his movies are Shakespeare adaptations: Macbeth (Throne of Blood), The Bad Sleep Well (Hamlet), and Ran (King Lear).</p>
<p>Many people have suggested that Yojimbo is based on Dashiell Hammett’s Red Harvest, due to the many plot similarities. Interestingly, he said it was another Hammett novel that inspired him — The Glass Key.</p>
<p>In other words, this kind of cultural exchange — artists recognizing other artists, taking how that art made them feel, processing it, and making art of their own with influence — is normal, fine, and good. It’s great to do something like “take Shakespeare or Hammett and turn their work into a samurai story.”</p>
<p>Getting inspired by someone and incorporating elements of their work into your own is great! Romeo + Juliet is a Shakespeare adaptation set in the modern times. Throne of Blood transposes a story about a Scottish king to Japan; it might not even be the same time period — I checked with a very knowledgeable friend; apparently, all anyone knows is that it’s before the Meiji Restoration and may be during the Sengoku period. Remember when I mentioned the colors in black and white not always matching ‘real’ colors? Yeah, they painted some of the Throne of Blood set black to match the way the fog looked in black and white.</p>
<p>So that’s fine. That’s great. We like that. Throne of Blood wasn’t entirely adapted from Shakespeare, of course. It was influenced a lot by Japanese Noh (a kind of Japanese dance-theatre). It’s pulling from disparate places to achieve a specific, desired effect. There’s nothing wrong with any of that.</p>
<p>But… If you’re going to market yourself on the concept of authenticity… then you do have to try to be authentic; if it matters to you so much that you’d go to Japan to do research to try to get the environment right, but then the costumes aren’t, you’d give us all the impression you really just wanted a nice vacation to Japan and didn’t really care about authenticity.</p>
<p>So what <em>is</em> authenticity?</p>

<p>We exist in a world that moves independent of us. It is a real, material space; you can reach out and touch things. Events have happened prior to your coming and they will continue happening prior to your going.</p>
<p>There is a separate layer of existence that appears to be, insofar as we know, entirely human. That is, the way we think <em>about</em> things impacts what we <em>choose</em> to do, and the things we choose to do have a resulting impact on the world around us.</p>
<p>Think of it like this: three thousand years ago, a man in a now-dead religion (one practiced by no one and studied mostly by scholars and ancient history enjoyers) carves a statue to a god. He does so because something favorable had happened, and he believed this was the god’s doing. So he carves this statue and he places it there, and he says “I place you there so that all will know of your greatness.”</p>
<p>Three thousand years later, you and I are walking through a museum, and we see this bulbous, silly little guy, and one of us comments “oh he’s pretty cute” and we laugh and move on. We do not give the god reverence; we do not know what it did or who even carved it. All that we know is that he’s a cute little guy.</p>
<p>Now understand that there have been billions of humans and there will, in all likelihood, be billions more, each with their own lives, temporal and physical contexts, and all sorts of reasons to think <em>about a thing</em> like this cute little hypothetical idol statue in a specific way.</p>
<p>We usually define ‘authentic’ to be ‘genuine,’ that is, ‘it comes from the true source.’</p>
<p>Let’s say someone thought the hypothetical cute god statue was so cute, they wanted to merchandise it. Most people would understand the one actually carved three thousand years ago to be ‘authentically’ of its culture — that is, it’s a genuine historical artifact that has existed for thousands of years. If someone wanted to merchandise this god thing and made thousands of statues, the new ones would be… changed.</p>
<p>The rocks would be sourced from another place, the statue would likely be machine-carved, and there’s really no… history to it. Yes, the rock’s been around for a long time, but in <em>that</em> form? Even if someone hand carved it right here and now, its actual existence would be that of a souvenir, rather than ‘an actual item crafted years ago by some dude history has long forgotten.’</p>
<p>Somehow, the awareness of the context matters to people. They care more about the thing that’s ‘an original’ than ‘a replica.’ Somehow, the idea that I could one day buy <a href="https://www.classicaircraftsales.com/listings/p-47-thunderbolt">this P-47</a> fighter plane that actually served in combat excites me in a way that buying a working replica never could, even if I couldn’t tell the difference at a glance.</p>
<p>This becomes a bit more obvious when people start getting facts wrong. If someone makes a movie set during World War I and they show a P-47, then no matter how they say they wanted to get across the sense of what it was like to fight in World War I, my brain goes “right, but that’s a fighter from World War II. Very different kind of plane. This movie is bullshit.”</p>
<p>Now, we could get into a whole big thing about authenticity and its perception in youth cultural subgroups over time, but let’s get down to the brass tacks:</p>
<p>Generation X once valued authenticity heavily. This authenticity wasn’t “it comes with specific cultural context and the story about it is true,” but the idea of being authentic to yourself, refusing to sell out, that kind of thing.</p>

<p>An authentic artist would be one who’s writing and performing lyrics that matter to her, not one who walks into a production studio with fifty different producers who make music to allow her brand to sell more albums. Gen X spent a lot of time telling you how bad it was to “sell out” and become the man, which is why it’s such a shame that Gen X is, by and large, the generation keeping Trump — a man so inauthentic in all that he does to the point of declaring victory in a war that has barely started — in the White House now. But, hey, back in the 80s, 90s, and early 00s, you look at a lot of Gen X art, and yeah, you see a bunch of art that at least pays lip service to the idea that you should be true to yourself and not sell out.</p>
<p>(it doesn’t matter, but ‘to thine own self be true’ is Shakespeare. weird that he keeps coming up. this is not a point for later, or anything)</p>
<p>Well, regardless of how Gen X ended up, that desire for authenticity is <em>everywhere</em> — my friend Phil was <a href="https://bsky.app/profile/pabastien.bsky.social/post/3mi55so3nuk2m">pointing out earlier today</a>, as I wrote this section, that people started making chunky pasta sauce and branding it as ‘homestyle,’ sales skyrocketed because of the allure of an authentic, home-cooked meal.</p>
<p>People want things they can trust, things with a real context and history. They want to feel like it’s honest with them, rather than some cold, bland, corporate product.</p>
<p>So I get the idea of making a historical game and wanting to brag about all the research you did to get it right, but I balk at the suggestion that getting the Kurosawa estate’s permission (which is just something you can buy, by the way, and almost certainly what Sony paid for here — it’s not like people who actually knew Kurosawa and his artistic vision are meticulously making sure Sucker Punch and Sony accurately represent his style) or “well, look, the armor wasn’t really what the audience expects” don’t somehow render all of that pointless to have said in the first place.</p>
<p>But… some people might argue that despite some indications that Sucker Punch cared about authenticity, what they really cared about was making a video game that was authentic to samurai movies — that is, rather than being historically accurate, the game is channeling the same kind of storytelling, stylistic flourishes, and elements that we saw in the mid 20th century.</p>
<p>It would be like if I said I wanted to make an ‘authentic’ Baz Luhrmann film; I’m making something stylistically and thematically like something he would make, and not making something accurate to the real world. Okay. It’s possible to explore genre.</p>
<p>So did they make an accurate samurai movie adaptation?</p>
<p>No.</p>
<p>No they did not.</p>

<p>Most people think of katanas when they think of samurai.</p>
<p>They <em>should</em> think about the bow.</p>
<p>Yes, absolutely, sword combat is cool as hell to watch, and it’s even cooler with a good editor and fight choreography trying to make it look great. Throw in some awesome actors really selling it, the director knocking it out of the park, the set dressers and makeup team kicking ass, and hell yeah, shit starts to feel great.</p>

<p>Samurai fuckin’ loved the bow, almost as much as they loved sneak attacks. Oda Nobunaga, y’know, the legend of legends, revered in many works of Japanese fiction as the hottest, most badass dude who ever lived (this side of the other guy portrayed as the hottest, most badass dude to ever live, Sakamoto Ryoma), decided to go after his foes at the battle of Okehazama with what was <em>likely</em> either a pincer move or a surprise attack head on (there’s some historical debate on this).</p>
<p>As soon as guns came along, what do you think samurai did? Well, if you watch a lot of Western fiction, either about samurai or, like the Klingons, inspired by them — a culture all about acting honorably, you might go “I know where this is going,” and assume that there’d be a big fight between the samurai who fight under the ways of tradition and some young buck who didn’t get why when using guns was the tactical option.</p>
<p>Well, wouldn’t you know it, as soon as samurai — who, historically, were more archers than swordfighters — got guns, they went “holy shit, guns fucking rule” and started using guns. There was no big duel of honor between the new ways and the old (unless you wanna consider the Satsuma rebellion, but even then, that wasn’t a whole ‘samurai code’ thing).</p>
<p>In 1564, at Konodai, same kinda thing — one force wanted to trap the other, the other force realized they were going to be trapped, so they sent a team to sneak around and stab the trappers in the ass. As I recall, it worked.</p>
<p>Now, that’s war shit. What about on the individual level, the wandering samurai type who’s just going around having adventures, as depicted in the movies?</p>
<p>What about <em>another </em>guy portrayed as the hottest, sexiest man who ever lived: Miyamoto Musashi? Here’s a guy so good at kicking ass that he had a 62–0 undefeated record and was granted the title of “sword saint” (you know, like the final boss in Sekiro, Isshin the Sword Saint? You gotta be a real badass to get that title!). Wanna know one of his best stories?</p>
<p>Dude waited to duel someone until the sun was in his eyes so he could have a competitive edge, then beat the guy to death with a boat oar, rather than a sword. Another time, he had his foes chase him around a castle to tire them out before giving them a good walloping. In fact, central to the idea of Musashi being the greatest to ever do it is the idea that he did so <em>through his wits</em>.</p>
<p>This is supposed to be one of the most badass motherfuckers who ever lived, and he did so… on trickery.</p>
<p>Most countries have their origin myths, and Japan is no different. Entrenched in that origin myth is the idea of bushido, but 20th century bushido — the honor culture you hear so much of these days — doesn’t come from any lived combat experience.</p>
<p>It comes from a fucking nerd, a guy, disabled, like me, who fantasized about what life would’ve been like if only he could’ve been a samurai.</p>

<p>Yamamoto Tsunetomo was good enough at words that he ended up getting to study them before enjoying the tutelage of Buddhist and Confucian priests. Eventually, he ended up returning to his former lord, Nabeshima Mitsushige, who died. Tsunetomo intended to commit ritual suicide to follow his lord into the afterlife, but that was illegal, so he couldn’t.</p>
<p>As he got older, he told a samurai — it should probably be mentioned at this point that ‘samurai’ is less a ‘class of fighter,’ and more like ‘the nobility,’ meaning that you could, in fact, be a samurai without donning armor and running around with a katana (or, like we said, more likely, a bow) — his thoughts on the decay of the samurai as a class. This is what we know as the work Hagakure.</p>
<p>So, let’s think about this for a second: imagine this guy who’s never been a warrior himself, who longs for the glory days of battles he never fought, who knows nothing of war, imagining himself a great hero, and he can’t do <em>any</em> of the things he wants to do. Meanwhile, with peace settling in, he’s watching people who he would have liked to see fight these epic battles as not being warriors enough, so he decides to bitch about it, incorporating his understanding of ‘living with an eye toward dying.’</p>
<p>Most people didn’t give a shit.</p>
<p>For hundreds of years, people continued not giving a shit.</p>
<p>But then, more than two hundred years after it was written,</p>
<p>someone gave a shit.</p>
<p>By the 1930s, Hagakure was considered <em>the</em> authoritative text on what Samurai actually were. Did it matter that this was a story written during a time of peace by a man who knew nothing of war fantasizing about a death-driven warrior cult and describing samurai as if they ought to have been that?</p>
<p>Well, to the death-driven warrior cult, it meant a whole lot. Within about 30 years, the entire concept of what samurai were fueled imperial Japan’s war machine. Japan got extremely expansionist, violently attacking any nearby nation to steal their resources for Japan’s then extremely right wing military government.</p>
<p>This is how Japan entered World War II on the side of the Italian Fascists and the German Nazis: they all believed in the same thing, their own supremacy, their <em>strength</em>, their <em>warrior prowess</em>. Was it rooted in any actual history? Not really; the Italians saw themselves as virtuous and trod upon by other European nations, having missed out on a lot of the colonization that had made Europe, the Netherlands, France, Spain, and Belgium filthy rich. The Nazis incorporated a fictional, mythical people, the Aryans, as their forebears.</p>
<p>Japanese imperialists, of course, had Hagakure.</p>
<p>None of them, you’ll note, won World War II. They put up a fight, but the guys who actually knew what their whole deal was and were willing to do what it took to win, people whose cause was purely defensive — and not the “we attacked Iran first because they might attack us,” but the “they literally killed a bunch of our people in December 1941 and we decided we weren’t down with that,” types stepped up and rained hell on them.</p>
<p>You see, as it turns out, armed forces that deal with bullshit concepts like honor, wherein one does stupid shit because of inflexible traditions, tend to get their asses handed to them, which means <em>their tactics tend not to survive</em>. If you keep having stupid dudes lead stupid people into stupid fights they can’t win because of pride… those people tend not to win wars, which means their tactics tend not to survive either.</p>
<p>You cannot win if you are a supremacist, because you cannot, and never will be able to, accurately assess the enemy.</p>
<p>But you know where we <em>do</em> see a lot of this bullshit?</p>
<p>European nobility.</p>
<p>Take World War I’s Luigi Cardona, a man who would kill his own soldiers to keep them in line (like people often portray samurai), who got 300,000 people killed (just sit there and think about making a fuckup so bad you got three hundred thousand people killed). I’ve heard his name is still cursed to this <em>day</em> by Italians.</p>
<p>He was far from the only one; the Brits allowed nobles to buy their commands, and these guys, who had no qualifications or training other than money or favoritism, would repeatedly lead people to their deaths. Sound like anyone you know?</p>

<p>Kimitake Hiraoka, his real name, was descended from the Tokugawas, and raised by his grandmother, who was herself the granddaughter of a Daimyo. Her father had been a supreme court justice; his adoptive father had been a bannerman of the Tokugawa. She’d been raised in a prince’s house, and viewed herself as an aristocrat.</p>
<p>You can see where this is going.</p>
<p>Imagine a boy born at the height of Hagakure, raised by a grandmother with insane pretensions of imperial power. After he gets returned to his parents, he’s enrolled in an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gakush%C5%ABin">elite school</a> that was literally known for educating nobility. He writes a book at 16, while the war is going on, and starts getting mentored by a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasuda_Zenmei">nationalist</a> who was himself a big fan of Matoori Norinaga, a scholar who was all about Japanese traditional values and revering the emperor.</p>
<p>Yukio Mishima could not have been a more perfect candidate for fascism.</p>
<p>Fascists are often weak people who dislike their lot in life and are extremely susceptible to blaming other people; they will often fall in line for a big figurehead, whether that’s Hitler or a king or an emperor like Napoleon, who promises to get them out of there. Fascists want to be special, and they want to be powerful. They fantasize about strength — it’s why fascists will often lose wars; they envision themselves as <em>already strong</em>, they just need to engage in violence to take it.</p>
<p>The Imperial Japanese Army was no different. While Mishima himself could not fight in the war early on, he was eventually drafted… and told he wasn’t good enough. Apparently he almost failed to pass his certification for the “barely good enough” category.</p>
<p>So, okay, here’s a guy who’s been raised from childhood to believe he’s special due to his noble upbringing, who gets mentored by a nationalist Japanese soldier, and then he graduates top of his class, gets honored by the literal emperor of Japan himself, who he’s been raised to revere as a god…</p>
<p>…and he is then told that, contrary to all the people praising him his entire life, he’s <em>not actually even good enough to be a conscript</em>.</p>
<p>What happens next?</p>
<p>Well, while his parents were thrilled he wasn’t gonna die in the war, Mishima started developing an obsession with kamikaze and commando units. Shortly after the war ended, his mentor killed a superior for daring to suggest the Emperor was why they lost. Everything started spiraling out of control… except for the fact that he was becoming an increasingly successful celebrity.</p>
<p>The American occupation forces had absolutely no interest in allowing Japanese nationalism to continue, so they made it illegal. What’s a latent fascist to do? Well, not wanting to become a bureaucrat as his father had hoped, Mishima gets <em>really</em> into martial arts and bodybuilding.</p>
<p>And he starts writing a lot of stories about people who die, often through suicide. He also writes about failed suicides.</p>

<p>…and the fascism starts to come out in his work, with The Sound of Waves, which is all about living a traditional Japanese lifestyle. I talked about this <a data-discover="true" href="https://docseuss.medium.com/i-was-a-teenage-exocolonist-and-so-can-you-0946b1d9ade0">fascination</a> with ‘cozy’ and ‘wholesome’ ideas in fascist spaces before; fascists are notably obsessed with a sort of “return to a simpler life” thing. Same reason why lots of ’em obsess over the Saturday Evening Post paintings of Norman Rockwell while disagreeing with his <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murder_in_Mississippi_(painting)">politics</a>.</p>
<p>So, in 1967, a nerd who fantasized about being big and strong, who missed out on the wars he thought would glorify him, who thought the world would be better if the Emperor was still treated as the absolute authority, who obsessed over being perceived as something other than what he was…</p>
<p>…writes about Hagakure.</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">…In short, I have come to be more and more deeply possessed by Hagakure. But I, who follow the way of the artist and entertainer condemned by Hagakure, have been conflicted by the torment between the action ethic and my art.</p></blockquote>
<p>Later:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">All at once I recognized in Hagakure a philosophy of life, and somehow I felt that its beautiful, pristine world could stir up the quagmire that was the world of literature. For me, the meaning of Hagakure is in the vision of this pristine world… Hagakure <strong>is the womb from which my writing is born</strong>. It is the eternal supplying source of my vitality…</p></blockquote>
<p>He seems to relate to Tsunetomo, and I must say, I certainly see the same frustration in both men, denied the glory they imagined warriors must have, both horrifically wrong in the end.</p>
<p>And, like all other fascists, Mishima wildly overestimated himself. He thought he was unique and special because he’d been given the best schooling and raised in the upper classes. And, certainly, as a writer, he was skilled. But… still, when you read the man talking about his nihilism, his lack of sympathy for other people, his narcissism (which he associates with his bodybuilding efforts), what you really get is a person so desperate to matter and so willing to despise other people, that he convinces himself, like all other fascists, to succumb to the fascist death drive.</p>
<p>So he and a couple other people tried to take over a government building.</p>
<p>It didn’t really work out.</p>
<p>He went to the roof and shouted a speech, hoping that people would rally to his cause and reinstate the emperor.</p>
<p>They laughed at him instead.</p>
<p>Mishima is what following Hagakure gets you.</p>
<p>So he tried to commit ritual suicide. It took three swings to get his head off, because his people couldn’t even do that right.</p>
<p>These days, Mishima’s just another weird author, a guy who fell so far into nationalism and fascism that he ultimately killed himself for no good reason. He could have written better and more interesting books — like I said, he was a talented writer. Instead… like the Imperial Japanese army, he died ignominious.</p>
<p>Pathetic, really.</p>
<p>It’s Kurosawa we think of when we think great Japanese artists. Mishima? Hard not to laugh at his stupid turn towards fascism’s death drive. He permanently ruined any chance he had at greatness by being a stupid fuckhead.</p>

<p>Throughout the 50s and 60s, the period associated with the best samurai films, much of the storytelling is focused on the then-immediate feelings of the filmmakers. They excoriate imperial Japan by tearing Bushido — and Hagakure — apart at the seams.</p>
<p>Yes, of course, there are heroic figures — the samurai tale is heavily inspired by the Western, after all.</p>
<p>Much ado is made about how The Magnificent Seven is inspired by Kurosawa, and a lot of people have internalized this as “Westerns saw a resurgence after being inspired by samurai movies.” I recall a conversation some time ago where someone suggested that prior to Kurosawa in particular, Westerns were all Roy Rogers, and after Kurosawa, we got all the greats.</p>
<p>This, of course, ignores that films like High Noon and Winchester 73 were being made contemporaneously.</p>
<p>I recall learning in one of my classes that Japanese films prior to World War II were, by and large, more like filmed versions of plays and not that many have survived. During the American occupation, American films were pushed heavily on Japan under the American regime. That said, Kurosawa was already making Hollywood-style movies, to the point where Sanshiro Sugata — yes, the film that inspired Sanshiro Segata, the Sega mascot — was viewed as “too British-American” by the Japanese censors.</p>
<p>American Westerns and Samurai films have a lot of cultural cross-communication; to prioritize one over the other would be a mistake. Besides, as everyone knows, the first feature length film was an Australian Western anyways (yes, it was about Ned Kelly).</p>
<p>But in Japan, especially early on, before they started cranking out fifty Zatoichi movies and a hundred Lone Wolf and Cubs, which could be seen as serialized, commercialized adventures, yeah, a lot of the samurai fiction is explicitly there to call out the Imperial Japanese mindset at the time. Kobayashi and Kurosawa were at the forefront, but they were far from the only ones.</p>
<p>Basically “fuck your concept of honor, it’s all just fascist bullshit and a way for rich assholes to fuck over the rest of us” is at the heart of a lot of Japanese approaches to samurai storytelling. Beyond that, a lot of it’s just genre fare — great genre fare, of course, but genre fare nonetheless.</p>
<p>But genre fare is not what Sucker Punch or Sony ever talks about, however. They talk about Kurosawa, about <a href="https://variety.com/2018/gaming/features/ghosts-of-tsushima-interview-1202848106/">Seven Samurai and Sanjuro</a>, not Toho or Daiei films that were cranked out so fast you’d sometimes get multiple adventures in a year!</p>
<p>They go for the high art, rather than the fun pop art side of things, so we should judge them by that metric.</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">the western (mis)understanding of japan</h2>
<p>I have watched a lot of Japanese films and anime. I have also watched a lot of Western films and cartoons. If there’s anything that strikes me about how Americans portray Japan, it’s that Westerners talk about honor <em>constantly</em>. In fact, you will note that every screenshot in this article from Ghost of Tsushima contains the word HONOR in it (or some form thereof).</p>
<p>Many people have argued that since the game was so well liked in Japan (the former Yakuza/Super Monkey Ball boss <a href="https://kotaku.com/yakuza-director-praises-ghost-of-tsushima-says-japan-s-1844541108">Toshihiro Nagoshi</a> said Japan should’ve made it and really seemed to like it), there’s nothing wrong with it. I’ve talked to some people about it and have some thoughts, but they’re way, way, way too far out of my league to really get deep into it, and hey, this is ultimately a reader supported blog that I do to supplement my income so I can get some medical care for being disabled.</p>
<p>While watching these things does not make me an expert any more than that time I helped translate an episode of Monster or transcribed episodes of Digimon for a fan site, I do want to note one thing: there’s a story I’ve only ever seen from the American side of things.</p>
<p>You know the one. There’s a character, usually a young man, who is told he must act by the strict rules of the organization he works for. Something happens, and the character finds his resolve tested. Maybe he even breaks the rules, which never made much sense to him anyways. Our hero is pragmatic and on the side of good — though in some forms, he’s a tough guy who might be more of an antihero type. This hero ultimately, by breaking the rules, is able to stop the bad guy.</p>
<p>Now, reading this, you probably think I’m talking about Jin Sakai in Ghost of Tsushima, and I very well could be, but I was actually talking about hit 1971 film Dirty Harry.</p>
<p>“Why should I have to follow the rules? Why can’t I just do what I want?” is that kind of whiny, American exceptionalist bullshit you find all over the goddamn place. It’s <em>also</em> something you often find in fascist storytelling — the idea of the individual against the horde, the person who doesn’t get the rules or care about the — no, fuck off Pete Hegseth —</p>

<p>— I mean, seriously though, think about his stupid bullshit for a second. Dude’s constantly talking about ‘warfighters’ and ‘lethality,’ and fucked up so badly in his logistics that America went from “technically the most capable military in the world” to “quite possibly one of the worst militaries out there right now.” How he hasn’t been killed by his own men by now is a mystery.</p>
<p>Anyways, Ghost of Tsushima follows a lot of similar beats. You are Jin Sakai. Your home is invaded by the Mongols, portrayed as monstrous, brutal foreign invaders — similar to your average fascist yarn about Mexicans stealing our jobs. Your uncle is a noble, which means you are too; in fact, he wants to adopt you as his son, and he’s proud of the honorable man you are.</p>
<p>But, facing off against the toughest military in the world at the time, you’re going to struggle a bit, and with your uncle kidnapped by the Mongols, what choice do you have to do but a <em>little</em> ninja action?</p>
<p>In the Kokiji (considered to be Japan’s oldest surviving literary work), ninjas, more commonly known in Japan as Shinobi, were indicated to have been around since at least the late 500s. So Jin’s pulling from a long history of — oh, wait, sorry, what’s that? Ninjas don’t get mentioned at all?</p>
<p>That’s weird.</p>
<p>To put this into perspective, by the time of Ghost of Tsushima, ninjas had, ostensibly, existed for more than <em>three times the existence of the United States</em> (which, funnily enough, wouldn’t exist for another 502 years after the game takes place). Plenty of wars had been fought with subterfuge by this point. There is nothing Jin Sakai does in the game that, as far as I can tell, would be something samurai would disapprove of.</p>
<p>Even Hagakure wouldn’t be published for another 450 years or so after the game took place. There is no historical context, none that I can find anyways, that indicated <em>any</em> kind of “no! we only honorably attack men from the front!” type of bullshit that Jin’s uncle, Lord Sakai, could have been subscribing to.</p>
<p>When we look at Japanese fiction, they don’t portray themselves this way at all. When we look at American fiction, we can find a fair amount regarding the British.</p>
<p>I don’t know exactly where this originated, but it’s a big part of America’s founding mythology (like I said, every country has one), and it goes something like this: America beat the British, a dramatically superior force, thanks to learning that it was stupid to dress up in bright red coats and stand in lines shooting at each other. In the New World, you had to adapt to the environments, and that meant having to interact with the local populace.</p>
<p>We often think of racism as things like “being genocidal” and “hurling offensive slurs,” but then there’s the other kind of presuming to speak for people, writing an idealized, noble version of a populace, rather than trying to understand or relate to them.</p>
<p>When it came to James Fenimore Cooper and his wildly popular Natty Bumppo stories, he portrayed Bumppo as raised by natives, trained by the Moravian church, and capable of using his superior wits, grit, and honor. If you want noble savage tropes, believe me, you got ’em. Cooper’s work reflected — and possibly popularized — attitudes at the time that natives were just <em>mysteriously disappearing</em> and it was up to the colonists to continue using the land that the natives had abandoned, uh, some… unspecified reason.* Until recently. Nobody knows what’s up with that, I guess, right?</p>
<p>(*it was the detrimental effects of their colonialism, namely ethnic cleansing)</p>
<p>Anyways, this kind of shit got entrenched in the American cultural consciousness to the point that Mark Twain wrote an entire essay on why Cooper sucked shit (at one point he says something to the effect of “there are 115 literary offenses and he’s committed 114. on this page. alone.”).</p>
<p>So I think about watching movies growing up, like Disney’s Johnny Tremaine, or Michael Mann’s brilliant adaptation of Cooper’s The Last of the Mohicans, and how so much of American revolutionary mythology suggests that the British were, in fact, really bad at combat because they kept trying to play by the <em>rules </em>of combat.</p>
<p>Now, I’m not here to teach you the history of European wars in the 17th and 18th centuries, right up until 1776, where the Americans went all Revolutionary War on England’s ass, so I can’t say whether or not such a dichotomy, between well-trained American troops and overly-stiff British ones who couldn’t adapt to the environment, but I will say it’s common enough in our <em>storytelling</em> that the idea of the British guy obsessed with the rules of war making bad tactical decisions comes up <em>a lot</em>.</p>

<p>So, here’s the deal: Ruth Benedict was considered one of the foremost anthropologists of her time. She was, like many anthropologists, contracted by the United States of America’s government, under President Franklin Delano Roosevelt, to try to figure out what the <em>fuck</em> the Nazis were up to.</p>
<p>And she did, by writing a report that explored Nazi ideology as a fairly recent, incoherent belief system. My understanding is that this is still considered to be a good understanding.</p>
<p>But, like in the aftermath of the Revolutionary War, a lot of Americans wanted to understand what was going down. I mean, how do you explain Kamikaze to the average 1940s person from Oklahoma? Can you? Well, Ruth Benedict was asked to try, and boy did she try.</p>
<p>Too bad she failed.</p>
<p>Well, no, that’s not quite right: she succeeded in explaining Japan, as long as you throw accuracy out the window. It’s an enjoyable read, because Benedict is a wonderful word-weaver, but… it’s also extremely inaccurate. Even the core idea the work is known for — the idea that the West is a ‘guilt culture,’ where one thinks about, say, the afterlife and acts based on concerns about how they will end up, and the idea that Japan is a ‘shame culture,’ where people act based on how others perceive them — is something many scholars have widely refuted.</p>
<p>Writer C. Douglas Lummis <a href="https://apjjf.org/C-Douglas-Lummis/2474/article">explains</a>:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">I first found Ruth Benedict’s The Chrysanthemum and the Sword in the Charles Tuttle Bookstore in Okinawa in 1960. I had just decided to spend some time living in Japan (little suspecting that “some time” would turn out to be a big part of the rest of my life) and I was delighted to discover that Benedict, whose Patterns of Culture I greatly admired, had written this book too. I read it avidly, and for some years was corrupted by the myth of (as Malinowski called it) the “ethnographer’s magic”. I walked around Japan like a miniature Benedict, seeing “patterns” everywhere, and thinking it was wonderfully clever to be able to “analyze” the behavior of the people around me, including even invitations to dialogue and expressions of friendship. I claim no monopoly to this kind of attitude; in those days it was rampant within the community of Westerners in Japan, and especially among the Americans, so many of whom saw themselves not only as miniature Benedicts, but also as miniature MacArthurs (some still do today). After some time I realized that I would never be able to live in a decent relationship with the people of that country unless I could drive this book, and its politely arrogant world view, out of my head. The method I chose was to begin the research that led to the following essay.</p></blockquote>
<p>He continues:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">When I finally managed to get [to the library where Benedict’s papers were being held], I made two major discoveries. One was Benedict’s “country report” on Germany. Benedict wrote this at about the same time she was doing her research on Japan, but the two works could not be more different. In Germany, Nazism is a recently cobbled together ideology; in Japan, totalitarian militarism is — just Japan.</p></blockquote>
<p>Lummis characterizes it as a deeply problematic work of Orientalism (referencing noted scholar Edward Said), noting that many Japanese scholars are aware of its shortcomings. You should read the whole piece, and I won’t summarize everything, but I want to get at this idea of <em>simplification.</em></p>
<p>As Lummis points out, the book really learns more about Japan’s fascist government than Japan as a culture, but then uses this simplified explanation to explain <em>Japan as a whole</em>. This would be a lot like deciding to write a critical analysis on all three hundred some million Americans by fixating entirely on conservative homeschooling families. Yes, one group might be like that, but you could hardly define a culture by examining just the one group.</p>

<p>Remember how I suggested that the way the Americans use the Japanese samurai tradition is more reflective of the American founding myths and attempts to understand its origins? Well, Lummis says that he thinks The Chrysanthemum and the Sword serves a purpose not as anthropology, but as political literature — a country’s way of understanding not the other, but <em>itself</em>:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">…all these images of Another Country served the function of increasing the reader’s awareness of the ruling spirit, the underlying nature, the dominating principle, of the home country.</p></blockquote>
<p>Lummis mentions Geertz’s excellent essay Us/Not-Us, where Clifford Geertz points out that Benedict begins like this:</p>
<p>“The Japanese… are the most alien enemy the United States [has] ever fought.”</p>
<p>Geertz continues:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">The habit of contrasting an “as-weknow” [sic] us with an “imagine-that” them is here carried to climax; as though American Indians and Melanesians had been but warm-ups for the really different. And what is more, the contrasting is now explicit and particular, not, as in Patterns of Culture, implied and general-specific this-es set against specific thats. <strong>I had thought to count the number of such “in America” I “in Japan” tropes in The Chrysanthemum and the Sword, but soon gave it up as a tiresome task leading to an unscalable number.</strong></p></blockquote>
<p>Geertz’s main idea here — I definitely recommend reading it — is that Benedict took all her natural writing traits, her experience working propaganda for the government during the war, and wrote her magnum opus by emphasizing the differences between America and Japan to make them seem as difficult to understand as possible.</p>
<p>In a climate where the ‘average understanding’ of Japan is “man, they’re so <em>weird</em>” (and consider, for a moment, how many depictions of Japan even now portray it as weird, loud, technologically advanced, and alien— like Thirty Minutes over Tokyo, from The Simpsons), it’s not hard to see how Ruth Benedict helped shape that perception.</p>
<p>What was she doing? Well, as Lummis points out, Benedict worked hard to go, more or less, “Japan is just weird, man.”</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">Benedict’s judgment on Japan can be seen in her answer to the question: why did Japan fight this war? Her answer makes no use of economic or political explanations. Japan did not follow the well-known logic of colonial and imperialist powers, seeking markets, resources, investment outlets and cheap labor. Nor did Japan follow the well-beaten path of tyranny, seeking power, glory, a central place in history. Nor had Japan (in contrast to Germany and Italy) passed over into an extraordinary state of political pathology: nowhere does she use the concepts of fascism, totalitarianism, or any similar notion. To admit the relevance of any of these explanations would be to admit that Japan’s behavior was understandable according to ordinary “Western” reason — that it was yet another rather extreme and badly-timed example of plain, old-fashioned imperialism.</p></blockquote>
<p>Why?</p>
<p>According to Lummis:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">Benedict was determined to show that Japan’s behavior was utterly different from anything known in the “West”, and understandable to “Westerners” only by means of her “ethnologist’s magic”, the anthropological method.</p></blockquote>
<p>Hey, everyone, look at me! I’ve got something to sell you! Reminds me a lot of how Ghost of Tsushima was marketed heavily as being ~like~ Kurosawa, because if you associate yourself with Kurosawa, you must be making some real serious art, right? It’s why Zatoichi never seems to come up in these interviews; the white boys know about the cultural impact of Kurosawa. If they <em>really </em>loved Japan and samurai stories, you’d think they’d be in love with <em>all </em>of it. But if you’ve got something to sell… well, maybe, like Ruth Benedict, you might not be inclined to tell the truth.</p>
<p>People do that, for whatever reason; they alter the truth as it suits what they’re selling, and I’ve never been happy with that. If Kurosawa’s the main guy you checked out, well, it might’ve been good to check out other works of samurai cinema, ranging from serious to schlock.</p>
<p>I’d rather see the entire mess of influences — I’d love to see the weird picks, the deep cuts, the surprises.</p>

<p>But, what was Ruth Benedict trying to sell? According to Lummis, the idea that Japan had to be changed and America had to be the nation to change it. Rather than being racist towards Japanese people on the whole, she fixated on Japanese culture:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph=""><em>The Chrysanthemum and the Sword</em> told its readers that Japanese culture must be changed and explained how it could be changed, under the force of the U.S. military occupation.</p></blockquote>
<p>As he continues, he presents evidence that suggests that Benedict pulled heavily from her traditional literary understanding, trying to take a culture and make it make sense narratively. Lummis suggests that refusing to learn Japanese or visit Japan meant that Benedict, more or less, would not run into any evidence that would conflict with her theories, while her friends at the time acted like this would make her somehow <em>more</em> objective, rather than a fabulist.</p>
<p>And then, as we have discussed, Lummis lays it out:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">The Meiji elites, using compulsory education, military conscription, institutional reorganization, and many other forms of indoctrination and force, sought to organize the various cultures of the Japanese and Ryukyu Archipelagos — and later the cultures of Taiwan and Korea as well — into a single nation-state under the direct rule of the Tokyo government, the whole apparatus mystified under the newly-organized emperor system and legitimized by means of the “invented tradition” of a modernized version of the ethic of the old <em>bushi</em> class.</p></blockquote>
<p>It’s a really interesting read, and he keeps proving his point; he managed to meet Robert Hashima, Benedict’s primary source, and talked to him, noting his unusal upbringing and distinct attitude, and how he ended up working with Benedict. And Lummis summarizes it thusly:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph=""><em>The Chrysanthemum and the Sword</em> is the product of a remarkable convergence of conceptions. Benedict, Hashima, and Japan’s wartime militarists — though each for entirely different reasons — all promoted the myth that Japanese society was something like a family or tribe, that there were no functional class differences within it, that the ideas of democracy and rebellion were inconceivable within it, that its value system was traditional, that the core of its values was unchanged over the millennia, resulting in a national identity that was culturally determined and immutable, at least in the absence of powerful external force — in short, that the system was not the product of state or class oppression and that it was incomprehensible in terms of such categories as capitalism, colonialism, militarism which were being applied to other societies. <strong>To be totalitarian and to be Japanese were one and the same.</strong></p></blockquote>
<p>A friend of mine introduced me to The Chrysanthemum and the Sword when I first began thinking about this piece several years ago; we’d been talking about how a lot of business guys tried to understand China in the 70s or so, so they got really into reading The Art of War, hoping it would help them perform better in business.</p>
<p>The Chrysanthemum and the Sword, my friend said, occupied a similar role; as Japan’s economy was on the rise, and companies like Sony were pushing the Walkman, American businessmen tried to understand Japan, and reached for the ultimate text on the Japanese mind, hoping for a competitive edge. So, of course, they went with Ruth Benedict.</p>
<p>The Klingons appear to have been influenced by Benedict — and popular cultural depictions as well. When the Klingons first appeared in Star Trek, they were <em>very</em> orientalist in their depiction. The bat’leth, their signature martial weapon, was heavily influenced by Asian weaponry. And, by the time The Next Generation rolled around, the Klingons were a proud warrior culture, very similar to Ruth Benedict’s depiction of Japanese culture.</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">james clavell’s <em>bull-shit</em></h2>
<p>Alright. So. The <em>real</em> big change in Western perceptions of Japan was in a television show released in the late 1970s, James Clavell’s Shogun. In it, some dumb white asshole washes up on Japanese shores. His name’s Blackthorne.</p>
<p>It’s a rip-roaring adventure story, but it gets a <em>lot</em> wrong about Japan in the process. I’ve heard the newer show is <em>much</em> more faithful, thanks in part to the heavy involvement of the Japanese crewmembers involved in its production, which is great, but I haven’t gotten around to seeing it yet.</p>
<p>Blackthorne ultimately works his way up to being an ally of lord Toranaga, who ultimately becomes Shogun. I’ve been playing a little Assassin’s Creed Samurai, and I would not be surprised to hear they lifted some of Yasuke’s interactions from Shogun (Yasuke himself was a historical figure, but the actual <em>scenes </em>of Assassin’s Creed Shadows with Yasuke so far sure remind me of Lord Blackthorne’s early experiences).</p>
<p>The television show was <em>huge</em>. Chances are, unless you’re in your 50s, you cannot really conceive of a media event quite like this. The finale of something like Lost or Breaking Bad? They were nothing compared to Shogun, which averaged more than 23 million viewers an episode. That’s <em>massive</em>, even by today’s figures — the new show shattered records at <em>nine million viewers, </em>for instance.</p>

<p>And wouldn’t you know it, one of the people who watched it was <a href="https://www.criterion.com/current/posts/7102-all-in-the-game-an-interactive-homage-to-the-samurai-genre">a young Nate Fox</a>:</p>
<blockquote><p data-selectable-paragraph="">Nate: When I was growing up, my best friend’s family was from Japan. One night, his mom was over at our house watching this miniseries called <em>Shogun</em> with my mom, and she acted as an interpreter and called out what was wrong. I felt like the curtain was being parted a little bit on this place that I’d only heard about from [my friend] Keita.</p></blockquote>
<p>So, if he’s like anyone else watching Shogun, sitting there watching people talk about committing harakiri or Blackthorne yelling and going “I don’t understand this! Why are you doing this?” it isn’t hard to see why his attitudes towards Japan and stories <em>about</em> Japan might mirror that same sense of “Japan is strange with a cultural difference completely alien to our own” that might spring to mind. As an American, Nate Fox (and much of Sucker Punch’s studio leadership)’s cultural context includes stories about an individual going against conventional wisdom to save the day — whether Dirty Harry or Natty Bumppo or even George Washington — then it makes sense that you take a little of that, a little of Shogun, and bada bing, bada boom, you’ve got the boilerplate samurai story.</p>
<p>There’s a character, usually a young man, who is told he must act by the strict rules of the organization he works for. Something happens, and the character finds his resolve tested. Maybe he even breaks the rules, which never made much sense to him anyways. Our hero is pragmatic and on the side of good — though in some forms, he’s a tough guy who might be more of an antihero type. This hero ultimately, by breaking the rules, is able to stop the bad guy.</p>
<p>This time, I’m talking about Jin Sakai.</p>
<p>When Nate Fox <a href="https://variety.com/2018/gaming/features/ghosts-of-tsushima-interview-1202848106/">said</a> “this is a story about real people trying to survive in the brutality of this invasion,” I find it hard to buy the idea that these are real people, because the characters in the main plot function less as dramatic, interesting characters and more as characters who exist to allow Jin to examine the honor-dishonor dichotomy.</p>
<p>Except… because the game is rooted in a <em>nonexistent concept of honor</em>… that is, <em>at best</em>, based on a 20th century interpretation of a 17th century quasi-religious anti-intellectual’s beliefs in what samurai should be that conveniently served the purpose of giving them power, any comment it could have on that philosophy could only matter by contending with the people who <em>created</em> that philosophy — the 20th century fascists. Ghost of Tsushima does not do this.</p>

<p>I think there’s little to no value at all in examining the ramifications of a nonexisting cultural system like the one presented in Ghost of Tsushima. The Japan envisioned by James Clavell and Ruth Benedict does not, and has never existed. At best, that Japan barely existed — between 1900 and 1945, give or take — and existed only as a concerted effort by nationalists to reshape Japan’s existing identity for its own.</p>
<p>There is no “honor culture” in Japan, unless you buy Benedict’s “shame culture” concept, but that never existed either. She avoided talking about totalitarianism or fascism because doing so would, as our friendly anthropologists Geertz and Lummis pointed out earlier, kinda get in the way of her brand-building.</p>
<p>What good is a story in which a protagonist says, basically, “existing rules are bad and don’t make pragmatic sense. I should move on with this” if it frames this as a historical truth that isn’t? If these characters are <em>real</em>, but grappling with a pure hypothetical, like the nonexistent honor culture, what good is it?</p>
<p>In my Cinemas of the Southern Cone class with Dr. Tamara Falicov a decade ago, we watched how filmmaking made after the Argentinan dictatorship couldn’t <em>directly </em>call out the government, so it resorted to historical analogue with the movie Camila. Later, as tensions released, movies that could more directly address the dictatorship, such as The Official Story or Chronicle of Escape, were made.</p>
<p>I see a lot of that in samurai films; directors like Kobayashi and Kurosawa made samurai films, sure, but there was a time when it was easier to do so by making samurai analogous with the Japanese Imperial Army-led version of Japan. These movies were not authentic to the historical time period they were set in; they used common visual tropes to <em>really</em> talk about what the filmmakers’ frustration with fascism itself.</p>
<p>The American/Westernized version of Japan still carries Ruth Benedict’s stench. Jin Sakai never mentions ninjas, but nearly every one of his uncle’s lines is about honor and treats the Japanese almost like the same logic Americans use to joke about red coats — it’s a stupid fighting style that would never survive contact with an enemy determined to win. It’s foolish and heavily rules focused, at the cost of innocent people’s lives.</p>
<p>Ghost of Tsushima’s ability to be dramatically powerful is lost because its protagonist fights a straw man, a racial stereotype that presents a Japan as it’s never been.</p>

<p>Years ago, when Ghost of Tsushima first came out, I recall reading some comments saying that Japanese right wing social media loved the game, arguing it was historically accurate in order to use it as a propaganda tool for their own nationalist ideals.</p>
<p>Some people say “Ghost of Tsushima is great because some Japanese people like it,” but… is the Turner Diaries great because Stephen Miller seems to love it? Probably not. It’s not to say that Ghost of Tsushima is the Turner Diaries. James Clavell really liked Japan; there’s nothing to suggest he was writing Shogun with the same attitude as William Luther Pierce, and I wouldn’t suggest that Nate Fox and company had similar intentions, but as you can see, “some people from this country like it, so it must be fine” isn’t a great argument.</p>
<p>It was the same mistake that Ruth Benedict made by only having one primary source in “Bob,” as she called him in The Chrysanthemum and the Sword. One person does not represent a nation.</p>
<p>In the years since Ghost of Tsushima’s 2020 release, several Japanese teams have released their take on samurai games. Nagoshi’s former RGG Studio team rereleased (but barely upgraded, so it feels like a bad PS3 brawler, which it originally was) Yakuza Ishin! which placed series’ lead Kazuma Kiryu as Sakamoto Ryoma. Rise of the Ronin, by Team Ninja, also covered a similar subject — including Sakamoto Ryoma — but was <em>way</em> more fun to play (because nobody’s as good as Team Ninja at doing this shit).</p>
<p>Yakuza Ishin’s story’s held back by the original game being a low-budget PS3 title. Rise of the Ronin is held back by the fact that it’s a Koei Tecmo game and story is not something Team Ninja has ever <em>really</em> done a good job with (but, again, they are the literal best in the world at combat).</p>
<p>People love samurai. They love the aesthetics of chanbara storytelling, and it makes sense: the Western, Chanbara, and Swashbuckler stories are all primal adventure stories about a person wandering into town and having to do exciting violence.</p>
<p>But when I watch samurai movies — or any Japanese media, really — honor rarely comes up. When it does, it’s treated more the way someone might address fascism in their own country — not a “this weirdo culture makes no sense to me lol why don’t you just be pragmatic and not give a shit about honor.”</p>

<p>Benedict’s biggest contribution to anthropology was the idea of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guilt%E2%80%93shame%E2%80%93fear_spectrum_of_cultures">‘guilt culture’ and ‘shame culture,’</a> but, as Japanese scholars have pointed out, this values the western ‘guilt culture’ as mattering more, because it’s personal, rather than caused by peer pressure or class structures.</p>
<p>A single person standing alone against societal pressure, we understand — go watch High Noon, for instance — and has an incredible appeal. To have integrity in face of evil is difficult. It is. I’ve lost more than you know trying to do what I understand to be right. But that’s <em>very different </em>from a story in which a person experiencing guilt (Jin Sakai doesn’t want to see more of his people killed) dealing with shame (which is inferior, because the inflexible samurai tactics and obsession with honor got them their asses handed to them by the Mongols), ultimately rejects Japanese Traditions in favor of a personal code.</p>
<p>In this way, all Western samurai stories can be seen as retreading Ruth Benedict’s footsteps, in the same way that all too many horror stories can be seen as retreading guilt because of Edgar Allen Poe’s Telltale Heart. At least Poe’s work had the redeeming quality of being good.</p>
<p>Struggling with notions of samurai honor is a conflict that does not actually exist. It is not a conflict worth examining. These are not real people dealing with real problems. You are arguing with an fictional construct.</p>
<p>Do not write your samurai stories like this.</p>

<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">strangely, i love ghost of tsushima</h2>
<p>Sucker Punch made <em>the</em> reason to buy a PlayStation 3 with their excellent InFamous games (especially 2 and Festival of Blood). I <em>love</em> their games, and I was excited as hell to play Ghost of Tsushima when it came out; I was rewarded with one of the best-playing, most thrilling open world sandbox action games (though the controls always take more acclimation than I’d like after an extended period away from the game — they’re distinct and have a tiny learning curve).</p>
<p>But hey, here I am saying the game’s got some problems. That can be a sticky situation! Let’s talk about that, because it’s a super important thing to understand when dealing with fiction. I know a lot of people who truly loved Harry Potter. Now that they know JK Rowling’s an awful person who hates trans people and has dedicated her life to genociding them for no good reason, they avoid her shit.</p>
<p>But some of them, to soften the blow, tell themselves “I never really liked it.” It’s easy to convince yourself you were always pure, you were always a good person with good views.</p>
<p>But… we should be honest with ourselves because we need to untangle our feelings.</p>
<p>Here’s a game! The game does some shit that is not great. It also does a lot of great stuff. We need to be able to determine which is which, so we can better pinpoint the bad stuff.</p>
<p>Ghost of Tsushima, as a story, is pretty good, even if it’s dealing with nonexistent hypotheticals. Is it a little rote? Yes. You can see the setup of “I wanted you to be my son, but you’ve rejected what I stand for, how can I look at you?” line coming from a mile away. Tonally, the tragic final showdown with your uncle needs to happen — and it makes sense that his stick-in-the-mud beliefs get him here.</p>
<p>But it never quite feels <em>real</em>, because it’s dealing with Ruth Benedict’s fucked-up interpretation of Japan, with honor culture as ancient, intrinsic, and alien. If the writers had looked more at a real-world example of face-saving bullshit, like, say, Pete Hegseth’s idea of fascism and how someone might want to reject that in order to make a better world, I think that’d be a much, much stronger narrative than what we got.</p>
<p>Is Ghost of Tsushima fun to play? Yeah. Did I complete the map 100% twice? You know I did! I also played the expansion all the way through on the PC version, and its map, Iki Island, is game-sized in and of itself! I want to recommend it wholeheartedly… but I can’t. To play this game, it helps if you at least understand that it is <em>extremely </em>inaccurate and repeating stereotypes that might have encouraged <em>actual</em> Japanese fascists inadvertently, by promoting Ruth Benedict’s bullshit and rehashing James Clavell’s story beats.</p>
<p>So, I’m sitting here, having written about 15,000 words over the past 12 hours. My body isn’t working too good. I’m still disabled. I’m tired, too.</p>
<p>In a few minutes, after I go back through this piece and get all the pictures up before turning it over to a couple friends who volunteered to read it and point out any typos or other errors, I am going to play a video game.</p>
<p>A friend of mine is going to Japan. I can’t even imagine being able to afford a trip like that. I’d love to visit, but that’s thousands of dollars, and I still need to pay down my medical costs even more. I’ve got a long ways to go.</p>
<p>I’ve always loved Japan; many of its artists have been powerful influences on my life and my creativity, just as much as France or Germany or Mexico or any other nation on Earth.</p>
<p>Games, of course, are beautiful in that way. The past ten days or so, I’ve been exploring Australia — or, rather, Japanese game developer Hideo Kojima’s post-apocalyptic version of Australia. While frustrated with Ghost of Yotei’s spongier, more annoying combat than the first game, I can visit Japan too. There’s a lot of places I can go — places that aren’t just my apartment, or the doctor’s visit tomorrow, where I’ll be getting more physical therapy.</p>
<p>I am glad for games, truly. I love playing them when they’re strange and wonderful and weird and beautiful. I love going places, because as a disabled person with barely any money, I rarely get to.</p>
<p>It’s a shame, then, that the otherwise amazing world of Ghost of Tsushima had to be historically inaccurate in the way it is.</p>
<p>Playing Ghost of Yotei, I do think the game is trying its best to respect the Ainu culture (which is native to the area where it is set). The invidual quests have been delightful so far, some getting me to laugh my ass off because they’re so good. Narratively, this might be the best thing Sucker Punch has ever done. Too bad about the enemies having way too much health, but that’s okay. As games go, I’m spoiled for choice.</p>
<p>In Ghost of Yotei, I don’t think I’ve heard honor culture ideas mentioned even once.</p>
<p>It’s nice.</p>
