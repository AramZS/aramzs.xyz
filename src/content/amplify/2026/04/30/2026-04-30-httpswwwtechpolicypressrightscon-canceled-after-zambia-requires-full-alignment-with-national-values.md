<figure><img alt=" " data-nimg="1" src="https://cdn.sanity.io/images/3tzzh18d/production/ae854d287ddebe6b514c4e3720072ebc80d3f1e5-1200x675.png"/><figcaption>Felix Mutati, MP, Zambia’s Minister of Technology and Science, in a 2025 <a href="https://www.facebook.com/61553945632522/posts/the-minister-of-technology-and-science-hon-felix-c-mutati-mp-will-officially-lau/122257265174131521/">photo</a>. Earlier this week, Mutati <a href="https://www.facebook.com/100066659241624/posts/1313830454182250/?rdid=Yp22Qrxzh7nwjUwu#">announced</a> the government would seek to ‘postpone’ RightsCon just days before it was set to begin. </figcaption></figure>
<p>RightsCon, a global conference of thousands of advocates, technologists, academics, policymakers and others concerned with issues at the intersection of human rights and technology, was scheduled to kick off in Lusaka, Zambia, on May 5.</p>
<p>But with just days remaining and with many participants already en route, Zambian government officials announced they would <a href="https://www.facebook.com/100066659241624/posts/1313830454182250/?rdid=EL0QUGLy1Je8WOQJ#">postpone</a> the conference <a href="https://www.facebook.com/story.php?story_fbid=pfbid0YSNDNMcygu456iGDfF9GC6pX6TnBQEv15E7JtZG3NJZtZvddmB4y6F6hrceuqNd5l&amp;id=100069038505521&amp;mibextid=wwXIfr&amp;_rdr">in order</a> to “ensure full alignment with Zambia’s national values, policy priorities, and broader public interest considerations.”</p>
<figure><img alt=" " data-nimg="1" src="https://cdn.sanity.io/images/3tzzh18d/production/d49a3cf8ec8b5c0c38d81bd1fdb8514be66a98d6-1200x675.png"/><figcaption>Source: Zambia Ministry of Information and Media/<a href="https://www.facebook.com/story.php?story_fbid=pfbid0YSNDNMcygu456iGDfF9GC6pX6TnBQEv15E7JtZG3NJZtZvddmB4y6F6hrceuqNd5l&amp;id=100069038505521&amp;mibextid=wwXIfr&amp;_rdr">Facebook</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>On Wednesday, after attempting to negotiate a solution, RightsCon organizers <a href="https://www.rightscon.org/">canceled</a> the event. “We do not recommend registered participants travel to Lusaka for RightsCon,” an announcement said.</p>
<h2><strong>Mercurial ministers</strong></h2>
<p>As recently as last month, Zambia’s Ministry of Technology and Science indicated preparations were on track.</p>
<p>In a <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20260419122158/https://www.mots.gov.zm/?p=6792">post</a> on the ministry’s website, a government official welcomed the event and emphasized “its alignment with Zambia’s national development agenda.” Pictures depicted RightsCon director Nikki Gladstone briefing government officials along with Richard Mulonga, chief executive officer of <a href="https://bloggersofzambia.org/">Bloggers of Zambia</a>, an organization that campaigns for digital rights and free expression that served as a local civil society partner to the RightsCon organizers.</p>
<p>RightsCon was set to open just more than three months before Zambia's August 13 <a href="https://www.dw.com/en/africa-2026-elections-guide-ethiopia-south-africa-ramaphosa-uganda-zambia/a-75558971">general election</a>. Elected in 2021, President Hakainde Hichilema is seeking reelection amid <a href="https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/zambia-president-approves-law-expanding-parliament-months-before-election-2025-12-18/">complaints</a> around the expansion of the country’s parliament as part of a constitutional amendment package <a href="https://www.semafor.com/article/12/22/2025/zambia-amends-its-constitution-ahead-of-election">critics call</a> election engineering.</p>
<p>Zambia’s digital policies have drawn criticism, including a pair of new laws enacted last year that legal and human rights experts say <a href="https://www.journalofdemocracy.org/online-exclusive/how-zambias-cyber-laws-rebrand-repression/#author1">raise significant concerns</a> over government surveillance and repression of speech. Bloggers of Zambia <a href="https://drive.google.com/file/d/1zc5EmT431YgAhx2sEKd04GIiiSjOt5sy/view">is among</a> the domestic critics of the laws.</p>
<p>According to the nonprofit watchdog Freedom House, which ranks Zambia “partly free” on its 2025 Freedom on the Net index of digital rights and free expression, the government has <a href="https://freedomhouse.org/country/zambia/freedom-net/2025">imprisoned individuals</a> for online speech critical of the current government, and journalists are experiencing <a href="https://cpj.org/2026/04/zambian-lawyers-fight-for-media-freedom-as-journalists-harassed-ahead-of-election/">increased harassment</a>. (UNESCO plans to host a <a href="https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/world-press-freedom-day-2026-global-conference-shaping-future-peace">World Press Freedom Day event</a> in Lusaka May 4-5. The Facebook page for Zambia’s Ministry of Information and Media is still promoting the event as “back-to-back with RightsCon” in a header image on its <a href="https://www.facebook.com/people/Ministry-of-Information-and-Media-Zambia/100069038505521/">Facebook page</a>.)</p>
<h2><strong>Reactions from the digital rights community</strong></h2>
<p>Across the world, those planning to participate in the program and others in the field took to social media to <a href="https://bsky.app/profile/kendraserra.bsky.social/post/3mknm32shys22">express solidarity</a> with Access Now, the digital rights advocacy organization that convenes RightsCon, and disappointment that the gathering was canceled. Held for the past 14 years in various cities, RightsCon is a singularly important event for the field. (Tech Policy Press was scheduled to participate in two sessions during the week.)</p>
<p>“I feel for the staff of Access Now, all their partners and staff in Africa who have been pushing for a RightsCon to be held on the continent, and for human rights broadly,” <a href="https://bsky.app/profile/aliyabhatia.bsky.social/post/3mko5suymek2b">wrote</a> Aliya Bhatia, a senior policy analyst with Center for Democracy &amp; Technology’s Free Expression Project.</p>
<p>“The world is bleak. We find light in each other. Watching a government try to snuff that out is a serious blow, and it should be treated as one,” <a href="https://bsky.app/profile/mahsaalimardani.bsky.social/post/3mko4w5kdoc2s">wrote</a> Mahsa Alimardani, associate director of technology threats and opportunities at WITNESS.</p>
<p>“This is a serious attack on civil society,” <a href="https://bsky.app/profile/davidakaye.bsky.social/post/3mknkfzvi6222">wrote</a> David Kaye, a University of California, Irvine law professor and a former UN Special Rapporteur Rapporteur on the promotion and protection of the right to freedom of opinion and expression. “Thousands of people—activists, academics, government, etc.—are en route to Lusaka. To cancel at this late stage is just horrible.”</p>
<p>In a post on X, Paradigm Initiative, a <a href="https://paradigmhq.org/">nonprofit</a> delivering digital rights programs in Zambia as well as the Democratic Republic of Congo, Kenya, Nigeria, Senegal, and Zimbabwe, <a href="https://x.com/ParadigmHQ/status/2049613758029295951?s=20">said</a> it condemned the Zambian government’s actions “in the strongest terms.”</p>
<p>As 404 Media <a href="https://www.404media.co/rightscon-human-rights-conference-suddenly-postponed/">noted</a>, RightsCon has faced challenges in recent years due to adverse government actions. Travel to Lusaka was already <a href="https://www.rightscon.org/rc26-travel-gulf/">complicated</a> this year by the US-Israeli war in Iran. Some participating organizations have struggled with funding cuts due to the Trump administration’s <a href="https://www.techpolicy.press/with-us-commitment-to-internet-freedom-in-jeopardy-china-and-russia-set-to-gain/">demolition</a> of USAID and cancellation of State Department programs focused on internet freedom and democracy. And in 2023, as many as 300 participants from Global Majority countries were <a href="https://www.rightscon.org/letter-from-the-rightscon-team/?ref=404media.co">refused visas</a> when the event was hosted in Costa Rica.</p>
<p>Last year’s event, hosted in Taiwan, saw civil society leaders <a href="https://www.techpolicy.press/at-rightscon-human-rights-and-democracy-advocates-grapple-with-profound-change-in-world-order/">grappling</a> with a rapidly changing world order that is increasingly hostile to the people and ideas that RightsCon gathers. “The attacks are semantic, economic, and moral,” said Luisa Ortiz Pérez, executive director and co-founder of <a href="http://vita-activa.org">Vita-Activa.org</a>, in Taipei. “The moral injury is large.”</p>
