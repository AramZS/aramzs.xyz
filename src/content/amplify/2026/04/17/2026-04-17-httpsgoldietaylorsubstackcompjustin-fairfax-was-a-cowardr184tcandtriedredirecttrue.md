title: Justin Fairfax Was A Coward

<p>I believed Vanessa Tyson. I believed Meredith Watson. I believed them in 2019 when they told their stories, and I believe them today, standing over the fresh graves of a wife he murdered and the man he finally revealed himself to be.</p>
<p>I believed them because I was eleven years old the first time a man decided my body did not belong to me. I spent the next forty years learning what that boy took and what I had to build back by hand. I wrote a whole book about it because the silence was going to kill me before the memory did. So when two accomplished, educated Black women stood up in 2019 and said *this man did this to me*, I did not need a jury. I needed a mirror. I had already seen that face.</p>

<p>Justin Fairfax called them liars. He said he had been *lynched*. He invoked Emmett Till. He stood on the floor of the Virginia Senate and compared himself — a sitting lieutenant governor, Ivy-educated, a man with every microphone in the Commonwealth at his disposal — to a fourteen-year-old boy pulled from his bed and tortured to death for the crime of being Black in Mississippi. He compared himself to George Floyd. He wrapped his wounded pride in the shroud of our martyrs and asked us to carry him on our shoulders.</p>
<p>That was the moment I knew.</p>
<p>Not that he did it, necessarily — I already believed he did — but that he had no floor. No bottom. A man who will use Emmett Till to protect his political career will use anything to protect himself. Anything. Anyone.</p>
<p>Including, it turns out, the mother of his children.</p>
<p>Cerina Fairfax was a dentist. She was forty-something. She had filed for divorce. She was sleeping in a separate bedroom in the same house because that is what women do when they are trying to get out with the kids intact. She did the thing women are told to do. She followed the script. She hired the lawyer. She served the papers. She slept behind a closed door and hoped the man she married would be civilized enough to let her leave.</p>
<p>He was not.</p>
<p>He took her downstairs to the basement — because that is where cowards do their work, out of sight, away from the windows — and he shot her. Then he walked back upstairs, past the bedrooms where his teenage children were sleeping or trying to sleep, past whatever photographs of their family were still hanging on those walls, and he shot himself in what had been their marital bed. Their son, a boy who should have been worrying about a chemistry test, called 911 and told the dispatcher he thought his father had stabbed his mother. That is the sentence that child will carry for the rest of his life. That is the inheritance Justin Fairfax left his children. Not the Columbia Law degree. Not the lieutenant governor’s seal. A phone call in the dark.</p>
<p>I want to be clear about something, because I know how this works. There will be a certain kind of commentator, this week and next, who will want to talk about stress. About the divorce. About unpaid debts and the fall from grace and the weight of allegations that, they will remind us, were never proven in a court of law. They will want to make this a tragedy of a man overwhelmed.</p>
<p>It is not. It is a confession.</p>
<p>You do not murder your wife because you are stressed. You do not murder your wife because you lost a primary. You do not murder your wife because Capital One filed a warrant in debt against you for five thousand dollars. You murder your wife because you have always believed, at some rotted place in yourself, that her life was a thing you got to decide about. That her leaving was a thing you got to veto. That her body — like the bodies of the women who accused you years ago, like the body of whatever eleven-year-old girl on a bike was unlucky enough to cross a man like you on a summer afternoon — belonged to you.</p>
<p>Vanessa Tyson and Meredith Watson told us who he was. We had the information. A man who was willing to do what they said he did was always, always capable of this.</p>
<p>This is the part of the column where I am supposed to zoom out. Talk about the epidemic. Cite the statistic — three women a day in this country, killed by a current or former partner. Remind you that the most dangerous moment in an abusive relationship is the moment a woman tries to leave. Tell you that guns in a domestic violence home increase the risk of homicide by five hundred percent. All of that is true. All of that matters. Write it down.</p>
<p>But I am not in the mood to zoom out tonight. Tonight I am thinking about Cerina. I am thinking about a woman who built a practice, raised children, packed her own lunches, kept her own counsel, and decided she was done. I am thinking about the particular exhaustion of a woman who has already done the math and knows exactly how dangerous the next ninety days are going to be, and does it anyway because the alternative is a lifetime of small deaths instead of one big one. She was trying to save herself. She almost made it.</p>
<p>And I am thinking about two women, Vanessa and Meredith, who are somewhere tonight reading the news and feeling the specific, nauseous vindication of having been right about a man the world told them to shut up about. I hope somebody is with them tonight. I hope somebody is holding their hand. I hope they know that the rest of us — the ones who kept the receipts, the ones who never forgot their names — are standing with them.</p>
<p>Justin Fairfax wanted to be remembered as a rising star. A trailblazer. The second Black man elected statewide in Virginia. He wanted the Emmett Till comparison to stick.</p>
<p>It will not. I will not let it, and neither should you.</p>
<p>He was a coward. He was a coward when two women told the truth and he called them liars. He was a coward when he hid behind the bones of our murdered children to save his own career. He was a coward in that basement. He was a coward in that bedroom. He was a coward the whole way down.</p>
<p>History does not owe him the dignity he refused to give his wife.</p>
<p>And I, for one, am done pretending otherwise.</p>
