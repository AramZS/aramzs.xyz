<h3>The Uneven Reckoning That Emptied Monument Avenue and Killed the Confederate License Plate</h3>

<p>For more than a century and a half, Virginia was the Lost Cause’s most consecrated ground. As the former capital of the Confederacy, the Commonwealth did not merely tolerate Confederate mythmaking. It institutionalized it. Its boulevards were lined with bronze generals, its schoolchildren taught from textbooks that recast secession as a principled stand for states’ rights, and its state government lent its imprimatur to Confederate symbolism in the most public of spaces.</p>
<p>The <a href="https://www.13newsnow.com/article/news/state/virginia-governor-ends-renewal-of-plates-honoring-confederate-figures/291-0d8a3c55-7d47-4874-8e5a-69006aa03f35">signing of House Bill 1344 by Governor Abigail Spanberger</a> on Wednesday, which ended the renewal of license plates honoring Robert E. Lee and the Sons of Confederate Veterans, is, in one sense, a minor administrative act. In another sense, it is a fitting coda to a decade of accelerating reckoning, the latest step in Virginia’s long, uneven, and still-contested retreat from the Lost Cause’s grip on public life.</p>
<p>It couldn’t have occurred at a better moment: Confederate History Month.</p>
<p>The term “Lost Cause” itself originated with Virginian Edward Pollard’s 1866 book, <em><a href="https://archive.org/details/lostcausenewsout01poll/page/n5/mode/2up">The Lost Cause: A New Southern History of the War of the Confederates</a></em>, a justification of the Confederate war effort. Virginia was not merely a passive recipient of the mythology but, arguably, its birthplaces.</p>
<p>The Lost Cause manifested over many generations, from history textbooks to street names to various forms of memorialization. It matured in the late nineteenth century through historical writing, fiction, speeches, museums and shrines, reunions, monument building, funerals, magazines, and fundraising initiatives, with the United Daughters of the Confederacy eventually becoming its chief propagators.</p>

<p>In Virginia, this campaign found its most dramatic expression on Monument Avenue in Richmond, where, the political and business leaders of the former Confederate capital erected monuments of public memory, putting the Lost Cause myth front and center for more than a century.</p>
<p>The monuments were never simply commemorative. Although the Lost Cause originated in the immediate aftermath of the Civil War, it surged dramatically in the early 1900s in the era of Jim Crow laws and oppression, and again in the 1950s and 1960s, precisely the periods when Black Americans were asserting their civil rights most forcefully.</p>

<p>Of course, the timing was not coincidental. These were assertions of racial hierarchy dressed in the language of heritage and grief, and Virginia’s public landscape bore their imprint everywhere, eventually in the form of specialty license plates issued by the state’s own Department of Motor Vehicles.</p>
<p>The plates themselves had been addressed even before HB 1344. In 2015, Virginia’s Department of Motor Vehicles <a href="https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2015/08/07/430359408/virginia-recalling-specialty-license-plates-with-confederate-flag">ceased issuing Sons of Confederate Veterans plates</a> featuring the Confederate flag after a decision by a federal judge. But plates honoring Robert E. Lee personally and the organization more broadly continued to be renewed.</p>
<p>The more seismic transformation in Virginia’s relationship to the Lost Cause, however, came in the summer of 2020. The murder of George Floyd in Minneapolis ignited a national reckoning, and nowhere was its impact on public memory more dramatic than in Richmond. On June 10, 2020, a group of social justice activists pulled down the Jefferson Davis statue on Monument Avenue, and Richmond’s mayor moved quickly to remove the remaining city-owned Lost Cause statues, with the exception of the state-owned Lee statue, which did not come down until 2021 after an extended legal battle decided by the Supreme Court of Virginia.</p>

<p>The <a href="https://www.splcenter.org/presscenter/splc-reports-over-160-confederate-symbols-removed-2020/">scale of what Virginia shed in those years is difficult to overstate</a>. Before the 2020 protests, Virginia had 290 Confederate symbols, including 110 statues, according to a Southern Poverty Law Center report. The state removed or renamed 141, more than any other state in the nation. Richmond, which had twenty Confederate symbols including ten statues—half of them on Monument Avenue—lost all of them. The former Confederate capital had effectively dismantled its own monumental landscape.</p>
<p>What makes the vanity plate decision particularly resonant is what it represents symbolically. Monuments on public boulevards are a form of civic declaration. They assert that the community collectively honors these figures. License plates issued by a state government are something different and in some ways more intimate. They constitute a partnership between the state and the individual, a co-branding of private sentiment with public authority.</p>
<p>When Virginia issued Robert E. Lee plates, it was not merely tolerating a private preference but lending the Commonwealth’s name to it, affixing the state seal, in effect, to a Lost Cause tribute. Ending that practice withdraws the state from that partnership.</p>
<p>The arc of Virginia’s reckoning with the Lost Cause is neither straight nor finished. Some <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/dc-md-va/2026/01/31/virginia-confederate-statues-capitol-remove/">Confederate symbols remain</a>. Debates over names and statues and curricula continue. But the state’s trajectory, established dramatically in 2020 and consolidated in the years since, is unmistakable. The bronze generals are gone from Monument Avenue. The state no longer issues plates in Robert E. Lee’s honor. Legislators have argued that Virginia should modernize its laws to reflect who Virginians are today.</p>
<p>What Virginia is becoming, a state that treats its Confederate past as history to be grappled with honestly rather than mythology to be celebrated publicly, represents a profound, if still incomplete, departure from what it was for so long. The Spanberger license plate signing is a small piece of that larger transformation.</p>
<p>It is admittedly unremarkable in isolation, but it is meaningful in context, and legible only against the long shadow of what it is finally, gradually, replacing.</p>
