<p>I’m Tony Howard-Arias, and together with my wife Abby Howard, I run Black Tabby Games, an indie developer with a focus on choice-driven narrative games. You might know our work from <em>Slay the Princess</em>, or, if you’re looking for indie street cred, <em>Scarlet Hollow.</em></p>
<p>And if you’re reading this, it’s now been announced to the world that we’ve started a publishing arm! We’ve signed two games so far: <em>Prove You’re Human</em> — the sophomore title from Peabody-winning studio sunset visitor 斜陽過客 — as well as a yet-to-be-announced title by SmallBu, the twice-Emmy-winning animation duo behind <em>Later Alligator</em>, and if you were browsing YouTube at the same time as we were, <em>Baman Piderman</em>.</p>
<p>I want to take a moment to write about what we stand for as a publisher: what we’re doing, why we’re doing it, and what we think makes our approach unique and necessary during a tumultuous time in the games industry.</p>
<p><em>So… what are we doing?</em> We’re looking to use our marketing expertise and resources to help other developers with proven track records reach financial independence, so they can make what they want on their own terms.</p>
<p>We’re doing this by signing developer-friendly deals with a manageable number of studios. Even though Abby and I aren’t personally developing the games we publish, we want to make sure every game we sign feels like it belongs under the Black Tabby label. So you can expect any game in our curated catalog to have a narrative focus, a unique visual style, and a ton of heart and care poured into its development. You can also expect any game we publish to be some combination of funny, unsettling, and/or romantic.</p>
<p>Before we move on to the <strong>why</strong> of our new venture, I’d also like to elaborate a bit on the terms we offer, because it’s easy to say you’re being friendly to devs if you just say it with a smile while keeping your terms in a black box.</p>
<ul><li><strong>Deal size:</strong> Each of our deals are in the mid-six figure range (USD). We think this is an appropriate amount of funding that gives small teams flexibility while not requiring an explosive performance on launch to be profitable.</li><li><strong>Recuperation schedule: </strong>Our deal with sunset visitor 斜陽過客 is 70/30 pre-recoup, and 30/70 post-recoup.</li></ul>
<p>To those of you who don’t speak Publisher, this means that when <em>Prove You’re Human</em> releases, we’ll take 70% of the revenue that comes in until we make back the money we put into the project, after which point, we will only take 30% of sales.</p>
<ul><li>Some publishers add a “secondary” recoup period, where they either recuperate <em>with interest</em> or they have a sort of staircase of percentages based on sales. i.e., taking 70% of revenue until recuperation, and then taking 50% until making back 1.5x their initial investment, and then it flips in favor of the developer.</li></ul>
<p><strong>We don’t like this, so we’re not going to do it.</strong> We want to make sure studios we work with aren’t left unable to pay their bills due to an unnecessarily long recoup period.</p>
<p>We also don’t think any publisher should be taking more than 30% post-recoup. The developers are doing the majority of the work, even if the publisher is funding it, so they deserve the majority of a game’s success.</p>
<ul><li><strong>Rights revert to the developer three years after launch. </strong>A lot of contracts are for seven-year terms (or longer) with options for renewal. We think that three years after launch, a game is in a relatively stable state, and its creators should become its stewards for the remainder of its lifespan, including getting to keep all of the money it makes from that point forward! We don’t think longer terms automatically make a given deal bad, especially if developers want continued help managing the marketing for their games, but it’s an area where we feel comfortable providing an added benefit to the folks we work with.</li></ul>
<p>Doing this also puts us in a position where we don’t have to continue managing an ever-growing-library, freeing ourselves up to work with more teams, and, of course, remain focused on our primary goals: developing our own titles.</p>
<ul><li><strong>Marketing and editorial support.</strong> By only funding a small number of projects at any given time, we’re free to give the games we’ve signed the care and attention they deserve. Our goal is to do everything in our power to make sure that each game under our publishing label is a commercial success. If it feels like this should be a given for any game publisher, <strong>we agree</strong>, but that doesn’t reflect the reality of the business models governing most of the industry.</li></ul>
<p>The gulf between games that “do okay” and “hits” is massive. Looking at our own catalogue, at the time of writing, <em>Slay the Princess</em> has sold around 1.05 million units, and <em>Scarlet Hollow</em> has sold around 110k units. Despite our studio being around for six years, <em>Slay the Princess</em>, which released two and a half years ago, is directly responsible for over 80% of all of the money we’ve ever made.</p>
<p>It makes financial sense for publishers to prioritize hits over everything else on their roster.</p>
<p>To state the obvious, most publishers are only publishers. That is, their entire business model is predicated on titles they publish performing well enough that they not only fund future projects, but that they also fund the salaries of everyone they employ.</p>
<p>We believe this structure incentivizes them to “oversign” games. In other words, because hits are <em>so</em> valuable, and often hard to predict, they will sign more titles than they can adequately support in order to “fish” for the rare gem that will keep their business running. And once they find that gem, that’s where their attention goes.</p>
<p>Going back to our own titles, a standard publisher would be incentivized to pour their attention into <em>Slay the Princess</em> to make sure it was a continued success, which continues to fund the studio and keep people employed. And they would be disincentivized from spending too many resources on the Scarlet Hollows in their portfolio, which may not make enough to justify the time spent. This becomes a starker issue when there is one Slay the Princess and five Scarlet Hollows in a given portfolio, and people don’t have the hours in a day to pay attention to all of them.</p>
<p>Sometimes, it goes beyond being incentivized to do this. Signing more titles than a publisher can adequately manage sometimes becomes <strong>necessary</strong> to its survival as a business.</p>
<p>Our own core business is always going to be Black Tabby Games’ internally developed projects, meaning that our publishing arm won’t be chasing the endless growth desired by the modern video games industry. And while it would be naive to claim that every game we sign will be a breakout hit, <strong>we’re never going to sign more titles than we can give attention to</strong><em>.</em></p>
<p>This means that each game under the Black Tabby Publishing label will receive ongoing support from us, including collaboratively working on marketing, as well as editorial feedback from early stages to make sure that each title can connect with its audience.</p>
<p>Our approach to editorial feedback is similar to most book publishers: we provide external review to keep a project from going off the rails, but the artistic vision of the games we sign ultimately lies with their developers.</p>
<p>Remy from sunset visitor 斜陽過客, who comes from a performing arts background, likes to describe the role that Abby and I play here as something similar to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dramaturgy">dramaturgy</a>. By not being bogged down in the day-to-day activities of the writers’ room, we’re able to provide more distanced feedback and act as a collaborative sounding board.</p>
<ul><li><strong>Not</strong> <strong>demanding vertical slices to sign a deal. </strong>Investing in games is risky and expensive, and one of the ways publishers remove risk from themselves is by asking for “vertical slices” as part of a pitch.</li></ul>
<p>A “vertical slice” is essentially a small, playable section of your game that contains a bit of <em>every</em> gameplay element, so publishers can get a sense of how the big picture will feel before signing a deal. We have a few issues with this:</p>
<p><strong>Vertical slices require a large amount of upfront funding</strong>, between assets and programming. This means publishers aren’t supporting developers when they’re at the most vulnerable stages of work on a project.</p>
<p><strong>Rushing to finish a vertical slice stops developers from fully exploring an idea</strong>. <em>Prove You’re Human</em> is already turning out to be a much more compelling project than the initial pitch, and part of that comes from accepting a verbal pitch and giving a studio we already trust the space to explore the concept before bringing it together.</p>
<p>I don’t think this works for everyone, and this is a unique risk we’re taking that practically requires us to exclusively work with artists and developers who already have a number of finished projects under their belts.</p>
<p>This goes without saying, but our contracts also include a clause restricting the use of generative AI in the games we publish, a technology that runs antithetical to our values as a studio that hand-illustrates thousands of art assets for each of our titles.</p>
<p><strong>A quick word on the why:</strong></p>
<p>If it wasn’t clear from the previous section, we have our issues with the current publishing landscape. We don’t attribute much of this to malice or personal greed, but rather to the inevitabilities of an industry that becomes harder to survive in each year, between AAA budgets spiraling out of control and the expectations of exponential growth for new releases year over year.</p>
<p>We don’t think our model is going to “change publishing” or anything like that, but we are part of a larger wave of successful indies using their resources to help lift other indies, which, if enough people join in, hopefully can make at least some amount of difference in the industry (<a href="https://www.innersloth.com/outersloth/">Outersloth</a>, <a href="https://kineticgames.co.uk/news/announcing-kinetic-publishing">Kinetic</a>, and <a href="https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/poncle-launches-publishing-arm-for-third-party-developers">Poncle</a> to name a few).</p>
<p>For our own specific efforts, we’re addressing what feels like an underserved niche of established studios getting swept up in industry-wide trends and winding up on the receiving end of increasingly worse deals despite their proven track records.</p>
<p>Our model is predicated on being able to trust studios we sign as collaborators. It would be foolish to think everyone with a game design document can be trusted to deliver a good finished product, let alone on time and within budget. But I am confident we can trust sunset visitor 斜陽過客 and SmallBu, and we’re sure we’ll be able to find other trusted partners through the years, as well.</p>
<p>Hopefully we’ll be able to give them all the same opportunities that <em>Slay the Princess</em> has given us.</p>
<p>Thanks for reading! This wound up being longer than I thought it was going to be, but I guess we have a lot to say about it. If you’re still here, don’t forget to wishlist <em>Prove You’re Human</em> on Steam.</p>
<p>If you’re interested in pitching us, you can email your deck to <a href="mailto:publishing@blacktabbygames.com">publishing@blacktabbygames.com</a>. If we don’t get back to you, please don’t take it as a reflection of your work! Our time and energy is still primarily focused on developing our internal projects, and we want to make sure we’re prioritizing our current commitments rather than taking on more than we can handle.</p>
