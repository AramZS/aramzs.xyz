<h3>Or I how I learned to start worrying and love Claude Code</h3>

<p><em>This is an AI-themed post about my adventures with Claude Code</em>. <em>I‚Äôve been‚Ä¶err.. vibe-podcasting about AI in two recent episodes of Rethink, on the promise of AI <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/m002q39b">here</a>, and on authenticity <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/sounds/play/m002qthx">here</a>, where you can hear fake Ben Ansell. Ben AInsell if you will.</em></p>
<p>There are a few moments when the sheer volume of possibility overwhelms you. It‚Äôs the feeling you get sometimes if you walk into an enormous bookshop or library. So much knowledge out there, so impossible to attain even a fraction. Or perhaps it‚Äôs that feeling that us nineties kids will remember of when the ‚ÄòWorld Wide Web‚Äô first became popular and a few key strokes connected you with endless other minds.</p>
<p>My favourite lines in literature about this (possibly just my favourite lines, full stop) are ones many of you will know already. From the final pages of The Great Gatsby:</p>
<blockquote><p>Most of the big shore places were closed now and there were hardly any lights except the shadowy, moving glow of a ferryboat across the Sound. And as the moon rose higher the inessential houses began to melt away until gradually I became aware of the old island here that flowered once for Dutch sailors' eyes‚Äîa fresh, green breast of the new world. Its vanished trees, the trees that had made way for Gatsby's house, had once pandered in whispers to the last and greatest of all human dreams; for a transitory enchanted moment man must have held his breath in the presence of this continent, compelled into an aesthetic contemplation he neither understood nor desired, face to face for the last time in history with something commensurate to his capacity for wonder.</p></blockquote>
<p>Shivers.</p>
<p>Are we at another ‚Äòlast and greatest of all human dreams‚Äô? The genius of Fitzgerald‚Äôs writing here is it conveys not only nostalgia - none of us can ever ‚Äòrediscover‚Äô the American continent - but also possibility. Because, reading this we all know other such moments have happened afterwards - imagine the image of the earth seen by Yuri Gagarin. There is no ‚Äòlast time in history‚Äô for wonder it turns out.</p>
<p>Anyway, there‚Äôs probably a reason I didn‚Äôt do English A-Level, so enough riffing on metaphor and possibility from me. What I want to convey though, is one of those moments where you realise that the brakes have been taken off, that you can suddenly do far more, understand far more, change far more, than you thought you could a day ago. And then the question comes, well what do you do with that?</p>
<p>Just as scrolling the ‚ÄòWorld Wide Web‚Äô all day turns out to be bad for your brain; just as spending every waking hour reading in the library turns out to be bad for your eyes; will the arrival of agentic AI, turn out to be bad for our willpower, our sense of direction, our own‚Ä¶ well‚Ä¶ agency?</p>
<p>That was all a typically winding way to get to the subject of today‚Äôs post - my experiences over the past month with Claude Code.</p>
<p>I am very far from the first person to write such a piece. Indeed, I feel I am just adding to a Borgesesque library of such Substack posts. But what I don‚Äôt think most of the writing about Claude Code has conveyed is how you actually engage with it and what it does when you do.</p>
<p>In other words, I want to show, not tell. And maybe come back to what this all means after doing that.</p>
<p>And the easiest way I thought of to do that was to create a website showing the kinds of things you can do with Claude Code. Oh and spoiler‚Ä¶ I didn‚Äôt actually create the website, Claude Code did.</p>
<p><a href="https://benwansell.github.io">Here</a> it is. I have kept the front page very simple. There are six links to different projects. Feel free to start mucking around. I‚Äôll come back to them.</p>
<p>But before I do, I think it‚Äôs worth trying to explain what exactly Claude Code is.</p>
<p>It‚Äôs a golem that lives in your computer‚Äôs terminal and creates files for you.</p>
<p>You might wonder first off, what on earth the ‚Äòterminal is‚Äô. Fair point. Here‚Äôs a picture of my one.</p>

<p>Compelling.</p>
<p>I use a Mac and the Terminal app basically allows you to write commands in the computer language Unix that do the basics of computing: create files and folders, delete files and folders (important not to screw up‚Ä¶), download and run applications, process commands in some applications, and compile and run commands in other software languages including Python and R (the two I will be talking about today).</p>
<p>People substantially more skilled at computing than me might well use the Terminal to directly run a lot of their code. I‚Äôm a mere baby and so would prefer to use an IDE (Integrated Development Environment) such as RStudio or PyCharm, which provide both text editors for coding and drop down menus for such fripperies as saving your file or looking at data.</p>
<p>So does this mean Claude Code forces you to become a super-skilled hacker type from 1980s movies, mashing in instructions in green text on a dark screen? No. But it kind of is one. Claude Code is going to be working within the terminal, typing Unix commands, or developing code in other software languages and then compiling it in Unix. You have your own private 80s style hacker.</p>
<p>Your own interaction with the Terminal is twofold. First you need to open it to get Claude Code going by (once you‚Äôve installed it) typing the word ‚Äòclaude‚Äô. Second, your conversations with Claude Code are going to be done through the Terminal, much like your conversations with the chatbot Claude or ChatGPT are done through a web browser. Here‚Äôs what that process looks like.</p>

<p>I‚Äôve done two things here. The first is to type ‚Äúcd ~/Downloads‚Äù. ‚Äúcd‚Äù means change directory. Why am I telling you this? Because a crucial thing about Claude Code is it needs your permission to access a working directory. This is to stop it going places on your computer that it shouldn‚Äôt and potentially wrecking havoc. So I am putting it in my Downloads folder, which is where it will look for files, create files and folders and potentially delete them. And I had to previously give it permission to do that (it doesn‚Äôt ask every time if you‚Äôve done so beforehand).</p>
<p>The second thing I did once in this directory was to activate Claude Code by typing ‚Äúclaude‚Äù. This brought up the delightfully 1980s imagery. Notice it says Opus 4.6. That‚Äôs the current advanced model that Claude Code uses but if you want it to absorb fewer ‚Äòtokens‚Äô (more later), you can set it to other simpler models such as Sonnet.</p>
<p>Below that is the input line which currently says ‚ÄòTry ‚Äúfix typecheck errors‚Äù‚Äô. Don‚Äôt know what means so I am going to do something else extremely hackneyed - the old ‚ÄúHello World‚Äù request. Let‚Äôs go!</p>

Importantly, it also asks me if I want to actually do that, by pressing 1, 2, or 3. This permission structure is really important - it stops Claude Code from going off and just creating (or more worryingly, deleting) things without your say so. Of course if I pressed 2 I would give it permission not only for this but for related edits and while that‚Äôs usually fine it very much depends on the context.</p>

<p>So now we see it wrote the code (all two lines of it) to a file it created called ‚Äúhello.py‚Äù and then the ‚Äòbash‚Äô command tells us that it ran a Python compiler, and then below that we see the exciting output. It says it worked for 1m and 52s but the only reason it took so long is because I am writing this on a train and was out of internet range for approximately 1m and 51s. It then provides me with a suggestion for a new edit (looping five times). I‚Äôll spare you that.</p>
<p>Boring example but‚Ä¶ it shows a few key things about Claude Code. Firstly, that you speak to it in normal language, which it interprets as instructions for how to manipulate code. Second, that it asks permission at every step, including file creation and running code. And it really did create this file in my Downloads directory. Third, it provides some suggestions for next steps, creating a mildly addictive interaction process. And fourth‚Ä¶ well, did you spot it?</p>
<p>I never asked Claude Code to add an exclamation mark or a comma. But it did. Because that‚Äôs how the ‚ÄúHello, World!‚Äù program usually <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%22Hello,_World!%22_program">works</a>. In other words, there was a Generative AI process going on here too, where it took my command, interpreted it, and then did what it thought I wanted as opposed to what I literally said. That it turns out is the killer app. It not only follows your instructions but it interprets them, extends them, maybe warps them. It is a form of LLM after all.</p>
<p>Let‚Äôs now return to the <a href="https://benwansell.github.io">website</a> I created with six sample projects. Each of these took rather longer than one second (or indeed 1m 52s). But not usually much more than ten to fifteen minutes. They also absorbed a lot of ‚Äòtokens‚Äô. Tokens are the cost of using Claude Code.</p>
<p>I have a Claude Pro subscription - ¬£180 for a year - which provides me with a set amount of tokens I can use every five hours, and cumulatively every week. It then resets the token allowance at the end of each of these periods. Five hours sounds like a lot of time and tokens. It‚Äôs not. Doing something intensive with Claude Code for half an hour will probably use up your five hour allowance, so you will need to wait another four and a half hours. You can of course pay Claude more money for the privilege of carrying on when you hit your limit. How pricy? Depends on what you‚Äôre doing? Most people seem to throw an extra $5-10 at it to complete what they are currently doing. That could get pricy fast. With that said, I didn‚Äôt crack into this to make any of these projects - they all completed within the five hour token window.</p>
<p>If you need more tokens you might want a Claude Max subscription but now you are spending ¬£90 a month and there are still (much higher) limits. No free lunches and all that. And yes, this is very data intensive and so likely also energy intensive and that‚Äôs why all those data centres are being built and no-one can buy an SD card for love nor money. In my own little way I guess I am contributing to this global problem. I agree. Same thing when I get in my car, or leave the lights on, etc etc. As a society these are the costs that we will ultimately need to be trading off against the benefits of AI. And that is a much longer article.</p>

<p>On to the next thing. I thought I would now try some agent-based modelling. And so I went to the classic: Conway‚Äôs Game of Life. This is a super-interesting idea that John Conway came up with in Scientific American that could be done with a piece of grid paper. The idea was you fill in some squares on a grid and then use the following algorithm. If a square has fewer than two neighbours (in the eight possible squares near it) then it ‚Äòdies‚Äô of isolation. If it has more than three it also ‚Äòdies‚Äô from over-population. If it has two or three neighbours it‚Äôs in the Goldilocks zone and it lives. AND if an empty square has three neighbours it comes alive, as if through reproduction.</p>
<p>What is cool about Conway‚Äôs game is that with a very simple set of rules it can produce extremely different outcomes across the grid over various generations from tiny initial differences. It also allows for ‚Äòreplicators‚Äô - groups of cells that survive and can move around the grid.</p>
<p>Simple rules, simple presentation. Sounds like an algorithm that would be easy for Claude Code to produce. And indeed all I had to do was ask it to do that ‚Äúcan you create me a small video game that is based around conway‚Äôs game of life‚Äù. And it created me this entire <a href="https://benwansell.github.io/game-of-life/">website</a>.</p>

<p>People in the Valley can‚Äôt stop agenting. The only limitation in an endless factory of agents doing stuff for you is tokens - the price of using an agent. If you had unlimited tokens then I guess you could have unlimited agents doing unlimited stuff.</p>
<p>But what would that stuff be? Why would you want to do it? The analogy I used earlier of being in a library and being overwhelmed by the number of books isn‚Äôt quite right. Because those books were written by someone else - you are being overwhelmed by all the myriad knowledge already existing in the world.</p>
<p>But non-stop agenting, that‚Äôs as if you were overwhelmed in the library by books that you had asked to be written.</p>
<p>Why are you writing all these books? Who are they for? Can anyone possible consume, even understand all the apps and documents being produced by agents? I mean maybe the agents themselves can. But to what end?</p>
<p>The title of this post is ‚Äòthe Apprentice‚Äôs Sorcerer‚Äô. Yes I did that literary thing of flipping the normal ‚ÄòSorcerer‚Äôs Apprentice‚Äô. But I didn‚Äôt do this (just) to be clever. The myth of the sorcerer‚Äôs apprentice is of a wise magician creating a follower they cannot control. In Fantasia, this is Mickey and his out of control brooms. I think that‚Äôs how we often think about AI. Us clever humans unwittingly become Dr Frankenstein, building a monster that escapes our chains.</p>
<p>What if we are just not that clever but the agents really are? We are a mere apprentice unleashing sorcerers upon the world. We give vague instructions, the sorcerer gives us what we really meant but couldn‚Äôt express. We are Frankenstein building a Doctor, who in turn can build new Frankensteins. We are out of our league.</p>
<p>From an academic perspective, we sort of know what we want the agent to accomplish, but like a superior graduate student they rapidly outpace us. Do we really understand the results that we produced? Isn‚Äôt it more comfortable to just assume our magical agent got things right? If there are hallucinations in the code, will we ever be able to catch them?</p>
<p>Like many social scientists I have ever-changing views about the promise of AI. I veer from, it‚Äôs useless, hallucinations, etc etc to it‚Äôs life-changing, look at my pretty pdfs. When I worry about it, I worry about my kids getting jobs (though obviously I‚Äôd secretly like them to live at home with me forever anyway) or I worry about some kind of Skynetesque AI doomer scenario.</p>
<p>But maybe what I should be worried about is the over-abundance of wonder - of endless moments where mankind is ‚Äúface to face‚Äù but it turns out very much <em>not</em> ‚Äúfor the last time in history‚Äù with ‚Äúsomething commensurate to his capacity for wonder‚Äù. Can we come to terms with unlimited creative possibilities? Who is going to appreciate all the wondrous outputs of our agents? Who even has the time?</p>
<p>Perhaps it is pointless to resist. But I expect something innately human in us will do so. We will struggle against all the wondrous possibilities our agents provide, we will thrash impotently, we will push back subconsciously, viscerally, animally.</p>
<p>To paraphrase Fitzgerald, we will still desperately ‚Äúbeat on, boats against the current‚Äù but this time ‚Äúborne forward ceaselessly into the future.‚Äù</p>
