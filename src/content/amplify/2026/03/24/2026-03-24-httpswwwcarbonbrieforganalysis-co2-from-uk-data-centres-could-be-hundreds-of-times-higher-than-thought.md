<p>Emissions from the new data centres set to drive the UK’s AI “<a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/news/ai-to-power-national-renewal-as-government-announces-billions-of-additional-investment-and-new-plans-to-boost-uk-businesses-jobs-and-innovation">revolution</a>” could be hundreds of times higher than government estimates, according to analysis by Carbon Brief.</p>
<p>There are dozens of data centres being <a href="https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/sites/default/files/2026-02/2026-02-12-Demand-Connections-Call-for-Input.pdf">developed</a> across the country, potentially driving a surge in electricity demand.</p>
<p>Amid <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/committee/62/environmental-audit-committee/news/212132/mps-to-examine-data-centres-climate-impact-as-miliband-says-future-demand-inherently-uncertain/">uncertainty</a> about the scale and pace of this expansion, there are <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/committee/62/environmental-audit-committee/news/212132/mps-to-examine-data-centres-climate-impact-as-miliband-says-future-demand-inherently-uncertain/">mounting concerns</a> that new data centres could pose a threat to the nation’s climate goals.</p>
<p>UK government <a href="https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/687f74f4fdc190fb6b846868/compute-evidence-annex-final.pdf">analysis</a> concluded that the emissions from data centres would be negligible, even if they expand rapidly – a finding one campaigner tells Carbon Brief is “nonsense”.</p>
<p>In contrast, Carbon Brief analysis finds that emissions from powering data centres could be far higher than the government figures suggest, if at least a small amount of the electricity they need is generated by burning gas.</p>
<p>Data centres could run entirely on low-carbon electricity, but <a href="https://www.politico.eu/article/uk-ai-energy-council-climate-change-gas-data-centers/">some</a> in the <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/business/2025/09/17/britain-ai-investments-will-need-gas-nvidia-warns-miliband/">sector</a> have argued that the government’s AI ambitions require the UK to use more gas power.</p>
<p>If new data centres source a large amount of their power from gas, it could cause carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions equivalent to at least Denmark’s annual total.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/ai-five-charts-that-put-data-centre-energy-use-and-emissions-into-context/">Data centres</a> are energy-intensive computing facilities that are required to train and run complex AI models, among many other things.</p>
<p>The UK is one of the <a href="https://www.energy-uk.org.uk/publications/powering-the-cloud-how-data-centres-can-deliver-sustainable-growth/#_ftn1">top-ranking</a> nations for data-centre capacity, with <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/estimate-of-data-centre-capacity-great-britain-2024/estimate-of-data-centre-capacity-great-britain-2024">roughly</a> 1.8 gigawatts (GW) of facilities <a href="https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10315/">consuming</a> more than 2% of national electricity. This could <a href="https://www.neso.energy/news/our-clean-power-2030-advice-government">grow rapidly</a> in the coming years as the government aims to make the UK an “<a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-opportunities-action-plan-government-response/ai-opportunities-action-plan-government-response">AI superpower</a>”.</p>
<p>Companies have already “achieved financial commitment” to invest in 71 new data centres that, if built, would require around 20GW of electricity, <a href="https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/sites/default/files/2026-02/2026-02-12-Demand-Connections-Call-for-Input.pdf">according</a> to energy regulator <a href="https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/">Ofgem</a>.</p>
<p>(For reference, the UK’s average <a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/analysis-uk-renewables-enjoy-record-year-in-2025-but-gas-power-still-rises/">electricity demand</a> in 2025 stood at around 37GW.)</p>
<p>This potential <a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/net-zero-scenario-is-cheapest-option-for-uk-says-energy-system-operator/">increase</a> in electricity demand has raised <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/writtenevidence/151599/html/">concerns</a> from campaigners and some <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/c5yjr9mp92po">MPs</a> about the impact of data centres on the UK’s climate targets.</p>
<p>Last year, the government’s <a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/qa-the-uk-governments-carbon-budget-delivery-plan-for-2035/">plan</a> for meeting its 2035 climate target noted that AI growth was “not factored into” emissions projections, although energy secretary Ed Miliband has <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/publications/51827/documents/287568/default/">said</a> new data centres are captured in modelling of “overall electricity demand growth”.</p>
<p>The government is targeting a “<a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/analysis-how-the-uk-plans-to-reach-clean-power-by-2030/">clean power system</a>” by 2030, with just a small amount of gas generation remaining. Extra demand from new data centres could require a rollout of clean power that is <a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/net-zero-scenario-is-cheapest-option-for-uk-says-energy-system-operator/">even faster</a> than the growth <a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/qa-new-uk-onshore-wind-and-solar-is-50-cheaper-than-new-gas/">already underway</a>.</p>
<p>If clean-power growth does not keep pace, data centres could, therefore, prolong the use of gas power, either by requiring more gas to remain on the grid or by facilities building <a href="https://utilityweek.co.uk/electricity-constraints-forcing-data-centres-to-turn-to-gas-grid/">their own</a> on-site gas generation.</p>
<p>There is <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/committee/62/environmental-audit-committee/news/212132/mps-to-examine-data-centres-climate-impact-as-miliband-says-future-demand-inherently-uncertain/">significant uncertainty</a> around future emissions from UK data centres, which will depend on the number of centres built, how clean their power is and when they come online.</p>
<p>The government published an analysis of its AI strategy’s climate impact last year, alongside a data-centre “<a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/uk-compute-roadmap/uk-compute-roadmap">roadmap</a>”.</p>
<p>The analysis, released by the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/department-for-science-innovation-and-technology">Department for Science, Innovation and Technology</a> (DSIT) suggests emissions from future data centres will be minimal – reaching a maximum of 0.142m tonnes of CO2 (MtCO2) from 11.2GW of AI-related computing power by 2035.</p>
<p>(There is an additional 2.4GW of data-centre demand in this scenario that is not associated with AI, for which emissions are not calculated.)</p>
<p>This figure is based on what DSIT describes as a high-emissions, high-AI growth scenario. Yet it implies that each unit (kilowatt hour, kWh) of electricity supplied to the 11.2GW of AI data centres would be associated with less than 2g of CO2. In other words, their electricity supply would need to be almost completely decarbonised. The government <a href="https://www.carbonbrief.org/analysis-how-the-uk-plans-to-reach-clean-power-by-2030/">aim</a> is for 50gCO2/kWh by 2030.</p>
<p>In addition, the DSIT figure – for emissions associated with the entire UK data centre fleet in 2035 – is much lower than the <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/writtenevidence/151599/html/#_ftn14">emissions estimates</a> reported in planning applications for individual UK <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2025/sep/15/google-datacentre-kent-co2-thurrock-uk-ai">data centres</a> made by Google and other companies.</p>
<p>The chart below, based on Carbon Brief analysis, shows how data-centre emissions could be far higher than the government’s figures suggest.</p>
<p>Even if gas-fired electricity only accounts for 5% of their supply – indicated by the smallest blue column below – emissions from 11.2GW of data centres would be around 2MtCO2. This is more than 10 times higher than the government’s top estimate for 2035.</p>
<p>If the same data centres rely more heavily on gas, emissions could be hundreds of times higher, exceeding 30MtCO2. This is roughly equivalent to the <a href="https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/europe-environment-2025/countries/denmark/total-greenhouse-gas-emissions">annual emissions</a> of Denmark. Emissions could rise even higher if capacity increases in line with the extra 20GW of data-centre demand that Ofgem <a href="https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/">says</a> is in the pipeline, as indicated by the red columns</p>

<p>If data-centre expansion reaches 20GW and those centres rely heavily on gas power, then the figure could be as high as 70MtCO2, the annual emissions of Sweden. This would also be nearly 500 times higher than the government’s upper estimate, which it says is based on a “pessimistic decarbonisation” scenario.</p>
<p>(The numbers are not directly comparable as, unlike the AI-specific 11.2GW figure, it is unclear how much of this 20GW would be for AI, specifically.)</p>
<p>The government’s modelling states that AI emissions in 2035 would be “equivalent to below 0.05% of the UK’s <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/energy-and-emissions-projections-2023-to-2050">projected total emissions</a>”. It also says “this could be equivalent to the annual emissions of approximately 5,000 to 23,600 UK <a href="https://www.lifetimesavings.co.uk/average-co2-emissions-per-household/">households</a>”.</p>
<p>On the contrary, Carbon Brief’s analysis suggests data centres could, in fact, be equivalent to as much as 20% of the UK’s projected total emissions in 2035.</p>
<p>As for the number of households, Carbon Brief estimates that future data centres could result in emissions equivalent to as many as 11.4m homes, roughly a third of all UK households.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.foxglove.org.uk/tim-squirrell-head-of-strategy/">Dr Tim Squirrel</a>, head of strategy at <a href="https://www.foxglove.org.uk/">Foxglove</a> – part of an NGO group <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/writtenevidence/151599/html/#_ftn14">calling for</a> more government scrutiny of data-centre emissions – tells Carbon Brief the DSIT figures are “nonsense and threaten to derail our carbon budgets”. He says:</p>
<blockquote> <p>“The figures that DSIT projects here wildly downplay data-centre emissions, even by the standards of the most optimistic energy transition scenario. There is no way that the amount of compute they anticipate can be built and produce the miniscule emissions they’re calculating.”</p> </blockquote>
<p>In its analysis, the government <a href="https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/687f74f4fdc190fb6b846868/compute-evidence-annex-final.pdf">attributes</a> the low emissions figures to “more efficient models and hardware” and “the UK’s ambitious targets for electricity grid decarbonisation”.</p>
<p>When asked by Carbon Brief, DSIT declined to provide any more information about its analysis.</p>
<p>While the UK is <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/news/government-to-tackle-speculative-demand-grid-connection-requests">prioritising</a> data centres for AI, there is mounting industry <a href="https://www.politico.eu/article/uk-ai-energy-council-climate-change-gas-data-centers/">pressure</a> to <a href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/business/2025/09/17/britain-ai-investments-will-need-gas-nvidia-warns-miliband/">allow</a> gas-power expansion for this “<a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/c23ljy4z05mo">critical</a>” infrastructure, as is happening in, for example, the <a href="https://globalenergymonitor.org/report/betting-big-on-data-centers-u-s-now-leads-world-for-new-gas-power-development/">US</a> and <a href="https://www.theregister.com/2023/07/22/microsoft_power_plant/">Ireland</a>.</p>
<p>Developers in the UK have <a href="https://utilityweek.co.uk/electricity-constraints-forcing-data-centres-to-turn-to-gas-grid/">reportedly</a> already “turned to gas” via private electricity supplies, due to struggles securing a connection to the public network.</p>
<p>Yet, new data centres could be completely emissions-free if they are powered entirely with on-site clean energy or using electricity from a decarbonised grid.</p>
<p>As it stands, most data centres are connected to the electricity grid. <a href="https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10315/">Some</a> enter <a href="https://solarenergyuk.org/resource/power-purchase-agreements/">power purchase agreements</a> (PPAs) in which they financially support renewable-energy operators, allowing them to describe their electricity as clean.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.techuk.org/who-we-are/our-team.html">Katie Davies</a>, head of energy and infrastructure policy at <a href="https://www.techuk.org/">techUK</a>, a trade association representing the technology sector, highlights this expansion of PPAs as important for driving the growth of wind and solar power:</p>
<blockquote> <p>“In doing so, data centres actively contribute to additionality by unlocking extra carbon-free capacity that might not otherwise come online.”</p> </blockquote>
<p>A <a href="https://auroraer.com/resources/aurora-insights/newsletters/can-the-grid-keep-up-new-study-probes-uk-data-centre-growth">report</a> last year by <a href="https://auroraer.com/">Aurora Energy Research</a> found that data centres could provide a “route-to-market” worth up to £35bn for 19GW of UK renewables. However, it added:</p>
<blockquote> <p>“If renewables capacity and networks don’t keep pace, additional data centre demand will likely be met by carbon-intensive sources of generation.”</p> </blockquote>
<p>The UK’s “<a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-opportunities-action-plan/ai-opportunities-action-plan">AI opportunities action plan</a>” includes the establishment of “<a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/ai-growth-zones">AI growth zones</a>“, which the government says will be in areas with “available clean energy”. It is also <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/news/government-to-tackle-speculative-demand-grid-connection-requests">overhauling</a> the grid connection queue, which Davies says is important:</p>
<blockquote> <p>“Reducing this queue through strategic alignment and the removal of speculative applications will be vital to ensuring [data-centre] operators do not have to turn to higher-carbon energy sources as a last resort.”</p> </blockquote>
<p>Responding to Carbon Brief’s analysis, a government spokesperson said:</p>
<blockquote> <p>“We want the UK to be at the forefront of AI, but we are clear this must be done sustainably. That is why our AI growth zones are supporting development in areas with access to clean power, while the AI Energy Council is exploring how AI can be powered by responsible, clean-energy sources.”</p> </blockquote>
<p>There is considerable uncertainty around data-centre power demand and emissions, with much of the relevant information <a href="https://auroraer.com/resources/aurora-insights/newsletters/can-the-grid-keep-up-new-study-probes-uk-data-centre-growth">not in</a> the public domain. Carbon Brief has performed some rough calculations based on available data.</p>
<p>The government figure comes from an <a href="https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/687f74f4fdc190fb6b846868/compute-evidence-annex-final.pdf">annex</a> to DSIT’s <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/uk-compute-roadmap/uk-compute-roadmap">UK compute roadmap</a>. DSIT analyses the emissions impact of expanding the UK’s data-centre capacity to between 7.4GW in a “low compute-demand scenario” and 13.6GW in a “high compute-demand scenario” by 2035. (The majority of the demand in each scenario is from AI.)</p>
<p>DSIT also uses an “AI environmental impacts model” to estimate the greenhouse gas emissions from AI compute, only covering the 11.2GW AI component of data-centre capacity. It concludes that AI emissions in 2035 could range from 0.025MtCO2 to 0.142MtCO2. This includes both “direct” and “indirect” emissions, indicating that it covers more than just emissions from the electricity used to power the data centres.</p>
<p>A <a href="https://www.thetimes.com/uk/environment/article/ai-data-centres-uk-climate-change-7l5bwnmtd">widely</a> <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/feb/23/new-datacentres-risk-doubling-uk-electricity-use-ofgem-peak-demand">reported</a> <a href="https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/sites/default/files/2026-02/2026-02-12-Demand-Connections-Call-for-Input.pdf">consultation</a> by the energy regulator, <a href="https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/">Ofgem</a>, found that there are proposals for around 140 new data centres in the UK, which would require 50GW of electricity if they were all built.</p>
<p>In reality, it is highly unlikely that all of these data centres will be completed, with a “<a href="https://www.ofgem.gov.uk/sites/default/files/2026-02/2026-02-12-Demand-Connections-Call-for-Input.pdf">significant number</a>” expected to fail when trying to secure funding or planning permission.</p>
<p>The 20GW figure used in this analysis is based on the 71 “mature” projects that have “achieved financial commitment with final investment decision”, according to Ofgem.</p>
<p>Carbon Brief used the top government figure of 0.142MtCO2, even though it represents a “pessimistic grid decarbonisation” and “high compute demand” scenario.</p>
<p>To calculate the emissions from powering data centres in the future, Carbon Brief assumes a data-centre “load factor” of 90%, which is in line with <a href="https://www.areswms.com/accessares/fast-take/convergence-ai-renewable-energy-and-digital-infrastructure">other</a> <a href="https://energyfuturesgroup.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/Review-of-Large-Load-Tariffs-to-Identify-Safeguards-and-Protections-for-Existing-Ratepayers-Report-Final.pdf">analyses</a>. The analysis uses different shares of gas in the centres’ power supplies to indicate a range of future possibilities, assuming emissions from gas power are 0.4MtCO2 per terawatt hour.</p>
