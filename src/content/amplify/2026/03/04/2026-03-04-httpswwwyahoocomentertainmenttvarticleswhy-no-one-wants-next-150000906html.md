title: Why no one wants to be the next Doctor Who

<p>He who pays the Piper… has to wait until she’s finished making her latest Netflix show. That seems to be the case with <em>Doctor Who</em>, which, with a 2026 Christmas special in the offing featuring Billie Piper’s return to the Whoniverse – <a data-ylk="slk:as spectacularly teased at the end of the most recent season;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/0/billie-piper-to-be-next-doctor-who/">as spectacularly teased at the end of the most recent season</a> – will have to hold off until she’s done shooting series three of <em>Wednesday</em> in Wicklow, Ireland.</p>
<p>Exactly when Piper will be available is just one of myriad uncertainties engulfing the BBC’s biggest franchise, which continues to be buffeted like the Tardis in a time warp as it readjusts to life after a traumatising team-up – and then divorce – with Disney+.</p>
<p>The latest word – as reported in the Sun – is that, whatever else she will be up to when she returns to <em>Doctor Who</em>, Piper will not be playing the Doctor. This raises the question of why she was shown taking up the mantle from the departing Ncuti Gatwa as he signed off with the traditional transformation in May 2025.</p>
<p>Credit: BBC Studios; Bad Wolf; Disney+</p>
<p>If we can’t believe in a <em>Doctor Who </em>regeneration scene – a staple of British broadcasting all the way back to the <a data-ylk="slk:first one in October 1966;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/tv/0/doctor-who-first-christmas-special/">first one in October 1966</a> (when William Hartnell transformed into Patrick Troughton) – what can we believe in anymore?</p>
<p>Assuming that Piper isn’t playing the Doctor, it does open the possibility that she is back as some version of her character Rose Tyler – Christopher Eccleston’s sidekick during his one unhappy season in 2005. In which case, who is to be the new Doctor, and how can the BBC rejuvenate a franchise that risks becoming lost in televisual time and space after fan backlash against Gatwa and his predecessor, Jodie Whittaker?</p>
<figure><img alt="Billie Piper (right) as Rose Tyler in Doctor Who in 2005, alongside Christopher Eccleston's Doctor (centre) and Bruno Langley as Adam" data-nimg="1" src="https://s.yimg.com/ny/api/res/1.2/glAMJVrPrNla2CFtmfMBkA--/YXBwaWQ9aGlnaGxhbmRlcjt3PTk2MDtoPTU5OTtjZj13ZWJw/https://media.zenfs.com/en/the_telegraph_258/57e08aead8b0a2a5c197e65098302778"/><figcaption>Billie Piper (right) as Rose Tyler in Doctor Who in 2005, alongside Christopher Eccleston&amp;#39;s Doctor (centre) and Bruno Langley as Adam</figcaption></figure>
<p>That’s a lot for showrunner Russell T Davies to reflect on. He will also have to reckon with a looming budget headache after Disney walked – <a data-ylk="slk:some might say, ran screaming;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/business/2025/10/28/disney-dumps-bbc-doctor-who-deal-woke-rebrand/">some might say, ran screaming</a> – away from its co-production deal with the BBC after two underwhelming attempts to turn <em>Doctor Who </em>into a Marvel-style global franchise. Backed by Disney’s financial might, the budget for the Gatwa seasons is reported to have been £6m to £8m per episode – an eye-watering sum beyond the means of a cash-strapped BBC currently facing further cuts and one which may well require it to seek another streaming partner to help cover the cost of a fully operational Tardis.</p>
<p>As Piper is taken up for the foreseeable, Davies and the BBC at least have a decent chunk of time to get their sonic screwdrivers in a row and put the finishing touches to the Christmas script, which <em>Doctor Who </em>composer Murray Gold says has been completed, but in “multiple versions”. With cameras continuing to roll on <em>Wednesday </em>– several of the cast were in attendance at a secret Foo Fighters gig in Dublin last week – filming of the <em>Doctor Who </em>Christmas special is not expected to commence until September at the earliest.</p>
<p>That gives the BBC another six months to ponder not just the seasonal one-off but the entire future of a franchise that was humiliatingly cold-shouldered by Disney, which seemed to <a data-ylk="slk:regret its partnership with the BBC the moment the ink was dry;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/tv/2025/10/29/doctor-who-and-disney-was-a-disaster-waiting-to-happen/">regret its partnership with the BBC the moment the ink was dry</a>.</p>
<p>“The writing has been on the wall for ages. There has been a complete lack of enthusiasm over at Disney,” one insider close to <em>Doctor Who </em>told entertainment news website Deadline as it emerged last year that streaming numbers for <em>Doctor Who </em>on Disney+ had been abysmal, although some close to the production have blamed Disney for not putting enough marketing muscle into the show. “It was pretty apparent from early on that this wasn’t for the long term,” a Disney executive also revealed to the website. “Everyone got the impression that it wasn’t doing what it needed to do [on Disney+] to be sustained.”</p>
<p>There were also mutterings that the Doctor was “too woke” for Disney as the political landscape shifted under Donald Trump. According to this theory, Middle America was never going to be enthusiastic about a series that featured trans actress Yasmin Finney, a kiss between the Doctor and <em>Frozen</em> star Jonathan Groff and a villain portrayed by drag queen Jinkx Monsoon.</p>
<p>Others, however, say that <a data-ylk="slk:if the episodes were good enough, none of this would matter;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/tv/0/doctor-who-ncuti-gatwa-what-went-wrong/">if the episodes were good enough, none of this would matter</a>. Davies, for his part, insisted that the controversy about diversity on the show existed only in the imagination of sad men tapping on keyboards in their bedrooms. “There are online warriors accusing us of diversity and wokeness and involving messages and issues,” he said. “And I have no time for this. I don’t have a second to bear [it]. Because what you might call diversity, I just call an open door.”</p>
<figure><img alt="Russell T Davies" data-nimg="1" src="https://s.yimg.com/ny/api/res/1.2/71NPZq8EZSEo76KQkZYUpA--/YXBwaWQ9aGlnaGxhbmRlcjt3PTk2MDtoPTYwMztjZj13ZWJw/https://media.zenfs.com/en/the_telegraph_258/05af798694fa3970c8ead72fc5425cfa"/><figcaption>Russell T Davies</figcaption></figure>
<p>The big headache, according to new reports, is that all the negativity around Gatwa’s exit after two seasons has put potential Doctors off the part. <a data-ylk="slk:Even the actor himself said the show had left him “old” and “tired”;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/2025/07/13/doctor-who-ncuti-gatwa-show-getting-old/">Even the actor himself said the show had left him “old” and “tired”</a>. “From the moment it was confirmed Ncuti was leaving, bosses have subtly been putting feelers out among the industry for an actor who could play the Doctor,” a source told the Sun. “Despite once being one of TV’s most coveted roles, it’s now seen as a bit of a poisoned chalice. Plus, anyone taking on the job will want to know if it’s going to be a one-off festive special or go beyond that as they will want to plan their workload over coming years.”</p>
<p>Someone thinking about stepping into the Tardis would have to consider the show’s current uncertain future, plus the critical opprobrium that Gatwa and Whittaker both had to bear. There is also the sense that, with the role having become a focal point for the BBC’s push for diversity, a white actor might now be considered a challenging fit for the Doctor. But not so long ago, the part was considered one of the most prized roles in British television, a star-making turn for Matt Smith and David Tennant, and a cherished part of UK popular culture.</p>
<p>Whoever the new Time Lord is, they would have to understand that playing the Doctor is about more than just showing up on set each day. “There is more to that role than performing,” said someone close to the production. “You have got to be an ambassador for the brand and embrace being that generation’s Doctor. Matt Smith and David Tennant fully understood the responsibility it carried.”</p>
<p>Casting aside, Davies – or whoever succeeds him – will need to bring the Doctor back to basics. It is telling that the best episodes of recent seasons were the ones that relied on story over spectacle and which the BBC could have easily made without Disney’s millions – a case in point being the 2024 episode “73 Yards”, a chilling folk horror story where the Doctor’s assistant, Ruby (Millie Gibson), was haunted by the ghostly apparition of an old woman.</p>
<p>There were barely any special effects – the spell was cast through a gripping plot alone. Why not do more of that, lean into British science fiction’s long-running tradition of turning minimalism into a virtue?</p>
<figure><img alt="Director Peter Hoar is of the opinion that an inflated Disney budget did not benefit Doctor Who" data-nimg="1" src="https://s.yimg.com/ny/api/res/1.2/Z4ot8NuA6abTvRKVmqLmcQ--/YXBwaWQ9aGlnaGxhbmRlcjt3PTk2MDtoPTYwNTtjZj13ZWJw/https://media.zenfs.com/en/the_telegraph_258/4837c8b3a6de4ec956376d88bfbb45d4"/><figcaption>Director Peter Hoar is of the opinion that an inflated Disney budget did not benefit Doctor Who</figcaption></figure>
<p>That is likely to be the approach taken by <em>The Last of Us</em> and <em>It’s a Sin </em>director Peter Hoar, who is planning a <a data-ylk="slk:reboot of the stark late-1970s BBC show Blake’s 7;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.telegraph.co.uk/tv/2026/01/20/blakes-7-reboot-producers-open-letter/">reboot of the stark late-1970s BBC show <em>Blake’s 7</em></a> – a sort of miserable Beckettian take on <em>Star Trek</em>. As he pointed out, <em>Doctor Who</em> (on which he worked) “wasn’t a better show with more money. And that’s a good thing, because we haven’t got the money any more, nobody has.”</p>
<p>It’s a worthwhile reminder that the series never relied on whizz-bang effects. Classic <em>Who </em>was all about great writing, riveting acting – and cheesy monsters cobbled together from whatever materials were to hand. As Hoar said: “I could tell they didn’t have money, but I was able to compartmentalise and enjoy the ride knowing that the sets wobbled.” In other words, is it time to bin the CGI and bring back the bin bag?</p>
<p>“You don’t wish for less money – it always gives you options. I wouldn’t go so far as to say a blessing in disguise. But I think if they wanted to do a more budget-conscious version of the series, it doesn’t need to be a problem,” says Tom Spilsbury, a lifelong Whovian and former editor of <em>Doctor Who Magazine</em>. “Good creepy ideas don’t necessarily need to cost huge amounts of money in location or effects. One of the most successful episodes of the last couple of decades was <em>Blink </em>with the Weeping Angels [creepy statues]. That was conceived as a cheap one and didn’t even have the Doctor in it that much.”</p>
<figure><img alt="Jon Pertwee and monsters in a 1972 adventure" data-nimg="1" src="https://s.yimg.com/ny/api/res/1.2/DygBUttEOoGBwahlygR9sA--/YXBwaWQ9aGlnaGxhbmRlcjt3PTk2MDtoPTYwMDtjZj13ZWJw/https://media.zenfs.com/en/the_telegraph_258/df76727763a38512b5c73e9a6a45f036"/><figcaption>Jon Pertwee and monsters in a 1972 adventure</figcaption></figure>
<p>The other argument is that the current BBC take on <em>Who</em>, which began with Davies when he revived the franchise in 2005 and has carried through to his two most recent seasons, has gone as far as it can, and that the character would benefit from some time away. You can only make a splash so often before the public becomes bored – for instance, the buzz around Piper’s apparent regeneration as the Doctor died within a few days.</p>
<p>“Obviously, <em>Doctor Who </em>is very important to the BBC and always will be,” says Spilsbury. “I suspect <em>Doctor Who, </em>in some form, may outlast the BBC. But it doesn’t necessarily need to be on all the time. It ebbs and flows like everything. You can’t always be the new, exciting thing.</p>
<p>“Sometimes it takes time for people to miss something before it can have a resurgence. I do wonder whether we’re at that point again with <em>Doctor Who</em>. Every time they’ve reinvented it and had a new Doctor, that gets a bit of interest. But how many times can you pull that trick before it feels like diminishing returns? Just thinking out loud, I don’t think it would be a bad thing at all if it went away for a few years – it doesn’t need to be forever. If it were for five years or so, I think suddenly there would be that sort of feeling, like in 2005 of, ‘Oh yes… we’ve really missed this’.”</p>
