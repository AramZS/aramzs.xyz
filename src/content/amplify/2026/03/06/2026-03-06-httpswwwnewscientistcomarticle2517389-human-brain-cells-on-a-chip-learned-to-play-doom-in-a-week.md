title: Human brain cells on a chip learned to play Doom in a week

<p>Neuron-powered computer chips can now be easily programmed to play a first-person shooter game, bringing biological computers a step closer to useful applications</p>
<figure><figcaption><p>A screen image of Doom being played by human neurons on a chip</p><p>Cortical Labs</p></figcaption></figure>
<p>A clump of human <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/2363095-80000-mouse-brain-cells-used-to-build-a-living-computer/">brain cells</a> can play the classic computer game <em>Doom</em>. While its performance is not up to par with humans, experts say it brings biological computers a step closer to useful real-world applications, like controlling robot arms.</p>
<p>In 2021, the Australian company <a href="https://corticallabs.com/">Cortical Labs</a> used its neuron-powered computer chips <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/2301500-human-brain-cells-in-a-dish-learn-to-play-pong-faster-than-an-ai/">to play </a><a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/2301500-human-brain-cells-in-a-dish-learn-to-play-pong-faster-than-an-ai/"><em>Pong</em></a>. The chips consisted of clumps of more than 800,000 living brain cells grown on top of microelectrode arrays that can both send and receive electrical signals. Researchers had to carefully train the chips to control the paddles on either side of the screen.</p>
<p>Now, Cortical Labs has developed an interface that makes it easier to program these chips using the popular programming language Python. An independent developer, <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/sean-cole-8985a4207/">Sean Cole</a>, then used Python to teach the chips to <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/2445450-generative-ai-creates-playable-version-of-doom-game-with-no-code/">play </a><a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/2445450-generative-ai-creates-playable-version-of-doom-game-with-no-code/"><em>Doom</em></a><em>,</em> which he did in around a week.</p>
<p>“Unlike the <em>Pong</em> work that we did a few years ago, which represented years of painstaking scientific effort, this demonstration has been done in a matter of days by someone who previously had relatively little expertise working directly with biology,” says <a href="https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=bvWRHNcAAAAJ&amp;hl=en">Brett Kagan</a> of Cortical Labs. “It’s this accessibility and this flexibility that makes it truly exciting.”</p>
<p>The neuronal computer chip, which used about a quarter as many neurons as the <em>Pong</em> demonstration, played <em>Doom </em>better than a randomly firing player, but far below the performance of the best human players. However, it learnt much faster than traditional, silicon-based machine learning systems and should be able to improve its performance with newer learning algorithms, says Kagan.</p>
<p>However, it’s not useful to compare the chips with human brains, he says. “Yes, it’s alive, and yes, it’s biological, but really what it is being used as is a material that can process information in very special ways that we can’t recreate in silicon.”</p>
<p>“<em>Doom</em> is vastly more complex than earlier demonstrations, and successfully interacting with it highlights real advances in how living neural systems can be controlled and trained,” says <a href="https://people.uwe.ac.uk/Person/AndrewAdamatzky">Andrew Adamatzky</a> at the University of the West of England in Bristol, UK.</p>
<p><a href="https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=jLnsiBEAAAAJ&amp;hl=en">Steve Furber</a> at the University of Manchester, UK, agrees that <em>Doom</em> is a significant level up from playing <em>Pong</em>, but he says there is still a lot we don’t understand about how these neurons are playing the game, such as how the neurons know what is expected of them or how they can “see” the screen with no eyes.</p>
<p>Even so, the jump in capability is exciting, says <a href="https://www.reading.ac.uk/biomedical-engineering/staff/yoshikatsu-hayashi">Yoshikatsu Hayashi</a> at the University of Reading, UK, and brings us significantly closer to useful real-world applications, such as controlling a robotic arm with biological computers, a task which Hayashi and his colleagues are attempting with a similar computer made from <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/2444465-a-glob-of-jelly-can-play-pong-thanks-to-a-basic-kind-of-memory/">jelly-like hydrogel</a>. “[Playing <em>Doom</em>] is like a simpler version of controlling a whole arm,” says Hayashi.</p>
<p>“What’s exciting here is not just that a biological system can play <em>Doom,</em> but that it can cope with complexity, uncertainty, and real-time decision-making,” says Adamatzky. “That’s much closer to the kinds of challenges future biological or hybrid computers will need to handle.”</p>
