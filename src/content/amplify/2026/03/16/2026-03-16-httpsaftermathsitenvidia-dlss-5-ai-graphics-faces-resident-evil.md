<figure><figcaption>Digital Foundry / YouTube / Nvidia </figcaption></figure>
<p>Nvidia, the biggest company at the heart of <a href="https://aftermath.site/ram-prices-hdd-prices-ai-bubble-computer-expensive/"><u>an AI-driven hardware crisis that’s making everything unaffordable</u></a>, has decided to return to its old stomping grounds, video games, and ruin those too. Today it <a href="https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-dlss-5-delivers-ai-powered-breakthrough-in-visual-fidelity-for-games?ref=aftermath.site"><u>proudly debuted</u></a> new AI-powered DLSS 5 tech that CEO and head <a href="https://x.com/Papapishu/status/1730102262389727706?ref=aftermath.site"><u>jacket nerd</u></a> Jensen Huang declared “the GPT moment for graphics — blending handcrafted rendering with generative AI to deliver a dramatic leap in visual realism while preserving the control artists need for creative expression.” Artists, among others, beg to differ.</p>
<p>A <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4ZlwTtgbgVA&amp;ref=aftermath.site"><u>video by Digital Foundry</u></a> demonstrating the tech has been met with near-unanimous derision, in large part due to the way it mangles faces of characters like Grace and Leon from<em> Resident Evil Requiem</em>, as well as… pretty much anyone who’s not white. In some cases it appears to strip away lighting sources, while in others it paves over any notion of artistic intent, replacing even photorealistic characters’ trademark wrinkles and fissures with porn-y airbrushed sheen. Game developers and those who support them have reacted with an outpouring of both trenchant observations and, of course, memes:</p>
