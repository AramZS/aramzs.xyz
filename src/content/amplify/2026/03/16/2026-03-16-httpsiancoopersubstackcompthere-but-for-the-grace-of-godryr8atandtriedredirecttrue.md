<h3>I.</h3>
<p>The WhatsApp group for our apartment building is abuzz.</p>
<p>“There’s a homeless guy by the patio.”</p>
<p>“He’s hiding in the back.”</p>
<p>“We’re on hold with LAPD.”</p>
<p>A neighbor jumps in to tell the group he’ll handle it.</p>
<p>Later that day, I run into the neighbor. “What happened?” I say.</p>
<p>“I grabbed a knife and told the homeless guy he had three seconds to get out of here.”</p>
<p>He must see the look on my face.</p>
<p>“There’s only a sliding glass door between my living room and the patio area. And my wife was home.”</p>
<p>He tells me this like I’m part of his crowd. And he’s not wrong. But I’m also one of them, too.</p>
<p>What he doesn’t know is that it’s been less than a year since I was looking up safe places for Meg and I to park at night in case we had to sleep in our car.</p>
<p>Any difference between me and the man he had chased out earlier that day can be measured in nothing more than in seconds and inches.</p>
<h3>II.</h3>
<p>Awareness of those among us who sleep on the street hangs over life here in LA. Many nights, when I take Lemon for her final bathroom break, I see someone huddled beneath a blanket on the porch of the shuttered butcher’s shop on Main Street or passed out under a doorway by one of the storefronts a block or two down.</p>
<p>Most everyone feels it. We don’t often say it aloud, but we know that we’re just a bad break or a couple missed paychecks away from being out there too.</p>
<p>Sometimes, that comes out sideways. My neighbor is far from the only one to point his anger toward the unhoused themselves rather than at the political choices that have left us all more vulnerable to losing shelter.</p>
<p>The root cause of homelessness is stupidly simple. There are <a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2023/01/homelessness-affordable-housing-crisis-democrats-causes/672224/?gift=8ErDlFA90ecwkm989StDs1gd7E_PY06WV3ydxTDyPGA&amp;utm_source=copy-link&amp;utm_medium=social&amp;utm_campaign=share">not enough homes to go around</a>. It’s way too hard to build more. And the law itself is often the biggest barrier.</p>
<p>In 1960, LA was <a href="https://la.curbed.com/2018/9/10/17827982/single-family-houses-los-angeles-zoning-rules-explained">zoned to house 10 million people</a>. Then homeowners pushed for new zoning rules that restricted over <a href="https://phys.org/news/2022-03-la-region-residential-zoned-exclusionary.html">75 percent of the land in greater Los Angeles</a> to single-family housing only: no apartments allowed.</p>
<p>The homeowners got rich as scarcity drove property values into the stratosphere. The rest of the community paid the price. Today, even with every bit of land put to use, LA can only hold about 4.3 million people.</p>
<p>What this means is that during a 65-year period when the population of the United States has roughly doubled, the carrying capacity of its second largest city has dropped by more than half. As a result, the cost of a starter house can easily push past $1 million.</p>
<p>That’s a recipe for disaster. Especially because the same pattern is happening all over. In California as a whole, <a href="https://phys.org/news/2024-05-staggering-california-residential-zoned-family.html">96 percent of residential land</a> is zoned exclusively for single-family homes.</p>
<p>You want to put up a four-story apartment that could house 50 people on a lot currently occupied by a bungalow that holds two? Too bad.</p>
<p>Other big cities also struggle with high rates of homelessness for similar reasons. It’s not just zoning — factors like construction costs, willful opposition from neighbors, regulations, and even good intentions run amok matter too. (Read Ezra Klein and Jerusalem Demsas if you want to really get into it here.)</p>
<p>But when the law makes it impossible to build enough housing to serve everyone who lives in a particular place, people always end up sleeping outside.</p>
<p>The problem has even spread to rural states like Montana. An influx of well-paid remote workers and still wealthier second-home buyers price out locals, who then end up on the street, <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2025/jan/27/kalispell-montana-homelessness">often with brutal consequences</a>.</p>
<h3>III.</h3>
<p>It’s May 2020. Pandemic season. I start noticing an old man sleeping in a white Honda Accord parked on the block behind our apartment.</p>
<p>One day, we get to talking. His name is Tony. He’d lived in one of the buildings on that street for almost 20 years. A few years back, the rent had gotten too high, so he’d moved a few miles south to cheaper digs in up-and-coming Mar Vista. Then he’d hurt his back and leg on the job.</p>
<p>He can’t work as a building inspector anymore. Too many stairs to climb. His Social Security check isn’t enough to cover rent. So now, he lives in his car.</p>
<p>He says he feels safer parking in front of his old home.</p>
<p>“The manager’s a real piece of work. But at least he knows me.”</p>
<p>We chat a few times a week. I learn that he’d been a teacher at a school on Long Island, decades ago. History, maybe? He mentions that he’d once dreamt he fought in one of Caesar’s legions. The dream was so vivid he wonders if it was a memory from a past life.</p>
<p>Sometimes, he vanishes for a while. When he reappears, he tells me he’s been in the hospital.</p>
<p>Eventually, Meg and I move away. The last time I speak with Tony, it’s over the phone. He says he lost the Accord to a drunk driver who ran him into a telephone pole while driving east on Wilshire.</p>
<h3>IV.</h3>
<p>Let’s crank up the temperature on this simmering pot even further. This week, I read a report that the White House and Congressional Republicans want to <a href="https://www.vox.com/homelessness/402972/housing-first-homeless-hud-scott-turner-housing-affordable-voucher">end federal funding for a policy called housing first</a>.</p>
<p>There’s a lot of bad ideas in the air right now. But this is one of the worst.</p>
<p>Housing first is a really simple public policy that has helped a great many people. It’s exactly what it sounds like: Get unhoused people into housing before worrying about anything else.</p>
<p>Homelessness advocates recognize that most homeless people fall into one of two groups. Some people living on the street just don’t have enough money to stay indoors. But others are also struggling with addiction or mental illness.</p>
<p>People who are homeless only because they can’t afford housing have at least a shot of getting off the street without intensive community support. No, it isn’t fair or easy, just not impossible.</p>
<p>But those living with addiction or mental illness stand virtually no chance on their own. This latter group almost inevitably become what’s called chronically homeless. They live on the street not just for months or years, but decades.</p>
<p>So what housing first does is give chronically homeless people a place to live, no questions asked. The only real barrier is space. In communities that have enough open rental units, housing first has <a href="https://www.motherjones.com/politics/2015/02/housing-first-solution-to-homelessness-utah/">largely ended chronic homelessness</a>.</p>
<p>I’ve seen housing first up close. Back when I was in social work school, I spent nine months working with a mental health crisis team at a nonprofit called Step Up that serves LA County’s homeless population.</p>
<p>I did weekly 1:1 therapy sessions with a guy named Anthony, who took meth at night to stay awake so he could protect himself. He had a schizophrenia diagnosis and would rarely make it to the end of a sentence without landing somewhere wildly different from where he’d started.</p>
<p>Anthony was on the waiting list for an apartment of his own. I don’t know if he ever got one.</p>
<p>At the time, Step Up ran five or six apartment buildings in Santa Monica and LA. Some were proper studio units, others were just tiny bedrooms with communal bathrooms.</p>
<p>Each building was home to dozens of people who had spent a decade or more living on the street. Nearly all had been diagnosed with schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, or the like. Thanks to housing first, they were able to sleep indoors, in their own beds, reasonably safe from harm.</p>
<p>Some of these folks could barely function, at least not in the way we expect of each other. Charles was a hoarder, among other things. His room was packed to gills with an astonishing array of junk: stacks of newspapers, broken bits of equipment, a rogue tire. I truly don’t know where a man with no job and no social skills managed to acquire it all.</p>
<p>Around the office, he had an almost mythical reputation. Every few months, some poor social worker would clean his room out, only to come back a week later and see the junk pile had been reborn anew. Charles would laugh with a glint in his eye that said no matter how far off the deep end he may have been, he saw the humor in our shared predicament.</p>
<p>Other residents made me feel like there but for the grace of God go I. Joseph set up a gaming computer in his room. He was easy to talk to. Near the end of a shift, I’d stop by to check on him, before heading home to game for a while myself.</p>
<p>“I like being alone,” he told me once. “I’m my own best friend.”</p>
<p>I got that more than he knew. We both liked to cook, too. He did the best he could with an electric crock pot.</p>
<p>If you’ve never known what it’s like to live as an adult behind a door you’re not allowed to lock because your name is not on the lease, you may not be able to grasp the relief the comes when you can finally turn your own key. But it’s worth trying to.</p>
<p>We all think we can throw away certain people, although our idea of who will vary. But the world doesn’t work like that. Charles comes for all of us.</p>
<p>I think of my neighbors in the building, so afraid of our neighbors who live outside. And how folks outside must be so scared of the wrath they face as the targets of that fear. When I pass someone on the street pushing a shopping cart, they usually look down.</p>
<p>Suffering is ours to share. I don’t just mean feelings, either. This is about dollars and cents too.</p>
<p>When people struggling with addiction or mental illness live on the street, they almost always end up in one of two places: jail or the emergency room. Think about how expensive your last trip to the ER was. Then guess who foots the bill here? You and I: Joe and Jane Taxpayer.</p>
<p>As a society, we get a lot of mileage from the idea that we just can’t afford to help certain people, especially those we see as unworthy. But the great irony is that it’s cheaper to house people than to abandon them to the street. Cruelty is expensive.</p>
<p>In LA, yearly rent for a studio apartment runs between $20,000 to $30,000 at market rates. Meanwhile, researchers at the University of California estimate that a single chronically homeless person costs the government <a href="https://cdn.shopify.com/s/files/1/0072/3019/3782/files/jamboree-united-way-uci-oc-cost-study-homelessness-executive-summary.pdf">roughly $100,000 a year</a>.</p>
<p>For half that, we could not only put a roof over that person’s head, but give him or her essential supportive services like food, therapy, and community.</p>
<h3>V.</h3>
<p>Shouting drifts up from the street outside my window. This happens a lot in Venice. Nine times out of ten, the man yelling is wrestling with demons only he can see.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, I’m sitting snug in bed. Yeah, I should get back to sending out job applications, but there are few things more soul sucking than spending time on LinkedIn.</p>
<p>Instead, I pull up The New York Times. Near the top of the site is another article from their long-running “36 Hours in X Place” series. My eyes pause on a photo of downtown Valencia, Spain: a wide pedestrian plaza rimmed with palm trees, surrounded by what look like elegant four-story apartment buildings.</p>
<p>It’s a hopeful image.</p>
<p>We could use a little more of that in this country. I don’t want to live in the glass and steel canyons of Manhattan. But tell me what’s wrong with a city of walkable, tree-lined streets and enough apartment buildings to shelter all who live there?</p>
<p>No, more housing is not a cure for all that ails us. But it gives our communities a chance. The Buddha tried starving himself into enlightenment. But he only found it after he took shelter under the bodhi tree and accepted food from a woman who lived in the nearby village.</p>
<p>Housing first works. So does simply building enough units to meet the needs of those of us who can mostly hold down a job, but don’t make six figures or more. All that stands in the way is a lack of political will.</p>
<p>We can all work to change that. Zoning, construction, building permits, environmental reviews — these are largely in the hands of local government. So we need to vote for local officials who care.</p>
<p>Our leaders need to know that the solution to homelessness is to build more houses. Better yet, they need to get that a neighborhood where no one has to fear living on the street is one that is richer and safer for all.</p>
<p>But change needs to be personal, too. That’s where it starts. Here, we need only loosen our own grip, even if just a little at a time.</p>
<p>Say yes to the evolution of your own neighborhood. Say yes to your community changing to meet the needs of its people. Create a spirit of openness within yourself and the rest will come in time.</p>
<p>Nobody loves the thought of a construction site next door. But I’ve lived by more than one. I can tell you it’s not the end of the world.</p>
<p>I say that as someone who wants little more in life than to be able to sleep late in the morning, undisturbed by the ringing of hammers.</p>
<p>Even a small willingness to make way for change goes a long way here. We don’t have to be heroes. Just soften to meet the moment.</p>
<p>Right now, that’s enough.</p>
