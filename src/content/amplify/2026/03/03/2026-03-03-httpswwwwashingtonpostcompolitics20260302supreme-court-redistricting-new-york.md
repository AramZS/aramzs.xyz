(Shedrick Pelt/For The Washington Post)</figcaption></figure><p>The U.S. Supreme Court on Monday granted the bid of a Republican New York congresswoman to stop state officials from redrawing her district in a way that could favor Democrats.</p><p>The ruling is a victory for Rep. Nicole Malliotakis, a Republican representing New York’s 11th District, ahead of the November midterms. A state trial court ordered the state to redraw the district to include a greater number of Black and Latino voters, which could deliver New York City’s only Republican seat to a Democrat.</p><p>After a state appeals court denied Malliotakis’s request to halt that decision, the congresswoman filed an emergency petition to the Supreme Court saying the trial court’s order could throw the city’s elections into “chaos.” State officials counter that Malliotakis’s appeal to the Supreme Court is premature and intrudes on state court and election processes.</p><p>The majority did not explain its decision, but Justice Samuel A. Alito Jr. offered a concurring opinion saying the redistricting violated the Constitution’s equal protection clause.</p><p class="rw-outer-content"><span>Some content could not be imported from the original document.</span> <a href="https://washingtonpost.com/politics/2026/03/02/supreme-court-redistricting-new-york/">View content ↗ </a></p><p>“These cases concern a state-court order that blatantly discriminates on the basis of race,” Alito wrote.</p><p>Justice Sonia Sotomayor wrote a dissent, which was joined by the court’s two other liberal justices.</p><p>“Ignoring every limit on federal courts’ authority, the Court takes the unprecedented step of staying a state trial court’s decision in a redistricting dispute on matters of state law without giving the state’s highest court a chance to act,” Sotomayor wrote.</p><p>The Supreme Court has so far allowed states to redraw congressional districts as they wish. The high court in December cleared the way for Texas to redraw maps that could deliver five House seats to the GOP. In February, the justices similarly allowed a new California map that could deliver five House seats to Democrats.</p><p>But Malliotakis’s case stands apart because it did not concern an effort by state officials to flip House seats. It was instead a result of a lawsuit that alleged New York’s 11th District — which covers Staten Island and parts of southern Brooklyn — excluded Black and Latino voters.</p></body></html>
