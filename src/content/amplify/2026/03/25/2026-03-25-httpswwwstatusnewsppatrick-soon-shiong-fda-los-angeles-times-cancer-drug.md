<p>On Tuesday evening, the <strong>Food and Drug Administration</strong> released a blistering warning letter accusing billionaire <strong>Patrick Soon-Shiong </strong>of making “false” and “misleading” claims about his biotech company’s new cancer drug, <strong>Anktiva</strong>. During a recent appearance on “<strong>The Sean Spicer Show,” </strong>the red-pilled <strong>Los Angeles Times</strong> owner described his company’s drug as “the most important molecule that could cure cancer” and claimed “it actually can treat all cancers.” He later went even further, stating: “We have the therapy to prevent cancer if you were exposed to radiation, and that’s Anktiva.”</p>
<p>In a letter to <strong>Richard Adcock</strong>, <strong>ImmunityBio’s</strong> chief executive, the FDA said Soon-Shiong’s claims,along with other company promotional materials, violated federal drug regulations, giving ImmunityBio 15 days to respond and correct the flagged issues. After the letter was <a href="https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/warning-letters/immunitybio-inc-725468-03132026">made public</a>, shares of Soon-Shiong’s ImmunityBio <a href="https://apnews.com/article/patrick-soonshiong-immunitybio-anktiva-cancer-ebb9a297655dced61487b0317e320307">collapsed</a> more than 21% by market close. But the FDA scrutiny isn’t solely an issue for Soon-Shiong’s biotech venture. In recent months, Soon-Shiong has used the Times’ branding and resources to push the drug and the biotech company. In fact, <em>Status</em> found that Soon-Shiong appeared to make similar, though less explicit, claims—that Anktiva can treat other cancers—in a <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kdKE1HcNMS0">video</a> produced by<strong> LA Times Studios</strong> and posted to the company’s official <strong>YouTube</strong> channel.</p>
<p>A spokesperson for the Times did not respond to a request for comment. Neither did the FDA.</p>
