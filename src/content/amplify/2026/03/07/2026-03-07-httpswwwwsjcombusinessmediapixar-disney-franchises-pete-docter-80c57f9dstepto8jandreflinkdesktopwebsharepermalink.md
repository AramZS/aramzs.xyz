<h1>Disney Needs Its Next Hit Franchise. Can Pixar’s Reluctant Leader Deliver?</h1>
<p>The ‘Toy Story’ studio used to print money for Disney but hasn’t made an original blockbuster in nearly a decade. Its top creative executive admits he’s made mistakes.</p>

<p>By <a href="https://www.wsj.com/news/author/ben-fritz">Ben Fritz</a></p>
<p>March 6, 2026 7:30 pm ET</p>
<p>EMERYVILLE, Calif.—Pixar Chief Creative Officer Pete Docter finished strumming his miniature ukulele and folded his 6-foot-5 frame onto a couch to explain that the reason the studio behind “Toy Story” is struggling to find its next hit franchise might be him.</p>
<p>“I got into animation because it’s easier to draw people than talk to them,” he said, seated in his office beside sculptures of characters from his directorial successes “Monsters, Inc.,” “Up” and “Inside Out.” In his early years managing Pixar’s cadre of ambitious filmmakers, the 57-year-old admitted, “I probably overindexed on, ‘Do whatever you want.’ ”</p>
<p>Docter’s first three movies were among an extraordinary string of hits Pixar made from 1995 through 2019, many of which became long-lasting franchises for parent company <a href="https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/DIS">Disney</a>. With “Cars” merchandise, the “Ratatouille” theme park ride and multiple “Toy Story” sequels, Pixar created brands that printed money across the Disney empire.</p>
<p>Now Pixar is pumping out hit sequels like 2024’s <a href="https://www.wsj.com/business/media/inside-out-2-hits-155-million-in-u-s-for-years-biggest-opening-weekend-fb912752?mod=article_inline">“Inside Out 2,”</a> but it hasn’t had an original success since 2017’s “Coco.” Current and former employees say one of the biggest reasons is that the conflict-averse Docter encouraged new directors to make autobiographical movies that too many audience members struggled to connect with.</p>
