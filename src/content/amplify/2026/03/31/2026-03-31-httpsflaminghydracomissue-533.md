<h3>Issue No. 533</h3>
<h2>Surrealist Strategies</h2>
<p>When the painter Leonora Carrington’s lover was arrested by the Gestapo in 1940, she fled their <a href="https://www.worldofinteriors.com/story/leonora-carrington-max-ernst-surreal-farmhouse-france"><u>sanctuary in rural France</u></a> and promptly had a psychotic break. Her incandescent, floridly extramarital affair with the German painter Max Ernst, 26 years her senior, is the stuff of <a href="https://www.nationalgalleries.org/art-and-artists/164061"><u>Surrealist</u></a> <a href="https://www.sothebys.com/en/auctions/ecatalogue/2012/impressionist-modern-art-evening-sale-n08850/lot.48.html"><u>legend</u></a>, but their idyll came to an instantaneous end when the Germans invaded France and seized Ernst on charges of being a “degenerate” artist (“under the escort of a gendarme who carried a rifle,” Carrington later wrote). Friends persuaded Carrington to leave France for Spain, <a href="https://english.elpais.com/elpais/2017/08/15/inenglish/1502798938_665406.html"><u>her delirium gathering speed</u></a> along the way. She was 23 years old.</p>
<p>“In the political confusion and the torrid heat,” she later wrote. “I convinced myself that Madrid was the world’s stomach and that I had been chosen for the task of restoring this digestive organ to health.”</p>
<p>Those lines are from <em>Down Below</em>, the artist’s 1972 memoir about her flight from France and her subsequent confinement in a Spanish insane asylum. There, she was bound and subjected to various quack treatments, including injection with Cardiazol, a drug that gave her epileptic seizures. She became convinced that the ground floor of the asylum, which was referred to as “abajo” (“down below”) was Paradise.</p>
<p>“I believed that I was being put through purifying tortures so that I might attain Absolute Knowledge,” Carrington wrote, “at which point I could live Down Below.”</p>
<p>Carrington would eventually recover sufficiently to leave the asylum and escape her parents’ plans to send her to a sanatorium in South Africa. Giving her minder the slip in a Lisbon cafe, she made her way to Mexico, where she became one of the greatest surrealist painters in history, a leading feminist, and an occultist, her life and work suspended somewhere between the real and the worlds of witchcraft, fables and dreams. In Mexico, she formed an intense friendship with <a href="https://hyperallergic.com/remedios-varo-in-a-sphere-of-her-own-art-institute-chicago/"><u>Remedios Varo</u></a>, a Spanish-born female Surrealist painter; the two became obsessed with the imagery of the Tarot, witchcraft, the Kabbalah, and the <em>Popol Vuh</em>, the sacred text of the K’iche’ Maya people. Together, they performed ritual acts of kitchen alchemy, as a bemused <em>New York Times</em> reporter <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2002/10/13/magazine/the-mystery-woman.html"><u>found in 2002</u></a>.</p>
<p>“The two women whipped up magical potions in this kitchen,” wrote Elaine Mayers Salkaln, “many learned from the <em>curanderas</em> (female healers whom they met in the Indian market). They also played Surrealist jokes on their friends, serving fake caviar made with black squid juice and tapioca.”</p>
<p>Varo and Carrington are currently enjoying much-deserved posthumous celebrations of their work. (And I do like to believe that in some upper realm they’re enjoying it together, leaning their heads over the murky contents of a steaming pot, purple plumes rising around them.) In 2023, I saw a big show of Varo’s work at the Museo de Arte Moderno in Mexico City, and last week in Paris, I visited a Carrington exhibit at the Musée du Luxembourg. Both were packed with admirers, peering closely to capture every delicate detail of their dense, lush, mysterious works, crowded with figures, metaphors, dream-like flourishes, and Hieronymus Bosch-esque figures crowding the peripheries. In Carrington’s <em>Grandmother Moorhead’s Aromatic Kitchen</em>, a terrifying goose-like figure shares a blood-red kitchen with a retinue of ghostly figures, all busying themselves over food items and mysterious alchemical work. Behind the goose stands a horned, robed, gazelle-like entity, a witches’ broom pressed to its side. The scene is a nightmare and a celebration all at once; the kitchen is an altar, a laboratory, a place where the material things are transmuted into works of the spirit.</p>

<p>Varo and Carrington escaped Europe with fascism on their heels, crafting new lives in exile. They found one another, the occult, and a rich vocabulary to describe a feminism imbued with the power of witchcraft (as well as several husbands and lovers, less interesting to me).</p>
<p>The times are sinister and they’re also senseless. Surely some combination of the real and the surreal might save us, or at least give us the language to describe what’s going on. I believe this explains, at least partly, why we can’t stop looking at the Surrealists.</p>
<p>I’ve also been slowly making my way through a dread-filled reading of <em>The Third Reich of Dreams, </em>Charlotte Beradt’s account of what people in Germany began dreaming about as the Nazis came to power. There, surreal images helped people confront what was happening in their waking lives: a woman dreaming about her toaster becoming a spy in her home, a dreamer who determines to keep safe by “covering myself in lead” or by scribbling down nonsense equations in invisible ink—images and ideas with the predatory logic of a sinister-feeling figure from a Carrington painting.</p>
<p>In <em>Down Below</em>, at her most unwell, Carrington writes about trying to heal and unify the world by intently rearranging items on her dressing table.</p>
<p>“A box of Tabu powder with a lid, half grey and half black, meant eclipse, complex, vanity, taboo, love,” she writes.</p>
<blockquote>Two jars of face cream: the one with a black lid was night, the left side, the moon, woman, destruction; the other, with a green lid, was man, the brother, green eyes, the Sun, construction. My nail buff, shaped like a boat, evoked for me a journey into the Unknown, and also the talisman protecting that journey: the song “El barco velero.” My little mirror was to win over the Whole. As for my Tangee lipstick, I have but a vague memory of its significance; it probably was the meeting with colour and speech, painting and literature: Art.</blockquote>
<p>Later, with Varo and in a more sound mind, she would continue that work, though it was sadly cut short when Varo died of a heart attack in 1963, at only 54 years of age.</p>
<p>Both women sought meaning and purpose in both the physical and the unreal amid a dark and desperate time. Maybe we can learn something from the symbolic alchemy of Carrington’s dressing table, and from the kind that Carrington and Varo experimented with together in her kitchen once her mind had reunited with itself. There are connections in the world that can’t be made by logic or the conscious mind alone.</p>

<h2>There’s No App for That</h2>
<h4><em>by</em> Maria Bustillos</h4>
<figure><img alt="" sizes="(min-width: 720px) 720px" src="https://flaminghydra.com/content/images/2026/03/grem-1.jpg" srcset="https://flaminghydra.com/content/images/size/w600/2026/03/grem-1.jpg 600w, https://flaminghydra.com/content/images/size/w1000/2026/03/grem-1.jpg 1000w, https://flaminghydra.com/content/images/2026/03/grem-1.jpg 1600w"/></figure>
<p>I have a lot of trouble talking about genAI “writing” with people who are in favor of it because it is super embarrassing to me, and pitiful, that anyone would confuse slop with the real thing.</p>
<p>When I say “the real thing,” though, I don’t mean whether or not you can ID genAI writing in a newspaper quiz. I mean that real people with histories, memories, ideas, stories, etc. to share are <em>interesting</em>, and machines don’t have those things and are <em>not interesting</em>. So genAI “art,” just to begin with, has already whiffed on the original point of, and reason for, making writing, or art, or music. Then, when writers or artists reject the premise that genAI produces anything like what they would consider to be writing or art, they inevitably find themselves rejecting a person—as if saying no, you can’t sit with us—and that’s a horrible feeling.</p>
<p>This is going on all the time now. “Who’s a Better Writer: A.I. or Humans?” asked a would-be provocative headline in the <em>New York Times</em> a couple of weeks ago. The other day I read a listserv post in which this guy announced that readers should care only that a piece of writing moves or engages them, regardless of whether a real person wrote it or not, and he advanced this view in tones suggesting that it would be elitist to get all het up about that.</p>
<p>AI-powered “storytelling” applications are proliferating like freaking kudzu.</p>

<p>I know there are some out there, because some have lost their jobs over it, but I don’t know one single professional writer who would dream for even an instant of using genAI to do their work. The idea is very, very unpleasant. This to me would be like getting a machine to eat lunch for me or do other fun parts of my life; the reward and the doing of it are the same thing. You can’t obtrude a machine into this process. It would make no sense at all. But look at all this Squibler and Manus and DeepAI up there! <em>Perchance </em>someone enjoys pushing a button and “writing” comes out, I don’t know.</p>
<p>Most worryingly of all, it turned out that the problems with genAI are about a thousand times more serious even than I first imagined and I <a href="https://popula.com/2023/04/30/yakkin-about-chatgpt-with-david-roth/">started out</a> <a href="https://flaminghydra.com/yakkin-about-ai-2/">very scared</a> <a href="https://www.thenation.com/article/culture/internet-archive-publishers-lawsuit-chatbot/">in 2023</a>. Right off the bat it was obvious to me and a lot of other critics that ChatGPT was already beginning to flood and pollute the search engines, the open web and the whole digital commons with appalling errors and fakery, like a volcano of poison, disgorging some unknown but immense amount of weapons-grade toxic informational sludge over all the valuable information written by real people.</p>
<p>So there’s all this lousy writing oozing all over the place, but it’s always been more serious than that, and matters have only gotten exponentially worse since then. I had no idea in 2023 that the use of genAI would be implicated in the loss of people’s <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2025/may/31/the-workers-who-lost-their-jobs-to-ai-chatgpt">livelihoods</a>, their <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2026/mar/26/ai-chatbot-users-lives-wrecked-by-delusion">money</a>, their <a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/75-year-old-man-asks-wife-for-divorce-after-falling-in-love-with-ai-chatbot/">relationships</a> and even <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/mar/04/gemini-chatbot-google-jonathan-gavalas">their lives</a>. That is the sort of detail that doesn’t make it into genAI-booster listserv posts.</p>
<p>The quality of literary writing, important as it is to me, is just one small element of all that is going wrong with these applications. But it is something worth paying close attention to, because losing sight of the difference between fake and real creativity is a telling sign of our endangered humanity, and I believe that preserving the truth about writing and art might help get us past this dangerous moment.</p>
<p>A friend sent me a collection of Robert Bly’s translations of the poems of Tomas Tranströmer. As I read the book I researched a bit and came across <a href="https://rebeccasnow.co/2016/04/21/bly-vs-fulton-transtromers-poem-allegro/">a very good essay</a> on translation from a novelist and prof named Rebecca Snow, reflecting on how a skilled translator such as Bly makes use of nuance and imagination in order to put the sense of a literary work across in a rich, exciting way. Snow illustrates flawlessly how fluency and elegance in the target language can make the difference between a relatively flat, mechanistic translation and the transmission of an electrifying literary experience, like what Bly is able to achieve—not only through his gifts as a poet in his own right, but through his particular sympathy with Tranströmer.</p>
<p>She writes,</p>
<blockquote>None of us knew Swedish fluently, so we couldn’t give a true analysis, but as far as the English translations go, Bly rocked and rolled American style without losing the poem’s literary and solemn flair. In the first line, [translator Robin] Fulton is true to the word order in Swedish, beginning with the English version of “Jag spelar Haydn.” While this may work well in the original language, Bly makes it flow like we would say it in English, beginning with “After a black day, . . .” In the second line, “a little warmth in my hands” is more natural and less awkward than “a simple warmth in my hands,” and the two translations go on like this—Fulton making us think too much about it being a translation because of the stilted word choice, Bly making us unconscious that it’s a translation, but making us fully aware, at the same time, of the poem’s originality, power and grace. </blockquote>
<p>I value Google Translate super highly and use it most every day, but I don’t conflate its rough translations of news articles and web pages with the art of a human translator. The tools can’t mimic art specifically because they are tools not people.</p>
<p>I’ve seen people worrying that they’re somehow defective if they can’t immediately identify the fake writing, lacking a kind of discernment that they feel is expected of them like in the story of the Emperor’s New Clothes. But I don’t think that matters a bit. It’s the people trying to put the slop across who are enshittified, whether they succeed in fooling you or not.</p>
