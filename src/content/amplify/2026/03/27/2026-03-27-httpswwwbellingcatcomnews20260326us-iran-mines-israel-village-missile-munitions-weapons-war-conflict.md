<p>The US appears to have deployed the Gator Scatterable Mine system over Kafari, a village near Shiraz, in southern Iran overnight. Several people were killed according to <a href="https://t.me/Tasnimnews/401328">Iranian media</a>.</p>
<p>Three experts told Bellingcat the munitions appeared to be air-delivered US-made Gator anti-tank mines.</p>
<p>The US is the only participant in the Iran war known to possess Gator Scatterable Mines.</p>
<p>Bellingcat asked the US Department of Defense whether it had dropped the mines overnight, but did not receive a response at time of publication.</p>

<p>Dr NR Jenzen-Jones, Director of Armament Research Services, told Bellingcat that the images appeared to show US-made anti-tank landmines.</p>
<p>“These images show what appear to be American BLU-91/B scatterable anti-tank landmines.</p>
<p>“The BLU-91/B is dispensed from the CBU-78/B or CBU-89/B air-delivered cargo bombs (cluster munitions).</p>
<p>“The presence of square ‘aeroballistic adaptors’ indicates that the mines seen here were delivered by air. Similar mines can be dispensed from the vehicle- or helicopter-based Volcano system.”</p>
<figure><img alt="" sizes="(max-width: 1200px) 100vw, 1200px" src="https://www.bellingcat.com/app/uploads/2026/03/2026-03-26_10-03-29.jpg" srcset="https://www.bellingcat.com/app/uploads/2026/03/2026-03-26_10-03-29-1200x782.jpg 1200w, https://www.bellingcat.com/app/uploads/2026/03/2026-03-26_10-03-29-300x196.jpg 300w, https://www.bellingcat.com/app/uploads/2026/03/2026-03-26_10-03-29-768x501.jpg 768w, https://www.bellingcat.com/app/uploads/2026/03/2026-03-26_10-03-29.jpg 1358w"/><figcaption>Gator “SCATMINE” system. BLU-91/B pictured on the bottom left, BLU-92/B pictured on the bottom right. Source:</figcaption></figure>
<p>Amael Kotlarski, Weapons Team leader at Janes, also identified the mines as BLU-91/B ‘Gator’ anti-tank mines. Kotlarski told Bellingcat “the BLU-91/B is dispensed from either the US Air Force’s CBU-89/B (72 BLU-91/B and 22 BLU-92/B) or the US Navy’s CBU-78/B (45 BLU-91/B and 15 BLU-92/B).”</p>
<p>He elaborated that the BLU/92B is an anti-personnel mine, similar in appearance to a BLU/91B, though not identical.</p>
<p>“No BLU-92/B is observable in the photographic evidence presented so far. This could be that they have not been found, or that the dispensers were loaded solely with AT mines to help reduce the risk to civilians.”</p>

<h2>Gator Scatterable Mines System</h2>
<p>The Gator system is an air-delivered dispenser system or cargo bomb that distributes mines over an area. These dispensers contain a <a href="https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%2020-32%20W%20CH%201-4.pdf#page=100">mixture of either 94 or 60</a> BLU-92/B anti-personnel and BLU-91/B anti-vehicle mines depending on which dispenser is used. These dispensers release the mines over an area of <a href="https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%2020-32%20W%20CH%201-4.pdf#page=101">approximately 200 by 650 metres</a>. In the images reviewed by Bellingcat, it is not clear which dispenser was used, or how many dispensers were deployed.</p>

<h2><strong>BLU-91/B and BLU-92/B Mines and Self-Destruct Features</strong></h2>
<p>Both BLU-91/B and BLU-92/B mines are activated two minutes after being deployed; however, a very small number can <a href="https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%2020-32%20W%20CH%201-4.pdf#page=90">fail to properly arm and explode</a>. These mines also have self-destruct features with a variable delay which means they may randomly explode hours or days after they are dispensed. They may also explode if disturbed. These features make them particularly dangerous.</p>
<p>The <a href="https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%2020-32%20W%20CH%201-4.pdf#page=90">self-destruct can be set</a> for 4 hours, 48 hours, or 15 days, but the mines may <a href="https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%2020-32%20W%20CH%201-4.pdf#page=92">self-destruct before then</a>.</p>
<p>So far, only visual evidence of magnetically influenced BLU-91/B anti-tank mines has been posted online, but these mines are usually deployed alongside the anti-personnel BLU-92/B. The BLU-92/B also deploys tripwires.</p>
<p>In addition to the self-destruct features, the BLU-92/B anti-personnel mines have an anti-handling device (AHD) that is intended to make the mine explode when disturbed. While BLU-91/B anti-tank mines do not have an anti-handling device (AHD), they <a href="https://www.marines.mil/Portals/1/Publications/FM%2020-32%20W%20CH%201-4.pdf#page=90">“may detonate when moved</a>, because the mine may sense a significant change from its original orientation.”</p>
<p>Amael Kotlarski of Janes told Bellingcat that “The mine will go off if subjected to significant movement.” This could explain local reports that a man<a href="https://youtu.be/n4RpCzR5Uso"> was killed</a> when he picked one up near his car.</p>

<h2>Uniquely US Weapons</h2>
<p>The US is the only participant in the war known to possess these mines. They were developed after the US stopped supplying arms to Iran. A review of Stockholm International Peace Research Institute’s <a href="https://www.sipri.org/databases/armstransfers">(SIPRI) Arms Transfer Database</a>, and <a href="https://www.dsca.mil/Press-Media/Major-Arms-Sales">US Major Arms Sales</a> does not show any transfers of these mines to Israel.</p>
<p>Dr Jenzen-Jones also told Bellingcat that “Scatterable anti-tank landmines may have been employed to deny vehicles access to or from so-called ‘missile cities’. This could both prevent TELs [missile launch vehicles] from leaving, and limit efforts to re-establish access to facilities (for example, by preventing excavators from operating at collapsed entrances).”</p>
<p>Bellingcat geolocated <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n4RpCzR5Uso">some of the mines</a> to the village of Kafari, Iran (coordinates <a href="https://share.google/bWQS94YA01pYIUHAA">29.50544059, 52.48745447</a> and <a href="https://share.google/r6A4FUDJ93jApm80C">29.50964897, 52.48920842</a>). This video shows at least three mines approximately two kilometres away from the entrance to what is reported to be <a href="https://x.com/sam_lair/status/2028580753576169554?s=20">Shiraz South Missile Base</a>, an Iranian “missile city.”</p>

<p>Bellingcat asked the US Department of Defense to confirm whether they dropped mines in this area, how many were deployed, and what the intended target was. They did not respond at time of publication.</p>
