<p>If you look at the games I’ve made over the years, there a few things that are true for all of them:</p>
<ul> <li>no built-in violence</li> <li>no dice deciding outcomes</li> <li>the players control what’s true in the world</li> </ul>
<p>Sure, you can choose to make a Microscope history that’s all about war and fighting (for example), but there’s no “combat system” or anything that requires violence, the way a ton of other games do. And while Follow has you draw stones based on player input, and Kingdom has a few “hold up 1 or 2 fingers” randomizers, you never roll dice to see what happens.</p>
<p>And as far as players deciding what’s true… well that’s the bedrock of every game I’ve made.</p>
<p>But what if I did the exact opposite? Not for one or two: for all three. What if I made a game about fighting, that uses dice as a core mechanic, and where the players don’t control what happens in the world?</p>
<p>Say hello to my second <a href="https://arsludi.lamemage.com/index.php/4853/because-lab-is-experimental-and-summer-is-fun/">summerlab</a> game: <b>This Means War</b>. Well, technically it’s the first, because I was already working on it when I came up with the idea for <a href="https://arsludi.lamemage.com/index.php/4894/rules-of-law-is-ready-to-play/">Rules of Law</a> and took a little detour to make that game.</p>
<p>The core concept is that, for most people, war is something you can’t control, it’s something you endure. And the rules echo that: we explore the stories of people caught up in the war, but the players don’t get to decide the events of the war or its outcome. Players don’t even get to decide whether characters live or die, because that’s not how war works. Those decisions are in the hands of the rules, the machine that drives the war.</p>
<p>I may have also inadvertently made a game that works great for solo play or for GMs who want to simulate a war in their world. Go figure.</p>
<h3>This Machine Makes Wars</h3>
<p>Some games use tables, dice or cards to introduce random material into the fiction. Instead of any player deciding what kind of terrain you find beyond the mountains, or what kind of god the cult worships, you draw a card that tells you the answer. These systems are often called “oracles”.</p>
<p>The problem is that many oracles do not take into account what we already established in the game. The odds for each outcome stays the same regardless of what already happened or what we already know to be true. It’s less of a problem at the very start of a game, when nothing is established, but <a href="https://arsludi.lamemage.com/index.php/902/diminishing-returns-of-random-fiction/">it gets worse the longer you play</a>, because we have a stronger and stronger concept of the world and what’s happening, and an expectation that new fiction will fit that.</p>
<p>In other words, true-random is bad. Instead we want biased- or informed-random, where the new material responds to what has already happened in our game. That’s how drawing stones works in Follow: the stones we put in are based on our thoughts about the situation. The deck is stacked, in a good way.</p>
<p>This Means War takes a much deeper stab at this whole issue. You roll dice to see what happens, but the system reacts to previous events and the war that has already unfolded. There can be surprises, because war is full of surprises, but it’s a surprise because one side took a big risk (or made a terrible mistake) not because the system doesn’t know better.</p>
<p>And, shockingly, the players have zero influence over these big events. They’re just rolling the dice and following the instructions. The machine decides, and it knows what it’s doing.</p>
<p>Honestly it took a bit of doing, but I’m pretty happy with how it turned out. I wanted it to be realistic but not too complex, and I think it is just about right.</p>
<p>And because this system is an oracle for war, I get to call it THE WARACLE. Which alone was enough to make me want to design this game.</p>
<h3>The Human Side</h3>
<p>So if the Waracle is running the war and doesn’t need humans, what are the humans doing? Those nice people at the table?</p>
<p>Humans are in charge of human things. They tell the stories of people’s lives. Because the trick is that the game is not really about the events of the war. The war is a backdrop for the people living through the war… or dying in it. The Waracle controls the big picture so you can focus on those people.</p>
<p>It’s really a game with two distinct halves, one for the war and one for the people in the war. And — modularity warning! — you could swap out the people side of the game entirely and use a whole different system for playing characters. Or just use the Waracle side and roll dice and see how a war unfolds without ever getting into characters.</p>
<h3>What Shape Is The Table?</h3>
<p>On top of all that, there’s a whole other angle which has been boiling my brain, which is all the possible ways this game could be played.</p>
<p>Yeah you could do the normal pattern of sitting down together and taking turns playing scenes. But I’m not even sure that’s the most appropriate format. The unusual split between seasons of war and characters lives opens up all sorts of options.</p>
<p>For one, the pacing of the human side is not constant. Some seasons we just check-in and get slice of life views of each character as the war rolls on. Other seasons we might zoom in and play scenes digging into one character’s life. So each season is not the same amount of play-time. Maybe we zip through a year of war and then slow way down and spend several sessions playing characters caught up in one battle. Like Microscope, we zoom in to the parts that interest us.</p>
<p>It also lends itself to weirder patterns, like offline play with characters writing journal entries or letters to loved ones back home to talk about their lives. And because events are determined by the Waracle, you could have a single war that was shared by separate gaming groups. Some of us get together each season to roll the dice to see how the battle is going, but then the different groups meet separately to explore the lives of their characters. It doesn’t matter if you aren’t there to give input to the war-side, because there is no input to give. We’re all part of the same larger story, a shared world, even if we don’t see all the smaller stories in it.</p>
<p>Plus the possibilities I already mentioned of solo play or using the Waracle with GMed games.</p>
<p>The whole thing is a bit of an experiment — and intentionally very different from my other games — but hey, that’s what <a href="https://arsludi.lamemage.com/index.php/4853/because-lab-is-experimental-and-summer-is-fun/">summerlab</a> is all about…</p>
