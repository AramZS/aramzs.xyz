<body><div><i>Artificial intelligence drove chess toward perfect play, leading to more draws at top tournaments. Now grandmasters are winning by making less optimal moves.</i></div><figure><img alt="" src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iE3kQs_N4aes/v0/-999x-999.gif"/><figcaption><p>Illustration: Mathieu Labrecque for Bloomberg</p></figcaption></figure><div><p>At the biannual 2018 World Chess Championship, Magnus Carlsen — commonly regarded as the best chess player to ever live — defended his title against challenger Fabiano Caruana in <a href="https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLQsLDm9Rq9bGybC78VlSZR8BKvbIUAiw8" target="_blank">a best-of-12 format</a>. Classical chess allows players long stretches of time in which to make their moves, and the two logged more than 50 hours of play across 12 games. To the shock of the chess world, every single game resulted in a draw, a first in the history of the championship, which dates back to 1886. (Carlsen went on to win after three tiebreakers.)</p><p>This seemed to confirm a growing suspicion: Chess was dead — and draws had killed it. The “draw death” of chess was not a new fear. In 1925, then-World Champion José Raúl Capablanca became so worried about the increasing sophistication of the game’s top players — whom he believed were not far away from drawing games at will — that he proposed a new set of rules to save chess. But it never quite happened: Over the ensuing decades, the draw rate between masters playing classical chess hovered around 50%.</p><p>Then, right around the turn of the millennium, a dread force arrived: artificial intelligence. Chess has possessed better-than-human AI for three decades, going back to Garry Kasparov’s defeat at the hands of IBM’s Deep Blue in 1997. By 2006, you could run a chess program on any home desktop that would annihilate the best flesh-and-blood players. These are called “engines,” and the best of the modern ones, Stockfish — named after the air-dried fish often produced in Norway and cooked in Italy, a reference to its Norwegian and Italian creators — plays at a 3653 rating as of this month, nearly 800 points higher than Magnus Carlsen’s (and humanity’s) peak.</p><figure><img alt="Magnus Carlsen (left) and challenger Fabiano Caruana play the first game in the 2018 World Chess Championship match in London on November 9, 2018." src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/ixHi3fKeW9.c/v2/-1x-1.webp"/><figcaption>Magnus Carlsen (left) and challenger Fabiano Caruana play the first game in the 2018 World Chess Championship match in London on Nov. 9, 2018. / Photographer: Ben Stansall/AFP/Getty Images</figcaption></figure><p>Today, every serious chess player, from the most hapless patzer like me — <a href="http://chess.com">Chess.com</a> handle: ktlincoln — to Carlsen himself, uses AI regularly as a kind of coach and trainer. Engines are prohibited during games, but they’ve changed how players prepare for them.</p><p>The first few moves of a chess game establish the type of “opening” being played. These openings are known by names like the Ruy Lopez, the Italian Game and the Sicilian Najdorf, usually derived from their origins in the history of the game; many have been played for hundreds of years. Some are conservative and safe, others aggressive and risky, but all have their own theory that dictates the best options for responding to them on both sides of the board. Masters will typically play anywhere from the first 10 to 20 (or more) moves from memory.</p><p>In the past, the quality of an opening line was subjective. But now, everyone has access to the same engines, which means everyone can study the same judgments as far as how best to approach each opening. As a result, there has been a <a href="https://www.chess.com/article/view/the-draw-rule-is-classical-chess-dead">demonstrable increase</a> in draws among the game’s elite.</p><p>Carlsen’s answer to this conundrum has been to look outside of classical chess. After surviving another grueling challenge at the 2021 World Championship that included one eight-hour-long match and seven draws, he declined to defend his title for a fourth time, citing a lack of motivation. While he did not abandon slow chess completely — he won the prestigious Norway Chess tournament in 2025, and he remains the world’s highest-rated player — he has recently shown more interest in faster time controls like rapid and blitz. Carlsen is also a proponent and <a href="https://www.chess.com/news/view/12-million-raised-for-revolutionary-freestyle-series-of-tournaments">entrepreneur</a> of freestyle chess, which randomizes the starting position of the pieces, preventing preparation of any kind. He is currently the reigning title holder in all three formats. (Carlsen also has one of the lower draw rates of any top player in longform chess, mainly because he’s just better than everyone else. But when players face off against him, they have months to prepare and the understanding that playing for the draw is probably easier than trying to beat him outright.)</p><figure><img alt="Carlsen walks with the winner's trophy after the FIDE World Rapid and Blitz Championship on Dec. 30, 2025." src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iAl6Z1b.8GK4/v1/-1x-1.webp"/><figcaption>Carlsen walks with the winner's trophy after the FIDE World Rapid and Blitz Championship on Dec. 30, 2025. / Photographer: Mahmud Hams/AFP/Getty Images</figcaption></figure><p>Carlsen’s pivot seemed to suggest that the only hope for the future of chess was to minimize its exposure to AI. With the help of optimized play, the players had become too good. We’ve seen similar debates in other sports, from baseball’s <span><em>Moneyball</em></span> era to basketball’s reliance on the three-pointer. The more sophisticated the strategy becomes, the more it seems to conflict with the romantic notion that sports is about heart, not numbers. In chess, you can speed up the games, increasing the chances of blunders, or you can make preparation irrelevant by moving the pieces around.</p><p>But the world’s next generation of grandmasters, who all grew up with engines, started experimenting with a fresh approach and breathing new life into classical chess. They discovered that while AI prepared you for the best moves, it didn’t necessarily prepare you for playing against a human — one who is fallible, or imperfect.</p><p>One game from 2024 is particularly instructive. At the Candidates Tournament, which determines who gets to challenge the reigning world champion, the 18-year-old Indian GM Praggnanandhaa Rameshbabu left commentator (and former world-championship challenger) Peter Leko “speechless” when, in response to the Ruy Lopez, he played a move that most people consider suboptimal — the engines have all shown it to be flawed compared with other choices in the same position.</p><p>The conventional thinking has been that in modern chess, these engine-invalidated lines aren’t supposed to be strong enough for the current quality of play. Leko said he hadn’t seen that particular move in response to that particular opening in 25 years. Neither had Pragg’s opponent, who was forced “out of book,” meaning he could no longer rely on memorized moves. Pragg went on to win, ushering in a new paradigm in the age of engine-aided chess.</p><p>“Five years ago, players gave up on getting clear advantages out of the opening because it’s just not really possible anymore,” says Peter Doggers, author of <a href="https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/peter-doggers/the-chess-revolution/9781454959250/"><span><em>The Chess Revolution</em></span></a>. “They’re now more going for surprises. The computer says it’s equality, but it’s a funny move, it’s a strange move.” Doggers cited another game from the 2024 Candidates, in which <a href="https://www.chess.com/news/view/2024-fide-candidates-tournament-round-1">American GM Hikaru Nakamura</a> also opted for a supposedly weaker but far less studied version of an otherwise ubiquitous opening. While Nakamura managed a draw rather than a win, it showed how effective a surprising move could be.</p><figure><img alt="India's chess grandmaster Rameshbabu Praggnanandhaa gestures during a game in the Tata Steel Chess India tournament in Kolkata on Nov. 17, 2024." src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/imSa9QtbCENg/v1/-1x-1.webp"/><figcaption>India's chess grandmaster Rameshbabu Praggnanandhaa gestures during a game in the Tata Steel Chess India tournament in Kolkata on Nov. 17, 2024. / Photographer: Dibyangshu Sarkar/AFP/Getty Images</figcaption></figure><p>As much as chess players can prepare, they can’t memorize everything. When they’re sitting at the board, their computers slumbering at home, they will inevitably be defined by the limits of their knowledge and ability. As a result, the elite grandmasters have realized the most valuable move is often the one that forces their opponents to start thinking with their brains rather than their engines, even if it might not be the “best” possible move. (Although let’s be clear: It still has to be solid. There are weird moves and then there are bad moves, and bad moves won’t fly against a GM.)</p><p>“Best,” after all, is relative: Even if your AI tells you the best move available, it doesn’t do you much good if you don’t understand why you’re playing it. The German GM, trainer and opening theorist Jan Gustafsson calls this “hitting spacebar,” the keyboard shortcut for getting the engine to produce what it judges the top move. You might be able to hit the spacebar a bunch of times and think you’re a good chess player, but as soon as your opponent strays from the line that the engine gave you, you’re screwed — unless you actually know the subtleties of what’s happening on the board.</p><p>“You’re constantly translating [computer moves] into human chess,” Doggers says. “[If] there are four moves that are basically all equal, as a human being you have to be good enough to see that the third of the four is going to be the most annoying choice.”</p><p>You might think AI can do this for you, but so far it can’t. With the popularity of ChatGPT and Claude, many of us see AI as a system for explaining things. You ask a question and get answers with explanatory language. But chess engines are fundamentally different. AlphaZero, the engine that pioneered the “neural network” approach now incorporated into Stockfish, learned chess by playing against itself over and over. It couldn’t explain why it was making a move because it didn’t know why, at least not in the way we do: It just “understood” that more than any other move, this gave it a slightly better chance of winning the game.</p><p>Large language models, by contrast, are quite bad at chess. Sometimes <a href="https://www.chess.com/news/view/sign-of-real-intelligence-chat-bots-cheat-at-chess">they cheat</a>. If you give them a series of chess moves, they’ll try to explain themselves, but their explanations can be wrong or <a href="https://www.damiencharlotin.com/hallucinations/">made up</a>. All they can do is search their dataset for similar moves and then supply corresponding explanations.</p><p>It turns out the best way for machines to get better at chess is also the best way for humans: playing it over and over again, not reading about it. We like LLMs because they can put their actions into language that we understand. They seem intelligent, even emotional. But as in chess, the performance of these things can come at the expense of actual quality of output.</p><p>The computer scientist and writer Cal Newport <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TnK1gkoeH4E">recently described</a> how he thinks we’ll one day look back on chatbots as a kind of archaic AI, analogous to how Usenet anticipated the World Wide Web. Instead of this one-AI-fits-all-task approach that LLMs offer, we’ll have highly specialized AI applications that can each tackle the explicit goal for which they’ve been designed. Chess seems to validate Newport’s analysis, but it also demonstrates the appeal (and temptation) of LLMs: Nobody is arguing that <a href="https://stockfishchess.org/">Stockfish</a> is conscious, but Stockfish would kick Claude’s ass at chess.</p><p>At the same time, it suggests an essential idea about the future. The more we outsource our decision tree to AI, the less comprehensible it becomes to us; even top GMs <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GdaU7wpOArs">are often blown away</a> by the moves of the best chess engines. Ultimately, though, understanding is what matters. In the realm of human competition, the best performers will be the ones who can employ surprise, psychology and experience, who can take into account their situation and their opponent, who can adapt on the fly and trust their gut.</p><p>In other words, they’ll be the most human.</p><p><span><em>Kevin Lincoln is a journalist and filmmaker who lives in Los Angeles.
