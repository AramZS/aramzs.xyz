<h1>Can AI Save Local News?</h1>
<h2>The Philadelphia Inquirer and other regional outlets turn to AI to automate elements of reporting and expand coverage</h2>
<p>By <a href="https://www.wsj.com/news/author/alexandra-bruell">Alexandra Bruell</a></p>
<p>March 4, 2026 12:00 pm ET</p>
<figure>
<img alt="The Philadelphia Inquirer building and street signs for 6th Street and Market Street in Philadelphia." src="https://images.wsj.net/im-25992648?width=700&amp;height=466"/>
<figcaption>The Philadelphia Inquirer sees AI as an opportunity for growth after years of shrinking the paper’s footprint.</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The Philadelphia Inquirer hadn’t written much about suburbs like Lower Merion, Pa., and Cherry Hill, N.J. for years. Now it is revisiting those communities—with an assist from AI.</p>
<p>Last year, the Inquirer launched newsletters in four locations, amassing more than 50,000 free subscriptions.</p>
<p>Reporters are using artificial-intelligence tools to scan community meetings for topics that may prompt news, such as a zoning issue related to an ICE detention facility and a proposal for a new data center. The effort is partly funded by a partnership between OpenAI and <a href="https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/MSFT">Microsoft</a> and the nonprofit Lenfest Institute, which owns the Inquirer.</p>
<p>Matt Boggie, chief technology and product officer at the Inquirer, sees AI as an opportunity for growth after years of shrinking the paper’s footprint.</p>
<p>“If we go into these areas and can give context people appreciate, they’re more likely to become paying subscribers to the paper,” he said, adding that the newsletters are “a massive subscription driver” so far. The paper now has plans for eight more AI-assisted newsletters overseen by two new staffers.</p>
<p>Cash-strapped local papers have faced years of print circulation and web traffic declines, and cut staff as revenue dried up. Some newsrooms see an existential threat from the rise of chatbots and <a href="https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/google-ai-news-publishers-7e687141?mod=article_inline">AI-powered search engines</a> that answer users’ questions without requiring them to click on article links. But AI could be a potential savior as well: It promises a way to monitor police scanners and town meetings—the time-consuming bread and butter of much local journalism—more efficiently, and even opens the door to expanding coverage.</p>
