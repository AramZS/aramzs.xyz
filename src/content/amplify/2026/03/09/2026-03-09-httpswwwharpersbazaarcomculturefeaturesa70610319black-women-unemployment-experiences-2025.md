<p>When I was laid off in July 2025, I was let go in <a data-node-id="0.1" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="the worst job market since the Great Recession." data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/05/economy/us-jobs-data-layoffs-hiring" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/05/economy/us-jobs-data-layoffs-hiring">the worst job market since the Great Recession.</a> I applied for jobs I wanted and jobs I didn’t. I remixed and rewrote my résumé and cover letter almost weekly. I was immensely grateful that I have no dependents and no debt. The more time that passes with my career on hiatus, the more comfortable I’ve had to become living in the uncertainty of it all. It’s challenging to hype yourself up to apply for new opportunities with the stats about Black women’s unemployment blaring in the background.</p>
<p>It’s been one year since <a data-node-id="1.1" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="the Trump administration triggered mass layoffs in the federal government that roiled into the private sector" data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://msmagazine.com/2026/02/05/black-women-jobs-trump-layoffs-doge-cuts-fired/" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://msmagazine.com/2026/02/05/black-women-jobs-trump-layoffs-doge-cuts-fired/">the Trump administration triggered mass layoffs in the federal government that roiled into the private sector</a>. By July 2025, more than 300,000 Black women had exited the workforce, and <a data-node-id="1.3" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="that number scaled to 600,000 in November." data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://fortune.com/2025/11/22/the-exit-economy-black-women-labor-force-participation-inequality/" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://fortune.com/2025/11/22/the-exit-economy-black-women-labor-force-participation-inequality/">that number scaled to 600,000 in November.</a> Vulnerable to attacks against DEI and overrepresented in federal roles, <a data-node-id="1.5" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="Black college-educated women have been hit harder by layoffs than any other demographic." data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://www.epi.org/blog/black-women-suffered-large-employment-losses-in-2025-particularly-among-college-graduates-and-public-sector-workers/" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://www.epi.org/blog/black-women-suffered-large-employment-losses-in-2025-particularly-among-college-graduates-and-public-sector-workers/">Black college-educated women have been hit harder by layoffs than any other demographic.</a> Black women have traditionally excelled in federal employment because of governmental protections against biased hiring and the attraction of what was once seen as steady, stable long-term employment with reliable health and retirement benefits. This may no longer be the case.</p>
<p>In December, <a data-node-id="2.1" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="Minda Harts" data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://www.mindaharts.com/" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://www.mindaharts.com/">Minda Harts</a>, author of <em data-node-id="2.3">The Memo: What Women of Color Need to Know to Secure a Seat at the Table</em>, <a data-node-id="2.5" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="posted a plea to Threads" data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://www.threads.com/@mindaharts/post/DRu0C4CkuRG?xmt=AQF0KywIQjC87UT65z_QuOEbzY7I3BgIjNAsHMs-OXQzVKtMvC6V7XDl7zUfjc3WIdZTGHtM&amp;slof=1" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://www.threads.com/@mindaharts/post/DRu0C4CkuRG?xmt=AQF0KywIQjC87UT65z_QuOEbzY7I3BgIjNAsHMs-OXQzVKtMvC6V7XDl7zUfjc3WIdZTGHtM&amp;slof=1">posted a plea to Threads</a> asking for an end to articles on Black women’s massive job losses. I reached out to Harts over email and asked her to expand on her post. Harts responded that she wants to hold decision-makers accountable: “What I didn’t want is for corporations to hide behind ‘the data,’ as if layoffs of Black women were an unfortunate inevitability rather than the result of decisions about who is seen as essential, who is protected, and whose labor is valued when things get tight.”</p>
<p>And the stats don’t acknowledge Black women’s autonomy. “Black women are not just impacted by these moments, we are navigating, rebuilding, and redefining our careers in real time,” Hart says. “When we only focus on the stat, we risk flattening Black women into outcomes instead of honoring them as professionals with expertise, institutional knowledge, and leadership potential. I didn’t want the conversation to linger in loss.”</p>
<p>In an effort to shine a light on the stories behind the statistic, I reached out to four Black women laid off this past year about their experiences and what’s next.</p>
<p>Prior to the dismantling of our newsroom by layoffs, 26-year-old Ariyon Dailey was a social-media producer on the audience team but supported my newsletter team.</p>
<p>I remember in December of 2024, Dailey was making small talk about an upcoming big trip with her sorors that she was stressed about financially. She decided to toss the trip on a credit card and worry about paying it off in the new year. “I was ready for 2025 to be my year of financial stability,” she says now. “And to my surprise, January 7, I no longer had a job. I’m trying to repair all the damage I did in 2024, and I only got further in the hole in 2025.” By the time she found her new position in September, she’d amassed $11,000 in credit-card debt.</p>
<p>Dailey almost immediately began making <a data-affiliate="false" data-affiliate-network="" data-href="https://www.tiktok.com/@ariyondailey/video/7457730332473724206?is_from_webapp=1&amp;sender_device=pc&amp;web_id=7594613032031897118" data-node-id="9.1" data-product-url="https://www.tiktok.com/@ariyondailey/video/7457730332473724206?is_from_webapp=1&amp;sender_device=pc&amp;web_id=7594613032031897118" data-vars-ga-axid="36067f88-171c-4f98-959d-e1c10733d082" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="TikTok content about being laid off" data-vars-ga-link-treatment="(not set) | (not set)" data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://www.tiktok.com/@ariyondailey/video/7457730332473724206?is_from_webapp=1&amp;sender_device=pc&amp;web_id=7594613032031897118" data-vars-ga-product-brand="Program" data-vars-ga-product-id="152b275f-7037-4a4a-9cb2-6b37fb61949c" data-vars-ga-product-price="$0.00" data-vars-ga-product-retailer-id="447a65ed-71d6-423d-9890-0b32d2863a87" data-vars-ga-product-sem3-brand="Program" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://www.tiktok.com/@ariyondailey/video/7457730332473724206?is_from_webapp=1&amp;sender_device=pc&amp;web_id=7594613032031897118">TikTok content about being laid off</a>. “I need something to do, so even though I was struggling … I like being transparent with my audience,” she says. “I could provide truth about my own life, and that was really exciting, but very vulnerable and very sad, all at once.”</p>
<p>As the year continued, Dailey noticed more and more Black women losing their jobs: “Everything I do, with every fiber of my being, is for Black women, so to be a Black woman and be laid off, and then you look next to you and your sister’s laid off, and then your cousin’s being laid off, and then your classmate’s being laid off … what is happening right now?” Dailey does her best to return the support she received when she was laid off, but as she says, “I knew that there was community, but you never know where love is going to come from when you’re in a place where you need help.”</p>
<p>Eventually, she left Dallas to accept a role at the <em data-node-id="11.1">Houston Chronicle.</em> The day of our chat, she tells me she’s heading to the dealership later in the day to pick up her new car and that she bought a big bright-pink bow for it for the celebration photos. “Even though I’m up again,” she says, “I still feel like, ‘Damn, that was certainly a moment in my life I won’t forget.’ ”</p>
<p>Alexandra Robinson, a 34-year-old mother of four living in Portland, Oregon, with her husband, lost her dream job last year. “Up until the day we got the email, everyone who had been at the government longer was saying, ‘It can’t happen. They won’t be able to do it that fast. It’ll take months to years for them to even be able to accomplish what they’re saying,’ ” she says. “It took them two weeks.”</p>
<p>Robinson had been in her role as a realty specialist for a small government agency for nearly five years when she accepted the government buyout offer that eliminated her role but would continue to pay her salary and benefits through September. With four young children, she felt like it was the safest choice for her family. Her husband encouraged her to pursue her long-time dream of attending law school. She’s applied to several, and while she waits to hear back, she’s homeschooling their second grader and their kindergartner, as well as caring for their one- and three-year-olds.</p>
<p>“I’m still very disheartened and frustrated, [but] I also think that if you let it, it is a beautiful thing to be able to have the opportunity to pivot,” Robinson says. “My father was in his role for 31 years as a bus driver, but he has so many (other) talents … and it wasn’t until he retired in his 60s that he’s now able to explore those things.”</p>
<p>Robinson doesn’t intend on being anything other than 100 percent herself in her next role, unlike how she was in her previous position. “There are some things that I should have just said. There are some things that I should have just did, and I should have just said,” she says. “And I think that, in a lot of ways, I decided that I’m not going to keep sugarcoating who I am to make other people comfortable, because that was what I had to do a lot.”</p>
<p>This sense of having bit our tongues, only to still abruptly find ourselves back on the job market, is prevalent among Black women. <a data-node-id="17.1" data-vars-ga-call-to-action="Jodi-Ann Burey" data-vars-ga-outbound-link="https://jodiannburey.com/authentic" data-vars-ga-ux-element="Hyperlink" href="https://jodiannburey.com/authentic">Jodi-Ann Burey</a>, who wrote <em data-node-id="17.3">Authentic: The Myth of Bringing Your Full Self to Work</em><em data-node-id="17.4">,</em> put it this way over email: “Some [Black women] may internalize it as a personal failing. Those who understand the larger ecosystem of occupational violence targeting Black women may be overwhelmed because it feels like there’s no escape. It’s like we weren’t just laid off from a job but the entire market. It’s demoralizing, and for those in charge right now, that’s the point. It’s a deep betrayal when you did all the ‘right’ things and those things were and will never be enough to protect you or any of us.”</p>
<p>Black women are collectively coming to the conclusion that if it can all be lost in the matter of a moment, then it’s not worthing losing yourself over.</p>
<p>This is a calculus that Ramona Dallum, a senior vice president for a foundation in Louisville, Kentucky, has had to consider in the nine months since her layoff. Dallum is not optimistic that this moment will trigger a reckoning in the workplace. “I don’t think it’s going to make a change in the way that people who are hiring see us,” she says. “But I think it will make a change in the way that we see ourselves and what we will allow and not allow.”</p>
<p>She reflects on her time as an executive, giving 200 percent of herself, working nights and weekends to deliver on the standards she’d set. “Now, I know I was doing that at the expense of my own joy, of being able to go to the gym, go for that walk,” Dallum says. “Moving forward, I’m not going to do that. Not at the detriment of myself.”</p>
<blockquote data-theme-key="pullquote"><blockquote> It’s like we weren’t just laid off from a job but the entire market.</blockquote></blockquote>
<p>Dallum, who’s become a full-time caretaker for her 84-year-old mother following her mother’s bad fall and a stroke, is now questioning how her job loss will affect her own later years. She says, “I’m not sure what retirement looks like. As I’m caring for my mother, I’m thinking about who cares for me?” Although she is deeply overwhelmed by orchestrating her mother’s care, Dallum remains grateful for the timing of it all. If she were still employed full-time, she might not have been able to take on this responsibility so fully, “I’ve laughed with Mama in ways that I hadn’t laughed in the past,” she says. “I’ve learned things about her and stories that I wouldn’t have heard if I weren’t spending this time with her. That’s joy.”</p>
<p>Benét Wilson had not expected to return to the workforce after being laid off in September. “When they put us on that Zoom call and we got that speech, I actually smiled. I forgot my camera was on,” she says. “Then somebody messaged me, ‘You know we just got laid off, right?’ ”</p>
<p>She’d started her role as a lead credit-card writer a year prior, thinking that it would be her last job before retirement. She received severance, so she took some time to travel and figure out what was next. “I put together the Aunt Benét Retirement Tour,” she says. Wilson visited seven different cities, went on a cruise, and even took a trip to India. At 62, she was settling into the idea of being a retiree when she saw an opening at Investigative Reporters and Editors (IRE).</p>
<p>Although Wilson benefited from a stroke of divine timing, she doesn’t want to take away from the hardships others are experiencing right now. “When you start getting into the 40s and 50s, that’s when it gets tough,” she says. “I do realize age discrimination is a thing, but I have always managed to work around it, under it, because I brought skills and different things to the table.”</p>
<p>For decades, Wilson has done free résumé reviews for fellow journalists, with a special interest in supporting women of color and Black women. For Wilson, being of service to one another is simply what Black women do: “That’s what we’ve always done. Going back to slave times, going back to our ancestors, Black women have been the center.”</p>
<p>Throughout my interviews with Black women who’ve been laid off this past year, the word <em data-node-id="27.1">devastating</em> surfaced repeatedly. But so did the idea of possibility. Dailey told me if she ever got laid off again, she’d write a book or do nails or go back to school to become a dental tech. Robinson is awaiting her law-school acceptances. Dallum is creating services for the community she loves. And Wilson will wander whichever way the winds of fortune blow her.</p>
<p>Our work does not define us. We do. I still don’t know what’s next for me, but I do know the possibilities are endless (even if my bank account is not).</p>
