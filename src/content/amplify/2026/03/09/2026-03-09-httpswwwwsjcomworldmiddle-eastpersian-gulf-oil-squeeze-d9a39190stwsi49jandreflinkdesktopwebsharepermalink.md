<h1>The Long-Feared Persian Gulf Oil Squeeze Is Upon Us</h1>
<p>Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has ground to a virtual halt, unleashing the most severe energy crisis since the 1970s and threatening the global economy.</p>
<figure>
<img alt="An Iranian woman watches smoke rise from an oil refinery following an airstrike in Tehran." src="https://images.wsj.net/im-75379566/?width=1280&amp;height=1280"/>
<figcaption>An Iranian woman watches smoke rise from an oil refinery following an airstrike in Tehran. ABEDIN TAHERKENAREH/EPA/SHUTTERSTOCK</figcaption>
</figure>
<p>The chairman of oil producer <a href="https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/NO/XOSL/DNO">DNO</a> was flying from New York to Oslo early on Feb. 28 when he told staff to turn off the company’s oil wells in Iraq.</p>
<p>America and Israel had just attacked neighboring Iran. Bijan Mossavar-Rahmani wasn’t taking any chances, having weathered a drone strike on the company’s oil fields in Iraqi Kurdistan last summer. By the time he landed, the pumps had stopped—the first oil shutdown of the war.</p>
<p>To the south, another problem was brewing. An apparent recording of an Iranian naval captain telling ships not to enter the Strait of Hormuz spread through industry WhatsApp groups.</p>
<p>Tanker traffic slowed to a trickle. The doomsday some oil analysts believed could never happen was coming to pass. Unable to ship crude to world markets, much bigger producers in Iraq began to run out of places to put it.</p>
