<p>Weeks before Graham Platner promoted an antisemitic conspiracy theorist in a now-deleted social media post on Thursday, the controversial Maine Senate candidate appeared on a popular YouTube show whose host has spread specious claims about Jews and Israel.</p>
<p>Platner faced blowback this week for <a data-ylk="slk:boosting;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://jewishinsider.com/2026/02/graham-platner-stew-peters-post-x-maine/">boosting</a> a social media comment about a looming war with Iran by Stew Peters, a neo-Nazi influencer who has frequently espoused antisemitic tropes and engaged in Holocaust denial. Platner’s team said the post was made in error and “immediately” <a data-ylk="slk:removed it;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://image.jewishinsider.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/26130544/IMG_5098.jpg?_gl=1*q6qn19*_gcl_au*MTQ1NjQ3NzUzMC4xNzY2NDE0OTY4_ga=2.72248019.1186799155.1771770598-1719576982.1742846020">removed it</a> after learning it elevated a “despicable account.”</p>
<p>In late January, however, Platner sat for a <a data-ylk="slk:lengthy online interview;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.youtube.com/live/JLTfYFRF9yc?si=ovjiZi63jR6linbS">lengthy online interview</a> with Nate Cornacchia, a retired Green Beret who has also promoted antisemitic conspiracy theories. Near the end of their hour-long conversation, Platner, a fellow military veteran, called himself “a longtime fan” of Cornacchia’s YouTube channel, “Valhalla VFT,” and said it was “an absolute pleasure being” on the show.</p>
<p>Cornacchia, whose show claims nearly 500,000 YouTube subscribers, has in recent months <a data-ylk="slk:helped stoke;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://youtu.be/rJjWx3Zlv0s">helped stoke</a> a burgeoning far-right conspiracy theory alleging that Israel was involved in the assassination of the conservative activist Charlie Kirk.</p>
<p>Two days before speaking with Platner, for instance, Cornacchia <a data-ylk="slk:went on a podcast;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://youtu.be/jiiD7Ku3a9M">went on a podcast</a> hosted by Jake Shields, a former mixed martial arts fighter who is now a <a data-ylk="slk:prominent Holocaust denier;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.splcenter.org/resources/hatewatch/podcast-platforms-host-jake-shields-bigotry/">prominent Holocaust denier</a>, and pointed to what he described as “huge links” connecting Israel to Kirk’s killing. “The biggest one, the way I look at it, is because he was basically so important from the Zionist side to the young right wing, sort of that counter Nick Fuentes audience” that “Israel needs desperately,” he said.</p>
<p>“Charlie Kirk said that he was tired of being bullied by his Jewish donors” and that he “no longer could support the pro-Israel cause, and he was dead 48 hours later,” Cornacchia added on the show, where he also agreed with Shields’ assertion that President John F. Kennedy had “probably” been assassinated in a covert “venture between the CIA and Mossad,” the Israeli intelligence agency. “100%,” he replied.</p>
<p>In addition, Cornacchia has suggested that the global war on terror was conducted “on behest of Israel” and claimed Israel would benefit if another 9/11-style attack were carried out during the tenure of New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani, saying it would help to drum up Islamophobic sentiment and lead to another foreign military entanglement in the Middle East.</p>
<p>The Jewish state “got exactly who they wanted” in Mamdani, the city’s first Muslim mayor whom the Israeli government has <a data-ylk="slk:accused;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/01/02/nyregion/mamdani-israel-executive-orders.html">accused</a> of antisemitism, he <a data-ylk="slk:argued on his own show;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://youtu.be/SYi2WgHnKRU">argued on his own show</a> in November. “Thats their candidate, guys. You got to pay attention.”</p>
<p>The conservative YouTube commentator has also <a data-ylk="slk:questioned;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://youtu.be/SYi2WgHnKRU">questioned</a> why George Soros’ son Alex was <a data-ylk="slk:seen;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://x.com/alexandersoros/status/1985906188584960502?s=46t=lO03GaaQiKwBXh8cK5OsOQ">seen</a> posing for a photograph with Mamdani on the night of his election. “You may immediately be thinking, ‘Oh, well, of course, thats because Soros funds all the socialists,’” Cornacchia said of George, a Jewish billionaire donor to left-wing causes and a Holocaust survivor who is often a target of antisemitic attacks.</p>
<p>“No, no, no, no, thats surface level,” he told his viewers, claiming that the philanthropist “moves in coordination with our greatest ally,” a term he uses to sarcastically allude to Israel.</p>
<p>Platner’s interview with Cornacchia, which he <a data-ylk="slk:promoted;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://x.com/grahamformaine/status/2016634605785207031?s=46t=lO03GaaQiKwBXh8cK5OsOQ">promoted</a> on his social media channels, did not touch on such topics. His team did not respond to a request for comment on Friday about Cornacchia’s antisemitic remarks.</p>
<p>The 41-year-old Senate candidate, who is running in a competitive Democratic primary to challenge Sen. Susan Collins (R-ME), faced skepticism from critics last fall after he denied knowing a tattoo that was on his chest for years closely mirrored a Totenkopf, the skull-and-crossbones icon adopted by an infamous Nazi SS unit. A former acquaintance of Platner <a data-ylk="slk:who spoke with Jewish Insider;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://jewishinsider.com/2025/10/graham-platner-ss-tattoo-maine-senate/">who spoke with Jewish Insider</a> said he had identified the symbol as such more than a decade ago, a claim he has denied. He had the tattoo removed last October.</p>
<p>Still, even as he maintains a commanding polling advantage in the Maine Democratic primary against Gov. Janet Mills, Platner’s explanation of the tattoo — combined with past and recent online blunders — is raising questions about whether he can <a data-ylk="slk:weather scrutiny;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://x.com/nrsc/status/2027438960297771113?s=46t=lO03GaaQiKwBXh8cK5OsOQ">weather scrutiny</a> in a general election that party leadership views as key to reclaiming the Senate majority.</p>
<p>Mills, for her part, strongly hinted at such doubts in a snarky X post on Friday. “For what its worth,” she wrote, “I dont have any tattoos.”</p>
<p>And on the same day he was drawing backlash for amplifying a conspiracy theorist this week, Platner was <a data-ylk="slk:fielding a combative call;elm:context_link;itc:0;sec:content-canvas" href="https://x.com/isaacdovere/status/2027129752004317449?s=46t=lO03GaaQiKwBXh8cK5OsOQ">fielding a combative call</a> from a listener during an appearance on a podcast hosted by the comedian Tim Heidecker.</p>
<p>The caller cast suspicion on Platner’s claim that he did not know the tattoo represented a Nazi symbol until recently, citing his self-proclaimed knowledge of World War II history, and called on the candidate to apologize rather than “dodging around” the issue.</p>
<p>But Platner held firm. “I’m not going to apologize for something that I didn’t know about or do,” he insisted. “The moment that it was clear and I was putting it in that context I had it covered, because I don’t want that on my body.”</p>
