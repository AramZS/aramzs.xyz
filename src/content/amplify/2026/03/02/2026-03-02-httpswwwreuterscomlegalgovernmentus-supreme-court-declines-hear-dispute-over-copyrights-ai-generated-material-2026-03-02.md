<h1 data-testid="Heading">US Supreme Court declines to hear dispute over copyrights for AI-generated material</h1>
<p>By <a data-testid="AuthorNameLink" href="https://www.reuters.com/authors/blake-brittain/">Blake Brittain</a></p>
<p>March 2, 202610:01 AM ESTUpdated 1 hour ago</p>

<p>WASHINGTON, March 2 (Reuters) - The <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/legal/us-supreme-court/">U.S. Supreme Court</a> declined on Monday to take up the ​issue of whether art generated by artificial intelligence can be copyrighted under U.S. law, turning ‌away a case involving a computer scientist from Missouri who was denied a copyright for a piece of visual art made by his AI system.</p>
<p>Plaintiff Stephen Thaler had appealed to the justices after lower courts upheld a U.S. Copyright Office ​decision that the AI-crafted visual art at issue in the case was ineligible for copyright protection ​because it did not have a human creator.</p>
<p>Thaler, of St. Charles, Missouri, applied for ⁠a federal copyright registration in 2018 covering "A Recent Entrance to Paradise," visual art he said his AI ​technology "DABUS" created. The image shows train tracks entering a portal, surrounded by what appears to be green and ​purple plant imagery.</p>
<p>The Copyright Office rejected his application in 2022, finding that creative works must have human authors to be eligible to receive a copyright.</p>
<p>U.S. President <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/topic/person/donald-trump/">Donald Trump</a>'s administration had urged the Supreme Court not to hear Thaler's appeal.</p>
<p>The Copyright Office has ​separately rejected <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/us-copyright-office-denies-protection-another-ai-created-image-2023-09-06/">bids</a> by <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/legal/ai-created-images-lose-us-copyrights-test-new-technology-2023-02-22/">artists</a> for copyrights on images generated by the AI system Midjourney. Those artists argued that ​they were entitled to copyrights for images they created with AI assistance - unlike Thaler, who said his system created "A Recent ‌Entrance to ⁠Paradise" independently.</p>
<p>A federal judge in Washington <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/legal/ai-generated-art-cannot-receive-copyrights-us-court-says-2023-08-21/">upheld</a> the office's decision in Thaler's case in 2023, writing that human authorship is a "bedrock requirement of copyright." The U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/world/us/us-appeals-court-rejects-copyrights-ai-generated-art-lacking-human-creator-2025-03-18/">affirmed the ruling</a> in 2025.</p>
<p>Thaler's lawyers <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/us-supreme-court-asked-hear-dispute-over-copyrights-ai-creations-2025-10-10/">told the Supreme Court</a> in a filing that his case was of "paramount importance" considering the ​rapid rise of generative AI.</p>
<p>With ​a refusal by the ⁠court to hear the appeal, Thaler's lawyers said, "even if it later overturns the Copyright Office's test in another case, it will be too late. The Copyright Office ​will have irreversibly and negatively impacted AI development and use in the creative ​industry during ⁠critically important years."</p>
<p>"Although the Copyright Act does not define the term 'author,' multiple provisions of the act make clear that the term refers to a human rather than a machine," the administration said.</p>
<p>The Supreme Court previously <a data-testid="Link" href="http://reuters.com/legal/us-supreme-court-rejects-computer-scientists-lawsuit-over-ai-generated-2023-04-24/">rejected Thaler's request</a> ⁠to hear ​his argument in a separate case involving prototypes for a beverage ​holder and a light beacon concerning whether AI-generated inventions should be eligible for U.S. patent protection. His patent applications were rejected by ​the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office on similar grounds.</p>
