<h2>The pullbacks signal growing friction between the platform and some of the biggest holding companies as The Trade Desk pushes deeper into the supply chain<br/></h2>

<p><a href="https://www.adweek.com/contributor/trishla-ostwal/"><figure><img alt="Headshot of Trishla Ostwal" src="https://static-prod.adweek.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/trishla-ostwal-1x1-2022.jpg"/><figcaption><a href="https://www.adweek.com/contributor/trishla-ostwal/">Headshot of Trishla Ostwal</a></figcaption></figure></a></p>
<p>The world’s largest holding companies are pulling back from one of The Trade Desk’s most closely-watched, and arguably important, initiative: OpenPath.</p>
<p>OpenPath is meant to make ads perform better and help ad buyers directly purchase publishers’ inventory. If successful, it could offer a viable alternative to Google, and CEO Jeff Green bullishly anticipated 2025 as the year it enters the “steep acceleration phase of its S-curved growth.”</p>
<p>But some of the biggest advertisers that had invested in OpenPath have pulled back, wary of what they say is a lack of transparency in where exactly their ads are running alongside what they describe as hidden fees on the platform.</p>
