<p>It’s tough to be a robot on the mean streets of Philadelphia. Infamous for brutally destroying an <a href="https://www.cnn.com/2015/08/03/us/hitchbot-robot-beheaded-philadelphia-feat">experimental hitchhiking robot</a> in 2015, residents in the city of brotherly love are now sharing their sidewalks with a new kind of pedestrian: delivery robots.</p>
<p>A video recorded by Philadelphia street photographer Hugh Dillon late Saturday night shows that the city’s reputation as a haven for robophobia hasn’t improved much over the years. In the 30 second clip, the little bot is kicked, sat on, laughed at, and even humped by a crowd of onlookers as it prattles along to its destination.</p>
<p>After escaping the crowd’s wrath, the robot ambles over past Dillon, who’s able to get a clear shot of graffiti reading “DESTROY ME PLZ” scrawled on its plastic chassis.</p>
<p>“Honestly thought it was done for,” <a href="https://www.instagram.com/reel/DV43Z5ikTsw/">Dillon wrote</a> in the video caption. “One wanted to throw it, another sat on it. another person wrote destroy me on it… Then it calmly rolled on its merry way.”</p>

<p>Another video <a href="https://www.instagram.com/reel/DV6BgP-jL6z/">shared on Instagram</a>, presumably taken at an earlier point, shows the same bot rumbling along to deliver some food with the same graffiti scribbled on its exterior.</p>
<p>“He doesn’t even know where the f*** he’s going bro,” a Philly resident hollers after the robot. “F***ing re***d.”</p>
<p>“ROB HIM OF HIS COPPER IMMEDIATELY,” another enterprising Philadelphian commented.</p>
<p>The robots come courtesy of a partnership between Uber and robotics company Avride. According to <a href="https://www.nbcphiladelphia.com/news/local/uber-eats-robot-delivery-center-city-philadelphia/4366116/">local reporting</a>, each autonomous delivery vehicle has a one to two mile radius, and a maximum speed of five miles per hour.</p>
<p>Similar robotics companies have already worn out their welcome in other US cities like Los Angeles and Chicago, as their delivery bots <a href="https://futurism.com/robots-and-machines/rage-delivery-robots-chicago">hog sidewalks</a>, <a href="https://www.cbsnews.com/chicago/news/north-side-residents-petition-pause-to-delivery-robots/">injure residents</a>, and <a href="https://futurism.com/robots-and-machines/delivery-robot-yard-garden">rampage through gardens</a>. Compared to those cities, the gadgets are relatively new to the 215 — but given the city’s <a href="https://futurism.com/waymo-philly-hitchbot-debacle">infamously short fuse</a>, you can bet the good people of Philly are just getting warmed up.</p>
