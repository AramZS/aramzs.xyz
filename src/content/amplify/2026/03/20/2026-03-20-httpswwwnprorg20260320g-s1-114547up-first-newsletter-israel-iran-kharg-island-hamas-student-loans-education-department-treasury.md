<p><strong>Three major reports this month reveal that Trump has rapidly harmed American democracy</strong> since returning to the White House. Bright Line Watch, which surveys over 500 U.S. scholars, concluded that the U.S. now falls nearly halfway between liberal democracy and dictatorship. The organization's co-directors <a href="https://www.npr.org/2026/03/20/nx-s1-5754021/trump-democracy-autocracy-dictatorship-reports">spoke to NPR exclusively</a> ahead of the survey's publication next week. An annual V-Dem report dropped the U.S.'s democracy ranking from 20 to 51 among 179 countries. A Freedom House report released yesterday said that among free countries, the U.S. recorded some of the largest declines in political rights and civil liberties last year.</p>

<p><strong>The Trump administration announced a three-phase transition yesterday</strong> to shift significant management of the nation's federal student loan portfolio from the Education Department to the Treasury Department. <h3>Behind the story</h3>
<figure><picture><figure><img alt="Cesar Chavez, a farm worker, labor organizer and leader of the California grape strike, is seen in a California works office in 1965." data-template="https://npr.brightspotcdn.com/dims3/default/strip/false/crop/3000x1688+0+45/resize/{width}/quality/{quality}/format/{format}/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fnpr-brightspot.s3.amazonaws.com%2F21%2F5b%2Fdb7bbcac48d788ca84b0c42e7ded%2Fap26076698210998.jpg" src="https://npr.brightspotcdn.com/dims3/default/strip/false/crop/3000x1688+0+45/resize/1100/quality/50/format/jpeg/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fnpr-brightspot.s3.amazonaws.com%2F21%2F5b%2Fdb7bbcac48d788ca84b0c42e7ded%2Fap26076698210998.jpg"/><figcaption>Cesar Chavez, a farm worker, labor organizer and leader of the California grape strike, is seen in a California works office in 1965.</figcaption></figure></picture></figure>
<p>My phone kept going off on Wednesday afternoon with texts from different friends — each wanting to trade thoughts on what felt like the second death of Cesar Chavez.</p>
<p>His first death happened on April 23, 1993. He was 66 and died of natural causes. Over 50,000 people attended his funeral in Delano, Calif.</p>
<p>At that time, I was in elementary school in suburban Chicago, far from California. It was then that I first learned of Chavez and his movement's hard-fought efforts to secure better wages and improved working conditions for farm workers. As a daughter of janitors and a factory worker, I knew what better pay and the right to a union meant for people like us.</p>
<p>Chavez's second death landed on Wednesday after a <em>The New York Times</em> investigation revealed he had been <a href="https://www.npr.org/2026/03/18/nx-s1-5752253/cesar-chavez-sexual-assault-dolores-huerta-united-farm-workers">accused of sexual abuse and rape</a>.</p>
<p>For several years before joining <em>Morning Edition</em> as an editor, I covered sexual violence for <a href="https://www.propublica.org/people/adriana-gallardo">ProPublica</a>, an investigative newsroom. My work there was often not about catching the bad guys but rather about listening to the people they hurt. Consistent with <a href="https://rainn.org/facts-statistics-the-scope-of-the-problem/statistics-perpetrators-of-sexual-violence/">statistics</a>, the perpetrators whom I wrote about were often family, bosses, clergy or others in positions of power.</p>
<p>This week, many of the voices of the victims I spoke with hearkened back to the experiences that the <em>New York Times</em>'s investigation revealed in telling of the sexual abuse that Ana Murguia, Debra Rojas and Dolores Huerta shared with the publication. I was grateful to learn Murguia's and Rojas' names alongside the much more familiar one of Huerta, the civil rights icon in her own right who co-led the United Farm Workers movement that made Chavez famous.</p>
<p>I've learned that justice for many means the world recognizing the harm done to them — and the difficult work they have done to no longer live defined by it. And I've learned that sometimes sharing their stories is one way to prevent future harm.</p>
<p>My friends and I may be down a hero this week. But, we gained two new heroes in Ana Murguia and Debra Rojas, who, alongside Dolores Huerta, showed us it's never too late to speak up. In fact, it might be the only way out for them and others.</p>
