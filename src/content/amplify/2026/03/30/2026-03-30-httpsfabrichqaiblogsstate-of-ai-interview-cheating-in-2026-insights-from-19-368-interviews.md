<ul><li><strong>38.5%</strong> of all candidates flagged for cheating behavior</li><li><strong>Technical roles</strong> show 48% cheating rates vs 12% for sales</li><li><strong>61%</strong> of cheaters score above pass thresholds and would advance without detection</li><li><strong>3x increase</strong> in cheating from July to September 2025</li><li><strong>Junior candidates</strong> (0-5 YoE) cheat at nearly double the rate of senior candidates</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Normalization of AI</strong> - Just as spellcheck became standard for writing, candidates now view AI assistance as a standard tool for interviewing. The moral barrier has lowered significantly as generative AI has become a daily utility in their actual work.</li><li><strong>The Prisoner's Dilemma</strong> - Many genuine candidates feel forced to cheat because they assume their competition is already doing it. They fear that being the only honest participant puts them at an unfair disadvantage in a market where efficiency and speed are rewarded.</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>The Viral Effect</strong> - Social media platforms saw an explosion of content showing how to beat interviews using AI. Once candidates saw others getting offers with these tools, the Fear Of Missing Out (FOMO) drove mass adoption.</li><li><strong>Tool Maturity</strong> - Late 2025 saw the release of stable, invisible overlay tools. Before this, cheating was risky and buggy. The new generation of tools made it feel safe and easy, lowering the barrier to entry</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Better tech savviness - </strong>Candidates in these roles are more tech savvy and are more likely to try a new technology</li><li><strong>Better cheating results -</strong> Interviews in these roles have more objective questions which are easier to cheat on. Interviews for roles like sales, marketing and HR include more open ended questions with a higher focus on soft skills. </li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>High-Stakes Competition</strong> - The entry-level market is incredibly saturated. Junior candidates often feel that they need every possible advantage just to get a foot in the door.</li><li><strong>Knowledge Gaps</strong> - Unlike senior engineers who might use AI to speed up syntax they already know, junior candidates often use AI to generate answers for concepts they have never learned. With companies insisting on unrealistic knowledge expectations from junior candidates, interview cheating clears their path to getting a job. </li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Junior Roles</strong> - Most roles in this category fall under junior roles, and we previously discussed why cheating is more prevalent in younger professionals. </li><li><strong>Volume Approach</strong> - Candidates in this bracket often apply to hundreds of roles. They use AI tools to mass-interview efficiently, treating it as a numbers game rather than a curated career move.</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Stop using standard LeetCode-style questions -</strong> These are the easiest for AI to solve; switch to vague, real-world debugging scenarios or system design discussions where there is no single "correct" code block.</li><li><strong>Assume secure browsers are compromised -</strong> Do not rely on browser locking or tab-tracking software; assume candidates can bypass these and focus on behavioral signals instead.</li><li><strong>Create an AI use policy for interviews - </strong>Align within your team on what type of AI usage is okay with and what is not. This will help you to better educate your team as well as your candidates. </li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Rubric Hacking</strong> - AI models are trained on the exact same documentation and textbooks that hiring managers use to create scoring rubrics. This means the AI generates the textbook-perfect answer that interviewers are conditioned to look for.</li><li><strong>False Confidence</strong> - Having the answer displayed on a screen eliminates the stuttering and nervousness typical of difficult questions. This artificial smoothness boosts their communication scores, helping them pass soft-skill checks.</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Redefine your pass criteria - </strong> Stop grading solely on the final answer; start grading the <em>journey</em>. A perfect solution with zero backspaces or hesitation should be treated as suspicious, not excellent.</li><li><strong>Adopt cheating detection tools - </strong>Do not just trust on interview scores. Adopt solutions that help you prevent and detect cheating for better hiring outcomes.</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Cheating as a fixed strategy</strong> - For the 30% who always cheat, this is a deliberate approach to interviews. They have invested in tools, learned the workflow, and apply it consistently regardless of role or company.</li><li><strong>Cheating as a situational response</strong> - The 23% who sometimes cheat likely respond to context: perceived difficulty of the role, time pressure, or how high-stakes the opportunity feels. They may cheat for dream jobs but interview honestly for backup options.</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Sunday is the worst day</strong> - Weekend interviews often happen from home with fewer distractions or observers. Candidates have more freedom to set up cheating tools, use secondary devices, or speak answers aloud without concern.</li></ul>
<ul><li><strong>Invisible tools dominate</strong> - The top two methods (79% combined) are designed to be undetectable by screen sharing. These tools use invisible overlays or audio-only input, making them impossible to catch through traditional proctoring.</li><li><strong>Old methods are dying out</strong> - Tab switching and second screens are now minority tactics. Candidates have learned these are easily flagged, so they have migrated to purpose-built cheating software.</li><li><strong>Human accomplices are impractical</strong> - Live help from a friend requires coordination and introduces lag. Automated tools are faster, more reliable, and do not require scheduling another person.</li></ul>
