<p>US science funder once again restricts awards to Harvard University and other research institutions.</p>
<figure><picture><source sizes="(max-width: 319px) 319px, (min-width: 1023px) 100vw,  767px" srcset="https://media.nature.com/lw767/magazine-assets/d41586-026-01667-6/d41586-026-01667-6_52477894.jpg?as=webp 767w," type="image/webp"/><img alt="Exterior view of the Lewis Science Library on the campus of Princeton University as a person walks by on the sidewalk." src="https://media.nature.com/lw767/magazine-assets/d41586-026-01667-6/d41586-026-01667-6_52477894.jpg"/><figcaption><p>Princeton University in New Jersey (its science library shown here) is one of four institutions to which the US National Science Foundation began restricting research grants last month.Credit: EQRoy/Alamy</p></figcaption></picture></figure>
<p>The US National Science Foundation (NSF) — <a data-label="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-01749-x" data-track="click" data-track-category="body text link" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-01749-x">a major funder of basic research</a> — has restricted the flow of new research grants to a group of elite universities, <i>Nature</i> has learnt.</p>
<figure><a data-track="click" data-track-label="recommended article" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01361-7"><img alt="" src="https://media.nature.com/w400/magazine-assets/d41586-026-01667-6/d41586-026-01667-6_52338146.jpg"/><p>Entire NSF science advisory board fired by Trump administration</p></a></figure>
<p>Internal agency documents obtained by <i>Nature’s</i> news team reveal that on 9 April, the NSF’s Office of Award Management (OAM), which finalizes grants and handles their finances, put limits on new funding to Duke University in Durham, North Carolina; Harvard University in Cambridge, Massachusetts; Princeton University in New Jersey; and Yale University in New Haven, Connecticut. A note applied to these universities in an NSF database reads: “Future Awards to Organization on Hold.” Since then, little fresh funding has been made available to these institutions by the NSF.</p>
<p>It is unclear why the NSF, which has an annual budget of US$8.8 billion, is limiting new funding to these particular universities or when the restriction will end. The agency declined a request for comment from <i>Nature</i>.</p>
<p>Last year, the administration of US President Donald Trump <a data-label="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-01289-4" data-track="click" data-track-category="body text link" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-01289-4">froze or terminated research funding for several US institutions</a>, alleging violations of federal anti-discrimination policy, such as a failure to protect students against antisemitism. Some institutions <a data-label="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-02403-2" data-track="click" data-track-category="body text link" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-02403-2">struck deals with the administration to restore funds</a>. Harvard, which had about 75% of its research grants terminated by the NSF, sued. <a data-label="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03036-1" data-track="click" data-track-category="body text link" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03036-1">A federal judge ruled last September that the terminations were illegal</a> and permanently banned US agencies, including the NSF, from taking similar action against Harvard in the future.</p>
<p>Legal scholars who spoke to <i>Nature</i> say that the latest restriction on new funding by the NSF could be in violation of that ruling. “The administration has bent itself into pretzels to continue actions against universities it dislikes, even in the face of court orders,” says David Super, a specialist in administrative law at Georgetown University in Washington DC.</p>
<p>The White House denied that the administration is taking action against the four universities.</p>
<h2>Research on hold</h2>
<p>An internal NSF list obtained by <i>Nature</i> shows that the OAM has stalled 33 research proposals by researchers from the four universities or their collaborators. Grant-making at the NSF has been slow this fiscal year owing to <a data-label="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03706-0" data-track="click" data-track-category="body text link" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03706-0">a 43-day government shutdown in late 2025</a> and <a data-label="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00601-0" data-track="click" data-track-category="body text link" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00601-0">the White House delaying the release of the agency’s budget</a>, but inside the OAM, processing has been consistent, with research grants taking 10 days to finalize, on average.</p>
<figure><a data-track="click" data-track-label="recommended article" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03036-1"><img alt="" src="https://media.nature.com/w400/magazine-assets/d41586-026-01667-6/d41586-026-01667-6_51596110.jpg"/></a><figcaption><a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03036-1">Harvard vs Trump: millions in grant money begin trickling back to scientists</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>Proposals from scientists at Duke, Harvard, Princeton, Yale and their collaborators, however, have been held by the office for an average of 91 days. Many were stalled even before the 9 April hold was applied to these universities.</p>
<p>To arrive at the OAM, proposals must be evaluated and found meritorious by a panel of independent scientists, then endorsed by NSF programme officers and approved by agency leadership. Agency staff members who spoke to <i>Nature</i> on condition of anonymity, because they fear reprisal, say that the type of hold being placed on new funds to the four universities is rare and used only in extreme situations, such as when a university closes or fails an audit.</p>
<p>Scientists at Duke, Harvard, Princeton and Yale are frequent recipients of NSF grants; in 2024, these universities received a combined 218 new grants from the agency. At the moment, researchers on these grants are still able to access funds when needed. But new grants have been slowed to a trickle. So far this fiscal year, the four institutions have received 13 new grants combined. And no awards have gone to scientists at Duke or Harvard since the 9 April freeze.</p>
<p>Princeton’s dean for research, Peter Schiffer, said in a statement that the agency “has not informed us of any blanket action involving our pipeline of NSF-funded projects.” The other three universities did not respond to queries by the time this story was published.</p>
