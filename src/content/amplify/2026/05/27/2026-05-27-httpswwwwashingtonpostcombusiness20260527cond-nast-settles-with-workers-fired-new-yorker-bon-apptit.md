<html><body><figure><img alt="" src="https://cloudfront-us-east-1.images.arcpublishing.com/wapo/F7JSUHFVYEI6HOVSXFQCFEYCDU.jpeg"/><figcaption>The New Yorker logo. (Jesse Dittmar/For The Washington Post)</figcaption></figure><p>Magazine publisher Condé Nast settled a labor dispute with three employees who were fired last year after confronting the company’s human resources chief, paying $400,000 to the group and reinstating their jobs, according to a statement from the NewsGuild of New York. </p><p>The three employees, including a New Yorker fact-checker and Bon Appetit<i> </i>writer and producer, resigned rather than return to work, according to the NewsGuild. A fourth employee, Wired senior writer Jake Lahut, declined a lesser settlement offer and is continuing his legal battle. </p><p>In a Wednesday press release, the NewsGuild declared victory, saying the fired staffers — and five others who were suspended after the clash — were “vindicated” by the settlement.</p><p>Susan DeCarava, the NewsGuild president, said the settlement “sends a forceful message: Workers united in solidarity have the power to push back against bully union-busting bosses and demand their workplaces be governed by respect rather than fear.”</p><p>Asked for comment, Condé Nast shared an email sent on Wednesday to U.S. staffers saying the resolution will allow “all parties can move forward constructively,” and that “both parties expressly denied wrongdoing and liability” in the agreement. </p><p>The email disputed the idea that fired staffers were reinstated and then resigned. “These employees accepted terms for severance and will no longer be at the company,” it said. Formerly suspended staffers, it said, are “on notice that similar activities in the future may lead to disciplinary action.”</p><p>The settlement follows a dispute involving more than a dozen staffers, who confronted Stan Duncan, the company’s chief people officer, about cuts that included shuttering Teen Vogue, and folding it into Vogue’s website.</p><p>Video clips of the incident reviewed by The Washington Post at the time showed Duncan telling staff they could not congregate outside his office and must return to work. One employee followed him down the hall asking if he was “running away.”</p><p>By that evening, four employees <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2025/11/21/conde-nast-new-yorker-firings-labor-dispute/" rel="" target="_self" title="https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2025/11/21/conde-nast-new-yorker-firings-labor-dispute/">had been fired</a>: New Yorker fact-checker Jasper Lo, Bon Appétit writer and producer Alma Avalle, Condé Nast Entertainment videographer Ben Dewey, and Jake Lahut of Wired. The company also suspended five other employees.</p><p>At the time, Condé justified its actions saying in a statement that “extreme misconduct is unacceptable in any professional setting.”</p><p>The union challenged the firings and suspensions, filing Unfair Labor Practice charges with the National Labor Relations Board.</p><p>Under the terms of the settlement, three of the fired staffers — Lo, Avalle and Dewey — had their disciplinary records expunged and were awarded a combined sum of more than $400,000. They each resigned.</p><p>The five suspended staffers had their disciplinary records cleared and were awarded back pay for unpaid days during their suspension.</p><p>Lo told The Post that he felt surprised by the settlement terms. </p><p>“How can you so forcefully say that I was in the wrong and then at the same time give such reparative proposals?” Lo said in an interview.</p><p>More than six months since the incident, Lo said he’s still struggling to come to terms with what’s happened. “I wish I’d never experienced this. I wish that nobody had to experience this,” Lo said. “It’s an extraordinary thing to have your whole self worth just thrown out the window.” </p><p>Lahut, who was a probationary employee at the time of his firing, declined a lesser settlement offer of four months of backpay, he told The Post. Lahut and the union are still pursuing his case before the NLRB. </p><p>Avalle said she felt relieved when she signed the settlement agreement on Tuesday night. “I feel like I’ve been made whole,” she said. “I really can’t imagine a more favorable outcome than what we’ve reached.” </p><p>After being fired, Dewey enrolled in classes as part of his goal to become a nurse. He said he plans to use the settlement to help fund his career pivot. </p><p>“I intend to blow it all on rent and groceries while I make this career change,” Dewey said. “Paying me almost two years’ salary to resign is a deal I’m very happy to take. I wish they had given it to me years ago.” </p></body></html>
