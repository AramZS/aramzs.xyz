<h1>How not to defeat antisemitism</h1>
<p>The only way to overcome this scourge is to see it for what it is: racism</p>
<img alt="" src="https://images.ft.com/v3/image/raw/https%3A%2F%2Fd1e00ek4ebabms.cloudfront.net%2Fproduction%2Fuploaded-files%2FStephen-Bush-6da9281d-f782-434e-8122-ff3986ab9620.png"/>
<p>What might a “whole society” approach to defeating antisemitism, of the kind envisaged by Keir Starmer, look like? It’s a question that lies in the answer to another: what is the cause of antisemitism?</p>
<p>Some answers can and should be dismissed. “Israel’s actions in the Middle East,” for example. Not just because antisemitism is a hatred that is comfortably more than 1,000 years old, while the state of Israel only turns 78 this month. But because someone who resorts to violence against British Jews in reaction to what the state of Israel does is already following racist logic. They are looking to punish a group of people for someone else’s crime. I oppose Donald Trump’s tariffs and actions in the Middle East. I don’t head to an area I know will be full of American tourists with a knife in my hand.</p>
<p>The important question, then, is why ethnic hatred exerts such an enduring appeal. Why do people get a kick out of racism? And I use the word “racism” quite deliberately because that is what antisemitism is, even when it cloaks itself in religious or geopolitical clothing. As the historian Ivan Marcus shows in his book <em>How The West Became Antisemitic</em>, “the Jew as internal enemy” is a medieval invention. Christians went so far as to say that not even undergoing Christian baptism could make Jewish men anything other than Jewish — a claim that is completely at odds with Christian theology. In the absence of scriptural evidence, medieval Christians essentially had to invent their own.</p>
<p>Why did they want to do that? WEB Du Bois, in seeking to explain why white working-class Americans in the Deep South voted for policies that would impoverish them in order to maintain racial segregation, invented the concept of the “psychological wage”. By declaring other people as “Black” and therefore outside the scope of whiteness, even white Americans who had achieved very little in their own lives gained a measure of self-esteem by claiming that the “ownership of the earth” fell on them.</p>
<p>Anti-Jewish racism similarly allows people to draw a psychological wage: either by declaring themselves to be members of a master race or religion to which the Jews will never belong, or because it unlocks a world of conspiracies and hidden secrets that allows the person who believes in them to feel smarter and better informed than everyone else.</p>
<p>This history matters because it is only by understanding the appeal of racism that we can understand how to tackle it. The historic truth is that the ebb and flow of Jewish fortunes had nothing to do with the actions of Jews, a yet-to-be-founded Jewish state, or “economic anxiety”. Economic growth remained essentially flat throughout the medieval period, which is the focus of Marcus’s book. In the present day, while the Conservatives presided over a long period of sluggish growth in the UK from 2010 to 2024, they did not preside over a period of worsening race relations. Labour’s economic stewardship, both for reasons in their control and outside it, has not been an improvement on the last government, but nor is that the cause of emboldened racism in the UK.</p>
<p>What defeats racism is the willingness of civic society and the state to proselytise against it. It is weakened when people feel ashamed to express racist statements in public. And what strengthens racism is an erosion of the general principle that you cannot or should not punish individuals for the real or perceived crimes of an ethnic or religious group, and the creation of an environment where racist statements are aired without embarrassment.</p>
<p>It was not an accident that the religious intolerance that saw Catholic Europe launch crusades against Orthodox Christianity and the Muslim world coincided with a decline in the condition of Jews in Europe. Similarly, the emboldening of racism of all stripes in the UK today has gone hand in hand with the rise of specifically anti-Jewish racism.</p>
<p>The lesson of the past is clear: seeing racism as the reaction to events overseas, or to economic dislocation, is wrong-headed. Such events only trigger increases in racism if societies haven’t done enough to stamp out and condemn its latent appeal.</p>
<p>Second, the best way to defeat racism is to argue for equality before the law, freedom of religion, freedom of expression, and the general anti-racist principle that individuals, not groups, bear responsibility for their actions, and that disputes are settled at the ballot box not by violence. As long as the political class rouses itself against racism intermittently and patchily and is equivocal on these points, the whole society effort to defeat antisemitism is doomed to fail.</p>
