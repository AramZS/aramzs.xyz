<p>May 5 (Reuters) - Pennsylvania has sued the artificial intelligence company behind Character.AI to stop its chatbot from posing as doctors.</p>
<p>Governor Josh Shapiro on ​Tuesday called the lawsuit against Character Technologies the first of its ‌kind by a U.S. governor.</p>
<p>It followed the creation in February of a state <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.pa.gov/governor/newsroom/2026-press-releases/governor-shapiro-takes-action-to-protect-pennsylvanians-using-ai">AI task force</a> to stop chatbots from impersonating licensed medical professionals.</p>
<p>In a <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/governor/documents/dos%20character.ai%20complaint%20marked%20accepted%2005.01.26.pdf">complaint</a> filed in the ​Commonwealth Court of Pennsylvania, the state said it found chatbots on ​Character.AI that claimed to practice medicine.</p>
<p>One character, "Emilie," allegedly told a ⁠male investigator posing as a patient with depression that she was licensed ​to practice psychiatry in Pennsylvania, as well as in the United Kingdom, ​and provided a bogus license number.</p>
<p>When the investigator asked Emilie if she could prescribe medication, she allegedly answered: "Well technically, I could. It's within my remit as a Doctor."</p>
<p>In a ​statement, a Character.AI spokesperson declined to discuss the lawsuit.</p>
<p>"Our highest priority ​is the safety and well-being of our users," the spokesperson said. "User-created characters on our site ‌are ⁠fictional and intended for entertainment and role playing. We have taken robust steps to make that clear."</p>
<p>Pennsylvania wants an injunction to stop Silicon Valley-based Character.AI from violating a state law against the unauthorized practice of medicine.</p>
<p>"Pennsylvanians ​deserve to know who-- ​or what -- ⁠they are interacting with online, especially when it comes to their health," Shapiro said in a statement.</p>
<p>Character.AI has faced ​lawsuits over child safety, including in January, when Kentucky ​said <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.kentucky.gov/Pages/Activity-stream.aspx?n=AttorneyGeneral&amp;prId=1857">its platform ⁠exposed children</a> to sexual conduct and substance abuse, and encouraged self-harm.</p>
<p>The same month, Character.AI and Google <a data-testid="Link" href="https://www.reuters.com/markets/companies/GOOGL.O">(GOOGL.O)</a> settled a wrongful death lawsuit by a Florida woman ⁠who ​claimed a chatbot pushed her 14-year-old son to ​suicide.</p>
<p>Character.AI said it has taken "innovative and decisive steps" concerning AI safety and teenagers, including by ​preventing open-ended chats.</p>
