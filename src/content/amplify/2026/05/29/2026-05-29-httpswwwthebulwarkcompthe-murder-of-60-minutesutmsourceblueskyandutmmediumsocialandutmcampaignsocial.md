<h2>1. Corporate</h2>
<p>This week a bunch of people you’ve likely never heard of were pushed out of <em>60 Minutes</em> by Bari Weiss. Simultaneously, Weiss handed control of the program to someone else you’ve probably never heard of.</p>
<p>I know how boring that sounds, but it’s important. What is happening at <em>60 Minutes</em> is a demonstration of how institutions fail in an age of autocracy. And it is an object lesson in the dangers corporate ownership poses to free media.</p>
<p>This is inside baseball. But you need to understand it. Let’s go.</p>
<hr/>
<p><em>60 Minutes</em> is the crown jewel of American broadcast journalism. When journalism migrated from the page to the screen, it was mostly a bad thing for society. But <em>60 Minutes</em> was the best-case scenario—an example of what serious investigative journalism could achieve in the TV format.</p>
<p>Part of the show’s success was economic. <em>60 Minutes</em> didn’t just produce outstanding journalism—it made money. It proved that good journalism could succeed on TV and didn’t need to be regarded as a charity or a loss-leader. In 2024, just as a for-instance, <em>60 Minutes</em> <a href="https://variety.com/2026/tv/news/60-minutes-shake-up-cbs-news-ousts-producer-tanya-simon-1236761176/">made</a> $206 million in advertising.</p>
<p>Because of this industry-leading success, the show has always been allowed to run independently within the organization that is CBS News. <em>60 Minutes</em> was its own beast, a state within a state. The show’s independence was such a big deal that when CBS corporate interfered with it in the 1990s, the whole world took notice and Michael Mann made <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P-_ZdJxgwcU">a big-budget movie about the incident</a>.</p>
<p>That’s the backdrop.</p>
<hr/>
<p>Last fall, David Ellison, the chairman and CEO of CBS’s parent company, Paramount Skydance, purchased Bari Weiss’s pro-Trump website, the Free Press, for $150 million. As part of the deal he put Weiss in charge of CBS News, where she quickly went to work making the division’s products more friendly to Donald Trump. We don’t need to recapitulate the entire history here; it is enough to note that, as a business matter, Weiss’s tenure has been an abject failure. Ratings are down across CBS News properties.</p>
<p>But as a <em>corporate</em> matter, Weiss has been a success. First, as a way of greasing the skids of Ellison’s purchase of Paramount, he publicly signaled that he would give Weiss a prominent role at CBS News long before he bought her. Then, once she was put in charge and started breaking things, she made Ellison’s purchase of Warner Bros. Discovery all the more politically appealing to Trump. Trump <a href="https://www.mediaite.com/media/trump-praises-great-person-bari-weiss-taking-over-cbs-greatest-thing-to-happen-to-free-and-open-press/">publicly praised</a> Weiss, and when Ellison and Netflix got into a bidding war for Warner-Discovery, the president stepped in to <a href="https://www.politico.com/news/2026/02/27/horrifying-hollywood-blasts-trumps-role-in-studio-sale-00802771">thumb the scales</a> and make sure everyone knew that he would approve a sale to Ellison but not to Netflix.</p>
<p>So under Weiss, CBS News has been a failure, both in terms of product and business. The journalism at CBS News is getting worse and the audience is leaving. But the particular manner in which CBS News has failed—by broadcasting its obeisance to Trump—has enabled tremendous success by the division’s corporate parent.</p>
<p>This is what happens in a command economy when the head of the government picks winners and losers. CBS News no longer exists as a unit whose purpose is to create journalism that attracts an audience and drives revenue. Its purpose is to keep the president happy so that Ellison’s other businesses prosper.</p>
<p>It’s not quite right to say that CBS News under Weiss is a charity. It’s more like an ongoing bribe.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>2. 60</h2>
<p>Weiss took control of CBS News partway through the show’s season and almost immediately she broke the show’s independence. Despite knowing nothing about either investigative reporting or broadcast journalism, she inserted herself into <em>60 Minutes</em>, first in trivial ways, and then in big ones: She spiked a story about CECOT and she approached Benjamin Netanyahu, who had been in talks to sit for an interview with Leslie Stahl, and allowed him to choose his own interviewer.</p>
<p>It was clear that Weiss intended to put <em>60 Minutes</em> completely under her personal control so that the show could no longer upset President Trump. After the current season wrapped last week, Weiss went to work with all of those firings we talked about at the very top.</p>
<p>You might recognize the names of correspondents Sharyn Alfonsi and Cecilia Vega. You probably don’t know who Tanya Simon and Draggan Mihailovich are because they were among behind-the-camera producers and editors who run the show. As of this week, Weiss has gotten rid of all of them.</p>
<p>Simon was the executive producer in charge of <em>60</em>. Weiss replaced her with Nick Bilton, a guy who has written for the <em>New York Times</em> and <em>Vanity Fair</em> and never done a blessed thing in broadcast news—let alone managed a giant organization.<a data-component-name="FootnoteAnchorToDOM" href="#footnote-1" id="footnote-anchor-1"><sup> [1] </sup></a></p>
<p>Ben Shapiro’s Daily Wire ran a piece heartily <a href="https://www.dailywire.com/news/bari-weiss-is-remaking-cbs-news-and-the-left-is-panicking">endorsing</a> Bilton’s hire.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>3. Indie Rock</h2>
<p>Corporate ownership of media companies is a smaller <em><strong>issue </strong></em>in normal times because the corporate owners don’t have to worry about the government seeking retribution.</p>
<p>In 2004, <em>60 Minutes</em> ran a story about George W. Bush that <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killian_documents_controversy">relied on forged documents</a>. It was a major scandal—but no one at CBS’s corporate parent worried that Bush’s FCC was going to pull their broadcast license or that Bush’s FTC would block the purchase of another corporate property in retaliation.<a data-component-name="FootnoteAnchorToDOM" href="#footnote-2" id="footnote-anchor-2"><sup> [2] </sup></a></p>
<p>We do not live in normal times. The clearest example of this I can give you is that Trump unilaterally imposed massive tariffs. The Supreme Court ruled that these tariffs were unconstitutional and that the companies who paid them should be refunded their money. Trump then went on TV to <a href="https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/21/trump-says-hell-remember-companies-that-dont-seek-tariff-refund.html">proclaim</a> that companies should let him keep the money, promising that he’d look favorably on those that don’t ask for it back—“I’ll remember them”—and implicitly warning he’d keep track of which businesses do ask for it back.</p>
<p>Command economy. Gangster government. Socialism. Whatever you want to call it, this is an authoritarian age. And in an authoritarian age, the corporate ownership of media is dangerous because corporations are financially incentivized to turn their media outlets into fleshlights for the Dear Leader.</p>
<hr/>
<p>We should be clear: Bari Weiss is murdering <em>60 Minutes</em>. She is doing so not out of incompetence or foolishness, but with malice aforethought. She is doing it at the behest of her corporate patron, because while she may cause CBS News to lose millions of dollars, her stroking of Trump will gain David Ellison <em>billions </em>of dollars.</p>
<p>That’s what is happening. And anyone who pretends that this is about “free speech,” or adapting to an age of “new media,” or confronting “dangerous ideas,” or reclaiming some “forgotten audience” is selling you something.</p>
<hr/>
<p>The only antidote is building media institutions independent of corporate ties. Which is what we’ve done here at <em>The Bulwark</em>.</p>
<p>There are downsides to being independent. You don’t have enormous corporate resources to tap. We can’t build out giant production teams; we can’t put reporters on the ground in the Middle East. We can’t do incredibly slick infographics. We can only grow as fast as our revenue grows—which means that everything we do has to be sustainable. Which is a fancy word for “slow.”</p>
<p>In truth, there’s only one upside to being independent:</p>
<p><em><strong>We’re</strong></em><strong> in control and our incentives are aligned with yours.</strong></p>
<p>No one tells us what to say, or whom to fire. We are not a loss leader for some other division. We exist to tell you the truth and create value for <em>you,</em> the people who read and watch us.</p>
<p>I don’t know Nick Bilton. Never met the guy. But I cannot imagine what sort of journalist would look at Bari Weiss and everything that has gone down at CBS News over the last few months and think, <em>Yeah, baby! I want to be part of that!</em></p>
<p>Maybe he’s attracted to the resources; to the ability to do all those things we can’t do. Or maybe it’s something else. I dunno.</p>
<p>But for me? Eight years ago I decided that as God is my witness, I was never going to be at the mercy of some corporate d-bag. Not ever again. I would live and die with you guys.</p>
<hr/>
<p><em>The Bulwark</em> isn’t <em>60 Minutes</em>. We’re never going to be <em>60 Minutes</em>. And that’s a shame, because <em>60 Minutes</em>—the real show, not the Frankenstein Bari and Bilton will ship under the legacy brand—ought to exist in the world. It’s good. It has value.</p>
<p>I hope someone out there builds a new <em>60 Minutes</em>, soon. (<a href="https://www.emersoncollective.com/">Hi Laurene!</a> 👋)</p>
<p>But what we’re doing here has its own value. It deserves to be nurtured and supported by the people who consume it—not just through membership, but through the discussions we have here together and evangelization and honesty and camaraderie.</p>

<p>Those mf’ers might be rich and powerful. But what they’re doing is bankrupt. Everyone can see it.</p>
