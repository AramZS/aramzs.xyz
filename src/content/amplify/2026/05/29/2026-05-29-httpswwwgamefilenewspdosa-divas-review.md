<p>“There was a Sri Lankan place, not many in Seattle, that opened up during the pandemic,” the video game developer Chandana Ekanayake recently recalled.</p>
<p>“Food was excellent. I got to support this place as much as possible. They lasted a year and had to shut down, which was unfortunate.”</p>
<p>“There’s a Vietnamese place we would overtip,” he remembered. Ekanayake just wanted it to remain open.</p>
<p>“I love this place. It's close to the house. And I would make sure we're spending more than we probably should. But I want to make sure that they are also staying around, and luckily, they are.”</p>
<p>Hardships have shaped Ekanayake’s work as the co-founder and creative director of <a href="https://outerloopgames.com/">Outerloop Games</a>: hardships in small businesses, in this economy, in capitalism, in the lives of people whose cultures are being mixed into the rest of North America.</p>
<p>The studio works to create games that empathize, sympathize, resonate, and self-reflect with the awareness of everything going on in the world, while still figuring out a sense of good, a sense of justice to aid marginalized folks who have been going through extremely tough times.</p>
<p>Outerloop’s latest is <a href="https://outerloopgames.com/dosa">Dosa Divas</a>, a role-playing game released last month for consoles and PC. It’s an interactive tale of sisters Samara and Amani and their food truck/mech Goddess, as they fight corporate lawyers and cops in turn-based combat. They gather ingredients from their surroundings to whip up dishes, while also trying to right previous wrongs with old friends, coworkers, and family members, after a decade away.</p>
<p>It’s a game about all those hardships and what people can do about them.</p>
<p>It hit me hard.</p>
