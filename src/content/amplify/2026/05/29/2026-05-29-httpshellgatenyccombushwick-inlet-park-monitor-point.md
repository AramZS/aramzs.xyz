<p>A rapidly-intensifying battle over a swanky <a href="https://www.monitorpointonquay.com/project-gallery?ref=hellgatenyc.com"><u>new high-rise development</u></a> proposed for a piece of public land on the Greenpoint waterfront could end up being the first test of a new housing appeals board next month, if the developer can't convince the City Council that it's offering New Yorkers a good deal.</p>
<p>The 600-foot-tall Monitor Point development has <a href="https://www.monitorpointonquay.com/about?ref=hellgatenyc.com"><u>been in the works since 2021</u></a>, when Gotham Organization won a contract from the MTA to <a href="https://www.mta.info/press-release/mta-unveils-monitor-point-proposal-redevelop-40-quay-street-mixed-income-housing?ref=hellgatenyc.com"><u>relocate its subway deep-cleaning HQ </u></a>at 40 Quay Street to another location—in exchange for a lease on the idyllic waterfront site to build luxury towers. (It must also <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/27/nyregion/monitor-point-ship-real-estate.html?ref=hellgatenyc.com"><u>make a permanent home</u></a> for the Greenpoint Monitor Museum).</p>
<p>Now, the project is barreling toward City approval, with the City Council set to vote on Gotham Organization's <a href="https://legistar.council.nyc.gov/MeetingDetail.aspx?ID=1404689&amp;GUID=4465A321-7F6D-4536-8142-A59709AE9454&amp;Options=info%7C&amp;Search=&amp;ref=hellgatenyc.com"><u>zoning modification requests</u></a> for the project on June 25.</p>
<p>But Monitor Point is facing virulent pushback from the Greenpoint community as it speeds toward the vote, with some locals arguing that the <a href="https://gothamist.com/news/a-600-foot-waterfront-apartment-complex-is-brooklyns-latest-housing-fight?ref=hellgatenyc.com"><u>developer's promise</u></a> to make 40 percent of its housing units affordable isn't good enough for a project that sits on public land. Under the current plan, 460 of 1,150 units will be priced for renters earning 40 to 100 percent of <a href="https://www.nyc.gov/site/hpd/services-and-information/area-median-income.page?ref=hellgatenyc.com"><u>the area median income</u></a>, meaning a one-bedroom would rent for about $1,200 to $3,050 per month. The rest would be market rate, ranging from $4,000 per month for a studio to $9,500 for a three-bedroom.</p>
