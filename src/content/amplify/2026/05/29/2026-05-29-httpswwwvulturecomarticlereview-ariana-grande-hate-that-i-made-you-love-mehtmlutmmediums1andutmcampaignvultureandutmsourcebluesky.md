<p>Breakup rumors have circled <a href="https://www.vulture.com/tags/ariana-grande/">Ariana Grande</a> and her partner, Ethan Slater, since last year’s <em>Wicked: For Good</em> press run, when TikTok and blog theories toyed with the idea that Grande’s commitment to work was pushing her lover away (despite his role as her Glinda’s Tinman). The couple individually responded via public acts of encouragement: Slater attended her December <em>Saturday Night Live</em> hosting gig; in a March Instagram Story, she <a href="https://people.com/ariana-grande-is-very-proud-of-ethan-slater-as-off-broadway-show-closes-11932066">congratulated</a> him on <em>Marcel in the Night</em>, an Off Broadway play he co-wrote and starred in. Whether or not there was ever actually a schism, the rumor mill was confident and uninformed. Grande now appears to address it all in “<a href="https://www.vulture.com/article/ariana-grande-hate-that-i-made-you-love-me-fans.html">Hate That I Made You Love Me</a>,” the glum sigh of a lead single from her forthcoming eighth album, <em>Petal</em>, and first new music as herself since <a href="https://www.vulture.com/article/ariana-grande-eternal-sunshine.html"><em>Eternal Sunshine</em></a>’s parade of deluxe add-ons ended in early 2025. She returns with a shrug for unnamed nuisances, and that attitude unfortunately extends to the beat and vocal delivery. This feels like a snooze compared to most of the songs that have launched her album campaigns, making it one of her worst lead singles yet (though not as bad as “Problem”). But it’s also a (too) time-tested hit formula.</p>
<p>The song can either scan as a snapshot of an eventful couple’s spat, a villain anthem like “Thank U Next,” or a chop at parasocial observers who get bored and invent celebrity drama.</p>
