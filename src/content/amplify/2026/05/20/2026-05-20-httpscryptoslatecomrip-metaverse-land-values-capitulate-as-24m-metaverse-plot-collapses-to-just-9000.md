<h2>Metaverse land never recovered. The numbers now show how far it fell</h2>
<p>The biggest metaverse land deals of the 2021 and 2022 boom now map to four- and five-digit values when priced against current collection floors, rather than the six- and seven-figure valuations buyers once paid.</p>
<p>The decline runs through the entire metaverse land trade. A CoinGecko <a href="https://www.coingecko.com/research/publications/metaverse-land-prices">study</a> found that average metaverse land prices were already down 72% from their highs by June 2024, with Sandbox off 95%, Decentraland off 89%, and Otherdeed for Otherside off 85% from peak-cycle average floor levels.</p>
<p>The famous parcels that once stood in for scarcity and status now read like artifacts from a pricing regime that assumed virtual neighborhoods would become high-traffic digital cities.</p>
<p>The broader NFT market also failed to recover its old price structure. DappRadar said NFT trading reached $25.8 billion in 2021, and its January 2022 <a href="https://dappradar.com/blog/opensea-nft-trading-volume-hits-4-5b-in-january">report</a> said that month alone hit a record $16 billion in sales before wash-trading distortions were stripped out. Later data shows a market that kept moving while getting cheaper.</p>
<p>DappRadar’s Q2 2025 <a href="https://dappradar.com/blog/state-of-the-dapp-industry-q2-2025">report</a> said NFT trading volume fell 45% quarter over quarter to $867 million even as sales rose 78% to 14.9 million.</p>
<p>In Q3 2025, the same tracker said the market logged $1.6 billion in trading volume across 18.1 million sales. Trading activity persisted, while the premium attached to many collections collapsed.</p>
<p>The metaverse land unwind is best understood as a repricing because buyers treated digital land as if it would become a durable asset, with brands, traffic, and resale scarcity. The market now prices much of it as illiquid optionality.</p>
<h2>The splashy land deals now look like relics</h2>
<p>The clearest case studies are the deals that once stood in for the entire boom. In December 2021, a 3×3 Snoopverse estate next to Snoop Dogg’s property in The Sandbox sold for about $450,000, or about 71,000 SAND. That nine-parcel estate now screens at about $1,025 on a floor-equivalent basis. That is a drawdown of about 99.8% from the reported sale price.</p>
<p>The Decentraland Fashion District deal points the same way. Metaverse Group bought a 116-parcel estate in November 2021 for about $2.4 million. That estate is now not worth materially more than $8,929 on a floor-equivalent basis, down about 99.6% from the original purchase price.</p>
<p>In June 2021, Republic Realm bought 259 parcels for about $913,228. At the same current floor-equivalent value, that estate screens at about $19,935, down about 97.8%.</p>
<p>The Sandbox “city” deal is another clean marker because of its scale. Republic Realm’s 24×24 Sandbox estate, or 576 parcels, was purchased for $4.3 million in late 2021. Marked to the current floor-equivalent price, that estate screens at about $65,583, down about 98.5%.</p>
<p>Otherside’s trophy sales show the same baseline collapse. A May 2022 DappRadar <a href="https://dappradar.com/blog/otherdeeds-land-parcel-sold-for-almost-1-million">report</a> said Otherdeed #24 sold for 333 ETH, or close to $1 million, while the floor now sits around $167.</p>
<p>Even so, against the current Otherdeed floor, the category baseline has fallen so far that these headline purchases now imply floor-equivalent markdowns approaching 100%.</p>

<p>Floor-equivalent pricing is the fairest way to present these comparisons. It shows what happened to the market’s baseline. The market that once paid a premium for celebrity adjacency, branded districts, and virtual location now assigns only a thin residual value to the category as a whole.</p>
<h2>NFTs kept trading, but the pricing model broke</h2>
<p>The land collapse sits inside a broader NFT reset. The first quarter of 2022 was the strongest in NFT history at $12.46 billion in trading volume. By June 2022, monthly trading had fallen below $1 billion for the first time in a year. However, the bust did not totally erase the market.</p>
<p>DappRadar’s 2024 overview <a href="https://dappradar.com/blog/dapp-industry-report-2024-overview">report</a> said NFT trading volume fell 19% year over year in 2024 and sales fell 18%, making 2024 one of the weakest years since 2020. Then 2025 showed a split market, lower dollar volume, higher unit activity, and more trading in cheaper assets.</p>
<p>That split is visible in the quarterly numbers. In Q2 2025, DappRadar said volume fell to $867 million while sales rose to 14.9 million. In Q3 2025, DappRadar’s <a href="https://dappradar.com/blog/state-of-the-dapp-industry-q3-2025">tracker</a> said the market posted $1.6 billion in volume and 18.1 million sales.</p>
<p>October 2025 added another signal. DappRadar said the market reached $546 million in monthly volume and 10.1 million sales, the highest monthly sales count of the year. Traders were still buying NFTs. They were spending far less per item.</p>
<p>A blue-chip proxy shows how severe the repricing was outside land. CoinGecko’s BAYC <a href="https://www.coingecko.com/en/nft/bored-ape-yacht-club">page</a> shows Bored Ape Yacht Club at about 5.22 ETH, or about $11,410, versus an all-time high floor of 153.7 ETH, or about $420,430. That leaves BAYC down about 96.6% in ETH terms and 97.3% in dollar terms. Even one of the category’s most recognizable collections never came close to reclaiming its old clearing level.</p>
<p>The financing layer also broke. DappRadar’s NFT lending <a href="https://dappradar.com/blog/gondi-leads-as-nft-lending-is-down-94">data</a> said lending volume fell 97% from its January 2024 peak of nearly $1 billion to just over $50 million in May 2025. Borrowers were down 90%, lenders were down 78%, and average loan sizes shrank from $22,000 at the 2022 peak to about $4,000.</p>
<p>NFT lending helped support high-end prices during the boom. Once traders could no longer borrow against expensive JPEGs and land deeds at scale, premium valuations lost another key support.</p>
<p>The broad NFT market kept operating, though its price ladder dropped sharply. Land was one of the boom’s purest narrative trades. It depended on the belief that digital location itself would become a durable asset class.</p>
<p>Other parts of the NFT market found cheaper pockets of demand. Land rarely did.</p>
<h2>The market outlook is narrower, cheaper, and less forgiving</h2>
<p>The current market does show signs of life. CoinGecko collection pages for <a href="https://www.coingecko.com/en/nft/sandbox">Sandbox</a>, <a href="https://www.coingecko.com/en/nft/decentraland">Decentraland</a>, <a href="https://www.coingecko.com/en/nft/otherdeed-for-otherside">Otherside</a>, and <a href="https://www.coingecko.com/en/nft/voxels-formerly-cryptovoxels">Voxels</a> show 60-day gains of 153.9%, 95.5%, 12.8%, and 41.8%, respectively.</p>
<p>Yet, those rebounds start from deeply depressed levels and leave the larger picture unchanged. The case studies still sit 98% to nearly 100% below their boom-era valuations on a floor-equivalent basis. That is what happens when a market loses both leverage and belief.</p>
<p>The category is also competing in a different NFT market than the one that existed in late 2021. In 2025, RWA NFTs <a href="https://dappradar.com/blog/state-of-the-dapp-industry-q2-2025">grew</a> 29% in volume and became the second-largest NFT category by volume during the quarter. Gaming-linked assets also gained ground.</p>
<p>Still, that shift does not prove metaverse land can recover soon. Traders moved on to RWAs when the old premise stopped working. They moved toward categories that looked more transactional, more utility-linked, or simply cheaper to own.</p>
<p>Some of that speculative energy has now rotated into <a href="https://cryptoslate.com/prediction-markets/">prediction markets</a>, where event-driven contracts are pulling more attention.</p>
<p>Corporate signals moved in the same direction. Meta changed its name in 2021 to emphasize the metaverse, and the company’s <a href="https://about.fb.com/news/2021/10/facebook-company-is-now-meta/">announcement</a> now reads like a document from another market cycle.</p>
<p>Meta’s 2025 earnings <a href="https://investor.atmeta.com/investor-news/press-release-details/2026/Meta-Reports-Fourth-Quarter-and-Full-Year-2025-Results/default.aspx">filing</a> said Reality Labs lost $19.2 billion in 2025 after years of multibillion-dollar losses. Virtual worlds remain active, though under a very different cost and growth calculus than the one that drove the land boom.</p>
<p>The market now trades digital assets with much lower ticket sizes, weaker financing, and a preference for narrower use cases. Metaverse land can still rally in short bursts, especially when crypto sentiment turns risk-on.</p>
<p>The last 60 days show that. The market still sits far below the assumptions embedded in the 2021 and 2022 trophy sales.</p>
<p>For land values to behave like property again, platforms would need more than token rebounds. Users who show up regularly, brands that stay, and a reason for virtual location to generate durable economic value instead of narrative premium are the only avenues to recovery.</p>
