<p>George Orwell was not really going for subtlety when he wrote the novel “Animal Farm.” (The Times reviewer in 1946 <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/1946/08/26/archives/books-of-the-times-the-pigs-lead-an-animal-revolt.html">called it</a> “neat, moderately clever and so simple and explicit” that it “could be read in grade schools with salutary results for democratic education.”) I mean no knock on the book: It’s just that, this being Orwell, he knew the power of words to mislead and obfuscate meaning, and aimed to communicate clearly. His allegory about animals overthrowing a terrible farmer and then, over time, becoming just as corrupt and despotic as the farmer himself was obviously about the Russian Revolution and Soviet totalitarianism, even if the characters were talking pigs and horses. Readers could see that from the start.</p>
<p>Orwell, however, was also a committed democratic socialist. He wrote into the book a character named Snowball — a pig who believed in building a society based on hard work, radical equality and the sharing of all resources. It was Snowball who came up with the seven commandments of Animalism that were to govern life on the farm, such as “No animals shall wear clothes” (because clothes represent vanity) and “No animal shall kill any other animal.” The most foundational principle was “All animals are equal.” Orwell would say that Snowball was more or less an analogue to the Russian revolutionary Leon Trotsky, and served as a foil for the boar Napoleon, who became the totalitarian dictator of the farm — the Stalin of Orwell’s book. And thus Snowball was run off the farm and into exile.</p>

<p>All of this does happen in the new animated adaptation of “Animal Farm,” directed by Andy Serkis from a screenplay by Nicholas Stoller, though one of the commandments — “No animal shall drink alcohol” — is transposed to an injunction against “naughty juice,” I guess because this is a children’s movie now. What else goes in a children’s movie? Well, if you use this one as a guide, here are some of the answers: original rap music, pigs wearing VR headsets, farm animals getting tipsy (on naughty juice) by the pool and juvenile body humor. (“We’re free; for example, I’m about to fart right now,” says Napoleon, and then he does, at length.)</p>
<p>For much of the movie, the outlines of Orwell’s novel are discernible, if updated to roughly the present day. A neglectful, alcoholic farmer is overthrown by his livestock, who take over his farm and set up a just society. Snowball (gender-swapped here to a female pig and voiced by Laverne Cox) at first guides the group, helping them set up the rules and suggesting they store half the grain they grow so they have something to eat in the winter. Younger pigs, like Lucky (Gaten Matarazzo), are energized by all the talk of freedom and self-determination on the farm, and other animals like the faithful horse Boxer (Woody Harrelson) are eager to work hard and contribute. But soon, Napoleon (Seth Rogen) gains the loyalty of the farm animals and deposes Snowball, and things begin to change around Animal Farm. Especially when Napoleon becomes interested in striking a deal with the local villain-mogul, Freida Pilkington (Glenn Close).</p>
<p>There’s a reasonably OK movie somewhere inside “Animal Farm,” but it’s drowning in ideological confusion, which wouldn’t be such a big deal — one rarely asks children’s cartoons featuring talking pigs to be wellsprings of thoughtful political theorizing — except that this is “Animal Farm.” Frankly, I have no idea what this movie thinks its point is. On the one hand, it makes feints toward an anti-consumerist or even anticapitalist stance: Pilkington is clearly the villain of the film, a corrupting influence on Napoleon, and she drives, hilariously, a car that looks suspiciously like a Tesla Cybertruck. Napoleon uses a few phrases, like “many animals have been saying,” that ring a very particular syntactic bell in 2026. The animals become morally worse the more they covet and consume stuff. Conditions on the farm deteriorate the more they try to expand the operation. This doesn’t <em>feel</em> like a movie that thinks employment and investment and profits and layoffs are good for society.</p>
<p>On the other hand, the film’s credits show a series of cutesy illustrations seemingly designed to represent moments of — revolution? upheaval? — from history. Depicted are the following: enslaved people (well, pigs) building the pyramids; the Trojan War, complete with Trojan Pig; the murder of Julius Caesar; Vikings; the French Revolution, with guillotine; and World War I bombers. The final image shows youthful protesters holding signs that say “Take Back Power!” and “No War,” as well as pitchforks, while tugging on a rope to topple a statue. A remix of the Propellerheads song “History Repeating” plays: “Just little bits of history repeating / And I’ve seen it before / And I’ll see it again ….” Are these little doodles of history meant to be a statement of equivalence? Of goodness? Badness? Futility? Pretty hard to say.</p>
<p>And then there’s this: The film’s distributor, Angel Studios, is selling among its movie merchandise <a href="https://shop.angel.com/products/animal-farm-maffa-trucker-cap">a baseball cap</a> in a now-familiar red hue that has emblazoned upon its crown, in white lettering, the words MAKE ANIMAL FARM FICTION AGAIN.</p>
<p>So I don’t really know what’s going on here.</p>
<p>Adding to the confusion, the movie’s ending has been entirely changed from the novel. This is something of a tradition for screen adaptations of “Animal Farm.” Orwell’s original novel ended in a dystopia, a farm on which the animals’ revolution had been all but forgotten. The capitalist humans and Communist pigs feasted together and had become indistinguishable from one another. In 1955, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2000/03/18/books/how-the-cia-played-dirty-tricks-with-culture.html">a film version was released</a>, secretly produced by the C.I.A. Perhaps rightly guessing that audiences, seeing Orwell’s original ending, would interpret it as a critique of both capitalism and Communism, they removed the humans, leaving only the Communists to be the bad guys.</p>
<p>Here we get quite a different kind of ending, but one that feels even less coherent. I will let you discover it for yourself, but I can tell you this: While we are given that moment in which human and animal become indistinguishable, the movie keeps going, and the whole third act goes off the rails in an attempt to avoid dystopia altogether. Maybe children are presumed too delicate to handle that sort of ending. Instead, the animals chide one another for looking for freedom in the wrong place and in the wrong leaders, like Napoleon and even Snowball.</p>
<p>Then they resort to the blandest and least-earned conclusions imaginable. “You know what’s always right? Helping each other,” the animals agree. “Not because we have to, but because we choose to. That’s what freedom is.”</p>
<p>I suppose that Orwell, the democratic socialist, might have agreed.</p>
