<body><figure><img alt="Commuters pass along King Charles Street outside the Foreign Office." src="https://assets.bwbx.io/images/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/iTLaNwpAZW5U/v1/-1x-1.webp"/><figcaption><p>Commuters pass along King Charles Street outside the Foreign Office.</p> / Photographer: Chris Ratcliffe/Bloomberg</figcaption></figure><div><p>In the midst of two major wars, the UK’s Foreign Office is undertaking job cuts so severe that it’s about to halve the size of its team dealing with the fallout from the conflict with Iran.</p><p>The office established a crisis bunker at its London headquarters at the start of the conflict, with officials working round-the-clock shifts in a windowless room to support and evacuate British nationals in the region. Now, that group is being swept up in the downsizing drive overseen by Foreign Secretary <a href="">Yvette Cooper</a>. </p><p>The Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office contingent works alongside teams from the Home Office, the Department for Transport and the Ministry for Housing, Communities and Local Government, which aren’t subject to the same cuts as the diplomatic corps. Almost three months on, efforts by the UK and its allies to resolve the situation in Iran have made little progress, with ship traffic still halted through the Strait of Hormuz. </p><p>The Iran team isn’t the only high-priority Foreign Office group downsizing at a critical time. Every official below the most senior has been made to re-apply for their own jobs and will find out by next week whether their role is being kept, according to people familiar with the matter, who requested anonymity discussing internal business. Around half the Iran crisis team is expected to take up the redundancy offers, they said.  </p><p>The Foreign Office confirmed the job cuts in a statement to Bloomberg. </p><p>“As part of our workforce reforms, we are offering colleagues who wish to move on the opportunity to do so through a discretionary exit scheme,” the department said in a statement. “The scheme is open to all staff, and applications are being assessed to ensure we retain the expertise needed to maintain a more resilient, agile and modern organization.”</p><p>While staffers in cut positions can apply for another posting, unsuccessful applicants will be made jobless by summer. Bloomberg first reported how the Foreign Office’s ambition to reduce its headcount by up to 25% was denting morale at the department responsible for handling global crises. The foreign policy wing of the British state currently employs around 17,000 staff in 281 offices worldwide, according to the government website.</p><p>Read More: <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-09/uk-budget-cuts-spark-hunger-games-culture-among-diplomats">UK Budget Cuts Spark ‘Hunger Games’ Culture Among Diplomats</a></p><p>Ambassadors returning from senior positions abroad have been arriving back in London to learn they haven’t got a new posting, with diplomats comparing the competition over a dwindling number of roles to <span><em>The Hunger Games</em></span> — the dystopian franchise in which teenagers compete in a battle to the death. </p><p>Senior officials told Bloomberg that job cuts and a promotion freeze have prompted what one called a confidence crisis. In recent weeks that’s been compounded by the high-profile way in which former top Foreign Office civil servant <a href="">Olly Robbins</a> was forced out over the decision to appoint <a href="">Peter Mandelson</a> as US ambassador early in Prime Minister <a href="">Keir Starmer</a>’s tenure. </p><p>On Tuesday, the Foreign Office confirmed that the UK’s No. 2 diplomat in Washington, James Roscoe, had abruptly left the post, without explaining why. Roscoe had filled in for Mandelson for a time after his removal last year. </p><p>One official told Bloomberg last month that the cuts amounted to salami-slicing, while another spoke of a condescending and demoralizing process in which diplomats with decades-long experience were having to point to sections of a so-called capability wheel to make arguments to stay in post.</p><p>Read More: <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-22/uk-s-revered-and-once-discreet-civil-service-turns-on-starmer">UK’s Revered, Once Discreet Civil Service Turns on Starmer</a></p><p>The UK’s global influence has already been significantly dented in the aftermath of the Brexit divorce with its biggest trading partner. Under President <a href="">Donald Trump</a> it’s struggled to maintain the integrity of the transatlantic alliance, despite the close security and intelligence ties shared by both countries.</p></div><div><ol></ol></div></body>
