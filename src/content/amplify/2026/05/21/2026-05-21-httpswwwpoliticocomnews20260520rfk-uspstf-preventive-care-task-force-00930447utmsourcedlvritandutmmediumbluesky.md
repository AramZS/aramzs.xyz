<p>Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. has fired the leaders of the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force, an influential group of external experts who decide which medical services are preventive and must be covered fully by insurers under the Affordable Care Act.</p>
<p>The move has raised concerns among public health advocates and patient groups that Kennedy wants the task force to endorse his personal views about what’s preventive. In some cases, Kennedy’s views aren’t in line with mainstream public health assessments.</p>
<p>Kennedy told lawmakers last month that the task force has been <a data-tracking="mpos=center&amp;mid=ar_body&amp;lindex=60&amp;lcol=" href="https://www.politico.com/live-updates/2026/04/16/congress/rfk-preventive-task-force-obamacare-insurance-00876202"><u>“lackadaisical and negligent</u></a> for 20 years,” and that he was planning to bring in new members “who have a clear mission.”</p>
<p>At an April hearing of the House Energy and Commerce Health Subcommittee, he <u>cited the task force’s failure</u> to recommend early screening for Alzheimer’s disease as an example of a decision he disagreed with. “If it had been doing its job, we would have early screening,” he told Ohio Republican Troy Balderson.</p>
<p>Some advocates for people with diseases <a data-tracking="mpos=center&amp;mid=ar_body&amp;lindex=61&amp;lcol=" href="https://www.politico.com/newsletters/future-pulse/2025/08/08/kidney-patients-see-opportunity-in-rfk-jr-s-upheaval-00499060"><u>are already lobbying</u></a> to expand the range of services the task force says should be fully covered and are hoping Kennedy will help. For example, the American Association of Kidney Patients, whose corporate members include drugmakers AstraZeneca, Novartis and Sanofi, has warred with the task force for years over whether to expand screening for kidney disease.</p>
<p>But some patient groups and lawmakers have pushed back against Kennedy’s critique, including Senate Majority Whip John Barrasso (R-Wyo.), who in a different hearing with Kennedy last month <u>praised the task force’s work</u> on prevention of breast and cervical cancers, among other health issues.</p>
<p>The firing of John Wong, who chaired the group, and Esa Davis, the deputy chair, comes days before a <u>May 23 deadline for nominations</u> to be submitted for new task force members. The fired task force leaders would have typically been involved in selecting new leaders of the group, which is made up of 16 experts in primary care.</p>
<p>HHS has canceled most of the task force meetings scheduled after President Donald Trump took office, preventing the group from making any draft recommendations for preventive services. To Barrasso, Kennedy acknowledged he’d been slow to get it up and running and said <u>he was</u> seeking to ensure task force members have a broader range of medical expertise. “We just want to get the best people and have a more representative group of specialties,” he said.</p>
<p>In letters dated May 11, which POLITICO obtained, Kennedy told Wong and Davis that their appointments to the task force had been terminated, effective immediately.</p>
<p>That decision followed a review of current appointments to the task force that is part of the Department of Health and Human Services’ “implementation of updated governance and oversight procedures” for the task force and for the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality, according to the letters.</p>
<p>AHRQ is part of HHS and supports the task force.</p>
<p>The decision to terminate the two USPSTF leaders was made to “avoid uncertainty that could jeopardize the validity of future Task Force actions,” the letters state.</p>
<p>They note that it was an administrative decision and didn’t reflect the performance of the two leaders.</p>
<p>“To the contrary, the Department is taking this step to help protect the Task Force and preserve confidence in the continuity and durability of its work,” the letters continue.</p>
<p>Wong and Davis were invited to reapply to be task force members. They did not immediately respond to requests for comment.</p>
<p>HHS did not respond to questions about why the two were fired. Wong’s term was due to expire in March 2027, while Davis’s term was set to come to an end in March 2028.</p>
<p>Roger Klein, the AHRQ director, told Wong and Davis in an email obtained by POLITICO that Kennedy’s decision to remove them is in line with the secretary’s authority to appoint members for the group and to supervise the task force, confirmed by <u>a Supreme Court</u> decision last year.</p>
<p>Klein wrote that Kennedy had identified “institutional questions” related to the circumstances of the two leaders’ appointments. Klein didn’t specify what the circumstances were.</p>
<p>Wong and Davis were appointed as task force leaders during the Biden administration.</p>
<p>“What I can say is that the Department’s goal throughout has been to protect the integrity and continuity of the Task Force’s mission,” Klein wrote.</p>
<p>Klein didn’t respond to a request for comment.</p>
<p>Kennedy delegated to Klein the authority “to administer and implement the Department’s supervisory framework governing support for, and oversight of, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force,” according to <a data-tracking="mpos=center&amp;mid=ar_body&amp;lindex=64&amp;lcol=" href="https://public-inspection.federalregister.gov/2026-09841.pdf?utm_campaign=pi+subscription+mailing+list&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_source=federalregister.gov"><u>a Federal Register notice</u></a> dated May 13.</p>
<p>The notice did say that the secretary retains the authority to appoint or remove task force members and to review and take final action on “non-routine Task Force recommendations,” among other responsibilities that cannot be delegated.</p>
