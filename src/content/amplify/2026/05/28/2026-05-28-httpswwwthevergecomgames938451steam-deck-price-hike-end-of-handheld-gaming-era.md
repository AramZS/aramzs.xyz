<p>For a few glorious years, a $399 portable gadget could run almost anything you’d want to play. In 2022, the Steam Deck finally made PC gaming portable <em>and</em> affordable. I played through the vast majority of <em>Elden Ring</em> on a Steam Deck, agape that such a rich world could comfortably fit between my two hands.</p>
<p>Today, that Steam Deck experience <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/938340/valve-steam-deck-price-increase">starts at $789</a> — nearly double the price.</p>
<p>Similarly, a Nintendo Switch cost $299 at launch — but after Nintendo’s Switch 2 upgrades and “changes in market conditions,” the starting price of today’s Nintendo handheld gaming experience <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/926606/nintendo-switch-2-price-hikes-earnings-results">will soon be $499</a>, more than a disc-less PS5 cost at launch.</p>
<p>You might say <em>so what: doesn’t everything cost more right now?</em> <a href="https://www.theverge.com/tech/880812/ramageddon-ram-shortage-memory-crisis-price-2026-phones-laptops">Welcome to RAMageddon</a>, tariffs, and <a href="https://www.theverge.com/science/888526/what-trumps-war-on-iran-means-for-the-us-energy-crunch">rising oil prices</a> due to Trump’s war on Iran. And I can’t fully blame Nintendo or Valve. Heck, I credit them for being among the last to raise prices.</p>
<p>“Console gaming is continuing its slow and steady march towards becoming a niche, luxury good,” my colleague Andrew Webster <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/926772/nintendo-switch-2-price-hike-console-gaming-future">wrote earlier this month</a>, pointing out how both Sony and Microsoft have hiked prices multiple times and that Nintendo was one of the last holdouts. (He also wrote how <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/789734/game-consoles-too-expensive">everything about buying games is getting more confusing and expensive</a> — and remember when game consoles <a href="https://www.theverge.com/report/782532/game-console-price-hikes-xbox-ps5-switch">used to go down in price instead of up</a>?)</p>
<p>Meanwhile, desktop PC gamers are beginning to worry their hobby may never be affordable again, now that RAM and storage prices have skyrocketed and every chipmaker is chasing AI servers. (<a href="https://www.theverge.com/tech/935026/nvidia-is-officially-no-longer-a-gaming-company-in-financial-earnings">Nvidia isn’t even officially a gaming company</a> anymore.)</p>
<p>But handhelds hit different. They were supposed to be the affordable alternative to consoles and PCs, and I can’t help feeling sad they had such a short time in the full sun.</p>
<p>There wasn’t even enough time for a true Valve or Nintendo competitor to emerge — no other manufacturer ever meaningfully challenged them on price, <a href="https://www.theverge.com/pc-gaming/618709/steam-deck-3-year-anniversary-handheld-gaming-shipments-idc">ceding the market accordingly</a>. When Microsoft finally woke up to the Steam Deck’s threat of pushing Windows gamers toward Linux, <a href="https://www.theverge.com/report/785828/microsoft-xbox-ally-x-pricing-next-gen-consoles-report">it did so at $1,000 instead of $400,</a> pricing the Xbox Ally X like a PC instead of a console.</p>
<p>At $789 rather than $399, the Steam Deck may no longer be a threat to Microsoft’s dominance over Windows gaming. For those with the spare cash, it makes a $1,000 Microsoft / Asus Xbox Ally X actually look good, considering <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/799698/xbox-ally-x-review-asus-microsoft-full-screen-experience">how much more power</a> you get at that price and how <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/843010/xbox-ally-x-sleep-battery-drain-two-months-later">Microsoft keeps plugging away</a> at fixing its flaws.</p>
<p>Every other handheld gaming PC worth its salt costs even more than that now: the Lenovo Legion Go S <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/915278/lenovo-legion-go-s-price-hike-discontinued-ramageddon">is nearly double its launch price</a> at a staggering $1,579.99, <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/906752/lenovo-legion-go-2-suddenly-costs-650-more-as-ramageddon-lays-waste-to-gaming-hardware">the Legion Go 2 costs nearly $2,000</a> with the same chip as the Xbox Ally X, and <a href="https://wccftech.com/msi-claw-8-ex-ai-with-arc-g3-extreme-spotted-at-an-australian-retailer-starting-at-1780/">leaked retail listings suggest </a>Intel’s new handheld platform won’t be much cheaper. <a href="https://www.theverge.com/pc-gaming/769609/msi-claw-8-ai-plus-intel-review">The MSI Claw 8 AI Plus</a> has gone from $1,000 to $1,299 (though I still see it on sale <a href="https://go.skimresources.com/?id=1025X1701640&amp;xs=1&amp;url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.bhphotovideo.com%2Fc%2Fproduct%2F1869644-REG%2Fmsi_claw_8_ai_a2vm_001us_claw_8_ai_a2vm.html">for “only” $1,099</a>).</p>
<p>At these prices in today’s world, we’re just not talking about the same kind of product anymore. It’s no longer “you can afford to try a handheld and experience the joy of gaming everywhere.” It’s “you probably have to choose a handheld instead of something else.”</p>
<p>That kind of zero-sum thinking may impact the value of these handhelds in other ways, too — one of the joys of the Steam Deck was <a href="https://www.theverge.com/2022/8/10/23298962/steam-deck-marvels-spider-man-remastered-playstation-portable-games">how it made PlayStation’s biggest games portable</a>, but Sony reportedly <a href="https://www.theverge.com/games/932817/playstation-exclusive-games-pc">won’t bring its big single-player games to PC anymore</a>. It’s taking that ball and going home.</p>
<p>When I bought my Steam Deck in 2022, I wasn’t sure it would pay off, that I would truly become a handheld gamer again. I already had a perfectly good homebuilt PC.</p>
<p>But I didn’t need to be sure. It only cost $400. That’s not pocket change — but it’s not rent money, either. I know I wouldn’t have bought a $1,000 handheld back then. I’m torn on whether I would now.</p>
