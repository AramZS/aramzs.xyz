<p>The U.S. Department of Education announced Thursday that it’s reallocating funds for minority-serving institutions to the Strengthening Institutions Program, a capacity-building grant for low-resourced colleges that serve high numbers of low-income students.</p>
<p>The grant competition will support colleges’ efforts related to “workforce readiness,” “responsible use of artificial intelligence” and the development of short-term programs.</p>
<p>The investment in the Strengthening Institutions Program for fiscal year 2026—at the expense of MSIs—comes as no surprise to MSI advocates, who have been <a href="https://www.insidehighered.com/news/institutions/minority-serving-institutions/2026/02/25/congress-gave-msis-funding-uncertainty">predicting the move</a> for months. In March, the Education Department <a href="https://www.insidehighered.com/news/government/politics-elections/2026/03/26/ed-leaves-msi-programs-out-grant-eligibility">came out with</a> an eligibility application for grant programs serving low-resourced institutions that only named SIP and made no mention of Hispanic-serving institutions, predominantly Black institutions or other MSI programs.</p>
<p>The department called MSIs “unlawful” and “unconstitutional” in its <a href="https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-and-us-department-of-labor-make-historic-grant-investments-programs-bolster-postsecondary-outcomes">recent press release</a> about SIP’s one-time expansion. The Trump administration has long taken issue with MSIs’ <a href="https://www.insidehighered.com/news/institutions/minority-serving-institutions/2025/07/08/breaking-down-differences-among-msis">enrollment thresholds</a> for particular racial or ethnic groups, whereas SIP has no race-related criteria.</p>
<p>This is the <a href="https://www.insidehighered.com/news/institutions/minority-serving-institutions/2025/09/15/ed-reallocates-msi-funding-hbcus-tribal">second time</a> the administration has shifted discretionary funds away from MSIs to other grantees; last year it directed the money to historically Black colleges and universities and tribal colleges.</p>
<p>“The Strengthening Institutions Program and its accompanying grant awards will equip eligible colleges and universities with the resources they need to prepare students for real-world, in-demand, high-wage careers," U.S. Department of Labor assistant secretary for employment and training Henry Mack said in the release.</p>
