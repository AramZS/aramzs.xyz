<p>On Monday, as <strong>The Washington Post</strong> celebrated its two <strong>Pulitzer Prize</strong> wins, the newspaper found itself scrambling to bring back two previously laid-off video operators to help livestream the celebration because, after the company’s massive layoffs in February, no one remained who could operate the system.</p>
<p>Back inside the paper’s <strong>K Street</strong> headquarters, the former employees encountered a number of changes that stood in stark contrast to their hollowed-out team: a newly renovated opinion studio outfitted with fresh couches, extensive new video equipment, a bar setup, and the kind of natural <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/video/opinions/will-pension-padding-return-to-new-york/2026/05/07/a616376d-afd8-4e6b-a10e-9ff6277f7052_video.html">wood-paneled</a> backdrop increasingly favored by podcasters and creators. Behind the couch sat a small framed American flag print and a larger image of a cowboy on horseback, reflecting the Americana-coded aesthetic that several staffers told <em>Status</em> they viewed as emblematic of the section’s abrupt right-ward shift under opinion editor <strong>Adam O’Neal</strong>.</p>
<p>The studio renovation reflects a much broader effort by O’Neal and owner <strong>Jeff Bezos </strong>to <a href="https://www.status.news/p/washington-post-opinion-cuts-adam-oneal">remake</a> The Post’s opinion offerings into a more personality-driven and video-focused operation, one increasingly geared toward commentary that Bezos has described as centering on the twin issues of “personal liberties and free markets.” The transformation is unfolding after<strong> Donald Trump’s </strong>return to powerand Bezos works to repair his strained relationship with the transactional president, a dynamic that has heightened anxiety inside the <strong>Matt Murray</strong>-led newsroom over the paper’s direction. O’Neal has dutifully carried out that vision, pushing aggressively into video, podcasts, and newsletters, while steering the section toward a more contrarian and conservative-friendly identity.</p>
<p>One current staffer questioned why O’Neal and the opinion section would need a makeshift version of <strong>Joe Rogan’s </strong>podcasting set. “It’s what the owner wants, I guess,” they sighed.</p>
<p>The investment in a new studio space for the opinion section’s <strong>“Make It Make Sense”</strong> <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/podcasts/make-it-make-sense/">podcast</a>, hosted by O’Neal and other members of The Post Editorial Board, has raised eyebrows internally about Bezos’ priorities at the storied Washington newspaper of record after layoffs decimated the newsroom. The video gear alone cost the paper $80,000, according to a person familiar with the matter, although the official line is that the set was built in a cost-effective manner, with the opinion budget having already been cut by more than half.</p>
<p>But with only three people remaining on the video team, down from 60 staffers two years ago, some inside the paper are questioning how the efforts to embrace video will function logistically. One former staffer called the investments “haphazard,” telling <em>Status</em>, “No one is even there anymore to make sure things run properly.”</p>
<p>So far, the investment has produced an astonishingly small audience: just 182 <strong>YouTube</strong> subscribers, with <a href="https://www.youtube.com/@mimsshow">many videos</a> attracting only a few dozen views.</p>
<p>“It does feel like this is just for an audience of one,” one former staffer told <em>Status</em>, alluding to Bezos. “That audience of one is happy to put down money for a studio and programming that doesn’t serve any of the existing audience.” And while O’Neal has discussed making the paper’s editorials more appealing across the ideological spectrum, the former Postie added that the content being produced “isn’t something people on the right want either.”</p>
<p>Coming up on a year into O’Neal’s tenure atop the opinion section, he has earned little goodwill among many staffers, several of whom were eager to stress the divide between the newspaper’s opinion operation and the rest of the newsroom. The tension dates back to the upheaval that followed Bezos’ decision to block the editorial board’s 2024 <strong>Kamala Harris </strong>endorsement. The subsequent directive by the <strong>Amazon</strong> billionaire to reorient the section around his libertarian priorities just as Trump took office triggered resignations, subscriber backlash, and growing concerns inside the newsroom over the owner’s editorial influence.</p>
<p>“I try to avoid reading what the opinions section publishes,” one current staffer told <em>Status</em>. “I can’t tell if some of these arguments are being made in good faith or not. Sometimes it just seems like rage bait,” they said, citing a recent opinion <a href="https://x.com/postopinions/status/2052881486408192471?s=46&amp;t=oGvVrgrwDues7rQHhCtU9g">video</a> covering how Washington taxpayers are increasingly subsidizing those who take public transportation. “It’s reductive to blame all of The Post’s problems on the opinions section,” the staffer noted, but added that so far, “Adam is not helping the cause.”</p>
<p>The newsroom has also taken notice of the opinion section’s outsized interest in <strong>Zohran Mamdani</strong>, having published more than 10 op-eds targeting the Democratic Socialistmayor just this year alone. “There’s this bizarre obsession with Mamdani,” one staffer said, pointing out that the coverage has raised eyebrows internally, given that The Post has no real reason to monitor every beat of <strong>New York</strong> politics. “We’re not even local,” the person added.</p>
<p>A spokesperson for The Post noted that the opinion section saw a six-month high in page views in January, and that the paper has seen an uptick in reader engagement with new subscribers. And while the progress might appear limited, unsigned editorial pieces have become a core product of the section, growing from 6% to 25% year-over-year of the page views section-wide, they said.</p>
<p>Still, some inside The Post question whether the effort is truly about building a sustainable digital business at all, particularly when it comes to Bezos’ motivations. While the opinion section’s new video ventures have clearly struggled to attract an audience, Bezos hasn’t publicly indicated any unhappiness with the section’s trajectory. A Bezos spokesman did not respond to a request for comment.</p>
<p>In March, <strong>The</strong> <strong>New York Times </strong>reported that when former Post opinion editor<strong> David Shipley</strong> warned Bezos that a shift in editorial ideology risked alienating subscribers, the billionaire owner <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/14/business/media/washington-post-jeff-bezos-layoffs.html">responded</a>, “I don’t care.” To current Post staffers, the transformation increasingly appears less like a conventional audience strategy and more like an attempt by Bezos, who has fostered a noticeably cozier relationship withTrump, to reposition the storied newspaper around a worldview more aligned with his own political goals.</p>
