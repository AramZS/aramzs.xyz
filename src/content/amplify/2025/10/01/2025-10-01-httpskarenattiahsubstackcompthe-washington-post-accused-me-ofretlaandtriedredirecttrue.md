<p>Hello everyone. The last few weeks have been quite—— hectic.</p>
<p>Two weeks ago, I broke the news of my <a href="https://karenattiah.substack.com/p/the-washington-post-fired-me-but">sudden and wrongful termination</a> from the Washington Post in the aftermath of the Charlie Kirk murder and Colorado shootings on Sept 10th.</p>
<p>I posted the termination letter that the Washington Post sent a few days ago, which you can read for yourself.</p>

<p>My alleged crime? Doing my journalistic duty, condemning political violence and posting the truth about America, forced performative mourning, and the documented patterns of white male extremist violence.</p>
<p><strong>My plan now: I’m going to fight this. </strong></p>
<p>As documented in<a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2025/09/24/business/media/washington-post-karen-attiah.html"> The New York Times,</a> I am a long-time Washington Post Guild member, and I have filed a grievance to challenge the termination.</p>

<p>Tomorrow, October 2nd, is the anniversary of the brutal murder by Saudi agents of Global Opinions columnist and Saudi writer Jamal Khashoggi, <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2023/10/01/jamal-khashoggi-post-columnist-murder-anniversary/">whom I hired and edited at the Washington Post's opinions section</a>. He, too, was silenced by Saudi media and banned from writing before finding a home to write at the Post. And now, that very same Washington Post that platformed Jamal as a cause for global press freedom has now unjustly removed my voice.</p>
<p>Speaking the truth cost me my Washington Post job. For Jamal, it eventually cost him his life.</p>
<p>So indeed, this is bigger than me, or a job— this fight is about America’s democracy and the First Amendment. It is about global journalistic freedom. It is about the human right to speak the truth about power, even when it is uncomfortable or difficult.</p>
<p>And I know I’m not alone in this.</p>
