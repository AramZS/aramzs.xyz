<h2 data-gu-name="standfirst">A federal judge had blocked Trump’s request to deploy California national guard to Portland </h2>
<p>Before a federal judge blocked <a data-component="auto-linked-tag" data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/donaldtrump">Donald Trump</a> from putting members of California’s national guard on the streets of Portland, Oregon, late on Sunday, the state’s Republican party welcomed the planned deployment in celebratory posts on social media.</p>
<p>“President Trump on Sunday deployed 300 California National Guard troops to Portland, Oregon after a judge ruled that the Oregon National Guard could not be deployed to keep federal facilities and personnel in Portland safe,” Oregon Republicans wrote on their official <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.facebook.com/oregonrepublicanparty/posts/pfbid022PFtHyDvS2uPkTSKKXb12T1ptcU3AQAAN7J7nmXVciQN8HCze1PTTDNaKM1NELktl">Facebook</a>, <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.instagram.com/p/DPcU1xwAbcv/?hl=en">Instagram</a> and <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://x.com/Oregon_GOP/status/1974950362592485821">X</a> accounts.</p>
<p>On all three platforms, the statement was illustrated with an image that seemed designed to support Trump’s false claim that protests against immigration sweeps in <a data-component="auto-linked-tag" data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/portland">Portland</a> are so out of control that the city is “burning to the ground”. On one side of the image, a line of police officers held riot shields; on the other, a crowd of young men held up flares that lit up a night sky filled with red smoke.</p>

<p>On closer inspection, however, it turned out that the image was not a photograph of a real event in Portland, but instead a fabrication created by combining two photographs of scenes that unfolded in South America nearly a decade apart.</p>
<p>The image of the police officers comes from a photo of “South American riot police” that was <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.gettyimages.com/detail/photo/south-american-riot-police-royalty-free-image/157402471">uploaded to the Getty Images archive</a> in 2008. A clear visual clue that the photograph was not taken in Portland was that the first officer’s shield is marked “Policia”, the Spanish or Portuguese word for police.</p>

<p>The description on the Getty website does not specify the country it was taken in, but <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.gettyimages.com/detail/photo/two-south-american-riot-police-royalty-free-image/173594929">another, similar image</a> uploaded a few days later by the same photographer says that it was taken in Ecuador. The initials of an Ecuadorian police unit from that time are visible on <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.gettyimages.com/detail/photo/row-of-south-american-riot-police-royalty-free-image/173587144">a third photograph</a> in the set.</p>
<p>The image of the fiery demonstration that the officers seem to float above in the composite shared by the Oregon Republican party turned out to be <a data-link-name="in body link" href="https://www.pexels.com/photo/anonymous-people-standing-on-street-among-smoke-during-protests-at-night-4636148/">another image</a> from the free archive site Pexels. It was taken by a Brazilian photographer in 2017.</p>

<p>When a Guardian reporter pointed out on social media that the image was not a genuine photograph of the generally small and tame protests outside the Immigration and Customs Enforcement field office in Portland, the Oregon Republican Party’s X account replied: “We’re not reporters, just bad memers.”</p>
