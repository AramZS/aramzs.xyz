<p>Samsung warned us last month that <a href="https://www.theverge.com/news/780757/samsung-brings-ads-to-us-fridges">ads were coming</a> to the giant Android tablets embedded in its Family Hub smart fridges. I’ve been eyeing mine ever since — and the first ones are about to arrive. Starting November 3rd, the <a href="https://www.samsung.com/us/home-appliances/refrigerators/all-refrigerators/?shop=Buy+Online&amp;key_category_features=Family+Hub%C3%A2%C2%84%C2%A2&amp;CID=afl-ecomm-rkt-cha-040122-url_Skimlinks.com&amp;utm_source=url_Skimlinks.com&amp;utm_medium=affiliate&amp;utm_campaign=1&amp;utm_content=2116208&amp;rktevent=Skimlinks.com_TnL5HPStwNw-n24x3XuwNzu1Wqrmci_bmw&amp;ranMID=47773&amp;ranEAID=TnL5HPStwNw&amp;ranSiteID=TnL5HPStwNw-n24x3XuwNzu1Wqrmci_bmw">$2,000-plus connected fridges</a> will get a new widget that serves up ads, Shane Higby, head of Home Appliance Business at Samsung Electronics America, confirmed to <em>The Verge</em>.</p>
<p>The ads will be part of a new widget on some of the smart fridges’ “Cover screen themes” (like a tablet or smartphone’s home screen). The widget, which Samsung shared with me ahead of today’s <a href="https://news.samsung.com/us/samsung-family-hub-2025-update-elevates-smart-home-ecosystem/?utm_source=pr_media&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_campaign=general">announcement</a>, has four rotating screens. One showing news, one calendar events, one the weather forecast, and one with “curated advertisements.”</p>

<p>This widget appears in the bottom third of the fridge’s screen and rotates every 10 seconds among the four screens. You can swipe to rotate through them faster. <a href="https://news.samsung.com/us/samsung-family-hub-2025-update-elevates-smart-home-ecosystem/?utm_source=pr_media&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_campaign=general">Samsung says</a> the widget will only appear on the Weather and Color theme screens, not on the Art or Album ones. A new Daily Board screen also won’t have the widget, but it will show an ad in one of the six tiles.</p>
<p>The update will start rolling out to all US-based Family Hub fridges with the larger 21.5- and 32-inch screens this week, starting on Monday, October 27th, with the ads beginning to appear a week or so later.</p>

<p>The problem here isn’t just the ads themselves (although they are a problem); it’s that they are being added to the device <em>after</em> it’s in my home. If I had signed up for Telly — the <a href="https://www.theverge.com/televisions/777588/telly-tv-hands-on-ads">free TV that comes with its own ad billboard</a> attached to the bottom of the screen — I’d be fine with ads, because they were part of the deal. No one who bought an Echo Show or a Family Hub fridge did so knowing it would show them ads.</p>
<blockquote><p>I can’t help but feel it’s a slippery slope to breakfast with full-screen Cialis ads.</p></blockquote>
<p>I’ll admit Samsung’s current advertising widget is <em>slightly</em> less offensive than the full-screen ads on the Echo Show — although the nearly 32-inch screen in the fridge is a lot more visible in my home than the 8-inch Show screen. It will also, according to Higby, initially only show ads for Samsung products and services, such as the Samsung fridge water filter shown in the demo image. But I can’t help but feel it’s a slippery slope from this to breakfast with full-screen Cialis ads.</p>
<p>It seems that Samsung plans to open this to third-party advertisers. Higby told me via email that while the ads may include Samsung appliance care and accessories, “… future promotions will depend on the feedback and insights gained from the program.” A presentation by Samsung Ads exec Travis Scott Howe to advertisers earlier this year touted this new program as a way to bring “<a href="https://www.adexchanger.com/tv/samsung-might-be-putting-ads-on-their-smart-fridges-after-all/">your brand message to every screen in the connected home</a>.”</p>
<h2>How to turn off ads on your Family Hub</h2>
<p>The widget will appear by default on the fridges as part of the software update. However, Samsung is doing better than Amazon here by offering users the option to turn off ads. To do this, go to the Settings page on the fridge, scroll to Advertisements, select it, and you’ll be taken to a screen where you can toggle off ads.</p>
<p>This will remove the widget entirely. If you think you might actually like the widget’s other features (calendar, weather, and news), you can “X” out a particular ad, and it won’t pop up again. But then you’ll get another ad.</p>
<p>As mentioned, the widget is coming to all Family Hub smart fridges with the larger screens in the US (no other countries). However, Samsung confirmed it will not be on fridges with the 9-inch screens or on any of the other <a href="https://www.theverge.com/2024/12/18/24324086/samsungs-screens-everywhere-initiative">appliances with 9- or 7-inch AI Home screens</a> that Samsung recently launched, which include ovens, washing machines, and dryers.</p>

<p>When I interviewed Jeong Seung Moon, Samsung’s head of the R&amp;D team for the Digital Appliances Business at Samsung Electronics, <a href="https://www.theverge.com/tech/654685/samsung-screens-everywhere-home-appliances-strategy-interview-exclusive">earlier this year</a>, he said there were “no plans regarding the inclusion of advertisements on AI Home screens.” Clearly, plans changed.</p>
<p>If we’re splitting hairs here, technically the Family Hub screen isn’t an <a href="https://news.samsung.com/global/samsung-expands-ai-home-across-multiple-home-appliances-bringing-screens-everywhere-vision-to-life">AI Home screen</a>. That designation seems to apply only to the 9- and 7-inch screens. I’ve asked numerous Samsung folks about the distinction here, but no one has a straight answer. Either way, my guess is we’ll see ads on those screens eventually, too.</p>
<h2>Added value?</h2>
<p>When I asked Higby why they were bringing ads to the fridges, he said via email: “This pilot further explores how a connected appliance can deliver genuinely useful, contextual information. The refrigerator is already a daily hub, and we’re testing a responsible, user-controlled way to make that space more helpful.”</p>
<p>This is similar to the justification Panos Panay, Amazon’s head of Devices &amp; Services, made to me last month when I asked him about advertising on its Echo devices. <a href="https://www.theverge.com/report/797672/amazon-echo-show-ads-alexa-plus#:~:text=At%20Amazon%E2%80%99s%20hardware,a%20customer%20needs.%E2%80%9D">He said</a> it was looking to be “elegantly elevating the information that a customer needs.”</p>
<blockquote><p>“We’re testing a responsible, user-controlled way to make that space more helpful”</p></blockquote>
<p>Both companies claim they want to offer “curated,” “relevant” ads that might “enhance the experience.” I can buy that to some extent when it’s ads for features that your smart fridge or smart display offers. This tech is complicated and capable, and most people only tap into a fraction of what it can do.</p>
<p>But I don’t see any future where third-party advertisements will ever be welcome in people’s homes in this form — even if they happen to show me a brand of pet food right when my dog is looking at me with hungry eyes.</p>
<p>The bigger issue here is that of trust. While Samsung says the ads on Family Hub will be “contextual or non-personal” and that the fridges “are not collecting personal information or tracking consumers,” that’s today. There’s understandable concern among smart home users about how the data <a href="https://www.consumerreports.org/electronics/privacy/smart-appliances-and-privacy-a1186358482/">smart appliances collect</a> will be used. And now they have to worry about whether, one day, that will be to serve up more ads.</p>
<p>These appliances are in our homes. It’s one thing to have cookies tracking you on websites on your personal computer or phone, so that the air fryer you once eyed follows you everywhere online. It would be another thing to start seeing personalized ads on screens around your home, something that would not only feel intrusive but could be damaging in multi-person households. Personalized ads can reveal things you might not want your spouse, roommates, or kids to know about. (Remember how Target <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/kashmirhill/2012/02/16/how-target-figured-out-a-teen-girl-was-pregnant-before-her-father-did/">figured out a girl</a> was pregnant before her family did?)</p>
