<p>Tesla held a special pop-up event over the weekend for Art Basel Miami Beach, the international art fair in Florida. The pop-up was <a href="https://www.notateslaapp.com/news/3385/tesla-announces-new-autonomy-visualized-event-in-miami-this-week">dubbed</a> ‚ÄúThe Future of Autonomy Visualized‚Äù and reportedly featured Elon Musk‚Äôs Cybercab prototype and Optimus robots. But a video from the event is going viral for all the wrong reasons. And it‚Äôs pretty hilarious.</p>
<p>The video, which appears to have first been posted to <a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/teslamotors/comments/1pfzxt0/tesla_optimus/">r/teslamotors</a> on Reddit, shows an Optimus robot knock over several bottles of water on a table before lifting its arms into the air. The arms move in ways that would be consistent with taking off a VR headset, and then they fall heavily, with one hitting a water on the table that seems to explode and shoot water everywhere. After that, Optimus appears to go lifeless and falls backward.</p>
<p>The video is just five seconds long, but it tells quite a funny story in that short burst of time.</p>
<p>What‚Äôs happening here? Many people online speculate that an unseen person was controlling the Optimus robot, and that person took off their headset before disconnecting. And that seems to be the most likely explanation.</p>
<p>This kind of robotic control is called teleoperation, and it has been in existence since at least the 1940s. Sometimes called a waldo, Walt Disney showed off how he made the 1964 New York World‚Äôs Fair attraction Carousel of Progress with a similar technology.</p>
<figure><img alt="Gif: Disney" src="https://gizmodo.com/app/uploads/2024/03/99369f1ea659fe58de7b960a87d20a43.gif"/><figcaption>A robot from the 1964 World‚Äôs Fair attraction Carousel of Progress is demonstrated by Walt Disney and an engineer using a waldo-style manipulation device. Gif: Disney</figcaption></figure>
<p>Disney and his engineers didn‚Äôt have VR headsets, of course, but the basic idea is the same. Someone who can‚Äôt be seen by the intended audience is manipulating controls that actually move the robot in real time.</p>
<p>This new viral video from Art Basel isn‚Äôt the first time Musk has been caught stretching the truth about his Optimus robot‚Äôs capabilities. Back in January 2024, Musk posted a video showing <a href="https://gizmodo.com/elon-musk-tesla-robot-optimus-laundry-folding-shirt-hum-1851170890">Optimus folding a shirt</a>. Musk‚Äôs legion of fans on X celebrated the achievement, since folding laundry is both a practical application of humanoid robotics that people have been wanting for decades, as well as a maneuver that‚Äôs hard for humanoid robots to do well.</p>
<p>But it turned out Musk wasn‚Äôt being very transparent when he initially posted the video. Some eagle-eyed social media users started asking why we could see what appeared to be a hand coming briefly into frame in the lower-right corner. The hand seemed to match up perfectly with what Optimus was doing.</p>
<figure><img alt="Tesla's Optimus Robot Folding Laundry" src="https://gizmodo.com/app/uploads/2024/07/tesla-folding-laundry.gif"/><figcaption>Tesla‚Äôs Optimus robot folding laundry in Jan. 2024 with an annotation of a red arrow added by Gizmodo showing the human hand. GIF: Tesla / Gizmodo</figcaption></figure>
<p>That was almost two years ago, and there have been other examples from Musk‚Äôs Optimus hype machine. For example, Musk hosted an event in Los Angeles in October 2024, announcing his Cybercab concept car that he promised was just 2-3 years away. After the event, there was a party featuring Optimus robots that were pouring beers and handing them out to guests. Guests were impressed until tech evangelist Robert Scoble started asking some questions. Scoble was told that the robots were <a href="https://gizmodo.com/elon-musks-beer-pouring-optimus-robots-are-not-autonomous-2000510899">teleoperated</a>.</p>
<p>The Tesla Diner in Los Angeles has featured Optimus robots handing out popcorn, though many people have speculated that those bots are teleoperated, as well. Tesla didn‚Äôt respond to questions on Monday about whether there was someone operating Optimus at the Miami event from a remote location.</p>
<p>Ever since the shirt-folding video, it‚Äôs become pretty standard for people on social media to ask whether a given robot video shows real autonomy. There was even a period last year when just about every robot maker started slapping text on their videos to assure viewers their creations <a href="https://gizmodo.com/robot-legit-elon-musk-fake-optimus-tesla-demo-astribot-1851455513">weren‚Äôt being teleoperated</a>. Musk‚Äôs showmanship and sleight of hand have naturally made people suspicious.</p>
<p>Musk was recently asked whether a video of Optimus ‚Äúlearning Kung Fu‚Äù was autonomous. Musk replied, ‚ÄúAI, not teleoperated.‚Äù But obviously, people were skeptical given the fact that you can see a guy holding a video game controller in the background. Gizmodo couldn‚Äôt independently verify that the video shows Optimus working autonomously.</p>
