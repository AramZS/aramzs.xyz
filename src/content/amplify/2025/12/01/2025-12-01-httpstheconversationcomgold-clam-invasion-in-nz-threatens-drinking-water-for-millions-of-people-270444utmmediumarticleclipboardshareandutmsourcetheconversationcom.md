<p>As a geochemist studying New Zealand’s freshwater systems, I’ve spent years tracking the subtle chemical shifts in our rivers and lakes.</p>
<p>But nothing prepared me for the rapid transformation unfolding in the Waikato River since the invasion of the Asian clam (<em>Corbicula fluminea</em>, also known as the freshwater gold clam).</p>
<p>First detected in May 2023 in Lake Karāpiro, a reservoir lake on the Waikato, this bivalve is now altering the river’s chemistry in ways that could jeopardise drinking water for up to two million people, disrupt hydroelectric power and undermine decades of ecosystem restoration efforts.</p>
<p>Our team’s work reveals how these clams are depleting essential minerals like calcium from the water, impairing arsenic removal during treatment and signalling a rapid escalation with broader impacts ahead.</p>

<p>Native to eastern Asia, the gold clam can self-fertilise and spreads via contaminated gear, birds or floods. Climate change will likely accelerate its invasion.</p>
<p>The problem is already spreading quickly beyond the Waikato River. A recent <a href="https://www.trc.govt.nz/council/news-and-events/council-news/freshwater-clam-found-in-new-plymouth-lake">detection in a Taranaki lake</a> has led to waterway closures. And warnings for the Whanganui River underscore the urgent need for national vigilance.</p>
<h2>A silent invasion with big consequences</h2>
<p>The Waikato River stretches 425 km from Lake Taupō to the Tasman Sea, powering nine hydroelectric dams and supplying drinking water to Auckland, Hamilton and beyond.</p>
<p>It’s a taonga (cultural treasure) central to Māori identity and the subject of a landmark restoration strategy, <a href="https://waikatoriver.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2019/03/Vision-and-Strategy-Reprint-2019web.pdf">Te Ture Whaimana o Te Awa o Waikato</a>, that aims to revive the river’s mauri (life force).</p>
<p>In late 2024, arsenic levels in treated Waikato water briefly exceeded safe limits of 0.01 milligrams per litre (mg/L), triggering alarms at treatment plants. Investigations ruled out typical culprits such as geothermal spikes. Instead, our analysis points to the clams.</p>
<p>By filtering water and building calcium carbonate shells, the clams are drawing down dissolved calcium by 25% below historical norms. But calcium is crucial for water treatment processes because it helps bind and remove contaminants such as <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jhazmat.2008.10.038">arsenic</a>.</p>
<p>Our modelling estimates the clams are forming up to 30 tonnes of calcium carbonate daily in Lake Karāpiro alone. This suggests lake-wide densities averaging around 300 individuals per square metre. <a href="https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/64359-Corbicula-fluminea-suppression-trial-step-one-baseline">2025 surveys</a> show hotspots with up to 1,134 clams per square metre.</p>
<p>The result? Impaired arsenic removal. Without stable calcium, flocs (clumps of particles) don’t form properly, letting arsenic slip through.</p>
<p>While the exceedances were <a href="https://hamilton.govt.nz/your-council/news/community-environment/no-cause-for-concern-after-slightly-elevated-levels-of-arsenic-detected-in-treated-waikato-river-water">short-lived</a> and contained through quick adjustments, they exposed vulnerabilities in a system optimised for historically consistent river chemistry.</p>

<h2>How the clams are changing the river</h2>
<p>The gold clam isn’t just a filter-feeder; it’s an ecosystem engineer. Each clam can process up to a <a href="https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10452-021-09871-4">litre of water per hour</a>, sequestering calcium for shells while releasing ammonia and bicarbonate.</p>
<p>Our data from 2024-2025, collected at multiple sites, show these shifts are most pronounced in deeper waters. Statistical tests confirm patterns absent in pre-invasion records.</p>
<p>Longer residence times in the reservoir lake (up to seven days) exacerbate the issue. Faster flushing correlates with higher growth rates, as clams ramp up activity. But prolonged retention in warmer months can lead to hypoxia (low oxygen), with the potential to trigger <a href="https://doi.org/10.1111/fwb.13033">mass die-offs</a> that release toxins or <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gca.2010.03.024">mobilise sediment-bound arsenic</a>.</p>

<p>These changes threaten more than water treatment. Clams could biofoul dam intakes and reduce hydroelectric efficiency in a river that generates 13% of New Zealand’s power (<a href="https://environment.govt.nz/assets/Publications/Files/waikato-scoping-report-appendices-23-26.pdf#:%7E:text=MRP%20operates%20the%20Waikato%20River%20hydro%20system,power%20sources%20(located%20in%20the%20South%20Island).">25% at peak</a>). Native species like kākahi (freshwater mussels) face <a href="https://doi.org/10.1002/ece3.8737">competition</a> and <a href="https://niwa.co.nz/freshwater/stopping-gold-clam-its-now-or-never/understanding-clam-impacts/exploring-clam-impact-native-aquatic-plants#:%7E:text=native%20aquatic%20plants-,The%20invasive%20gold%20clam%20(Corbicula%20fluminea)%20can%20reach%20very%20high,no%20added%20NH4%2DN.">shifts in nutrient cycling</a> could fuel <a href="https://www.mpi.govt.nz/fishing-aquaculture/recreational-fishing/where-unsafe-to-collect-shellfish/what-are-toxic-algal-blooms">algal blooms</a>, clashing with restoration goals.</p>
<h2>Climate risks and stressors in a warming world</h2>
<p>Amid these ongoing changes, climate projections indicate that hot, dry events – such as <a href="https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10584-022-03427-7">prolonged heatwaves or droughts</a> – are likely to become more frequent. Such conditions could reduce river flows and elevate water temperatures, lowering dissolved oxygen levels and creating low-oxygen zones.</p>
<p>If clam densities continue to rise exponentially, a mass die-off might occur. This would release pulses of <a href="https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1899/04-073.1">ammonia</a> and organic matter that further deplete dissolved oxygen. This, in turn, could promote <a href="https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/es980541c">arsenic mobilisation from sediments</a> and harmful algal blooms in nutrient-enriched, stagnant waters.</p>
<p>This could necessitate supply restrictions for affected communities. Ecologically, it might kill fish and disrupt native biodiversity. Economically, it could interrupt industries reliant on the river.</p>
<h2>From the Waikato to a nationwide threat</h2>
<p>The invasion isn’t contained. The clam, which can produce up to <a href="https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/long-term-biosecurity-management-programmes/invasive-freshwater-clam-corbicula/about-invasive-freshwater-clams-and-where-they-have-been-found">70,000 juveniles</a> annually, thrives in warm, nutrient-rich waters. It is notoriously hard to eradicate once established.</p>
<p>In mid-November, the Taranaki Regional Council confirmed the gold clam in <a href="https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/578559/lake-rotomanu-closed-to-motorised-boats-after-discovery-of-invasive-clam">Lake Rotomanu</a>. Just days later, warnings were issued to boaties on the <a href="https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/11/18/gold-clam-threat-prompts-warning-to-whanganui-river-boaties/">Whanganui River</a>, urging rigorous “check, clean, dry” protocols.</p>
<p>Without intervention, the clams could reach other systems, including the Clutha or Waitaki, and compound pressures on New Zealand’s already stressed freshwaters.</p>
<p>Our research highlights the need for integrated action. Monitoring should expand, incorporating environmental DNA for early detection and calcium isotope tracing to pinpoint clam impacts. Water providers could trial calcium dosing during peak growth periods.</p>
<p>But solutions must honour Te Tiriti o Waitangi principles. Collaboration with iwi and blending mātauranga Māori (indigenous knowledge) with science, such as using tikanga indicators for water health, is essential. Biosecurity measures including gear decontamination campaigns are critical to slow spread.</p>

<p>This invasion intersects with New Zealand’s evolving water policy framework, particularly the <a href="https://www.dia.govt.nz/water-Services-Policy-and-Legislation">Local Water Done Well</a> regime which replaced the repealed Three Waters reforms in late 2023.</p>
<p>Councils are now implementing delivery plans and focusing on financial sustainability and infrastructure upgrades. The <a href="https://www.taumataarowai.govt.nz/">Water Services Authority Taumata Arawai</a> continues as the national regulator, enforcing standards amid an estimated <a href="https://infrastructure.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2023/12/Infrastructure-NZ-Policy-Postions-Water-Infrastructure-002.pdf">NZ$185-260 billion infrastructure deficit</a>.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/579978/no-more-regional-councils-major-shake-up-of-local-government-announced">Recent government announcements</a> propose further streamlining, including replacing regional councils with <a href="https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2511/S00205/simpler-more-cost-effective-local-government.htm">panels of mayors or territories boards</a>, while encouraging amalgamations to <a href="https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/26-11-2025/why-regional-councils-will-cease-to-exist-and-what-comes-next">simplify planning and infrastructure delivery</a>. These changes aim to make local government more cost-effective and responsive to issues such as housing growth and infrastructure funding.</p>
<p>But a hot or dry event could test the effectiveness of water policy, potentially straining inter-council coordination for shared resources such as the Waikato River and highlighting gaps in emergency response.</p>
<p>Globally, the gold clam has cost <a href="https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ddi.13501">billions in damages</a>. New Zealand can’t afford to wait. By acting now, we can protect Te Awa o Waikato and safeguard water security for generations.</p>
