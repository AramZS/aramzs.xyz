<ul> <li><strong>Black A3 watercolor paper</strong> — the canvas. Thick enough to handle multiple media.</li> <li><strong>Derwent Chinese White drawing pencil</strong> — for shading, textures, and soft highlights. Great for layered pencil work but won’t reach stark white on its own.</li> <li><strong>Uni Posca white pen (0.7mm)</strong> — my thinner acrylic marker for fine line-work and labels.</li> <li><strong>Bourne bullet-tip white marker (5mm)</strong> — the bold acrylic for titles, border, and big white planes.</li> <li><strong>UV / moonlight pen</strong> — the invisible ink that shows up under a UV torch. This lets me add secret messages and eldritch imagery that only reveal under black light.</li> <li><strong>Small UV torch</strong> — I use a curing mini UV used for miniatures; cheap and effective.</li> <li><strong>Yellow and orange colored pencils</strong> — for warm “candlelight” glows behind labels.</li> <li><strong>Fine eraser &amp; pencil sharpener</strong> — again, precision counts with a black substrate.</li> </ul>

<ol> <li>Start with the coastline and cliffs, because the sea edge defines how the rest sits.</li> <li>Place the lighthouse on a promontory so it visually dominates the harbor.</li> <li>Lay the main road from harbor to town center; cluster high-traffic buildings (inn, town hall) along it.</li> <li>Scatter lesser alleys, docks, and industrial spaces nearer the shore.</li> <li>Reserve the hills and trees behind town for wilderness that feels threatening — make those areas visually distinct later.</li> </ol>
<ul> <li>To indicate the geometric, civilized structures — smooth planes for walls, flat shading for whitewashed facades.</li> <li>To render the wild areas — trees, cliffs, rocks — with a sharp, scratchy technique that reads as chaotic and dangerous.</li> </ul>

<ul> <li>Plan where the big white planes will be — landmark walls, the lighthouse, the university facade — and use the larger pen for those.</li> <li>Use the finer pen for the building interiors: window panes, shutters, roof ridges, and small signs.</li> <li>Because the pens are thicker than any fine liners I normally use on white paper, adjust the density of texture; broad cross-hatch and stipples will look heavy if overused.</li> </ul>

<ul> <li>Finish a step completely before making corrective changes.</li> <li>Squint frequently to see the overall contrast and balance.</li> <li>Test the map under different lights — white light and low light — to see how it reads at the table.</li> </ul>

<ul> <li>Test the UV pen on your black paper; it slightly changes surface sheen and can show under bright light as a texture difference.</li> <li>Decide whether players find the map already aware it contains secrets. I usually give the map to players with a hint that “the ink you need is to hand,” so they know to look for a code or tool.</li> <li>Use scratchy, unreal drawing styles for eldritch images — avoid realism; make the shapes feel unnatural.</li> </ul>

<ul> <li>Sketches of a few monstrous silhouettes placed where the party might later encounter cult activity.</li> <li>Notes like “Necronomicon in the archives” (a public-level hint) and “I left it here” (a double-locked indication that needs context).</li> <li>Small glyphs or cult sigils that can be traced to NPCs or hidden rooms.</li> </ul>
<ul> <li>Not all “mistakes” are bad; they can add character consistent with a horror theme.</li> <li>If a pen leaks, consider repeating the motif to make it look deliberate.</li> <li>Make a double border — one thick, one thin — but wait for each layer to dry completely before proceeding.</li> </ul>

<ul> <li>Give players the physical map at campaign start, but accompany it with a UV torch or a hint that “the town hides more than what the eye shows.” This invites immediate investigation.</li> <li>Alternatively, let players find the map as part of an early quest. In that case, hiding the UV ink until they get the torch heightens the drama of discovery.</li> <li>When players decode Layer B, keep Layer C available. The point of double-locked secrets is to reward in-world effort — speaking to NPCs, collecting items, or returning to locations with new perspective.</li> </ul>
<ul> <li><strong>Smudging and drying:</strong> Let each pen pass dry fully. Acrylic markers take longer and will smear if you brush across them.</li> <li><strong>Hand orientation:</strong> Left-handed creators should work right-to-left; right-handed do left-to-right.</li> <li><strong>Too much detail:</strong> Finish a layer, step back, and then de-clutter with extra pencil planes or subtle glows. Don’t rework mid-layer.</li> <li><strong>Stipple and fine texture limits:</strong> Acrylic spreads and won’t produce crisp stipples like fines liners on white paper. Use hatching, cross-hatching, and larger textural marks to imply detail.</li> <li><strong>Digital scans:</strong> If you want to give a digital copy to remote players, scan in high resolution and consider a slight adjustment to contrast so the white reads well on screens. UV ink will not scan visibly; create a separate “decoded” digital layer (a PNG or an annotated PDF) for players who unlock secrets remotely.</li> </ul>
<ul> <li>“I left it here” (with an arrow to a rooftop that later requires an NPC’s key or a grappling hook to access).</li> <li>“Stand at the bell tower and face the moon at the full.” (A ritual trigger that needs timing.)</li> <li>“Ask the cobbler about the smell.” (An NPC-specific clue — players must find and question the cobbler.)</li> </ul>
<ul> <li>A physical handout with the UV secrets intact for local groups.</li> <li>A digital, decoded dossier for players not present at the table or for reproduction purposes.</li> </ul>
