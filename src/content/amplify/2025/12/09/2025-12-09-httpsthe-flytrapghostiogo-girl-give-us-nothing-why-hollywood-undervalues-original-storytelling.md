<p>After three seasons steeped in cringe, <em>And Just Like That</em> has been put out of its misery. Packaged as a spinoff of <em>Sex and the City</em>, the show followed Carrie Bradshaw (Sarah Jessica Parker) as she navigated her 50s. Debuting in December 2021, the series premiere opted for an immediate water cooler moment: Carrie’s husband, Mr. Big (Chris Noth), has a fatal heart attack while riding his Peloton. At first, this narrative decision seemed primed for rich storytelling. How would widowhood irrevocably change Carrie, and how would she <a href="https://www.vulture.com/article/and-just-like-that-episode-1-season-1-recap-hello-its-me.html?ref=the-flytrap.ghost.io"><u>process her grief</u></a>? But that initial promise of introspection and nuance never materialized.</p>
<p>Instead, as the first season progressed, <em>AJLT </em>made <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2021/12/09/arts/television/review-and-just-like-that.html?ref=the-flytrap.ghost.io"><u>narrative decisions</u></a> that were completely antithetical to the character arcs in <em>Sex and the City</em>. Carrie built her career on writing frank explorations of sex, dating, and relationships. No topic was too taboo, from orgasms and masturbation to threesomes and fetishes. The <em>AJLT</em> version of Carrie has devolved to feed into society’s misogynistic shaming of women’s bodies. In the <a href="https://www.usatoday.com/story/life/health-wellness/2023/06/28/and-just-like-that-sex-city-carrie-bradshaw-vagina-episode/70361854007/?ref=the-flytrap.ghost.io"><u>second episode</u></a>, Carrie, now a podcaster, refuses to read an advertisement about a vaginal odor product. She’s too embarrassed to say the word “vagina.” Foregoing this ad leads to the podcast’s cancellation and the entire studio shutting down.</p>
<blockquote>Hollywood’s aversion to original stories isn’t a bug; it’s a long-standing feature. </blockquote>
