<p>By Ana Lankes</p>
<p>In 1968 Oscar Niemeyer, <a data-analytics="in_body:link_1:para_1" href="https://www.economist.com/leaders/2025/08/28/brazil-offers-america-a-lesson-in-democratic-maturity">Brazil’s</a> most celebrated architect and a communist, put his principles aside to design the headquarters of the armed forces, which had recently seized power in a <a data-analytics="in_body:link_2:para_1" href="https://www.economist.com/asia/2025/07/09/is-thailand-heading-for-another-coup">coup</a>. Today the modernist marvel hulks like a <a data-analytics="in_body:link_3:para_1" href="https://www.economist.com/science-and-technology/2025/08/27/a-successful-test-flight-puts-musks-starship-back-on-track">spaceship</a> in the centre of Brasília. But in the first week of January 2023, its clean lines were disturbed by hundreds of tents haphazardly arranged outside the building. Was some kind of festival taking place? The camp was dotted with stalls handing out beer, grilled ribs and bowls of rice with salted beef, known as <i>arroz carreteiro.</i></p>
