<p>I hate doing Google’s CAPTCHAs.</p>
<p>Part of it is the sheer hassle of repeatedly identifying objects — traffic lights, staircases, palm trees and buses — just so I can finish a web search. I also don’t like being forced to donate free labor to AI companies to help train their visual-recognition systems.</p>
<p>But a while ago, while numbly clicking on grainy images of fire hydrants, I was struck by another reason:</p>
<p>The images are deeply, overwhelmingly <em>depressing.</em></p>
<p>CAPTCHA images are never joyful vistas of human activity, full of Whitmanesque vigor. No, they’re blurry, anonymous landscapes that possess a positively Soviet anomie. Here’s a typical one …</p>

<p>Each cube here is a tone poem in melancholia. Looking at these leaden vistas of America makes you, slightly but noticeably, feel worse than you did before.</p>
<p>Why are these photos so depressing? What is it about their composition that is so enervating? I’ve been musing on this for a few months now, and had <a href="https://twitter.com/Emdashes/status/1339769599848570886">some</a> terrific <a href="https://twitter.com/pomeranian99/status/1339749551725547520">exchanges</a> about <a href="https://twitter.com/Emdashes/status/1339752195433123842">it</a> with <a href="https://twitter.com/toddpruzan">Todd Pruzan</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/Emdashes">Emily Gordon</a>, two friends of mine, on Twitter.</p>
<p>I think I’ve figured it out, and so now I present — <strong>The Six Reasons CAPTCHA Pictures Make You Feel Like Crap:</strong></p>
<h2 data-selectable-paragraph="">1) They’re devoid of humans</h2>
<p>CAPTCHA images are pictures of the outside world, but it’s a world that is unsettlingly bare of people. This is likely for privacy reasons, which is a laudable motive on Google’s part. But it winds up making the pictures look totally postapocalyptic. Each CAPTCHA depicts a world blasted by a neutron bomb, where the objects survive but none of the people…</p>
